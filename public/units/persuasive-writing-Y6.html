Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to write a sophisticated persuasive piece on racial justice and speaking up against injustice — using precise evidence, counter-narrative, rhetorical mastery, and a powerful authorial voice to argue for change.

Success Criteria:

  • I can argue for racial justice and the importance of speaking up against systemic injustice.
  • I can use evidence (historical, statistical, experiential) to support my argument with precision and credibility.
  • I can acknowledge and dismantle counter-arguments by exposing their flawed premises.
  • I can use advanced rhetorical techniques (anaphora, epistrophe, chiasmus, loaded language, inclusive/exclusive language) with mastery.
  • I can write with an authoritative, passionate voice that holds its ground under pressure.
  • I can structure a multi-layered persuasive piece: hook, context, argument, evidence, dismantling counter-arguments, conclusion with call to action.

    WEEK 1 — INTRODUCTION TO THE HATE U GIVE

    Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)"Think about what it means to be silent when you witness an injustice. Think about what it costs — to the person who suffers, to the community, to the bystander. Write an argument about why speaking up matters — and why silence is complicity."Think
    Task (45 min)Cold Task: Write a persuasive piece arguing for the importance of speaking up against racial injustice and injustice of all kinds. Year 6: 450–650 words. Focus: Can you make a clear, passionate argument about speaking up?Independent writing

    Differentiation: Context sheets on systemic racism available. Sentence starters for persuasive techniques.

    Lesson 2: A Black Girl in America (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (20 min)Read *The Hate U Give* — Prologue and Chapter 1 (Starr's two worlds — Garden Heights and Williamson). Angie Thomas establishes immediately: Starr lives in two worlds and must decide how much of each to show. This tension is the heart of the novel — and of speaking up.Analyze
    Discuss (15 min)"What is the 'hate u give'? The title comes from a Tupac quote: 'Thug Life' — what the system does to Black communities comes back around. But it also means: what you give (to others) is what you get (yourself). The novel argues: silence is violence. Speaking up is a responsibility."Understand title/theme
    Share (5 min)"What does it cost to speak up? What does it cost to stay silent?"Consider

    Success Criteria Check: Can explain the connection between the novel's title, Starr's dilemma, and the theme of speaking up.

    Lesson 3: Why Silence Is Complicity (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"In The Hate U Give, Starr learns: There is no neutral. When you witness an injustice and do nothing, you are choosing the oppressor. This is not a comfortable idea — but it is true."Understand complicity
    We Do (20 min)Class discussion: When has speaking up been hard? When has silence been easier? What makes people stay silent? (Fear, lack of knowledge, not wanting to stand out, thinking someone else will act, not believing it will help.) But what is the COST of silence?Discuss
    You Do (10 min)Write in journals: "One time I stayed silent when I should have spoken up. One time I spoke up when it was hard."Reflect

    WEEK 2 — EVIDENCE AND RESEARCH

    Lesson 1: The History (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"To argue for racial justice, you must know the HISTORY. The积累 of systemic inequality did not happen by accident — it was constructed, law by law, policy by policy." Brief overview: Redlining, Jim Crow, sundown towns, the War on Drugs vs the War on Poverty funding, school-to-prison pipeline. This history creates the present.Present history
    You Do (25 min)Research: Choose one historical policy/practice that contributes to current racial inequality. Find: What was it? When was it? Who did it affect? Is it still happening today? Record evidence.Research

    Lesson 2: Statistics and Stories (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"Numbers tell part of the story. Stories tell the other part. You need both." Show examples: STATISTIC: "Black Americans are 3 times more likely to be killed by police than white Americans." STORY: The name of a person who was killed. Both are necessary.Note
    We Do (15 min)Class research: Find 1 statistic and 1 story (real person) about racial injustice in the US or Australia. Create a shared evidence board.Research together
    You Do (15 min)Add to your evidence folder: 1 historical fact, 1 statistic, 1 real person's story.Collect evidence

    Lesson 3: Collecting Your Evidence (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Deep research: For your argument, find: 2 historical facts with dates and sources / 2 statistics with sources / 1 real person's story / 1 expert quote. Record sources for each.Research
    Share (5 min)Share one piece of the most powerful evidence from your researchShare

    WEEK 3 — ADVANCED RHETORICAL TECHNIQUES

    Lesson 1: Anaphora and Epistrophe (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"The Hate U Give is a novel but it is ALSO a rhetorical argument about the cost of silence and the power of speaking up. Thomas uses specific techniques to make Starr's voice powerful."Note
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)ANAPHORA: Repeating a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses — "I breathe. I speak. I refuse." EPISTROPHE: Repeating at the END — "The system broke him. The system owned him. The system killed him." Both create rhythm and emphasis. Martin Luther King Jr: "I have a dream..." is anaphora.Note devices
    You Do (15 min)Find an example of anaphora or epistrophe from a famous speech (MLK, Barack Obama, or a contemporary activist). Identify the device. Then write one of each for your topic.Practice

    Lesson 2: Chiasmus and Parallelism (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"CHIASMUS: A balanced reversal — 'We must learn to be masters of our fate or we will be slaves to our fear.' PARALLELISM: Similar structure for rhythm — 'Ask not what your country can do for you, ask what you can do for your country.' Both create memorable, powerful sentences."Note
    We Do (20 min)Practice: Write a chiastic sentence about justice or speaking up. Write a parallel sentence about silence vs speaking. Share with class.Practice
    You Do (10 min)Add 1 chiasmus and 1 parallelism sentence to your argument notes.Add to notes

    Lesson 3: Loaded Language and Framing (I Do → You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"Language is never neutral. How you FRAME an issue determines how people respond to it. 'All lives matter' vs 'Black lives matter' — both are phrases, but they frame the debate differently."Note framing
    We Do (15 min)Analyze: How do different news sources frame the same event? How does the framing change the meaning? This is not about right or wrong — it is about understanding how language shapes perception.Analyze framing
    You Do (15 min)Write: For your argument, what FRAME are you using? Are you arguing for justice, equality, humanity, change? Frame determines response. Write your frame statement.Define frame

    WEEK 4 — STRUCTURING THE ARGUMENT

    Lesson 1: The Architecture of Justice (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)The structure of an advanced persuasive piece on justice: HOOK (powerful first line — a question, a statement, a scene) → CONTEXT (what is the issue and why does it matter NOW?) → ARGUMENT (what is your position and why is it urgent?) → EVIDENCE (history + statistics + story) → COUNTER-ARGUMENT DISMANTLING (expose the false logic, don't just answer it — show WHY it is wrong) → CONCLUSION (restate urgency, call to action).Note structure
    You Do (25 min)Plan your piece using this architecture. For each section: write your specific plan in 1–3 sentences.Plan

    Lesson 2: Dismantling Counter-Arguments (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"The counter-argument section is not just 'some people say X but I say Y.' It is: 'Some people argue X — but this argument is built on a FALSE PREMISE, and here is why.' You do not just disagree — you dismantle."Note
    We Do (20 min)Common counter-arguments against speaking up: "It won't change anything" / "You're playing the race card" / "All lives matter" / "That was the past" / "Individual responsibility not systemic." For each: What is the FALSE PREMISE? How would you dismantle it? Write responses.Practice
    You Do (10 min)Choose ONE counter-argument you will dismantle in your piece. Write it and your dismantling.Write counter

    Lesson 3: Drafting the Opening (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (5 min)"The opening must stop the reader. It must make them read the next sentence. Try: a rhetorical question, a startling fact, a scene, a bold statement."Note
    You Do (35 min)Draft your HOOK. Try 3 different options: Question opening / Scene opening / Bold statement opening. Choose the most powerful. Then write: Hook + Context + Argument statement.Draft opening

    WEEK 5 — DRAFTING

    Lesson 1: Writing the Body (You Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (40 min)Write the BODY of your piece: Evidence paragraph 1 (history + statistic + story) → Evidence paragraph 2 (more evidence) → Transition to counter-argument. Follow your plan.Write body
    Share (5 min)Share the strongest evidence paragraph from your draftShare

    Lesson 2: The Counter-Argument Section (I Do → You Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"Write the counter-argument section: State the false argument clearly. Expose its false premise. Show why it is wrong with evidence and logic. Then pivot: THIS is why we MUST speak up."Note
    You Do (35 min)Write the counter-argument dismantling section of your piece. Make it precise and devastating.Write counter

    Lesson 3: Writers Workshop (You Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Peer Feedback

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Complete your full draft. Conference with 4–5 students. Peer feedback: Is the argument airtight? Is evidence strong? Does the voice hold authority? Is the counter-argument dismantled or just answered?Draft and conference
    Share (10 min)Read one paragraph that you believe is the strongest in your pieceShare

    WEEK 6 — REVISION AND VOICE

    Lesson 1: Revising for Authority (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Revision

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"Read your piece aloud. Does it sound like someone who KNOWS what they are talking about? Or does it sound like someone who is unsure? If you feel doubt in your writing, the reader will feel it too."Check voice
    We Do (15 min)Peer swap: Read your partner's piece. Mark places where the voice FEELS STRONG and where it wavers. Give specific feedback: "The sentence '...' sounds really strong because..."Peer response
    You Do (15 min)Revise to strengthen your voice throughout. Remove hedging language ("I think," "maybe," "sort of"). Replace with confident, authoritative statements.Revise

    Lesson 2: Strengthening the Call to Action (I Do → You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"The CONCLUSION is your moment of maximum impact. You have argued. You have presented evidence. You have dismantled objections. Now END with a call to action — what do you want your reader to DO with this?"Note
    You Do (30 min)Rewrite your conclusion. Make it: MEMORABLE (the last line should stay with the reader) / POWERFUL (the language should hit hard) / ACTIONABLE (the reader knows what to do).Strengthen conclusion

    Lesson 3: Polishing (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Editing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Final polish: Check all evidence is cited. Check rhetorical devices are present and effective. Check counter-argument dismantling is precise. Check voice is strong throughout. Confirm every paragraph earns its place.Final polish
    Share (5 min)Share your final hook and final line — the first and last impressionShare

    WEEK 7 — FINAL DRAFT AND PRESENTATION

    Lesson 1: Final Draft (You Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Publishing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (45 min)Write or type final published version. Double line spacing. Add title (can be a powerful phrase or question). Add your name.Write final

    Lesson 2: Presentation Preparation (We Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Oral

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (45 min)Prepare to present your piece. Practice reading it aloud: 5 times if possible. Mark your script: Where do you SLOW DOWN? Where do you PAUSE? Where do you raise your voice? Where do you let the sentence land? This is not a performance — it is speaking truth.Prepare

    Lesson 3: Speaking Truth (We Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Presentation

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (45 min)Each student reads their piece to the class. After each: 2 students say what struck them and why. Class observes silence and respect — this is sacred ground. No jokes, no off-task behaviour. This is people speaking truth.Present

    WEEK 8 — HOT TASK AND REFLECTION

    Lesson 1: Hot Task (Assessment)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Hook (5 min)"Think about what you argued in Week 1. Think about what you now know about the history of racial injustice, the power of evidence, and what it means to speak up. Write something that shows your growth as a writer and a thinker."Reflect
    Task (45 min)SAME TASK: Write a persuasive piece arguing for the importance of speaking up against racial injustice. Year 6: 450–650 words. Focus: Evidence (historical, statistical, story), advanced rhetorical techniques, counter-argument dismantling, authoritative voice, memorable conclusion with call to action.Independent writing

    Compare Cold Task → Hot Task using rubric. Note growth in craft, evidence, and voice.

    Lesson 2: Comparing Our Work (We Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Analysis

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (20 min)Compare Cold Task vs Hot Task — what improved? Look for: Historical understanding / Evidence quality and diversity / Rhetorical device mastery / Counter-argument dismantling / Authoritative voiceCompare
    Share (25 min)Students share specific growth and what they are most proud of learningCelebrate

    Lesson 3: Reflection and Celebration (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Reflection

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Reflection (25 min)Self-assess: What did you learn about racial justice, about speaking up, about rhetoric, about being an agent of change through writing? What will you take forward? Rate yourself against success criteria (1–4). Write one goal.Self-assess
    Celebration (15 min)This is a moment to sit with. Read one student's most powerful passage. Discuss: What does it mean to use writing as a tool for justice? What responsibility do we have?Community reflection

    ADAPTATIONS

    EAL Students

  • Pre-teach vocabulary: justice, systemic, oppression, complicity, structural, equity, intersectionality, privilege
  • Provide historical context sheets
  • Sentence starters for rhetorical devices
  • Allow home language for initial brainstorming
  • Visual timeline of key historical events
  • One-on-one conference for comprehension and language support

    • Gifted Students

  • Write about a specific intersection of racial justice and another issue (environmental racism, food apartheid, education inequity)
  • Compare Thomas's rhetorical approach to another activist writer (James Baldwin, Ta-Nehisi Coates)
  • Write a counter-narrative from an opposing viewpoint, then dismantle it
  • Study the rhetoric of a real social movement (Black Lives Matter, civil rights movement)

    • Students with Additional Needs

  • Reduce word count to 300–450 words
  • Provide pre-researched evidence bank with key facts/statistics
  • Sentence starters for each paragraph type
  • Graphic organiser for structure (especially counter-argument section)
  • One-on-one support during writing
  • Extra time for all tasks
  • Allow oral presentation option

    • ASSESSMENT NOTES

    Formative Assessment (Ongoing)

  • Evidence folders with historical/statistical/story research
  • Rhetorical device practice pieces
  • Draft writing with peer feedback
  • Teacher conferences (Weeks 3, 5, 7)
  • Voice self-assessment and reflection

    • Summative Assessment

  • Cold Task → Hot Task comparison (same rubric, measure growth)
  • Final persuasive piece assessed with rubric
  • Presentation observation (focus on voice, authority, presence)

    Use the comparison template to document:

  • Cold Task score → Hot Task score
  • Evidence quality, diversity, and integration
  • Rhetorical device mastery
  • Counter-argument dismantling quality
  • Voice and authority
  • Next writing goal
