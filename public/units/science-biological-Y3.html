Unit Content

Ecosystems and Living Things — Biological Sciences Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 3

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum Science v9 — Biological Sciences

Mentor Text: The Lorax by Dr. Seuss (1971)

Unit Big Idea: All living things depend on each other and their environment to survive. Changes to an ecosystem affect all living things.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Draw and label what you think an ecosystem looks like

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Describe an ecosystem and explain how living things depend on each other

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to investigate how living things depend on each other and their environment to survive, describe the components of an ecosystem (living and non-living), explain relationships in food chains and food webs, and describe how living things adapt to their environment.

Success Criteria:

  • I can identify and describe the components of an ecosystem (biotic = living, abiotic = non-living).
  • I can explain the relationship between producers, consumers, and decomposers in a food chain.
  • I can describe how living things adapt to their environment.
  • I can explain the effects of natural and human changes on ecosystems.
  • I can use science words: ecosystem, habitat, community, population, food chain, food web, producer, consumer, decomposer, adaptation, interdependence.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: The Lorax and What Is an Ecosystem?

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Draw an Ecosystem

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Show images of three ecosystems: a coral reef, an Australian bushland, and a pond. "What do you see? What do you think all of these have in common?"Observe images, share
    Task (35 min)Cold Task: "Draw and label what you think an ecosystem looks like. Include at least 5 living things and 2 non-living things. Explain in writing why they belong together." Year 3: 5–8 labels + 2–3 sentences.Draw, label, write
    Share (10 min)3–4 students share their drawingsShare

    Differentiation: EAL students may label in home language first then add English. NEP students: reduced labels (3 living, 1 non-living).

    Success Criteria Check: Student produces a labelled drawing showing some understanding of living/non-living distinction.

    Lesson 2: Meet The Lorax

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Engage (Hook)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Read Aloud (20 min)Read *The Lorax* in full, showing illustrations. Stop at key moments: the Once-ler describing the Truffula trees, the Lorax emerging from the stump.Listen, observe
    Notice and Wonder (15 min)"What do you notice?" (Record on chart — living things, environment, characters) / "What questions do you have?"Share observations
    Connect (10 min)"The Lorax is a story about an ecosystem. Who are the living things? What has happened to their environment? Why?"Contribute to discussion

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify at least 3 living things and 1 change to the environment from The Lorax.

    Lesson 3: What Is an Ecosystem?

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Engage (Concept Introduction)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Build the Definition (10 min)"ECOSYSTEM = all living things + non-living things working together in an area." Use The Lorax: truffula trees (living) + air/water (non-living) + animals (living) = ecosystem.Contribute examples
    Vocabulary Building (15 min)Introduce word wall: ecosystem, environment, habitat, community, population, species, biotic, abiotic. Use images.Add words to notebooks
    Apply the Concept (15 min)Students revisit Cold Task drawing. "Now that you know what an ecosystem is, add or change one thing in your drawing." Share with partner.Revise drawing
    Exit Ticket (10 min)"In one sentence, explain what an ecosystem is using at least 2 vocabulary words."Written response

    Success Criteria Check: Can define an ecosystem in own words using at least 2 vocabulary words.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Living vs Non-Living

    Lesson 1: Living or Non-Living?

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Quick Sort (10 min)Place items on tables: feather, rock, leaf, plastic plant, real plant, toy car, shell, pinecone, dried pasta, photograph of a dog. "Living or non-living? How do you know?"Physically sort, justify
    Establish the Rules (10 min)What do ALL living things have in common? Movement, breathing/respiration, growth, reproduction, response to environment. MRS GREN acronym.Copy MRS GREN
    Sort and Justify (20 min)In pairs, students sort remaining items. Must explain WHY each item is living or non-living using MRS GREN.Pair work
    Share (10 min)Pairs share one surprising sort decisionReport back

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify at least 4 MRS GREN characteristics and correctly classify 8+ items.

    Lesson 2: Mini Ecosystem Investigation

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explore (Hands-On Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Set Up (10 min)Distribute clear containers with pond water or leaf litter samples. "We are going to observe a REAL mini ecosystem. We are scientists." Observe only, don't disturb.Receive samples, observe
    Observe (25 min)Circulate and prompt: "What can you see? How many different living things can you find? What are they doing? What non-living things can you see?" Magnifying glasses and white paper provided.Observe, draw, count
    Predict (5 min)"What do you think will happen to your mini ecosystem if we leave it on the desk for a week?"Make predictions
    Record (10 min)Students complete observation sheet: name, date, living things found, non-living things, one interesting observationComplete sheet

    Safety Note: Remind students not to touch water or leaf litter directly. Wash hands after lesson.

    Success Criteria Check: Completes observation sheet with at least 3 living things identified and 2 non-living components noted.

    Lesson 3: Recording Observations

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explore (Data Recording)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Compare Samples (15 min)Students share what they found. Class tally: most common creatures (mosquito larvae, water fleas, worms, snails, algae).Contribute to tally
    Surprising Discoveries (10 min)"What surprised you? Was there anything unexpected?" Connect to ecosystem concept.Share surprises
    What We Know Now (15 min)Revise Cold Task drawings: add labels for MRS GREN characteristics. What would you change? Why?Revise drawings
    Connect Forward (10 min)"Next week we are going to learn about food chains. How do you think the tiny creatures in your sample fit into a food chain?"Think and share

    Success Criteria Check: Can explain at least one way living things in the mini ecosystem depend on each other.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: Food Chains

    Lesson 1: What Do Living Things Need?

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Prior Knowledge Probe (10 min)Brainstorm: "What do ALL living things need to survive?" Sun, water, food, air, shelter, space. Connect to pond sample: "What were your creatures using for food? What do they breathe?"Contribute
    Mini-Lesson (20 min)Every living thing needs ENERGY. Where does all energy start? THE SUN. Plants use sunlight to make food (photosynthesis). Animals eat plants or other animals. Energy flows: Sun → plants → animals. Draw on board.Copy diagram
    Apply to The Lorax (10 min)"Use The Lorax. Where does the Bar-ba-loot get its energy? Where does the Once-ler get his energy? Where does the Lorax fit?"Identify energy sources
    Exit Ticket (10 min)"Where does all energy in an ecosystem come from? How does it reach animals?"Written response

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify the sun as the source of all energy and trace it through at least one living thing.

    Lesson 2: Producers, Consumers, Decomposers

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Introduce the Roles (15 min)PRODUCERS (make their own food using sun — plants), CONSUMERS (eat other living things — animals), DECOMPOSERS (break down dead things — fungi, bacteria). Use The Lorax: truffula trees = producers, Bar-ba-loots = consumers, decomposers break down dead trees.Listen and categorise
    Food Chain from The Lorax (15 min)Build on board: Sun → Truffula trees (producer) → Bar-ba-loot (consumer). When trees die, decomposers break them down. When the trees are cut down, the Bar-ba-loots have no food.Contribute to chain
    Sort Activity (15 min)Sort organism cards: grass, cow, mushroom, sun, eagle, plankton, worm, apple tree, shark, mold. Into producers/consumers/decomposers.Sort organism cards
    Share (5 min)Pairs share sorted groups. Were there disagreements?Share and discuss

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly categorises at least 8/10 organisms and explains why each belongs.

    Lesson 3: Food Chains vs Food Webs

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain + Elaborate

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Food Chain Model (15 min)Draw simple chain: grass → kangaroo → dingo. Label producer/consumer. Add arrows showing energy flow. "Energy goes FROM the plant TO the animal. The arrow points the way energy travels."Draw in notebooks
    Food Web Introduction (15 min)"But in real ecosystems, animals eat MANY things." Add to diagram: grass also eaten by emu, dingo also eats lizard, emu eaten by dingo. This is a FOOD WEB — interconnected food chains.Observe and add
    What Happens If One Link Breaks? (10 min)"The Lorax showed us what happens when the truffula trees disappear. The Bar-ba-loots have no food. This is what happens when one link breaks."Discuss
    You Do — Draw (10 min)Year 3: Draw a food chain with 4 organisms. Year 4: Draw a food web connecting 5+ organisms in an Australian ecosystem.Independent work

    Differentiation: EAL students use pre-drawn template. Gifted: create a Great Barrier Reef food web with 8+ organisms.

    Success Criteria Check: Draws a food chain with correct directional arrows and can explain what would happen if one organism was removed.

    WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Australian Ecosystems

    Lesson 1: Australian Ecosystems

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Four Ecosystems (15 min)Show images: Great Victoria Desert, Daintree Rainforest, Great Barrier Reef, eucalypt bushland. Compare: what is the same? Different?Observe and compare
    Ecosystem Comparison Chart (20 min)Create chart: Desert — sparse water, extreme heat / Rainforest — high rainfall, dense canopy / Ocean — saltwater, coral / Bush — moderate rainfall, eucalypts. Students copy chart.Copy chart
    Why These Animals Live Here (10 min)"The koala lives in the bush because it eats eucalypt leaves. Each animal is SUITED to its environment." Introduce ADAPTATION.Listen and note
    Apply: Find the Ecosystem (5 min)Show 4 animal cards: dingo, clownfish, emu, green tree python. Students match to correct ecosystem and explain WHY.Match and justify

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly matches at least 3 Australian animals to their ecosystem and gives a reason.

    Lesson 2: How Do Living Things Adapt?

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is an Adaptation? (15 min)"An adaptation is a feature or behaviour that helps a living thing survive in its environment." Physical: camel hump stores water. Behavioural: frog burrows underground in summer.Give examples
    Australian Adaptations (20 min)Desert: kangaroo rat — large ears release heat. Rainforest: cassowary — bright colours for mating, strong legs. Eucalypt: koala — pouch (rearing young), strong sense of smell.Take notes
    Compare Across Ecosystems (10 min)"How is the way a desert kangaroo keeps cool different from a rainforest animal?" Compare behavioural adaptations.Compare adaptations
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Name one adaptation an Australian animal has. Explain why this adaptation helps it survive."Written response

    Success Criteria Check: Names one adaptation and explains its survival purpose.

    Lesson 3: The Lorax Connection

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Return to The Lorax (15 min)"What living things lost their homes? What happened to the ecosystem?" Re-read final pages.Recall and discuss
    Ecosystem Impact Map (15 min)Build: Once-ler cuts down truffula trees → Bar-ba-loots lose food → Swomey Swomeys lose singing trees → Humming-Fish lose clean water → Ecosystem collapses.Contribute to map
    What Could Have Been Different? (15 min)"What if the Once-ler had kept some trees? What could the Lorax have done differently?" Apply understanding.Brainstorm solutions
    Apply to Australia (5 min)"In Australia, we have cut down huge areas of forest for farms and cities. What might have happened to animals like the koala?"Connect to Australia

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 consequences of removing trees from the ecosystem.

    WEEK 5 — ELABORATE: Changes to Ecosystems

    Lesson 1: Natural Changes vs Human Changes

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Two Types of Change (15 min)Natural: drought, flood, bushfire, volcanic eruption, season changes. Human: deforestation, pollution, building cities, farming, fishing. Sort examples.Sort examples
    Impact Continuum (20 min)"Some changes recover quickly. Some permanently damage. Sort these by impact: Low Impact/Recoverable → High Impact/Long-term."Sort spectrum
    Connect to The Lorax (15 min)"What type of change was happening in The Lorax? Natural or human?"Classify

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly classifies at least 4 changes as natural or human-caused.

    Lesson 2: Case Study

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Case Study)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Great Barrier Reef (15 min)Images: coral bleaching = coral expels algae, turns white, dies. Fish lose habitat. Food chains break.Observe
    Investigate in Pairs (25 min)(A) Great Barrier Reef coral bleaching, (B) Tasmania old-growth forest logging, (C) Murray-Darling Basin water issues, (D) Perth urban sprawl. Answer: What changed? What species affected? What is being done?Pair investigation
    Jigsaw Share (10 min)Share findings. Create class list.Share and compile

    Success Criteria Check: Describes one real ecosystem change with at least 2 affected species and one solution.

    Lesson 3: How Living Things Adapt

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Three Adaptive Strategies (15 min)Migration: animals move to better environment (birds fly north in winter). Hibernation/Aestivation: animals sleep through harsh conditions. Camouflage: animals blend in.Take notes
    Australian Examples (15 min)Migration: grey nomad birds. Hibernation: echidna. Camouflage: thorny devil.Record examples
    Design Challenge (15 min)"An ecosystem has a 3-month drought. Design an animal that could survive. Give one behavioural and one physical adaptation."Design activity
    Share (5 min)3–4 students share designsShare

    Differentiation: Gifted: design for extreme environment with two physical and two behavioural adaptations.

    Success Criteria Check: Designs organism with one physical and one behavioural adaptation.

    Lesson 2: Design a Solution

    Peer Feedback (10 min)Pairs exchange proposals. One thing well done + one question.Give feedback

    Success Criteria Check: Creates proposal identifying one problem and proposing one solution with justification.

    Lesson 3: Presentations

    Debrief (5 min)"What was the most interesting solution?"Share

    DIFFERENTIATION NOTES

    EAL Learners

  • Visual Word Wall: ecosystem, producer, consumer, decomposer, adaptation, food chain, habitat, environment.

  • Food Web Challenge: Create a food web for a chosen Australian ecosystem connecting 8+ organisms.

  • Length Adjustments: 3–5 sentences for written tasks.
  • Oral Presentation Option: Verbally present instead of writing.
  • Scaffolded Templates: Partially completed organizers.
  • Partner Support: Front-of-room seating, one-to-one check-ins.
