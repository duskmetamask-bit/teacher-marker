Mentor Text: The Giver by Lois Lowry (1993)

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Critically analyse how Lowry constructs a dystopian world and what ideas she explores

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Same task, measure growth

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to engage in sophisticated literary analysis of a dystopian text, evaluate how authors construct worlds and ideas, compare multiple interpretations, and build rigorous arguments about literature.

Success Criteria:

  • I can critically analyse how an author constructs a dystopian world and what choices they make to create meaning.
  • I can evaluate multiple valid interpretations and defend my own position with evidence from across the text.
  • I can compare how different texts present similar ideas and critically evaluate their approaches.
  • I can synthesise author context, literary tradition, and textual evidence into a coherent analytical argument.
  • I can engage with challenging philosophical questions raised by the text and connect them to the real world.

    WEEK 1 — ENTERING THE DYSTOPIA

    Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (10 min)Show cover of *The Giver*. Introduce: This is a dystopian novel — a story about a world that seems perfect but has a dark secret. In this world, there is no war, no hunger, no colour. But there is also no choice, no love, no memory. This is the story of Jonas, who at 12 years old is "selected" for a special role.Engage with context
    Read (25 min)Read Chapters 1–6 (Jonas's world, the Ceremony of Twelve, his assignment as Receiver of Memory). The world is described: Sameness, colourless, no memory of the past, strict rules, assigned family units.Listen, follow, notice
    Task (20 min)Cold Task (same as Hot Task): In *The Giver*, Lois Lowry constructs a world that seems perfect but is deeply flawed. Critically analyse: How does Lowry build this world? What dystopian techniques does she use? What ideas about society and humanity is she exploring? What do you believe is her message? Support your analysis with specific details from the text. Year 6: 300–500 words.Independent critical analysis

    Differentiation: World-building vocabulary sheet. Context sheet on dystopian conventions.

    Lesson 2: World-Building (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Read (15 min)Reread Chapters 1–6. How does Lowry describe the world? What has she removed? (war, hunger, colour, choice, love, grief, memory of history). What has she kept? (rules, order, safety, comfort).Analyse description
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)World-building techniques: SELECTIVE OMISSION (what the author chooses NOT to show), CONVENTIONAL DESCRIPTIONS (the way characters casually accept the world), CONTRAST (what we learn to be disturbing vs what characters find normal). Model finding examples of each.Learn techniques
    Share (25 min)"What has Lowry removed from this world? What do these absences tell us about her argument?" List absences and their implications.Discussion

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify at least 3 world-building techniques used by Lowry.

    Lesson 3: First Analysis (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Model: "My first analysis: Lowry removes the capacity for pain, which means she also removes the capacity for deep joy. This creates a world that is comfortable but hollow. I know this because Jonas feels 'stirred' when he sees the apple — a tiny moment of feeling he cannot explain."Observe
    We Do (25 min)Together, build a world-building analysis chart: What Lowry showsWhat she omitsWhat this reveals about her argument. Use Chapters 1–6.Group analysis
    You Do (20 min)Write one analytical paragraph: "Lois Lowry uses [TECHNIQUE] to create [EFFECT] in her world-building. For example..."Written analysis

    WEEK 2 — POINT OF VIEW AND NARRATIVE VOICE

    Lesson 1: Jonas's Perspective (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Read (15 min)Read Chapters 7–12 (Jonas begins training, receives his first memory, discovers colour). His understanding begins to change.Follow
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)First-person narration from Jonas's perspective: We ONLY know what Jonas knows. We share his confusion and growing awareness. Lowry makes Jonas innocent and curious — a perfect vehicle for the reader's awakening. This is a classic technique in dystopian fiction.Learn concept
    You Do (25 min)Find 5 examples of Jonas noticing something unusual about his world that we (the readers) can see is wrong, but he cannot yet fully understand. For each, note: What does Jonas notice? What does he not yet understand? What do we understand as readers?Perspective analysis

    Lesson 2: The Effect of First-Person (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"If the story was told by the Giver, it would be different. If it was told by a Committee Elder, it would be different again. How does Jonas's perspective shape what we believe?"Analyse
    We Do (30 min)Explore the concept of the UNRELIABLE NARRATOR: Jonas is not lying, but he does not have full information. As readers, we gradually realise things Jonas has not yet understood. Find moments where we see the truth before Jonas does. Create a truth/trust chart.Guided exploration
    You Do (15 min)Write: "Lowry's choice to use Jonas's first-person perspective creates [EFFECT] because [REASON]. An example is..."Written perspective analysis

    Lesson 3: Voice Analysis (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (55 min)Analyse Lowry's narrative voice: 1. How does Jonas describe his world? (language, word choice, tone) 2. How does this language change as Jonas learns more? 3. What does Lowry achieve by keeping Jonas's voice relatively simple and direct? Write a full analysis with quotes and page numbers.Full voice analysis

    WEEK 3 — THEME ANALYSIS

    Lesson 1: Memory and the Past (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Read (15 min)Read Chapters 13–17 (Jonas receives painful memories, learns about the world's history, the war, the past).Follow
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)Theme 1: Memory and the past. The community has no memory of the past — only the Giver has memories. This means they have no understanding of: Pain (what makes pleasure possible), Love (what makes connection meaningful), History (why societies became what they are). Model: "Memory is not just recall — it is understanding. Without memory, there is no meaning."Learn theme
    You Do (25 min)Track how Jonas changes as he receives memories. For each new memory: What does he receive? How does it change him? What does he now understand that he did not before?Change tracking

    Lesson 2: Choice and Conformity (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Theme 2: Choice and conformity. Jonas's community has removed all choice: You do not choose your family, your job, your spouse, when you can have children, when you die. What does this mean for being human?Analyse theme
    We Do (30 min)Find all the choices that have been removed in Jonas's world. For each: What is the argument for removing it? (Safety, equality, efficiency) What is the cost of removing it? (Loss of identity, meaning, depth)Group analysis
    You Do (15 min)Write: "Lowry argues that [CHOICE] is essential to being human because... This is shown when..."Written theme analysis

    Lesson 3: Pain and Pleasure (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (55 min)Theme 3: Pain and pleasure are inseparable. The community removed pain → also removed deep joy, love, colour. Write a full thematic essay: 1. State the theme clearly. 2. Explain what Lowry seems to believe about pain and pleasure. 3. Provide 4 pieces of evidence from across the text. 4. Explain how each piece builds the theme. 5. What do you think about Lowry's argument? Do you agree or disagree? Why?Full thematic essay

    WEEK 4 — SYMBOLISM AND IMAGERY

    Lesson 1: The Giver's Eyes (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Read (15 min)Read Chapters 18–21 (Jonas and the Giver plan Jonas's escape, the "release"). The Giver's eyes hold all the memories — they are "deeper" than others.Follow
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)Symbol: The Giver's eyes. They are "deeper" — full of pain and knowledge. Why do eyes symbolise understanding in this book? Think about: What we see in others' eyes, what deep feeling looks like, what it means to "see" truly. The Giver can "see" because he carries all history.Learn
    You Do (25 min)Track all references to eyes in the novel. What does each reference mean in context? What does Lowry achieve by making eyes a recurring symbol?Symbol tracking

    Lesson 2: The Release (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Symbol: The Release. "Release" is the word for what happens when someone is killed. But the word "release" is used to make it sound gentle — like setting free a bird. This is language as propaganda. What else is "released" in this world? What does this tell us about the community's use of language to hide reality?Analyse
    We Do (30 min)Hunt for all uses of euphemism (gentle words for harsh realities) in the text: "the release," "elsewhere," "late"? List them. Explain what each euphemism hides. Analyse how this language choice reveals the community's control over truth.Group search
    You Do (15 min)Write about one euphemism: "The community uses the word 'release' to mean... This is a euphemism because... Lowry uses this to show..."Written analysis

    Lesson 3: Symbol Web (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (55 min)Create a complete symbol web for *The Giver*: SymbolLiteral meaningSymbolic meaningEvidence (quote + page)Connection to theme. Key symbols: The Giver's eyes, The Release, Snow/Sled/Riding, Colour, The river, Chirren (children), Memory, Elsewhere.Full symbol analysis

    WEEK 5 — AUTHOR'S CRAFT AND TECHNIQUE

    Lesson 1: Lowry's Style (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Read (15 min)Read Chapters 22–24 (Jonas's escape, what he finds). Lowry's prose is deliberately plain, simple, direct — especially at the start. It becomes richer and more lyrical as Jonas discovers more. Why?Analyse style
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)Style analysis: Lowry uses plain language to reflect the community's colourless, emotionless existence. As Jonas awakens, the prose awakens. This is intentional craft. She also uses: Short sentences for tension / Long passages for memory and depth / Silence and spaces for what cannot be said.Learn
    You Do (25 min)Find 5 examples of Lowry's style choices and analyse them: QuoteTechniqueEffect.Style analysis

    Lesson 2: Structure and Pacing (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)The novel ends with ambiguity — Jonas rides his bike "beyond." Lowry does not tell us if he lives. Why? What does this achieve?Analyse ending
    We Do (30 min)Track the pacing: Slow opening (world establishment), Accelerating middle (Jonas's training), Rapid end (escape). How does the pacing mirror Jonas's journey? How does Lowry control information release to create suspense?Group analysis
    You Do (15 min)Write: "Lowry's structural choices in [CHAPTER] create [EFFECT] because..."Written structural analysis

    Lesson 3: Critical Craft Analysis (We Do → You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided → Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (25 min)Together, write a comprehensive craft analysis: "Lois Lowry's use of [TECHNIQUE 1], [TECHNIQUE 2], and [TECHNIQUE 3] in *The Giver* creates [OVERALL EFFECT] which serves her purpose of [PURPOSE]. For example... This matters because..."Group writing
    You Do (30 min)Write your own full craft analysis of one chapter. Include 4 techniques with quotes, page numbers, and explanation of effect.Independent full analysis

    WEEK 6 — MULTIPLE INTERPRETATIONS

    Lesson 1: Debate: What Does Lowry Want Us to Think? (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Discussion

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (5 min)"There is no single correct interpretation of *The Giver*. Multiple interpretations can be valid if supported by evidence."Frame
    We Do (30 min)Class debate: 5 interpretations of Lowry's message: 1. The community's attempt to eliminate pain has also eliminated meaning — perfection is not possible. 2. Memory and history are essential to being human — we must remember the past to understand the present. 3. Choice and freedom are worth the pain they bring. 4. The transmission of knowledge is both burden and gift. 5. Utopia and dystopia are two sides of the same coin. Divide into groups. Each group defends one interpretation with text evidence. Debate.Full debate
    You Do (20 min)Write: Which interpretation do you find most convincing and why? What evidence best supports it?Written position

    Lesson 2: Interpretations Gallery (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"Literary criticism: scholars and readers have different interpretations. Let's examine 3 scholarly readings of *The Giver*." Present 3 possible readings: Religious (Jonas as Christ figure — sacrifice for others) / Political (the community as failed socialism/communism) / Philosophical (what makes life worth living).Learn
    We Do (30 min)Evaluate each reading: What evidence supports it? What evidence contradicts it? Which reading do you find most/least convincing and why?Group evaluation
    You Do (15 min)Write a defended position: "I believe [READING] is the strongest interpretation of *The Giver* because... The evidence that supports this is... One counter-argument is... But my position still stands because..."Written defence

    Lesson 3: Defending Positions (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (55 min)Write a full defended interpretation essay: 1. State your interpretive claim about Lowry's message. 2. Provide 4 pieces of evidence from across the entire novel. 3. Explain how each piece supports your claim. 4. Address one significant counter-argument. 5. Explain why your interpretation is still valid despite the counter-argument. 6. Reflect: How has engaging with other interpretations changed or strengthened your own view?Full essay

    WEEK 7 — CROSS-TEXT ANALYSIS

    Lesson 1: Compare to Another Dystopia (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Read (15 min)Compare to *Brave New World* (Huxley) — both remove pain, both sacrifice individual freedom for stability. Or compare to *The Hunger Games* (Collins) — both show young people challenging corrupt systems. Or compare to a film (*The Truman Show*).Cross-text
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)Compare: WORLD-BUILDING (how do they construct their worlds?) / CHARACTER (how do protagonists change?) / AUTHOR'S PURPOSE (what are they arguing?) / TECHNIQUE (how do they use language/style?) / RESOLUTION (how does the story end and what does that mean?).Framework
    You Do (25 min)Create a comparison chart: *The Giver* vs chosen comparison text. Note at least 5 points of comparison.Chart

    Lesson 2: Thematic Synthesis (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"Both *The Giver* and [comparison text] explore what happens when a society tries to eliminate pain. HOW do they explore it differently? What does this reveal about each author's argument?"Learn
    We Do (30 min)Synthesise: How does each text handle: The relationship between pain and joy? The value of memory? The cost of conformity? Which approach is more effective in conveying its message and why?Group synthesis
    You Do (15 min)Write a synthesis paragraph: "While *The Giver* argues [X] about pain and memory by using [TECHNIQUE], [COMPARISON TEXT] argues [Y] by using [TECHNIQUE]. This difference reveals that..."Written synthesis

    Lesson 3: Critical Review (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (55 min)Write a comprehensive critical review of *The Giver*: 1. What is the book about? (without spoiling) 2. What does Lowry do particularly well as a writer? (craft analysis with 3 specific examples) 3. What do you personally take away from this book? 4. What do you think Lowry's message is and do you agree? 5. How does it compare to other books in the dystopian genre? 6. Star rating (1–5) with justification.Full review

    ADAPTATIONS

    EAL Students

  • Provide comprehensive chapter summaries
  • Pre-teach philosophical vocabulary (dystopia, utopia, conformity, autonomy)
  • Allow bilingual dictionary use
  • Sentence starters for analytical paragraphs
  • Provide a symbol/template for each essay component
  • Allow oral analysis with teacher transcription
  • Visual symbol flashcards

    • Gifted Students

  • Research Lois Lowry's biography and other works, trace her authorial development
  • Compare to Huxley's Brave New World in depth
  • Write a formal literary essay on one theme
  • Research the literary criticism on The Giver
  • Write an alternative ending or a prequel
  • Create a critical edition with annotations explaining craft choices

    • Students with NEPs

  • Provide pre-digested chapter summaries
  • Reduce to key scenes for analysis
  • Allow focus on one theme and one symbol
  • Extended time for all tasks
  • One-on-one reading support
  • Provide a peer helper
  • Simplify to one technique and two pieces of evidence per paragraph
