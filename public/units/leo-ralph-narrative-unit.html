Learning Intention

We are learning to write an imaginative narrative that explores themes of friendship, belonging and identity.

Plan a narrative with a beginning, middle and end
Create a character with traits and feelings
Use descriptive language to bring my story to life
Show my character's emotions through actions and dialogue
Use paragraphs to organise my narrative

Week 1 — Introduction to Narrative

Foundation Week
Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)
40 min Assessment
Hook (5 min) Read Leo and Ralph pp. 1–10 aloud. Discuss: What do you notice about Leo? What do you notice about Ralph?
Task (35 min) Cold Task. Write a narrative about a child with an unusual or special imaginary friend. Year 3: 150–250 words. Year 4: 200–350 words. Same prompt as Hot Task in Week 8.
Differentiation: Word bank and sentence starters available for EAL/writing support students.
Lesson 2: What Makes a Narrative? (I Do)
30 min Explicit
Read + Think (15 min) Read Leo and Ralph pp. 11–35. Stop and identify: Who is the main character? What do they want? What happens in the beginning?
Mini-Lesson (10 min) Introduce: Beginning → Problem → Resolution. Use Leo and Ralph as the example. Students copy the structure into writing notebooks.
Share (5 min) Students share one thing they noticed about how the story is structured.
Lesson 3: Creating Your Character (We Do)
35 min Guided
I Do (10 min) Model: "Leo loves space; he is quiet, struggles to fit in, and wants a friend." Build a character profile on the board: NAME / APPEARANCE / PERSONALITY / WHAT THEY WANT / WHAT THEY LOVE.
We Do (15 min) Together, build a character for a shared class story using a character profile template.
You Do (10 min) Brainstorm your own character for your unit story. Complete character profile template.

Week 2 — Setting and Beginning

Craft Week
Lesson 1: Where and When? (I Do)
40 min Explicit
Read (10 min) Read Leo and Ralph pp. 36–60. Highlight how Carnavas describes Leo's bedroom, school, and town.
Mini-Lesson (10 min) Setting: WHOLE → SPECIFIC → SENSES.
Model: "The town was quiet" → "Leederville was a small town where everyone knew each other" → "In Leederville, you could hear the hum of the main street and smell the dust on the summer wind."
We Do (10 min) Together, describe a setting using the 3-level approach.
You Do (10 min) Draft the WHERE and WHEN of your narrative.
Lesson 2: Strong Openings (I Do → We Do)
35 min Guided
I Do (10 min) Show 3 opening lines — one boring, two engaging. Discuss what makes them work. Leo and Ralph opens with: "Leo loved space. He knew everything about it." Why does this work?
Mini-Lesson (5 min) Types of strong openings: Start with a character's voice / Start with action / Start with a problem.
We Do (10 min) Class writes 3 different openings for the shared class story.
You Do (10 min) Write 2 different openings for your narrative.
Lesson 3: Planning Your Narrative (We Do → You Do)
35 min Guided
I Do (5 min) Show how to plan Leo and Ralph on a story map: BEGINNING → PROBLEM → ENDING.
We Do (10 min) Class collaboratively plans a new story about an imaginary friend using a story map template.
You Do (20 min) Students complete their own story maps. Success Criteria Check: Story map has beginning, problem and resolution.

Week 3 — Building the Beginning

Draft Week
Lesson 1: Writing Your Beginning (You Do)
40 min Independent
I Do (5 min) Review: Strong opening, character introduction, setting establishment.
You Do (30 min) Draft the BEGINNING of your narrative (1–2 paragraphs).
Share (5 min) 2–3 students share their openings for feedback.
Differentiation: Scaffolding graphic organiser available. EAL students may use bilingual dictionaries.
Lesson 2: Showing vs Telling Emotions (I Do)
40 min Explicit
I Do (10 min) TELLING: "Leo was sad." SHOWING: "Leo's shoulders dropped. He stared at his shoes. 'I don't want to go,' he whispered." Model the difference using Leo and Ralph.
Mini-Lesson (10 min) Emotion thesaurus:
Sad → shoulders drop, voice goes quiet, pulls away from others
Happy → bounces, laughs, talks fast, eyes light up
Angry → voice rises, fists clench, walks away
We Do (10 min) Transform 3 telling sentences into showing sentences together.
You Do (10 min) Revise 1 paragraph of your beginning to SHOW emotions.
Lesson 3: Dialogue (I Do → We Do)
35 min Guided
I Do (10 min) Read Leo and Ralph pp. 61–80. Find examples of Ralph and Leo's dialogue. What do we learn about their friendship from what they say to each other?
Mini-Lesson (5 min) Punctuation for dialogue: "Help me," Leo said. "I can't do it alone," said Ralph.
We Do (10 min) Write 3 lines of dialogue between a child and their imaginary friend.
You Do (10 min) Add at least one line of dialogue to your narrative beginning.

Week 4 — The Problem

Structure Week
Lesson 1: What is the Problem? (I Do)
40 min Explicit
Read (15 min) Read Leo and Ralph pp. 81–120. What goes wrong? What does Leo want that he can't have?
Mini-Lesson (10 min) Types of problems:
Character vs self (loneliness, fear)
Character vs character (conflict with another person)
Character vs world (moving, change)
You Do (15 min) Decide what the PROBLEM is in your narrative. Write the problem in 2–3 sentences.
Lesson 2: Building Tension (I Do → We Do)
40 min Guided
I Do (10 min) Story mountain: SETUP → RISING ACTION → CLIMAX. Map Leo and Ralph on the mountain.
We Do (15 min) Together, plan the middle of the class story on the story mountain.
You Do (15 min) Draft the SETUP and RISING ACTION of your narrative.
Lesson 3: Writer's Workshop (You Do)
40 min Independent
Independent (35 min) Conference with 4–6 students individually. Students draft the middle section of their narrative.
Share (5 min) 1 student shares a strong passage for class discussion.

Week 5 — The Resolution and Ending

Resolution Week
Lesson 1: How Does It End? (I Do)
40 min Explicit
Read (15 min) Read Leo and Ralph — final 40 pages. How does Leo's story end? Is it satisfying?
Mini-Lesson (10 min) Types of endings:
Circle back to the beginning (opening image returns)
Character has changed (growth shown)
Big emotional moment (climax with feeling)

Carnavas uses CIRCLE BACK: "Leo loved space" → ending connects to space themes.
You Do (15 min) Plan and draft the ENDING of your narrative.
Lesson 2: Polishing the Middle (We Do → You Do)
40 min Guided
I Do (5 min) Model revising one paragraph — add a showing emotion, add dialogue, add a sensory detail.
We Do (10 min) Peer revision:交换 narratives. Give 2 stars and a wish.
You Do (25 min) Revise your narrative based on feedback.
Lesson 3: Paragraphs and Transitions (You Do)
35 min Independent
I Do (5 min) When does a new paragraph start? When reader needs a PAUSE or a SHIFT (time, place, speaker). Transition words: FIRST, NEXT, SUDDENLY, THEN, AFTER THAT, FINALLY.
You Do (30 min) Read through full draft. Add paragraphs. Add transition words.

Week 6 — Editing and Polishing

Polish Week
Lesson 1: Edit for Grammar and Punctuation (You Do)
40 min Independent
Mini-Lesson (10 min) Editing checklist:
✓ Capital letters at start of sentences and proper nouns
✓ Full stops, commas, question marks
✓ Variety in sentence starters
✓ No run-on sentences
You Do (30 min) Edit your narrative using the checklist. Highlight each correction in a different colour.
Lesson 2: Edit for Vocabulary (I Do → You Do)
35 min Guided
I Do (10 min) Dull → Vibrant:
"nice" → "warm and thoughtful"
"said" → "whispered, shouted, replied, mumbled"
"big" → "enormous, vast, towering"
You Do (25 min) Highlight all "said" and "nice/big/small/good/bad" in your narrative. Replace with more specific words.
Lesson 3: Publishing Plan (We Do)
35 min Guided
Mini-Lesson (5 min) Publishing options: Handwritten neat / Word processed / Illustrated version.
You Do (30 min) Begin final draft — neat handwritten or word processed.

Week 7 — Publishing and Reflection

Reflection Week
Lesson 1: Complete Your Published Narrative (You Do)
40 min Independent
You Do (40 min) Complete final published version. Add illustration or cover art.
Lesson 2: Reading Conference (We Do)
40 min Guided
We Do (40 min) Author's Chair: Students read excerpts aloud. Class gives specific feedback:
"I liked the part where… because…"
"The character really came alive when…"
Lesson 3: Self-Reflection (You Do)
40 min Independent
Self-Assessment (20 min) Complete reflection: What did you do well? What is one thing to improve? Rate yourself against success criteria (1–4).
Goal Setting (20 min) Write one writing goal for next term.

Week 8 — Hot Task and Celebration

Assessment Week
Lesson 1: Hot Task (Assessment)
40 min Assessment
Hook (5 min) Read Leo and Ralph — final pages again. Connect to unit journey.
Task (35 min) SAME TASK AS COLD TASK: Write a narrative about a child who has an imaginary friend. Year 3: 150–250 words. Year 4: 200–350 words.

Compare Cold Task → Hot Task using rubric. Note growth.
Lesson 2: Comparing Our Work (We Do)
40 min Guided
We Do (20 min) Compare a Cold Task sample → Hot Task sample. What improved? What changed? Annotate differences on the rubric.
Share (20 min) Students share one specific improvement they noticed in their own writing.
Lesson 3: Celebration (We Do)
40 min Guided
Celebration (40 min) Read a selection of Hot Task narratives aloud. Acknowledge individual improvements. Display narratives.

Rubric — Narrative Writing (Years 3–4)

AC English v9 Alignment: AC9E3LY01 (Yr3) / AC9E4LY01 (Yr4)

Criterion A — Excellent B — Strong C — Satisfactory D — Developing E — Beginning
Narrative Structure Sophisticated, well-crafted beginning, middle and end. Problem clearly established and resolved with emotional depth. Clear beginning, middle and end. Problem resolved satisfyingly. Beginning, middle and end present. Problem resolved. Structure present but uneven. Beginning or ending unclear. Minimal structure. Difficult to follow.
Character Development Character vividly drawn with distinct personality, motivations and emotions. Shows growth/change. Character has clear traits and motivations. Some emotional development shown. Character has at least 3 identifiable traits. Some showing of emotions. Character underdeveloped or inconsistent. Mostly telling rather than showing. Character flat or unclear.
Setting & Atmosphere Setting vividly established with sensory detail. Distinct atmosphere created throughout. Setting established with some sensory detail. Atmosphere mostly consistent. Setting mentioned. Limited sensory detail. Some atmosphere. Setting unclear or generic. Little atmosphere created. No clear setting.
Language Features Rich, precise vocabulary. Uses showing throughout. Dialogue purposeful and realistic. Good word choices. Shows emotions through actions/dialogue. Dialogue advances story. Some strong word choices. Some showing of emotions. Dialogue present. Repetitive language. Mostly telling emotions. Dialogue limited or unnatural. Limited vocabulary. No showing.
Punctuation & Grammar Accurate throughout. Varied sentence structures. No spelling errors. Minor errors. Good sentence variety. Some errors in punctuation or grammar. Reasonable sentence variety. Noticeable errors sometimes affect clarity. Limited sentence variety. Significant errors affect clarity.

🌿 EAL Students

  • Character profile template with visual prompts
  • Sentence starters for each paragraph component
  • Allow use of home language for brainstorming
  • Word bank displayed on classroom wall
  • One-on-one conference to clarify task
  • Visual word walls for key vocabulary

🚀 Gifted Students

  • Add a second character (imaginary friend's perspective)
  • Write the narrative from the imaginary friend's POV
  • Add a subplot
  • Extend to a chapter book format
  • Explore alternative tenses (first person, third person)
  • Create an illustrated picture book version

🎯 Students with NEPs

  • Adjust word count expectations
  • Provide graphic organiser for story map
  • Allow oral retelling as alternative (transcribed)
  • Extra time for all writing tasks
  • Provide a peer buddy for editing support
  • Simplify success criteria to 3 key goals

Assessment Summary

Formative Story maps, exit tickets, writer's notebook, peer feedback, teacher conferences (Weeks 4, 6)
Summative Cold Task → Hot Task comparison (same rubric, measure growth), published narrative with rubric, self-reflection with goal
