Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Write with critical language awareness and rhetorical precision

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Same task, measure growth

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to critically analyse how language choices create meaning and power, control sophisticated grammatical structures for specific rhetorical effects, adapt language to different genres and audiences, and use metalanguage accurately in analytical writing.

Success Criteria:

  • I can critically analyse how language choices (word selection, syntax, register) create power and meaning.
  • I can use metalanguage accurately in analytical writing about grammar and language effects.
  • I can control sophisticated grammatical structures (cleft sentences, passives, nominalisations, subordination) for specific effects.
  • I can adapt language to different genres (persuasive, informative, analytical, creative) while maintaining grammatical precision.
  • I can construct and evaluate arguments using grammatical choices.
  • I can edit for grammatical accuracy, register appropriateness, and rhetorical effect.

    WEEK 1 — CRITICAL LANGUAGE AWARENESS

    Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)"The Hate U Give is a powerful story about Starr, a Black teenager who witnesses a police shooting. The novel explores how language, media, and power intersect. Today you will show your grammar skills through writing about how language creates meaning and power."Engage
    Read (20 min)Read selected passages from *The Hate U Give* that show language variation — Starr's different voices (home vs school vs protest), media language vs community language, persuasive language in speeches. Notice how grammar changes with context.Listen
    Task (30 min)Cold Task (same as Hot Task): Write 2 analytical paragraphs: Paragraph 1: Analyse how a character in *The Hate U Give* uses language differently in different contexts. Use metalanguage to describe grammar choices. Paragraph 2: Write your own piece that shows conscious language choices — explain your decisions. Year 6: 400–550 words.Independent analytical writing

    Differentiation: Metalanguage glossary. Passage highlights showing language variation. Sentence starters for analytical writing.

    Lesson 2: Language and Power (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Mini-Lesson (20 min)Critical language awareness = understanding that language choices are never neutral — they create power, position audiences, and construct identities. In *The Hate U Give*: "Gangs" vs "community" — who uses which word and why? "Thug" vs "young man" — the power of labelling. Passive voice in news reports vs active voice in community accounts. "Victim" vs "survivor" — the power of terminology. Thomas shows us that grammar is political.Learn
    Activity (25 min)Analyse language choices in three texts about the same event (news report vs community account vs legal document). Compare: What nouns are used? What verbs are active vs passive? What adjectives are chosen? What is included vs excluded? Do a critical grammar analysis of all three.Practice
    Share (10 min)"How do grammar choices position the reader?" Discuss power and perspective.Discussion

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify how grammar choices create power and meaning.

    Lesson 3: Analysing Word Choices (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (40 min)Analyse a key speech from *The Hate U Give* (the "what do you want me to do" scene). Grammar analysis: What sentences are simple? What are complex? What connectives are used? What nouns and adjectives frame the situation? What verbs show agency? How does the syntax control the reader's emotional response? Write a full grammatical analysis of the passage.Guided analysis
    You Do (15 min)Write one paragraph of your own critical grammar analysis. Use metalanguage accurately.Written analysis

    WEEK 2 — METALANGUAGE

    Lesson 1: Grammar Talk (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Mini-Lesson (20 min)Metalanguage = the vocabulary we use to TALK ABOUT language and grammar. Key terms: syntax, morphology, semantics, pragmatics, register, genre, clause types (main, subordinate, relative), phrase types (noun phrase, verb phrase, prepositional phrase, adverbial phrase), clause functions (subject, predicate, object, complement), tense, aspect, voice, cohesion, coherence. Using metalanguage accurately is itself a grammar skill — it shows sophisticated understanding.Learn
    Activity (25 min)Complete a metalanguage matching activity: Match the term to the definition and an example from *The Hate U Give*. Terms: subordinate clause, passive voice, nominalisation, hedging, register, cohesive device, relative clause, fronted adverbial. Do 12 matches.Practice
    You Do (10 min)Write one sentence for each of 8 key metalanguage terms. Use the term accurately in context. Label the term.Written practice

    Lesson 2: Using Metalanguage (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Model using metalanguage in analysis: "In this sentence, the author uses a PASSIVE VOICE construction ('was shot') to distance the agent from the action. The fronted adverbial ('In the early morning') sets a temporal context. The noun phrase 'the young man' is a pre-modifier construction." Metalanguage makes analysis precise and academic.Observe
    We Do (35 min)Analyse sentences from *The Hate U Give* using metalanguage. Write full grammatical descriptions of 5 sentences. Use all relevant terms: clause type, clause function, phrase type, voice, tense, register. Check: Is my metalanguage accurate?Guided analysis
    You Do (10 min)Write a metalanguage analysis of one sentence. Use at least 6 metalanguage terms accurately.Written practice

    Lesson 3: Metalanguage in Analysis (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (55 min)Write a full analytical paragraph about how Starr's language changes between contexts (home vs school). Use metalanguage to describe: What grammatical choices are different? Why might these differences exist? What effect do the choices create? Use at least 8 metalanguage terms accurately. Underline all metalanguage usage.Full analytical paragraph

    WEEK 3 — RHETORICAL SYNTAX

    Lesson 1: Syntax for Effect (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Mini-Lesson (20 min)Rhetorical syntax = using sentence structures deliberately to create effects. Short sentences create impact and urgency: "He's dead." Long sentences create complexity and qualification: "The officer, who had been on duty for twelve hours, fired his weapon in what he claimed was self-defence." Question sentences engage: "Why did this happen?" Imperative sentences command: "Listen to Starr." Thomas uses all sentence types rhetorically.Learn
    Activity (25 min)Rewrite a passage with deliberate syntactic choices: Change all long sentences to short (notice the difference in impact). Change all short sentences to long (notice how urgency is lost). Add rhetorical questions. Change active to passive. Analyse how each change affects the rhetorical effect.Practice
    You Do (10 min)Write 4 sentences about a key scene, each with a different syntactic effect (short/impact, long/complex, question, imperative). Explain the effect of each choice.Written practice

    Lesson 2: Sentence Control (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Sentence control = being able to construct any sentence structure you need, not just the ones that come naturally. This means: Can you front any element for emphasis? Can you embed clauses at will? Can you nominalise any action? Can you use passive in any tense? Control means you CHOOSE, not just default.Learn
    We Do (35 min)Challenge: Construct sentences with specific requirements: "Write a sentence that fronted an adverbial, has an embedded relative clause, and ends with a passive construction." "Write a sentence using a nominalised verb as subject and an -ED clause as post-modifier." Do 8 challenges. Discuss: Did you have control? What was hard?Guided construction
    You Do (10 min)Complete 4 sentence control challenges. Check: Did you achieve the required structure?Written practice

    Lesson 3: Complex Sentence Design (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (55 min)Write a paragraph about a key theme in *The Hate U Give* (justice, identity, power). Design each sentence deliberately: Sentence 1 — short, declarative, impact. Sentence 2 — complex with multiple subordinate clauses. Sentence 3 — cleft sentence for emphasis. Sentence 4 — passive for formality. Sentence 5 — nominalised subject. Sentence 6 — rhetorical question. Explain the design decision for each sentence.Designed paragraph writing

    WEEK 4 — GENRE ADAPTATION

    Lesson 1: Persuasive Language (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Mini-Lesson (20 min)Persuasive language grammar: Modal verbs (must, should, will) for moral imperative. Inclusive "we" for solidarity. Short declarative sentences for impact. Repetition for emphasis. Rhetorical questions. Cleft sentences for focus. Passive for objectification of opponents. Conditional sentences for hypothetical futures. "If we don't act, then..." Thomas uses all of these in Starr's speeches.Learn
    Activity (25 min)Write a persuasive speech fragment (100 words) about a social justice issue. Use: modal verbs for moral imperative, inclusive "we," rhetorical questions, short sentences for impact, repetition, at least one cleft sentence. Analyse your own grammar choices.Practice
    You Do (10 min)Write a second version of the same speech with different grammatical choices. Compare effects.Written comparison

    Lesson 2: Analytical Writing (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Analytical writing grammar: Hedging for interpretive caution ("It appears that..." "This suggests..."). Passive for describing patterns ("Patterns can be observed..."). Nominalisations for compressing arguments. Complex sentences with multiple subordinate clauses for nuance. Discourse markers for logical flow ("Furthermore," "Consequently," "This indicates...").Learn
    We Do (35 min)Write an analytical paragraph about a language choice in *The Hate U Give*. Use all analytical grammar features. Check: Is my writing appropriately formal? Are my claims appropriately hedged? Is my evidence grammatically integrated?Guided writing
    You Do (10 min)Revise your paragraph for analytical precision. Check metalanguage use.Written revision

    Lesson 3: Creative Register (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (55 min)Write a creative piece in a character's voice (Starr, Maverick, Uncle Carlos). Show conscious grammatical choices: What register features do you use? How do you signal social context through grammar? What sentence structures reflect the character's education, community, emotional state? Write 200 words in one character's voice, then write a 100-word analysis of your own grammatical choices.Creative writing with analysis

    WEEK 5 — ARGUMENT CONSTRUCTION

    Lesson 1: Grammatical Persuasion (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Mini-Lesson (20 min)Argument construction grammar: CLAIM (main assertion) — declarative, fronted for emphasis. EVIDENCE (support) — nominalised subjects, passive for objectivity, specific detail. REASONING (connection) — complex sentences with BECAUSE/SINCE/THEREFORE, connective adverbials (consequently, thus). COUNTER-ARGUMENT (acknowledging opposition) — concessive clauses (although, even though, while), hedging to show fairness. REBUTTAL (responding to opposition) — boosting for your position, cleft sentences to foreground your argument.Learn
    Activity (25 min)Build an argument paragraph: Claim about Starr's dual identity. Evidence from the text (grammatical citations). Reasoning that connects evidence to claim. Counter-argument: "Some might argue that..." Rebuttal: "However, the evidence suggests..." Write the full paragraph.Practice
    You Do (10 min)Write a second argument paragraph on a different aspect of the text.Written practice

    Lesson 2: Evidence and Grammar (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Embedding grammatical evidence in argument: "In Chapter 3, Starr uses informal register markers (contractions, simplified syntax) when speaking with her family, which contrasts with the formal register (hedging, passive constructions) she adopts at school." This integrates grammatical analysis directly into the argument.Learn
    We Do (35 min)Write arguments that embed grammatical analysis: For each claim, add a grammatical observation with specific evidence (page references, quotes). Show how the grammar serves the argument. Do 4 integrated arguments.Guided writing
    You Do (10 min)Write one integrated argument paragraph that uses grammatical evidence to support a claim.Written practice

    Lesson 3: Counter-Argument (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (55 min)Write a full argument essay (3 paragraphs) about how Starr's language shows her navigating between two worlds. Include: Thesis with grammatical evidence, body paragraphs with claims-evidence-reasoning-counterargument-rebuttal, conclusion. Use metalanguage throughout. Use grammatical evidence to support all claims.Full argument essay

    WEEK 6 — METALANGUAGE INTEGRATION

    Lesson 1: Putting It Together (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (55 min)Write a complete analytical piece about how a key passage in *The Hate U Give* uses grammar to create power and meaning. Structure: Introduction (what passage, what I'll show), Analysis of passage (sentence by sentence, using metalanguage), Synthesis (what this means for our understanding of power and language). Use: metalanguage, rhetorical syntax analysis, genre awareness, argument construction. Share and discuss.Full analytical writing

    Lesson 2: Analysis Workshop (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (55 min)Workshop: Exchange analytical pieces. Check: Is metalanguage accurate? Is the grammatical analysis precise? Does the argument flow logically? Do the grammar choices serve the purpose? Give detailed feedback with specific suggestions. Revise based on feedback.Workshop process

    Lesson 3: Peer Analysis (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (55 min)Write a final analytical piece (this is your preparation for the Hot Task): Analyse a scene where Starr must speak differently in two contexts. Show: How does her grammar change? Why does it change? What power dynamics are at play? Use all metalanguage and analytical skills.Final analytical piece

    WEEK 7 — APPLYING GRAMMAR

    Lesson 1: Thomas-Style Sentences (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (55 min)Write Thomas-style sentences about a social justice theme. Include: register control (formal/informal balance), rhetorical syntax (varied sentence structures for effect), metalanguage in analysis, persuasive grammar features, complex sentence construction, passive voice where appropriate. Check each sentence.Integrated writing

    Lesson 2: Extended Writing (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (55 min)Write an extended piece that combines analysis and creative writing. Part A: Analyse how Starr's grammar changes in a specific scene. Part B: Write a new scene showing Starr navigating a grammatically complex social situation. Both parts must demonstrate: metalanguage use, rhetorical syntax, register control, argument construction.Extended writing

    Lesson 3: Editing (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (30 min)Final editing checklist: Metalanguage accuracy? Grammar analysis precise? Register appropriate for purpose? Argument logical? Sentence structures varied and controlled? Cohesive devices effective? Swap and mark.Peer editing
    You Do (25 min)Final edit of extended piece. Check all criteria.Self-editing

    ADAPTATIONS

    EAL Students

  • Provide metalanguage glossary with definitions and examples in L1
  • Sentence starters for analytical writing
  • Allow L1 analytical writing first, then translate
  • Visual grammar posters with examples
  • Pair with supportive partner
  • Focus on metalanguage accuracy and basic critical awareness first

    • Gifted Students

  • Explore critical discourse analysis (CDA) frameworks
  • Research language and power in media
  • Analyse multimodal texts (film, advertisements) for grammatical choices
  • Write a formal linguistic essay
  • Explore pragma-linguistic competence (appropriate language in context)
  • Research dialectal variation and social power

    • Students with NEPs

  • Reduce to 1 paragraph per task
  • Focus on metalanguage accuracy and one genre adaptation
  • Provide sentence starters
  • Extended time
  • Simplify: Focus on metalanguage + one rhetorical syntax choice
