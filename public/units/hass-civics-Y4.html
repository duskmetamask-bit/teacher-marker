Unit Content

Democracy and Law — Civics and Citizenship Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 4

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum HASS v9 — Civics and Citizenship (Years 3–4)

Unit Big Idea: Democracy and the law work together to protect citizens and ensure justice. Understanding how laws are made, how courts work, and how citizens can access justice helps us understand our rights and responsibilities.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Investigate how laws are made in Australia and explain one law you think should be changed and why

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Investigate how the legal system works, explain one legal process, and evaluate how citizens can access justice

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to understand how laws are made in Australia, how the legal system works, and how citizens can participate in law-making and access justice.

Success Criteria:

  • I can describe how laws are made at federal and state level in Australia.
  • I can explain the role of parliament, courts, and the police in the legal system.
  • I can describe how citizens can influence law-making.
  • I can explain what rights and responsibilities citizens have under the law.
  • I can use sources to investigate how the legal system works.
  • I can evaluate how fair and accessible the legal system is for all citizens.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: Laws and Democracy

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Laws and Democracy

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Show two laws: speed limits on roads / no littering laws. "What do these laws have in common? Why do we have them? Who made them? What would happen if we didn't have them?"Discuss
    Introduction (5 min)"In this unit, we will learn how laws are made in Australia, how the legal system works, and how citizens can participate in law-making. Laws are the rules that govern our society — everyone is affected by them."Listen
    Cold Task (30 min)"Part 1: How are laws made in Australia? Part 2: Choose ONE law you think should be changed (or a new law you think should exist). Describe the law and explain WHY it should change. Part 3: Write 3–4 sentences: How would you try to get this law changed? Write 6–10 sentences total." Year 4 expectation: structured, reasoned argument, process described.Independent

    Differentiation: Sentence starters: "This law should be changed because..." / "To change this law, I would..." / "The current law is..."

    Success Criteria Check: Student describes how laws are made and proposes a change to one law with reasoning.

    Lesson 2: Why Do We Have Laws?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Hook)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Are Laws? (10 min)"LAWS are rules that society sets for itself. They are enforced by the government and courts. Laws define what is ACCEPTABLE and what is NOT ACCEPTABLE. Breaking laws can result in fines, imprisonment, or other consequences."Learn
    Why Do We Need Laws? (15 min)"We need laws for: PROTECTION (laws protect people from harm — murder, assault, theft are illegal) / ORDER (laws provide predictability — you know what will happen if you break the law) / JUSTICE (laws apply equally to everyone) / SOCIAL COHESION (laws help people live together peacefully) / RIGHTS (laws protect people's rights against interference)."Learn
    What Happens Without Laws? (10 min)"Imagine a world WITHOUT laws: people could steal without consequence / the strongest could take whatever they wanted / there would be no justice / chaos and violence would reign / no protection for vulnerable people. Laws make civilisation possible."Imagine
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: Why do we need laws? What would happen without them?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 reasons why laws are needed and explain what would happen without them.

    Lesson 3: Making Laws in Australia

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Concept Introduction)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Who Makes Laws? (10 min)"In Australia, laws are made by PARLIAMENT. Parliament is made up of elected representatives — the people we vote for. Federal Parliament makes laws for matters in the Australian Constitution. State Parliaments make laws for state matters (most laws that affect your daily life)."Learn
    The Law-Making Process (15 min)"Federal law-making process: 1. BILL is introduced in either House (by a Minister or other MP) / 2. DEBATE — MPs discuss, propose amendments / 3. VOTE — if a majority votes yes, the bill passes that House / 4. Same process in the other House / 5. ROYAL ASSENT — the Governor-General signs it, making it law / 6. GAZETTAL — it is officially recorded and takes effect."Learn
    State vs Federal Laws (10 min)"STATE laws (state parliament): schools, hospitals, police, local government, roads, property / FEDERAL laws (federal parliament): defence, immigration, trade, tax, Medicare / Sometimes both state and federal laws apply to the same issue — this can be confusing."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: Who makes laws in Australia? How does a bill become law? What is the difference between state and federal laws?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the basic law-making process and explain the difference between state and federal laws.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: The Parliament

    Lesson 1: How Parliament Works

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Parliament? (10 min)"PARLIAMENT is the institution that makes laws. In Australia, we have a REPRESENTATIVE DEMOCRACY — citizens vote for representatives (MPs) who make decisions on their behalf. This is different from a DIRECT DEMOCRACY where citizens vote on every issue."Learn
    The Australian Parliament (10 min)"AUSTRALIAN FEDERAL PARLIAMENT has two houses: THE HOUSE OF REPRESENTATIVES (lower house, 151 seats — the 'people's house') / THE SENATE (upper house, 76 seats — the 'states' house'). Most laws start in the House of Representatives. The Senate acts as a house of review."Learn
    Government vs Opposition (10 min)"The party (or coalition of parties) with the most seats in the House of Representatives FORMS THE GOVERNMENT. The largest non-government party becomes the OPPOSITION. The government proposes most laws, controls the executive branch, and is held to account by the opposition."Learn
    Role of the Prime Minister (10 min)"The PRIME MINISTER (PM) is the leader of the government — the most powerful political figure in Australia. PM is not directly elected by the people but chosen by MPs. PM sets the government's agenda and chairs Cabinet."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the structure of the Australian federal Parliament and the difference between government and opposition.

    Lesson 2: The Two Houses

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    House of Representatives (10 min)"The HOUSE OF REPRESENTATIVES: 151 seats, each representing an electorate / members are elected by the people / the party with the most seats forms government / this is where most bills originate / it is designed to represent 'the people.'"Learn
    The Senate (10 min)"The SENATE: 76 seats (12 per state, 2 per territory) / designed to represent states, not individuals / senators in each state represent the whole state, not local areas (unlike the House) / reviews and can amend bills from the House / often called the 'house of review' / parties must get roughly 7–14% of votes in each state to win a seat."Learn
    Why Two Houses? (10 min)"The two-house system (BI CAMERALISM) exists because: it provides CHECKS AND BALANCES on power / it ensures both the people (HoR) and the states (Senate) are represented / it forces compromise — a bill must pass both houses to become law / it prevents rushed legislation — both houses must agree."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is the difference between the House of Representatives and the Senate? Why does Australia have two houses?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the difference between the two houses and explain why Australia has a bicameral parliament.

    Lesson 3: The Role of MPs

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Do MPs Do? (15 min)"MEMBERS OF PARLIAMENT (MPs) — both federal and state — do the following: REPRESENT their constituents (the people who voted for them) / ATTEND parliament and vote on laws / PARTICIPATE in debates / SERVE on committees / MEET with constituents / ADVOCATE for their electorate / SCRUTINISE government (question ministers, hold them to account)."Learn
    Your Federal MP (10 min)"Who is YOUR federal MP? (Find the electorate based on school address). Our federal MP represents [electorate name]. They sit in the [House of Representatives / Senate]. You can contact them — their details are publicly available."Learn
    Contacting MPs (10 min)"You can contact MPs: EMAIL / PHONE / LETTER / ATTENDING community meetings / SOCIAL MEDIA. MPs are obligated to represent their constituents — your voice can influence their decisions. Many MPs respond to correspondence, especially when there are many letters on the same topic."Learn
    Roleplay (5 min)"Roleplay: Citizen (student) contacts MP (student) about an issue. Practice being clear, polite, and specific."Practice

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify their federal MP, describe the role of MPs, and explain how to contact them.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: The Courts

    Lesson 1: The Australian Court System

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Court Hierarchy (10 min)"Australia has a HIERARCHY of courts: HIGH COURT OF AUSTRALIA (highest, hears appeals from all other courts) / FEDERAL COURT (handles federal law matters) / FAMILY COURT / STATE COURTS (magistrates, district, supreme) / Each level handles different types of cases."Learn
    State Courts (10 min)"In Western Australia: LOCAL COURT (minor criminal offences, civil disputes up to $75,000) / DISTRICT COURT (more serious criminal offences, civil disputes up to $750,000) / SUPREME COURT (most serious crimes, including murder — trials by judge and jury) / COURT OF APPEAL (appeals from Supreme Court)."Learn
    Federal Courts (10 min)"FEDERAL COURT OF AUSTRALIA: handles matters arising under federal law / includes competition law, intellectual property, administrative law / separate from the High Court."Learn
    Why a Hierarchy? (10 min)"The court hierarchy exists because: it allows for APPEALS (decisions can be reviewed by higher courts) / it provides specialist courts for different types of cases / it ensures consistency in the law / the High Court ensures the Constitution is upheld."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 levels of the Australian court system and identify what each level does.

    Lesson 2: What Happens in Court?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Role of Courts (10 min)"Courts do NOT make laws (parliament does that). Courts INTERPRET and ENFORCE laws. Courts decide whether a person has broken a law and what the consequence should be. Courts provide JUSTICE."Learn
    Criminal vs Civil Cases (10 min)"CRIMINAL CASE: the government (police/prosecutor) charges a person with a crime. If proven, the person is PUNISHED (fined, imprisoned). CIVIL CASE: one person sues another (e.g., a dispute, compensation). No punishment — compensation or orders."Learn
    The Jury System (15 min)"In serious criminal cases (Supreme Court), trials are by JUDGE AND JURY. 12 jurors are randomly selected from the public. The JURY decides whether the accused is guilty or not guilty based on evidence. The JUDGE decides on the sentence. This is a key part of our justice system."Learn
    Why Jury Trials? (5 min)"Jury trials are considered fair because: ORDINARY PEOPLE (not government) decide guilt / it prevents abuse of power by the state / it reflects community standards / it is a fundamental right of the accused."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the difference between criminal and civil cases and explain why jury trials are important.

    Lesson 3: Fair Trials

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Makes a Fair Trial? (15 min)"A fair trial requires: INNOCENT UNTIL PROVEN GUILTY (the accused does not have to prove innocence — the prosecution must prove guilt) / EVIDENCE must be presented / the ACCUSED can defend themselves / JURY must be unbiased / JUDGE must be independent / PUNISHMENT must be proportionate."Learn
    Legal Representation (10 min)"Accused persons have the right to LEGAL REPRESENTATION (a lawyer). If they cannot afford a lawyer, they can apply for LEGAL AID (government-funded legal assistance). In serious cases, if a person cannot afford a lawyer and legal aid is not available, the trial may not proceed."Learn
    What Is a Fair Trial? (10 min)"Fair trial rights are protected in: AUSTRALIAN CONSTITUTION (implied right to a fair trial in criminal cases) / INTERNATIONAL law (Universal Declaration of Human Rights, ICCPR) / STATE and FEDERAL laws. These rights ensure that innocent people are not wrongly convicted."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What makes a fair trial? Why is this important?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students create a visual of fair trial rights. Gifted students: research one specific famous trial (e.g., the Chamberlain case, the Mabo case) and present.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 elements of a fair trial and explain why fair trials are important.

    WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Citizens and the Law

    Lesson 1: Your Rights Under the Law

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Rights in Australia (10 min)"Australians have rights under the law. These include: EQUALITY BEFORE THE LAW (everyone is equal, regardless of race, gender, religion) / FREEDOM OF SPEECH (with some limits — defamation, incitement) / FREEDOM OF RELIGION / RIGHT TO VOTE / RIGHT TO PRIVACY / RIGHT TO LEGAL REPRESENTATION / FREEDOM FROM DISCRIMINATION."Learn
    How Rights Are Protected (10 min)"Rights are protected through: ANTI-DISCRIMINATION LAWS (federal and state — make it illegal to discriminate on basis of race, sex, disability, age, etc.) / THE CONSTITUTION (some implied rights) / INTERNATIONAL TREATIES (Australia has signed international human rights agreements that shape domestic law) / COURTS (courts can rule that laws violate rights)."Learn
    Are Rights Protected Enough? (10 min)"Australia does NOT have a comprehensive Bill of Rights. Some argue our rights are insufficiently protected — laws can be made that restrict rights without a comprehensive check. Others argue our system works because of our democratic traditions and independent courts."Debate
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What rights do Australians have? How are these rights protected? Are there gaps?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 rights of Australians and evaluate whether they are sufficiently protected.

    Lesson 2: Young People and the Law

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Young People Have Rights (10 min)"Young people have the SAME fundamental rights as adults. The UN CONVENTION ON THE RIGHTS OF THE CHILD (UNCRC — Australia has signed) states that children have all the rights of adults plus SPECIFIC protections: right to education / right to healthcare / right to be heard on matters affecting them / right to protection from exploitation."Learn
    The Age of Criminal Responsibility (15 min)"In Australia, the AGE OF CRIMINAL RESPONSIBILITY is 10 (in most states). This means: children under 10 CANNOT be held criminally responsible for their actions / children aged 10–14 can be held criminally responsible but must prove they understood their actions were wrong / adults (18+) bear full criminal responsibility. This is debated — some argue 10 is too young."Learn
    Youth Justice System (10 min)"Young people who commit crimes go through a different system: YOUTH COURTS (separate from adult courts) / focus on REHABILITATION and REINTEGRATION / detention as a last resort / aim is to address the causes of criminal behaviour. Youth detention rates are disproportionately high for Indigenous young people — a significant issue."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: How does the law treat young people who commit crimes differently from adults? Why might this be?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 ways the youth justice system differs from the adult system and explain why.

    Lesson 3: Access to Justice

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Access to Justice? (10 min)"ACCESS TO JUSTICE means that all citizens, regardless of wealth or background, can get a fair hearing in legal disputes. This is a FUNDAMENTAL PRINCIPLE of democracy. If only wealthy people can access justice, the system is unfair."Learn
    Barriers to Justice (10 min)"Barriers to justice: COST (lawyers are expensive, legal aid is limited) / COMPLEXITY (the legal system is hard to understand) / GEOGRAPHY (rural and remote areas have fewer legal services) / LANGUAGE (non-English speakers face barriers) / DISCRIMINATION (some groups face barriers in the system)."Learn
    Legal Aid (10 min)"LEGAL AID: government-funded legal assistance for people who cannot afford a lawyer. In WA: Legal Aid WA provides: duty lawyer services (free lawyer at court) / grants for private lawyers / community legal education. BUT: legal aid is not unlimited — many people who qualify for aid cannot get it due to funding limits."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is access to justice? What are barriers to justice? How is legal aid provided?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 barriers to accessing justice and explain how legal aid helps.

    WEEK 5 — EXPLAIN: Influencing Laws

    Lesson 1: How Can Citizens Influence Laws?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Citizens Can Influence Laws (10 min)"Citizens can influence laws in many ways: VOTING for MPs who share their views / CONTACTING MPs and senators / SIGNING PETITIONS / ATTENDING community consultations / SUBMITTING to inquiries / WRITING submissions to parliamentary committees / MARCHING in protests (peaceful assembly is protected)."Learn
    Submissions to Inquiries (10 min)"Parliamentary committees conduct INQUIRIES into specific topics. They call for PUBLIC SUBMISSIONS — anyone can write a submission explaining their position. Submissions are recorded, read by the committee, and can influence recommendations. This is a powerful tool for citizens."Learn
    The Power of Collective Action (10 min)"COLLECTIVE ACTION is more powerful than individual action. Petitions with thousands of signatures get more attention than individual letters. Community groups, rallies, media campaigns — these all amplify individual voices. One voice is easy to ignore. Thousands are hard to ignore."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: How can citizens influence laws? Which method do you think is most effective? Why?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 4 ways citizens can influence laws and evaluate which is most effective.

    Lesson 2: Petitions and Protests

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Petitions (10 min)"PETITIONS: a formal written request to parliament. In Australia: you can petition federal parliament (online via epetitions) / state parliaments have their own petition processes / valid petitions must follow specific rules (format, signatures) / if a petition gets enough signatures, it may be formally presented and debated."Learn
    Famous Petitions (10 min)"Famous petitions in Australian history: THE REMEMBRANCE (2003 — for a war memorial? Not the strongest example) / APOLOGY TO THE STOLEN GENERATIONS (not a petition but a campaign that influenced the apology). Petitions have been used effectively throughout Australian history."Learn
    Peaceful Protest (10 min)"PROTEST: a public expression of opposition or support for an issue. Peaceful protest is a constitutional right (freedom of political communication). Examples: anti-war protests (Vietnam, Iraq) / climate protests (School Strikes for Climate) / Indigenous rights protests (Invasion Day rallies). Protests raise public awareness and can influence government."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What is a petition? What is a protest? When might each be used?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the petition process and explain the right to peaceful protest.

    Lesson 3: Writing to MPs

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    How to Write to MPs (10 min)"Writing to MPs is effective when done well. Structure: WHO you are (name, address, that you are a constituent — if applicable) / WHAT the issue is (be specific) / WHY it matters (evidence, personal story, impact) / WHAT you want the MP to do (vote a certain way, raise in parliament, meet with you) / Thank them."Learn
    Sample Letter (15 min)"Read a sample letter to an MP: 'Dear [MP name], I am a resident of [suburb/electorate]. I am writing about [specific issue]. [Evidence/reason why it matters]. I ask that you [specific request]. Thank you for considering my views. Yours sincerely, [name]."Read
    Practice Letter Writing (10 min)"Write a letter to your federal MP about ONE issue. Use the structure. You don't need to send it — but practice the skill."Write
    Share (5 min)3–4 students share the issue they wrote about.Share

    Differentiation: EAL students use a letter template. Gifted students: research their MP's position on an issue before writing.

    Success Criteria Check: Can write a structured letter to an MP about a community issue.

    WEEK 6 — ELABORATE: Contemporary Issues

    Lesson 1: A Current Legal Debate

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Current Issues (10 min)"Laws are always evolving because society is always changing. CURRENT LEGAL ISSUES in Australia: Indigenous Voice to Parliament (should there be a constitutionally enshrined Indigenous voice?) / Encryption laws (balancing security and privacy) / Social media regulation (free speech vs harm) / Climate policy and legal accountability (should the government be legally required to reduce emissions?) / Refugee rights (offshore processing)."Learn
    Choose an Issue (5 min)"Choose ONE of the above issues (or another current legal issue) to investigate deeply for this week."Choose
    Research Both Sides (15 min)"For your issue: What is the debate? What are the ARGUMENTS FOR each side? Who are the KEY PLAYERS? Use at least 2 sources."Research
    Note-Taking (10 min)Record: Issue / Arguments for / Arguments against / Your initial position.Record

    Success Criteria Check: Researches a current legal issue and records arguments on both sides.

    Lesson 2: Arguments on Both Sides

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Debate Analysis (15 min)"Good citizens engage with COMPLEX ISSUES, not just one side. They understand arguments on BOTH SIDES before forming a view. This is called CRITICAL ENGAGEMENT."Learn
    Arguments Against (10 min)"For your chosen issue: What are the arguments AGAINST the position most commonly held? What evidence supports these arguments? Why do reasonable people disagree?"Analyse
    Arguments For (10 min)"What are the arguments FOR the position? What evidence supports this? Who supports this side?"Analyse
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What are the strongest arguments on each side of your issue?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can articulate arguments on at least 2 sides of a current legal issue.

    Lesson 3: Your Position

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Forming a Position (10 min)"To form a position: LISTEN to all sides / WEIGH the evidence / CONSIDER who benefits and who is harmed / THINK about your values / DECIDE. You can disagree with the majority position. You can be undecided. But you should have REASONS for your position."Learn
    What Is Your Position? (15 min)"Write 4–5 sentences explaining your position on your chosen issue. Use evidence. Acknowledge the counter-arguments. Be prepared to defend your view."Write
    Peer Discussion (10 min)Share positions with a partner. Partner: Does the argument make sense? Is evidence provided? Is the counter-argument acknowledged?Discuss
    Write Final Position (5 min)Revise your position based on peer discussion. Write a final 4–5 sentence position statement.Revise

    Differentiation: EAL students present their position verbally. Gifted students: write a formal 5-paragraph essay on their position.

    Success Criteria Check: Formulates a reasoned position on a current legal issue, acknowledging counter-arguments.
