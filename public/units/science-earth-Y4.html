AC9S4U01 — Describe how fossils provide evidence of past life and changes in Earth's surface and past environments

Mentor Text/Context: Natural history museum, geological formations, environmental science

Writing Focus: Scientific observation, evidence-based explanation, factual writing about Earth's history

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning about Earth's resources, how fossils form, what they tell us about past life and environments, and how scientists use evidence to understand geological timescales.

Success Criteria:

  • I can describe what fossils are and how they are formed.
  • I can explain how fossils provide evidence of past life and environments.
  • I can understand the basic geological timescale — that Earth is very old and life has changed over time.
  • I can explain what Earth's resources are and why they are important.
  • I can use evidence to support scientific explanations.

    WEEK 1 — INTRODUCTION TO EARTH'S RESOURCES

    Lesson 1: What Is Earth Made Of? (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Hook (10 min)Show images of Earth's layers (crust, mantle, outer core, inner core). "Earth is made of layers — like an onion. The outermost layer, the CRUST, is where we live. It is made of rocks, minerals, and soil. These are Earth's RESOURCES — things we use from the Earth."Understand layers
    I Do (15 min)"Earth's resources include: Minerals (iron, gold, copper) / Rocks (granite, sandstone, limestone) / Soil / Water / Air / Fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas). These resources have formed over BILLIONS of years."Learn resources
    You Do (20 min)Sort: Given 20 images of natural resources, sort into categories (rock, mineral, water, soil, fuel). Write why each fits the category.Sort
    Share (5 min)Which resource do you think is most important for human life? Why?Discuss

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 5 of Earth's resources.

    Lesson 2: Renewable and Non-Renewable (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"RENEWABLE resources can be replaced in a short time (years or decades): Sun, wind, water, soil, trees. NON-RENEWABLE resources took millions of years to form and cannot be replaced in human time: Coal, oil, gas, iron ore, gold, other minerals. Once used, they are gone."Learn distinction
    We Do (20 min)Sort: 20 resources into Renewable vs Non-Renewable. For each: Write name + category + reason. Check answers.Sort
    You Do (10 min)Write: "One renewable resource I use every day is ___. One non-renewable resource I use every day is ___."Write

    Lesson 3: What Are Fossils? (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"A FOSSIL is the preserved remains or traces of ancient living things — plants, animals, shells, bones, footprints. Fossils are found in rocks, particularly sedimentary rocks (formed from layers of sand and mud). They can be millions or hundreds of millions of years old."Learn fossil
    You Do (30 min)Examine images of famous fossils: Dinosaur bones, trilobites, ammonites, fern imprints, insect in amber. For each: Name what it is, estimate how old it might be, say what we learn from it.Examine
    Share (5 min)What is the oldest fossil you have seen or heard about?Share

    WEEK 2 — HOW FOSSILS FORM

    Lesson 1: The Fossilisation Process (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (20 min)"Step by step: 1. An organism dies in a place where it can be buried — like in mud, sand, or volcanic ash. 2. Layers of sediment (mud, sand) pile on top. 3. Over thousands to millions of years, the sediment hardens into rock — and the organism's body parts (bones, shells) slowly turn to stone. 4. Erosion (wind, water) removes the top layers, exposing the fossil."Learn process
    You Do (25 min)Sequence: Put the 6 steps of fossilisation in order. Write each step. Draw a picture for step 1 and step 6.Sequence
    Share (5 min)Why do most living things NOT become fossils? (They decompose, get eaten, or are not buried)Discuss

    Lesson 2: Types of Fossils (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"There are several TYPES of fossils. BODY FOSSILS: Actual parts of the organism — bones, shells, teeth, leaves. TRACE FOSSILS: Evidence of activity — footprints, burrows, coprolites (fossilised poo!), tooth marks. IMPRESSION FOSSILS: Prints or impressions left in rock — leaf prints, ripple marks, dinosaur skin prints. MOLD AND CAST: If a shell dissolves leaving a cavity (mold), later minerals can fill it (cast)."Learn types
    We Do (20 min)Match: Fossil type + description + example. Sort 12 fossil examples into categories.Match
    You Do (10 min)Write: "A trace fossil tells us about an organism's ___, not its ___." Complete.Write

    Lesson 3: Finding Fossils (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Investigation

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (40 min)Research: Where are famous fossil sites? Australia: Richmond in Queensland, opal fossils at Lightning Ridge,Winton (dinosaur fossils), the Flinders Ranges (ancient sea creatures). World: The Burgess Shale (Canada), Jurassic Coast (UK), Yanliao Biota (China). For each: What is found there? How old are the fossils? Why are these places special? Create a fossil site map.Research
    Share (10 min)Which fossil site would you most like to visit? Why?Share

    WEEK 3 — WHAT FOSSILS TELL US

    Lesson 1: Evidence of Ancient Life (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (20 min)"Fossils tell us: What organisms looked like (body shape, size, structure). What they ate (teeth, digestive system evidence). How they moved (footprints, tail marks). What the environment was like (coral fossils = ancient sea, leaf fossils = ancient forest). How life has changed over time (simple organisms → complex organisms). Why organisms became extinct (climate change, asteroid, disease)."Learn evidence
    You Do (25 min)Analyse fossil data: Given a set of fossil images and descriptions, for each: What does it tell us about the organism? What does it tell us about the environment? Write notes.Analyse
    Share (5 min)What is one thing you learned from a fossil that you couldn't learn any other way?Share

    Lesson 2: Extinction and Change (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"EXTINCTION = when a species dies out completely and no longer exists. The fossil record shows that MOST species that have ever lived are now extinct. Extinction happens naturally, but sometimes it happens fast — mass extinction events (5 major ones in Earth's history). The dinosaurs went extinct about 66 million years ago — likely from an asteroid impact."Learn extinction
    We Do (25 min)"Mass extinction events: The 'Big Five' — Ordovician (450 mya), Devonian (375 mya), Permian (250 mya — worst, 90% of species lost), Triassic (200 mya), Cretaceous (66 mya — dinosaurs). For each: What died? What survived? Why?" Research and record.Research
    You Do (10 min)Write: "The Permian extinction was the most devastating because..." Use evidence.Write

    Lesson 3: Reconstructing the Past (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (40 min)"You are a paleontologist. You have found a partial fossil of an unknown animal. Create a report: Sketch what you found. Describe what you think it looked like based on the evidence. Describe what environment it lived in. Estimate how old it is. Explain your reasoning." Write report with sketches.Write report
    Share (10 min)Present your animal reconstruction. Explain your evidence-based reasoning.Present

    WEEK 4 — GEOLOGICAL TIMESCALES

    Lesson 1: How Old Is Earth? (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"Earth is approximately 4.54 BILLION years old. This is almost impossible to imagine. If Earth's entire history were compressed into one year: Humans appear on December 31 at 11:59pm. Dinosaurs: June-August. First multicellular life: September. Earth forms: January 1."Understand age
    You Do (30 min)Create a timeline: On a strip of paper (or use a long corridor), mark key events. 4.5 billion years = your timeline. Mark: Earth forms (beginning), first life (3.8 bya), first animals (600 mya), first dinosaurs (245 mya), dinosaurs extinct (66 mya), first humans (300,000 ya). Calculate: If 4.5 billion = 1 year, 1 million years = ? hours?Create timeline
    Share (5 min)What surprises you most about Earth's age?Discuss

    Lesson 2: Eras and Periods (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"Scientists divide Earth's history into EONS → ERAS → PERIODS. Phanerozoic Eon (last 540 million years) has three eras: PALEOZOIC (ancient life, fish, amphibians, early reptiles, many extinctions). MESOZOIC (middle life, dinosaurs, mammals small). CENOZOIC (new life, mammals dominate, birds, humans)."Learn eras
    We Do (25 min)Create an era guide: For each era (Paleozoic, Mesozoic, Cenozoic): Write the time range. List key life forms. Write one interesting fact. Illustrate one dominant life form.Create guide
    You Do (10 min)Write: "The dinosaurs lived in the ___ era, which spans from ___ to ___ million years ago." Fill in.Write

    Lesson 3: Life Over Time (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (40 min)"Create a 'Life Over Time' display: Draw a large timeline spanning 4 billion years. Mark and illustrate: First life (single cell) / First multicellular / First fish / First land animals / First dinosaurs / First birds and mammals / Extinction of dinosaurs / First humans. Include dates and descriptions."Create display
    Share (10 min)Show your timeline. What pattern do you notice about life over time?Discuss

    WEEK 5 — EARTH'S RESOURCES

    Lesson 1: Minerals and Rocks (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (20 min)"MINERALS are natural, solid substances with a specific chemical structure. ROCKS are made of minerals. Three rock types: IGNEOUS (formed from cooled molten rock — e.g., basalt, granite). SEDIMENTARY (formed from compressed layers — e.g., sandstone, limestone). METAMORPHIC (changed by heat and pressure — e.g., marble from limestone, slate from shale)."Learn rock types
    You Do (25 min)Examine rock samples: Granite (igneous), Sandstone (sedimentary), Marble (metamorphic). For each: Describe appearance, test hardness (scratch with fingernail, nail, knife), identify rock type with reasoning. Record in a table.Examine
    Share (5 min)How can you tell a sedimentary rock from an igneous rock?Explain

    Lesson 2: Soil as a Resource (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"SOIL is a crucial resource — without soil, we cannot grow food. Soil forms from rocks breaking down over thousands of years + decomposing organic matter. Soil layers (horizons): O horizon (top — organic matter) / A horizon (topsoil — dark, fertile) / B horizon (subsoil) / C horizon (parent material — weathered rock)."Learn soil layers
    We Do (20 min)"Soil investigation: Take a soil sample. Observe: Colour, texture (sand/silt/clay), moisture, visible organic matter. Test: Put soil in water, stir, let settle — which layers form? (Sand settles fast, clay stays suspended, organic matter floats)."Investigate soil
    You Do (15 min)Write: "Soil is important because..." / "One way to protect soil is..."Write

    Lesson 3: Water Resources (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Investigation

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (40 min)"Water is a renewable resource — but clean water is limited. Only 3% of Earth's water is fresh (not salt). Most fresh water is in ice caps/glaciers. Only 1% is accessible lakes and rivers." Research: How is water accessed and treated? What is happening to water resources? Present 5 facts about water as a resource.Research
    Share (10 min)What is the biggest challenge facing water resources today?Discuss

    WEEK 6 — HUMAN USE OF RESOURCES

    Lesson 1: Mining and Extraction (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"MINING: Extracting minerals and resources from Earth. Surface mining (open-cut) vs underground mining. Australia is a major mining country — iron ore, coal, gold, copper, lithium, rare earths. Mining provides jobs and wealth but requires land and can cause pollution."Learn mining
    You Do (30 min)"Research a specific mine in Australia: Choose from: Olympic Dam (SA — copper, uranium, gold), Pilbara (WA — iron ore), Mt Isa (QLD — copper, lead), Collie (WA — coal). For your mine: What is mined? Where is it? How is it extracted? What are the environmental considerations?" Write a report.Research mine

    Lesson 2: Environmental Impact (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (30 min)"Mining and resource use impact the environment: Land clearing / Water pollution / Air pollution / Habitat destruction. BUT — there are also solutions: Rehabilitation (replanting mine sites), water treatment, pollution controls, renewable energy transition." Debate: Should we continue mining? Arguments for and against. Write the strongest argument for each side.Debate
    You Do (15 min)Write: "The most important environmental consideration when extracting resources is ___ because..."Write

    Lesson 3: Sustainability (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (40 min)"SUSTAINABILITY = meeting our needs now without compromising future generations' ability to meet theirs. Sustainable resource use: Reduce, reuse, recycle / Renewable energy / Protecting ecosystems / Balancing development with conservation." Create a sustainability action plan: Choose ONE resource issue. Propose 3 sustainable solutions. For each: Explain how it works, why it helps, what the challenge is.Create plan
    Share (10 min)Present your plan. What is the one change you think would make the biggest difference?Present

    WEEK 7 — INVESTIGATION

    Lesson 1: Fossil Dig Simulation (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Investigation

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (45 min)"You are paleontologists on a dig. You have a dig site (a tray with buried fossil replicas — shells, bones, plant impressions made in plaster or clay). Method: Sketch the site before digging. Dig carefully, documenting what you find, where, and in what orientation. Record findings. At the end, reconstruct what organism(s) were there and what environment it was." Simulate dig, record findings, write report.Dig and record

    Lesson 2: Rock and Mineral Investigation (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Investigation

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (45 min)"Test and identify: 10 rock/mineral samples (without labels). Test each: Colour, hardness (1-10 Mohs scale), streak (scratch on tile), luster (shiny/matte), crystal structure if visible. Use a mineral identification key to identify each sample. Write: Name + properties + how you identified it."Test and identify

    Lesson 3: Research Project (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Research

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (45 min)"Choose a topic: A famous fossil find / An extinct organism / A geological site in Australia / A resource issue / The geological timescale of a specific era. Research: Use at least 3 sources. Write: A 1-page information report with introduction, 3+ facts, conclusion. Include a labelled diagram or illustration."Research and write

    WEEK 8 — HOT TASK AND REFLECTION

    Lesson 1: Hot Task (Assessment)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Task (55 min)Part A: Label the process of fossilisation in 6 steps. Part B: Classify 10 resources as renewable/non-renewable with justification. Part C: Answer: "How do fossils provide evidence of past life and environments?" (8 sentences minimum). Part D: Calculate and place 5 events on a geological timeline. Part E: Explain one environmental impact of resource extraction and one sustainable solution.Independent assessment

    Compare Cold Task → Hot Task. Note growth in understanding.

    Lesson 2: Review (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Review

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (25 min)Review stations: 1. Fossil identification. 2. Rock/mineral identification. 3. Timeline ordering. 4. Renewable vs non-renewable sort. Rotate through all 4.Review
    Share (25 min)"What is the most fascinating thing you learned about Earth's history?" Write 3 sentences. Share with class.Reflect

    Lesson 3: Celebration (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Celebration

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Celebration (50 min)Display research projects, fossil dig reports, rock identification sheets. Award certificates: Best Fossil Excavation Report, Best Rock Identification, Most Sustainable Solution, Best Timeline.Celebrate

    ADAPTATIONS

    EAL Students

  • Visual fossil formation process chart
  • Word wall: fossil, sediment, erosion, extinct, mineral, renewable
  • Label images with names in home language + English
  • Sentence starters for scientific explanations
  • Use of visual timelines with images, not just numbers

    • Gifted Students

  • Research the Burgess Shale and what it tells us about soft-bodied animals
  • Investigate the dinosaur-bird link (theropod dinosaurs → modern birds)
  • Explore the Younger Dryas impact hypothesis or the Cretaceous-Paleogene extinction in detail
  • Research geoengineering as a solution to climate change

    • Students with Additional Needs

  • Reduce to core concepts: Fossils form / Fossils tell us about past / Earth is old / Some resources can run out
  • Provide labelled diagrams for fossil types
  • Use hands-on fossil dig with pre-made replicas
  • One-on-one support for written tasks
  • Extra time for all tasks
