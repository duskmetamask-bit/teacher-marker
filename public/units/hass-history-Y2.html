Unit Content

Family, Community, and Time — History Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 2

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum HASS v9 — History (Year 2)

Unit Big Idea: Families, communities, and societies change over time. Understanding how families and communities lived in the past helps us understand how we got to where we are today and where we might go in the future.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Investigate how your family and community have changed over time

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Present a comparison of past and present life in your family or community

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to investigate how families and communities in Australia have changed over time, and to understand how the past is different from the present.

Success Criteria:

  • I can describe at least 3 ways my family and community have changed over time.
  • I can use sources (photos, objects, stories) to find out about the past.
  • I can sequence events in my family's or community's history.
  • I can describe how daily life (school, home, work, play) was different in the past.
  • I can explain why communities change over time.
  • I can compare life in the past with life today and give reasons for differences.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: Family and Community Over Time

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Family and Community Change

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Show two photos: a classroom from the 1950s vs today. "What is the same? What is different? What do you think life was like for children who sat in that classroom?"Observe and compare
    Introduction (5 min)"In this unit, we will explore how FAMILIES and COMMUNITIES have changed over time. We will look at our own family histories, our community's past, and how life today compares to life in the past. You will become HISTORY DETECTIVES!"Listen
    Cold Task (30 min)"Ask someone at home: What is one way our family life has changed since you were young? What is one way our neighbourhood or community has changed? Draw and label at least 2 changes. Write 3–4 sentences explaining one change and WHY it happened." Year 2 expectation: 2 drawings + detailed labels + 3–4 sentences with reason.Independent

    Differentiation: Provide interview questions in advance. Sentence starters: "One change is that..." / "This changed because..." / "Another change is that..." / "This affected families by..."

    Success Criteria Check: Student describes at least 1 change in family or community life and gives at least 1 reason for the change.

    Lesson 2: What Is Family?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Hook)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is a Family? (10 min)"A family is a group of people who are related to each other and who love and care for one another. Families can look very different. Some families are big, some are small. Some have two parents, some have one, some have grandparents living with them, some have two mums or two dads. ALL families are real families."Discuss
    Family Roles (10 min)"In all families, people have ROLES — things they do for the family. Parents might work, cook, clean. Children might help, go to school, play. Grandparents might share wisdom, tell stories, help with care. What roles do the people in YOUR family have?"Discuss
    Families Change (10 min)"Families CHANGE over time. Children grow up and might move away. Grandparents might come to live with the family. New babies join the family. Families MOVE — some families move to new places. All families are always changing."Learn
    Draw My Family Roles (10 min)"Draw your family. Label one thing each person does for your family."Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 roles in their family and explain that families change over time.

    Lesson 3: What Is Community?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Concept Introduction)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is a Community? (10 min)"A COMMUNITY is a group of people who live in the same place and share things with each other. Your school is a community. Your neighbourhood is a community. Your city or town is a community. Your cultural or religious group is a community. You belong to MANY communities."Learn
    Communities I Belong To (10 min)"List the communities you belong to: School community / Neighbourhood / Sports club or dance class / Cultural community / Religious community / Online community. Which of these is the most important to you?"List
    What Do Communities Share? (10 min)"Communities share: PLACES (parks, shops, schools) / SERVICES (bus routes, libraries, hospitals) / CULTURE (traditions, celebrations, values) / HISTORY (the story of how the community grew). Communities have a PAST — they were not always the way they are now."Learn
    My Community Drawing (10 min)"Draw your neighbourhood community. Include: your home, 2 places you go to, 1 person who helps your community."Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 3 communities they belong to and explain what communities share.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Families Change

    Lesson 1: My Family Tree

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Family Trees Show History (10 min)"A family tree is a diagram that shows who is related to whom. At the bottom: YOU. Above: your parents. Above them: your grandparents. Above them: great-grandparents. If you go back far enough, everyone has LOTS of relatives!"Learn
    Generations (10 min)"A GENERATION is all the people born around the same time. You are one generation. Your parents are another. Your grandparents are another. How many generations can you name back?"Learn
    My Family Tree (15 min)Distribute family tree template. Students fill in: Me / My parents / My grandparents / Great-grandparents (if known). Ask: Who can you find out from? Who might know the family history?Complete
    Share (5 min)"Who in our class has the same type of family tree? (nuclear, extended, blended, single-parent)."Share

    Success Criteria Check: Creates a family tree showing at least 3 generations.

    Lesson 2: Family Stories

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Every Family Has Stories (10 min)"Every family has STORIES — things that happened in the past that family members remember and tell. Family stories help us know who we are. They connect us to our past."Listen
    Types of Family Stories (10 min)Family story types: ARRIVAL STORIES (how the family came to Australia or a particular place) / SPECIAL MOMENTS (a wedding, a birth, an achievement) / MEMORABLE EVENTS (a flood, a move, a celebration) / FUNNY STORIES (something that made the family laugh) / LESSONS (something the family learned the hard way)."Learn
    My Family Story (10 min)"Ask someone at home: What is one story our family tells that has been passed down? Who does the story go back to? What happened? Share what you learn tomorrow."Take home
    If No Family Stories (5 min)"If you don't know family stories yet, ask: What story would you like to find out? Who could you ask?"Plan

    Success Criteria Check: Asks at least 1 question about family history to someone at home.

    Lesson 3: How Families Have Changed

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australian Families Have Changed (10 min)"Australian families have changed a lot in 100 years. In the past: more people lived on farms, more extended families lived together, fewer mothers worked outside the home, families were more likely to stay in the same place."Listen
    Why Do Families Change? (15 min)"Families change because: WORK changed (more people work in offices, fewer on farms) / MIGRATION changed Australia (people came from new countries) / TECHNOLOGY changed how we communicate / LAWS changed (e.g., women's rights, child welfare) / EDUCATION changed (more children go to school for longer)."Learn
    Our Class Families (10 min)"Who in our class has family that came from another country? Who has family that has been in Australia for a long time? This shows how Australian families have changed — we are more diverse than ever."Discuss
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is one way Australian families have changed in the past 100 years? Why did this change happen?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students draw changes in families. Gifted students: interview a grandparent about family change.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 changes in Australian families over the past 100 years and give at least 1 reason for the change.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: Our Community Over Time

    Lesson 1: Our Community Yesterday

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Our Community Has a Past (10 min)"Our community existed BEFORE US. The streets, buildings, and places we use every day were built by people who came before us. To understand our community, we must understand its history."Listen
    Historical Photos (15 min)Show old photos of the local area from council archives, Trove (trove.nla.gov.au), or local historical societies. Is this the same suburb? What has changed? What is still the same?Observe
    Changes We Can See (10 min)"What changes do you see? (Old buildings replaced by new ones, fewer trees, more cars, different shops, new roads, different signs). These changes tell us about the past."Identify
    Draw One Change (5 min)"Draw one thing in our community that looks different today than it did in the old photos."Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Can compare at least 2 features of the local community between past and present using historical photos.

    Lesson 2: Who Lived Here First?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples (10 min)"Before European colonisation, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples lived all over Australia. They were the FIRST people here — and have been here the longest of any people on Earth. For over 65,000 years, they cared for this land."Listen
    What Was Community Like? (15 min)"Before 1788, Aboriginal communities: covered all of Australia / had over 250 language groups / had complex trade networks / had seasonal calendars tied to Country / lived sustainably for 65,000+ years / had governance systems, laws, and ceremonies. Children's lives: learned from Elders, played games, took on responsibilities gradually."Learn
    Connection to Place (10 min)"Aboriginal peoples did not just LIVE on Country — they BELONGED to Country. Country is the land, water, plants, animals, and spiritual connections that belong together. Your mob, your Country — this is identity."Understand
    Learning Local Language (5 min)"What Noongar words do we know for this area? Our school is on Noongar Boodjar (Noongar Country)."Learn

    Note: Ideally taught with support from local Aboriginal education resources.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 aspects of Aboriginal peoples' life before colonisation and explain that they are the first peoples of Australia.

    Lesson 3: Changes in My Neighbourhood

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    How Do We Know About the Past? (10 min)"We know about the past because: OLD PHOTOS show us / STORIES from old people / WRITTEN RECORDS (newspapers, council documents) / ARCHAEOLOGY (digging up the past) / BUILDINGS that still stand. These are all SOURCES."Learn
    Investigating Our Neighbourhood (15 min)"Let's research our neighbourhood's history. I will show you 3 sources. For each: What does this source show us? What question does it answer? What new question does it raise?" Sources: old map, old photo, recent aerial photo.Investigate
    Why Do Neighbourhoods Change? (10 min)"Neighbourhoods change because: more people move in (population growth) / new buildings replace old ones / new shops open / new roads are built / more cars = more parking = changes / people move away. Change is normal."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What is one reason our neighbourhood has changed? What was here before your house or school?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 changes in their neighbourhood and give at least 1 reason why.

    WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Daily Life Then and Now

    Lesson 1: School Then and Now

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Schools in the Past (10 min)"Schools in the past looked very different. In the 1800s: some children went to one-room schoolhouses with multiple ages in one class / slates (soft stone tablets) instead of paper / no electricity in early schools / very strict rules / some children (especially girls, Indigenous children, poor children) could not go to school."Listen
    Australian School History (15 min)"In Australia: schooling became more widespread in the 1870s–1900s / compulsory schooling laws came in (different ages in different states) / Aboriginal children were often NOT allowed to attend mainstream schools until 1960s–1970s / Technology: typewriters came in the 1900s, computers in the 1980s, tablets in the 2010s."Learn
    School Life in the 1950s–70s (10 min)"In the 1950s–70s: mostly white Australian population in schools / no computers or internet / school was more formal / corporal punishment was legal / children often walked to school alone."Learn
    Compare with Today (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: How is school today different from school in the 1950s? What do you think is better about today? What might have been good about the past?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 differences between school today and school in the past.

    Lesson 2: Home Life Then and Now

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Homes Have Changed (10 min)"Homes in the past were different. Long ago in Australia: no electricity (kerosene lamps, gas lights) / no running water in all homes / no refrigeration (ice boxes) / no air conditioning / simpler foods / more home-made things / no internet, no mobile phones, no TV."Listen
    A Day in the Life — 1950s Child (15 min)"A typical 1950s Australian child's day: walked to school (no school buses unless far) / school days were shorter / after school: played outside until dark / limited TV (ABC TV started 1956 — first TV in Australia) / homework was shorter / family dinner was a regular time / Sunday was church and family day."Learn
    A Day Today vs Then (10 min)"Compare: How do you get to school? (Car, bus, walk) / What do you do after school? / Do you watch TV? (How much?) / Do you have screen time limits? / What is your family's dinner time like?"Compare
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: How is your daily life at home different from a child's daily life in the 1950s? Which differences do you think are biggest?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can compare at least 3 aspects of home life today with home life in the 1950s.

    Lesson 3: Work Then and Now

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Work Has Changed (10 min)"The TYPES of work people do have changed a lot. Long ago in Australia: many people worked on farms / fewer women worked outside the home (mostly in 1950s–60s changes) / many jobs were done by hand / no computers or robots / children sometimes worked (not allowed in most places now). Today: more service jobs, more technology, more women in workforce."Listen
    Jobs in the Past (15 min)"In the past, jobs were very different: blacksmiths (horse shoe makers), chandlers (candle makers), seamstresses / telegraph operators / factory workers in manufacturing / milkmen, bread deliveries / most work was physical. These jobs mostly don't exist now."Learn
    Why Did Work Change? (10 min)"Work changed because: TECHNOLOGY (machines replaced hand work) / LAWS (child labour banned, workplace safety laws) / EDUCATION (more schooling for more jobs) / GLOBALISATION (work goes to places with cheaper costs). Jobs are always changing."Learn
    What Jobs Might There Be in the Future? (5 min)"What job do you think you might have when you grow up that doesn't exist today?"Think

    Differentiation: EAL students draw a job from the past. Gifted students: interview someone about how their job has changed.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 jobs from the past that no longer exist and explain why work has changed.

    WEEK 5 — EXPLAIN: Communication and Transport

    Lesson 1: How Did People Communicate?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Communication Has Transformed (10 min)"Communication — how we send messages to each other — has changed more in the last 30 years than in the previous 300. Long ago: written letters (took weeks to arrive) / telegrams (fast but expensive) / landline telephone (fixed location) / fax machines. Today: email (instant), mobile phones (anywhere), video calls (face to face anywhere)."Learn
    Timeline of Communication (15 min)"Communication through time: 1788–1850: spoken word, handwritten letters, word of mouth / 1850–1950: telegraph, telephone, newspapers, radio / 1950–2000: television, fax, mobile phones / 2000–present: internet, smartphones, social media, video calls."Learn
    Source Analysis: Old Letters (10 min)Show images of historical letters, telegrams, faxes. "These sources tell us how people communicated in the past. What do they look like? What does the message say? How is this different from how YOU send messages?"Analyse
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: How did people send messages to each other 100 years ago? How is this different from how you send messages today?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 forms of communication from the past and compare them to how people communicate today.

    Lesson 2: How Did People Travel?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Transport Has Changed (10 min)"How we get from one place to another has changed completely. Long ago: walking, horse and cart, sailing ships / 1800s: Cobb & Co coaches (long distance mail), steam trains / 1900s: cars, buses, trams, early planes / Today: planes (fast international travel), ride-share (Uber), electric vehicles, trains, bikes."Learn
    Travel in Australia's Past (15 min)"In Australian history: First Fleet sailed from England to Australia — took 8 months / 1850s gold rush: people came from all over the world to Australia by ship / 1900s: railways connected cities but NOT cross-continental / 1920s–30s: first commercial flights / Today: Perth to Sydney is 4 hours by plane. In the 1850s, it took WEEKS."Learn
    Compare Travel (10 min)"How did you get to school today? In the 1950s, most Australian children walked or rode bikes. What is the biggest difference between how you travel and how children travelled in the 1950s?"Compare
    Draw a Mode of Transport from the Past (5 min)"Draw one mode of transport from the past and label what it is and when it was used."Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Can compare at least 2 modes of transport from the past with how people travel today.

    Lesson 3: Technology Changes Lives

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Technology Changes Everything (10 min)"TECHNOLOGY is tools and knowledge that help us do things better. Technology has changed EVERYTHING: how we live, work, communicate, travel, learn, and play. Every generation has experienced technology change."Learn
    Then vs Now Technology (15 min)Compare pairs: candlelight vs LED / word processor vs fountain pen / smartphone vs landline / car vs horse / washing machine vs washboard / GPS vs paper map. "What technology is the BIGGEST change? The MOST SURPRISING?"Compare
    Good and Challenging Aspects (10 min)"Technology brings GOOD things (we can video-call grandparents, find information instantly) and CHALLENGING things (less face-to-face contact, screen addiction, privacy). Are the good things worth the challenges?"Discuss
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is one way technology has changed life for Australian families? What is good about this change? What is challenging?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students create a visual timeline of technology. Gifted students: research one specific technology (e.g., television, mobile phones, internet) and trace its impact.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 ways technology has changed daily life and give 1 positive and 1 challenging aspect.

    WEEK 6 — ELABORATE: Celebrations and Traditions

    Lesson 1: How Did Australians Celebrate?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Celebrations Reflect Their Time (10 min)"CELEBRATIONS are special times when people come together to mark something important. Celebrations reflect the time and culture they come from. What was important to people in the past? What celebrations did they have?"Listen
    Australian Celebrations History (15 min)"Celebrations in Australian history: AUSTRALIA DAY (26 January — marking British settlement, now controversial for many) / ANZAC DAY (25 April — commemorating WWI soldiers) / Empire Day (celebrating British Empire, no longer observed) / SUBMARINE CENTENARY / TERRITORY DAY / INDEPENDENCE DAY (different dates in different colonies before Federation). Celebrations change as values change."Learn
    How Have Celebrations Changed? (10 min)"In the 1950s: more British-oriented celebrations / fewer diverse cultural celebrations / Christmas and Easter were dominant / fewer public holidays specific to Australia / Today: more multicultural celebrations / NAIDOC Week (Indigenous recognition) / Harmony Day / many cultural festivals."Compare
    Draw a Celebration (5 min)"Draw one celebration that is important to you and write why it matters."Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 celebrations from Australia's past and explain how celebrations have become more diverse.

    Lesson 2: Cultural Celebrations in Australia

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australia Is Culturally Diverse (10 min)"Australia is one of the most culturally diverse countries in the world. Today: 30% of Australians were born overseas / 300+ languages are spoken / all major world religions are practised / people from all over the world call Australia home. This diversity has been growing since the 1970s end of the White Australia Policy."Learn
    Celebrations We Share (15 min)"What celebrations do people in our class share? Chinese New Year? Eid al-Fitr? Diwali? Hanukkah? Christmas? Easter? Buddhist festivals? These are all celebrated in Australia — by people from many different cultures."Discuss
    Learning About Each Other's Celebrations (10 min)"We are going to learn about celebrations from different cultures represented in our class. What celebration would you like to share? What do people do? What do they eat? What does it mean?"Plan
    Research and Share (5 min)"Take home: Find out about one celebration from a culture in our class. Bring back: What is it called? When is it? What do people do?"Research

    Note: Handle cultural diversity with respect and ensure all cultures are treated equally.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 celebrations from different cultures and explain why cultural celebrations matter in Australia.

    Lesson 3: Our Class Celebrations

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Celebrations in Our Class (10 min)"Let's make a class calendar of celebrations. We have been learning about many cultures. What celebrations do we have in our class? Let's add them to our calendar."Create
    NAIDOC Week (10 min)"NAIDOC Week is a special Australian celebration. It stands for National Aboriginal and Islander Day Observance. It is held in July (first Sunday to second Sunday). It celebrates Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples' culture and history. It is one of Australia's most important celebrations."Learn
    Harmony Day (10 min)"Harmony Day (21 March) celebrates cultural diversity. People wear orange or the national colour of their culture. The message is 'everyone belongs.' It reflects how multicultural Australia has become."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: Why is it important that Australia has celebrations like NAIDOC Week and Harmony Day?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students draw celebrations from their own culture. Gifted students: create a class celebration calendar with all cultures represented.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 Australian celebrations that reflect cultural diversity and explain why they matter.

    WEEK 7 — ELABORATE + EVALUATE: History Detectives

    Lesson 1: Source Analysis

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Sources Reveal History (10 min)"HISTORY DETECTIVES use SOURCES to find out about the past. A SOURCE is anything that tells us about history. PRIMARY sources are from the time being studied (a diary entry from 1950, a photo taken in 1950). SECONDARY sources are from later (a history book about the 1950s)."Learn
    Primary vs Secondary Sources (15 min)Show examples: PRIMARY: a letter written in 1955, a photo taken in 1955 / SECONDARY: a book about the 1950s published in 2020, a documentary about the 1950s. "Primary sources are usually more reliable because they are from the time."Learn
    Analyse an Old Photo (15 min)Distribute copies of an old photograph of the local area or school. Students answer: What do you see? What year might it be from? What questions does it raise? What can you NOT tell from this photo?Analyse
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What is the difference between a primary source and a secondary source? Which is more reliable? Why?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can distinguish between primary and secondary sources and analyse at least 1 historical source.

    Lesson 2: Investigate a Local Site

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Our Local Site Investigation (10 min)"Today we will be HISTORY DETECTIVES investigating our school site. Our school has a history. Who built it? When? What was here before? How has it changed?"Prepare
    Investigation Steps (15 min)Students investigate their local site using provided resources: old photos, school records, Google Maps history, interviews. Key questions: What was here before the school? When was it built? How has it changed? What stories do old staff or community members know?Investigate
    Record Findings (10 min)Students record findings: What I found out / My sources / My questions remaining.Record
    Share (5 min)3–4 students share one discovery about their local site.Share

    Success Criteria Check: Completes a source-based investigation of the local site and records at least 3 facts.

    Lesson 3: Prepare Hot Task

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Preparation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hot Task Preview (5 min)"For tomorrow's Hot Task: Present a COMPARISON of life in the past vs life today in your family or community. You may: compare a family tradition then vs now / compare a local place then vs now / compare your school then vs now / compare daily life then vs now."Preview
    Plan Your Comparison (15 min)Students choose their comparison focus and plan: What am I comparing? What was it like in the past? What is it like today? How and why did it change? What sources can I use?Plan
    Prepare Materials (15 min)Students prepare their presentation: drawings, photos, timeline, written comparison.Prepare
    Practice Share (5 min)"Rehearse telling your comparison to someone. Can you explain HOW and WHY it changed?"Practice

    Differentiation: EAL students may prepare a visual comparison. NEP students may present verbally.

    Success Criteria Check: Completes a Hot Task preparation plan with clear comparison focus and supporting materials.

    WEEK 8 — EVALUATE: Hot Task and Reflection

    Lesson 1: Hot Task — Past and Present Comparison

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Summative Assessment

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Set Up (5 min)"Today is your Hot Task. Present a comparison of life in the past vs life today. Choose ONE thing: your family, your home, your school, your neighbourhood, or your community. Describe: What was it like in the past? What is it like today? How and why did it change? Use at least 3 sources or pieces of evidence."Listen
    Presentations (35 min)Students present their comparisons. Teacher uses checklist: Clear comparison? / Past described? / Present described? / Reason for change given? / Sources used?Present

    Assessment Note: Use rubric to assess. Compare to Cold Task for growth analysis.

    Lesson 2: Cold Task vs Hot Task — Growth Reflection

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Self-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Compare (15 min)Place Cold Task next to Hot Task. "Use a green texta to mark 3 things you did BETTER in your Hot Task. Use a red texta to mark 1 thing you could improve."Compare
    Growth Statement (10 min)"I have grown as a history detective because..." Write 4–6 sentences.Write
    Share (10 min)Volunteers share one thing they know now that they didn't know at the start.Share
    Most Important Learning (5 min)"What is the most important thing you learned about how families and communities change?"Share

    Success Criteria Check: Completes self-comparison showing growth in historical skills.

    Lesson 3: Celebration — Our Histories

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Celebration

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    History Detective Certificate (10 min)Award "History Detective" certificates.Receive
    Class Book: Our Community Over Time (15 min)Each child contributes one page: a comparison of past and present life in their family or community. Bound and placed in the class library.Contribute
    What We Learned (10 min)"What is one thing you learned that you will remember about families and communities changing?"Share
    What's Next? (5 min)"What period of history would you most like to learn more about?"Share

    DIFFERENTIATION NOTES

    EAL Learners

  • Home Language Research: Students may investigate their family's country of origin as a family history connection.
  • Visual Presentations: Focus on drawings, photos, and objects rather than extended writing.
  • Partner Support: Paired for sharing activities.
  • Cultural Sensitivity: Respect all family structures, migration stories, and cultural practices.

    • Gifted Learners

  • Research Extension: Interview multiple family members across different generations to create a multi-generational history.
  • Heritage Investigation: Research a specific heritage place in the local area using primary sources.
  • Comparative Study: Compare Australian family life to family life in another country.

    • Students with NEPs

  • Reduced Written Expectation: 4–6 sentences for written tasks.
  • Oral Option: All tasks may be presented verbally.
  • Extra Time: Additional time for all tasks.
  • Word Banks: Provided for all written tasks.
  • Scaffolded Templates: Family tree template, Venn diagram template, timeline template.
