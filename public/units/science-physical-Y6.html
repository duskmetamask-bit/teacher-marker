Unit Content

Energy, Forces, and Motion — Physical Sciences Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 6

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum Science v9 — Physical Sciences

Unit Big Idea: Energy exists in different forms and drives all motion and change in the universe. Understanding the relationship between energy, forces, and motion is fundamental to understanding how everything from planets to particles behaves.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Design an investigation to explore the relationship between kinetic energy, potential energy, and motion

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Design a system that manages energy and forces to achieve a specific goal, with evidence-based analysis

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to investigate the relationship between energy and forces, describe how energy transforms between kinetic and potential forms, explain how forces cause acceleration and changes in motion, and apply physics principles to design and analyse real-world systems.

Success Criteria:

  • I can explain kinetic energy (energy of motion) and potential energy (stored energy) and calculate one form given the other.
  • I can describe how forces cause acceleration according to Newton's laws.
  • I can investigate and calculate the efficiency of energy transformations in mechanical systems.
  • I can explain how gravity, friction, and other forces affect motion and energy.
  • I can design and analyse systems using energy and force principles.
  • I can use evidence and data to support conclusions about energy and motion.

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Design an Energy and Motion Investigation

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Drop a ball from different heights. "Why does the ball speed up as it falls? Where does the energy come from? Where does it go when the ball stops bouncing?"Observe and wonder
    Task (35 min)Cold Task: "Design an investigation to explore the relationship between energy and motion. You must: What will you investigate? (e.g., how height affects speed, how mass affects how far something slides). What equipment will you need? What will you measure? What do you predict will happen? Draw your experimental setup. Write a hypothesis in the form: IF... THEN... BECAUSE..."Design investigation
    Share (10 min)3–4 students share their designs. Class discusses variables.Share

    Differentiation: EAL students draw investigation with teacher scribing. NEP students: design investigation with teacher guidance.

    Success Criteria Check: Student designs an investigation with variables, measurements, and prediction.

    Lesson 2: Forms of Energy

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Engage (Concept Review and Extension)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Energy Forms Review (15 min)"KINETIC ENERGY (KE): energy of MOVING objects. Any object in motion has kinetic energy. Formula: KE = ½mv² (half × mass × velocity squared). POTENTIAL ENERGY (PE): stored energy due to position or condition. GRAVITATIONAL PE: stored energy due to height. Formula: PE = mgh (mass × gravity × height). ELASTIC PE: stored in stretched/compressed objects (springs, rubber bands)."Learn energy forms
    Calculate Kinetic Energy (15 min)"KE = ½mv². A 2kg ball moving at 3m/s: KE = ½ × 2 × 3² = ½ × 2 × 9 = 9 Joules. A 2kg ball moving at 6m/s: KE = ½ × 2 × 6² = ½ × 2 × 36 = 36 Joules. Notice: doubling velocity QUADRUPLES kinetic energy. This is why car crashes at 100km/h are much worse than at 50km/h."Calculate KE
    Calculate Potential Energy (10 min)"PE = mgh. A 2kg ball at 5m height: PE = 2 × 10 × 5 = 100 Joules. Same ball at 10m: PE = 2 × 10 × 10 = 200 Joules. Doubling height doubles gravitational potential energy."Calculate PE
    Write (5 min)"KE = ½mv². PE = mgh. Velocity has a SQUARED effect on kinetic energy — small speed increases mean large energy increases."Write formulas

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly calculates kinetic and potential energy using formulas.

    Lesson 3: Newton's Laws Introduction

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Engage (Concept Introduction)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Who Was Newton? (10 min)"Sir Isaac Newton (1643–1727) was one of the greatest scientists in history. He discovered the laws of motion and universal gravitation. The story goes that he saw an apple fall from a tree and wondered: why did it fall DOWN? What force pulled it? That question led to the understanding of GRAVITATION."Learn context
    Newton's Three Laws Preview (15 min)"FIRST LAW (Inertia): Objects at rest stay at rest, objects in motion stay in motion, unless acted upon by an external force. SECOND LAW (F=ma): Force = mass × acceleration. More force = more acceleration. More mass = less acceleration for the same force. THIRD LAW (Action-Reaction): For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."Learn three laws
    Examples for Each Law (15 min)"FIRST LAW: Seatbelt keeps you from flying forward when a car stops suddenly — without it, your body would keep moving. SECOND LAW: Pushing an empty shopping cart is easy (low mass). Pushing a full one requires more force. THIRD LAW: When you jump off a boat, the boat pushes back in the opposite direction — this is why it rolls away."Match examples
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Give one everyday example of Newton's Third Law."Written response

    Success Criteria Check: States Newton's three laws in own words and provides one example of each.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Kinetic and Potential Energy

    Lesson 1: Kinetic Energy

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Kinetic Energy Investigation (15 min)"Let's investigate: Does kinetic energy increase linearly or exponentially with speed?" Setup: Ramp at fixed height. Release ball from top. Measure speed at bottom (using stopwatch over known distance OR motion sensor). Repeat from SAME height (same PE, same KE at bottom).Plan investigation
    Mass vs Speed Investigation (20 min)"Does mass affect kinetic energy?" Use balls of different masses (golf ball, tennis ball, cricket ball) released from same height. Measure speed at bottom. KE = ½mv². Calculate KE for each. What do you notice? (All reach similar speeds but KE is proportional to mass.)Test mass effect
    Speed Squared Effect (10 min)"If we release ball from HEIGHT h, it reaches speed v. If we release from height 2h, it reaches speed √2 × v. Speed increases with the SQUARE ROOT of height. But KE doubles because KE = ½mv² and v² doubles."Learn sqrt relationship
    Record (5 min)Complete investigation sheet. Graph: mass vs KE. Graph: speed vs KE.Record

    Success Criteria Check: Investigates kinetic energy, calculates KE for different masses and speeds, and correctly identifies that KE increases with mass and with the square of velocity.

    Lesson 2: Potential Energy

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Gravitational PE Investigation (15 min)"PE = mgh. Let's test: Does height affect PE linearly?" Use same object (e.g., 1kg mass). Release from different heights (10cm, 20cm, 30cm, 40cm). Calculate PE at each height. Plot graph: HEIGHT vs PE. Is it a straight line? (Yes — PE is proportional to height.)Test height effect
    Mass vs PE Investigation (15 min)"Does mass affect PE linearly?" Use different masses (0.5kg, 1kg, 1.5kg, 2kg) at same height (50cm). Calculate PE. Plot graph: MASS vs PE. Is it a straight line? (Yes — PE is proportional to mass.)Test mass effect
    Energy at Different Heights (15 min)"At the TOP of the ramp: maximum PE (stored), zero KE (not moving yet). At the BOTTOM: maximum KE (fast), zero PE (low height). MID-WAY: half PE, half KE (if no friction)." Calculate total energy at each point: PE + KE = constant (ignoring friction).Learn energy conservation
    Record (5 min)"Gravitational PE = mgh. GPE increases linearly with height and mass. Total mechanical energy is conserved in the absence of non-conservative forces."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Investigates gravitational potential energy, correctly calculates PE at different heights and masses, and explains energy conservation.

    Lesson 3: Energy Transformations

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Pendulum Investigation (15 min)"A pendulum demonstrates continuous energy transformation." Pull pendulum to one side (maximum PE, zero KE). Release (PE decreases → KE increases). At bottom (maximum KE, zero PE). Swings to other side (KE decreases → PE increases). Time one full swing (period). Does it depend on amplitude? (No — period depends only on length and gravity, for small angles.)Investigate pendulum
    Calculate Energy at Each Point (15 min)Pendulum bob mass = 0.5kg, string length = 1m. At top (0.5m above lowest point): PE = 0.5 × 10 × 0.5 = 2.5J. At bottom: all 2.5J is KE → v = √(2×2.5/0.5) = √10 ≈ 3.16m/s. Halfway: PE = 1.25J, KE = 1.25J.Calculate
    Energy Loss to Friction (10 min)"In real pendulums, the swing eventually stops. Where did the energy go? AIR FRICTION converts mechanical energy to thermal energy (air and string warm slightly). Total energy is CONSERVED — it just becomes less useful (low-grade heat)."Learn friction loss
    Real-World Application (10 min)"Suspension bridges: cars going over cause tiny vibrations (kinetic). Cables and supports convert some to thermal (friction). Engineers design bridges to minimise this. Bouncy balls: each bounce is slightly lower because friction and internal deformation convert some KE to heat."Apply to real world

    Differentiation: EAL students draw energy transformations. Gifted students: derive the period formula for a pendulum (T = 2π√(L/g)).

    Success Criteria Check: Investigates energy transformation in a pendulum, calculates PE and KE at multiple points, and explains why the pendulum eventually stops.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLAIN: Forces and Motion

    Lesson 1: Newton's First and Second Laws

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Newton's First Law — Inertia (15 min)"Objects resist changes in motion. This resistance is called INERTIA. More mass = more inertia. A heavy object is harder to start moving and harder to stop than a light one. This is why seatbelts work — your body wants to keep moving forward when the car stops." Demonstrate: coins on card, flick card — coins fall into cup (coins want to stay at rest).Learn First Law
    Second Law Investigation (15 min)"F = ma. Force = mass × acceleration." Test: Apply same force to different masses. Measure acceleration. Graph: force/mass vs acceleration. It should be constant. "If F is constant and m doubles, acceleration halves."Investigate Second Law
    Calculate with F=ma (15 min)"A 1000kg car needs to accelerate at 5m/s²: F = 1000 × 5 = 5000N. A person (70kg) accelerating at 3m/s² while running: F = 70 × 3 = 210N. An apple (0.2kg) falling at 10m/s²: F = 0.2 × 10 = 2N (weight)."Practice calculations
    Write (5 min)"F = ma. More force = more acceleration. More mass = less acceleration for the same force."Write formula

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly calculates force, mass, or acceleration using F = ma for at least 3 scenarios.

    Lesson 2: Newton's Third Law

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Action-Reaction Pairs (15 min)"Newton's Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. Forces ALWAYS come in pairs. You push on the ground (action) → the ground pushes on you (reaction). A rocket pushes gas out backward (action) → gas pushes rocket forward (reaction). Birds push air down (action) → air pushes bird up (reaction)."Learn Third Law
    Identify Action-Reaction Pairs (15 min)Scenarios: (1) Walking: foot pushes backward on ground → ground pushes forward on foot. (2) Swimming: hands push water backward → water pushes swimmer forward. (3) Jumping: legs push down on Earth → Earth pushes up on legs. (4) Gun firing: bullet pushed forward → gun pushed backward (recoil). Draw force diagrams showing both forces in each pair.Identify pairs
    Common Misconceptions (10 min)"Action and reaction forces ACT ON DIFFERENT OBJECTS — so they DON'T cancel out! Foot pushes on ground (on ground). Ground pushes on foot (on foot). These are different objects, so they don't cancel. Internal forces within a system can cancel, but action-reaction pairs act between two different objects."Address misconceptions
    Investigation (10 min)"Blow up a balloon. Let it go. The balloon flies forward. What is the action-reaction pair? Balloon pushes air out backward (action) → air pushes balloon forward (reaction). This is a rocket engine!"Investigate balloon rocket

    Success Criteria Check: Identifies action-reaction pairs in at least 5 scenarios and explains why they don't cancel.

    Lesson 3: Force Diagrams

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Drawing Force Diagrams (15 min)"A FORCE DIAGRAM shows all forces acting on ONE object. Each force is an ARROW pointing in the direction the force acts. Arrow length = magnitude. Label: WHAT is applying the force, WHAT type of force." Example — book on table: DOWN: Gravity from Earth (Weight = mg). UP: Normal force from table (N). Example — car accelerating: RIGHT: Engine force from road. LEFT: Friction and air resistance. DOWN: Weight. UP: Normal force from road.Learn to draw
    Practice Drawing (20 min)Draw force diagrams for: (1) Ball falling downward (gravity down, air resistance up). (2) Rocket taking off (thrust up, weight down). (3) Person in lift accelerating upward (weight down, tension up, net force up). (4) Skier sliding downhill at constant speed (gravity down slope, friction up slope, normal perpendicular).Draw diagrams
    Balanced vs Unbalanced (10 min)"If all forces ADD TO ZERO = BALANCED = object moves at constant velocity or stays still. If forces DON'T add to zero = UNBALANCED = object ACCELERATES in direction of net force."Learn balanced/unbalanced
    Exit Ticket (5 min)Draw force diagram for a plane flying level at constant speed. Label all forces.Draw diagram

    Success Criteria Check: Draws accurate force diagrams for at least 4 scenarios, correctly identifying all forces and their directions.

    WEEK 4 — ELABORATE: Gravity and Orbital Motion

    Lesson 1: Gravitational Force

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Universal Gravitation (15 min)"Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation: EVERY object in the universe attracts EVERY OTHER object with a force proportional to their masses and inversely proportional to the distance squared. F = Gm₁m₂/r². The more mass, the stronger the pull. The farther apart, the weaker the pull (inverse square law — double distance, quarter the force)."Learn gravitation
    Calculate Weight on Different Planets (15 min)"Weight = mg where g = GM/r² (G = gravitational constant, M = planet mass, r = distance from centre). Earth: g = 9.8 m/s². Moon: g = 1.6 m/s². Mars: g = 3.7 m/s². Jupiter: g = 24.8 m/s². A 60kg person weighs: Earth 588N, Moon 96N, Mars 222N, Jupiter 1488N." Calculate for different planets.Calculate weights
    Gravity and Free Fall (10 min)"In free fall (no air resistance), ALL objects accelerate at the SAME rate regardless of mass. On Earth: g = 9.8 m/s². This is because the greater mass of an object means greater gravitational force BUT also greater inertia (resistance to acceleration). These effects cancel exactly — so all objects fall at the same rate."Learn free fall
    Record (10 min)"Gravitational force acts between all masses. Weight varies with g. In vacuum, all objects fall at the same rate."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Calculates weight on different planets and explains why all objects fall at the same rate in a vacuum.

    Lesson 2: Orbital Mechanics

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is an Orbit? (15 min)"An ORBIT is when an object falls toward a planet/moon/star but is moving sideways so fast that it keeps missing — and therefore circles around and around. The combination of forward velocity and gravity creates a curved path. If velocity is too slow, the object crashes. If velocity is too fast, it flies away."Learn orbit
    Orbital Velocity Calculation (15 min)"For a circular orbit: centripetal force (gravity) = mv²/r. Solving for v: v = √(GM/r). Notice: orbital velocity depends on DISTANCE from the planet's centre — the farther out, the slower the orbital velocity. Low Earth orbit (ISS, ~400km up): v ≈ 7.66 km/s. Geostationary orbit (~36,000km up): v ≈ 3.07 km/s."Calculate velocity
    Model Orbits (15 min)Whirling a ball on a string: tension (string pulling in) acts as centripetal force (like gravity). If you cut the string (remove centripetal force), the ball flies off in a straight line — tangent to the circle. This is what would happen if gravity magically disappeared.Model orbits
    Why Satellites Stay Up (5 min)"Satellites don't stay up because they 'escape gravity.' They're constantly falling! But their forward velocity is so great that they keep missing Earth. The atmosphere causes drag, so satellites in low orbit eventually spiral down unless boosted."Explain

    Success Criteria Check: Calculates orbital velocity for satellites at different distances and explains why orbiting objects don't fall to Earth.

    Lesson 3: Space Travel

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Escaping Gravity (15 min)"ESCAPE VELOCITY: minimum velocity needed to escape a planet's gravity completely. Earth: 11.2 km/s (from surface). Moon: 2.4 km/s. This is why rockets need enormous thrust — to reach escape velocity. Once beyond Earth's gravity, they can travel to other planets with minimal thrust."Learn escape velocity
    Gravity Assists (15 min)"GRAVITY ASSISTS (slingshot maneuvers): spacecraft fly past a planet, stealing some of the planet's orbital energy. The planet slows down very slightly (loses energy) and the spacecraft speeds up (gains energy). This is how Voyager spacecraft reached the outer planets with limited fuel."Learn slingshot
    Mission Planning Challenge (15 min)"Plan a mission from Earth to Mars. When is the best time to launch? (When Earth and Mars are properly aligned — Hohmann transfer orbit.) How long does it take? (~9 months). Calculate: Total Δv (change in velocity) required. What gravity assist opportunities exist? What is the mission duration?"Plan mission
    Present Mission Plans (5 min)3–4 students present mission plansShare

    Differentiation: EAL students draw mission trajectory. Gifted students: calculate the exact Hohmann transfer orbit requirements between Earth and Mars.

    Success Criteria Check: Plans a space mission identifying key physics concepts (escape velocity, orbital mechanics, gravity assists).

    WEEK 5 — ELABORATE: Energy in Systems

    Lesson 1: Mechanical Energy

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Mechanical Energy Defined (15 min)"MECHANICAL ENERGY = Kinetic Energy + Potential Energy. ME = KE + PE. In an IDEAL system (no friction, no air resistance), ME is CONSERVED — it stays constant as energy transforms between KE and PE. In REAL systems, some ME is always converted to thermal energy via friction and air resistance."Learn ME
    Roller Coaster Analysis (15 min)"A roller coaster at the top of the first hill has maximum PE (high) and zero KE (starting from rest). As it descends, PE decreases → KE increases. At the bottom, maximum KE. Up the next hill: KE decreases → PE increases. If second hill is lower than first, coaster has enough energy to make it over. If friction is ignored: PE₁ + KE₁ = PE₂ + KE₂ at all points."Learn roller coaster
    Calculate Roller Coaster Energy (15 min)"Coaster car: mass = 500kg. Height of first hill = 40m. Height of second hill = 25m. At top of first hill: PE = 500 × 10 × 40 = 200,000J. KE = 0. ME = 200,000J. At bottom of first hill: PE = 0, so KE must = 200,000J. v = √(2×200,000/500) = √800 = 28.3 m/s. At top of second hill: PE = 500 × 10 × 25 = 125,000J. KE = 200,000 - 125,000 = 75,000J."Calculate
    Real Roller Coasters (5 min)"Real roller coasters: friction and air resistance convert some ME to thermal energy each time. The coaster can't reach the height of the first hill on subsequent hills — ME is not perfectly conserved. This is why coasters need chains to pull them up the first hill."Learn real vs ideal

    Success Criteria Check: Calculates mechanical energy at multiple points in a roller coaster system and explains real vs ideal conservation.

    Lesson 2: Energy and Efficiency

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Efficiency Investigation (15 min)"Let's investigate the efficiency of a ramp system." Measure: height of ramp (h), length of ramp (l). Calculate: Input work = force to push up ramp × distance. Output work = mgh (work done against gravity to reach height). Friction = input - output. Efficiency = output/input × 100%.Plan investigation
    Test Different Ramps (20 min)Test: same height, different ramp lengths (steeper vs shallower). Same ramp, different surfaces (cardboard, sandpaper, plastic). Same ramp, different masses. Record input force and calculate efficiency for each.Test ramps
    Graph Results (10 min)"What affects efficiency? (1) Shallower ramp: less force needed but longer distance — efficiency? (2) Smoother surface: less friction — efficiency increases. (3) Larger mass: efficiency? (Generally increases because friction force is relatively less significant compared to useful work output.)"Graph and analyse
    Write (5 min)"Efficiency of a ramp depends on: surface smoothness (friction), steepness (angle), and mass (inertia relative to friction). Reducing friction and optimising angle improves efficiency."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Investigates ramp efficiency, measures input/output work, calculates efficiency, and identifies factors that affect efficiency.

    Lesson 3: Real-World Systems

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Analyse a Real System (20 min)Choose ONE: (A) Bicycle — human chemical energy → kinetic energy through chain/gears. Identify energy transformations and efficiency losses. (B) Hybrid car — petrol engine + electric motor. How does it recover energy? (Regenerative braking converts kinetic to electrical.) (C) Hydroelectric dam — gravitational PE → kinetic → electrical. (D) Damper (bread) — chemical energy in wood → thermal energy to bread.Analyse system
    Energy Flow Diagram (15 min)Draw energy flow diagram for chosen system: INPUT (original energy source) → TRANSFORMATIONS (all steps) → USEFUL OUTPUT → WASTE (heat, sound, light lost). Calculate approximate efficiency at each step. Calculate overall system efficiency.Draw diagram
    Improve the System (10 min)"What could be improved to increase efficiency? What is the biggest source of energy waste? Propose one specific improvement with evidence."Propose improvement
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"In a bicycle, where are the main energy losses?"Written response

    Differentiation: EAL students draw energy flow diagrams. Gifted students: calculate the Carnot efficiency limit for a heat engine and compare to real engine efficiencies.

    Success Criteria Check: Analyses a real-world energy system, creates a complete energy flow diagram, and proposes one evidence-based improvement.

    WEEK 6 — EXPLAIN: Technology and Physics

    Lesson 1: Transportation Technologies

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Forces on Vehicles (15 min)"A car driving at constant speed: DRIVING FORCE from engine pushes car forward. FRICTION (rolling resistance) opposes motion. AIR DRAG increases with velocity² — at high speeds, drag is the dominant force opposing motion. NET FORCE = driving - friction - drag. When net force = 0, car moves at constant speed (cruise)."Learn forces
    Aerodynamics (15 min)"AERODYNAMIC DRAG: F = ½CρAv². Drag force increases with the SQUARE of velocity. Double speed → 4× drag. This is why car fuel efficiency drops dramatically at highway speeds. Cars are shaped to redirect air smoothly (low C_d). The coefficient of drag (C_d) for a sports car ~0.25, truck ~0.65, bicycle ~0.9."Learn aerodynamics
    Braking and Energy Recovery (15 min)"BRAKING: kinetic energy → thermal energy in brake pads (converted to heat, dissipated). This energy is wasted. REGENERATIVE BRAKING (hybrid/electric cars): kinetic energy → electrical energy → stored in battery. Can recover 30–70% of kinetic energy that would otherwise be wasted."Learn regen braking
    Fuel Efficiency Analysis (5 min)"Petrol cars: ~20% efficient (80% of chemical energy wasted as heat). Electric cars: ~80% efficient (energy in battery → motor → wheels). This is why electric cars are 4× more efficient even accounting for electricity generation."Compare

    Success Criteria Check: Explains forces on vehicles and calculates the effect of drag on fuel efficiency.

    Lesson 2: Sports Physics

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Projectile Motion (15 min)"Projectile motion is motion in two dimensions: horizontal (constant velocity, no acceleration) and vertical (accelerating due to gravity). The horizontal and vertical motions are INDEPENDENT. A ball thrown at an angle follows a PARABOLA — symmetric up and down if air resistance is ignored. Range is maximum at 45° launch angle."Learn projectiles
    Golf Ball Investigation (15 min)"A golf ball: dimples create turbulence in the air, which REDUCES drag (counterintuitive!). This allows golf balls to travel much farther than smooth balls. Magnus effect: a spinning ball curves because one side has faster relative air speed (more lift/drag). Golf players use spin to control ball trajectory."Learn golf
    Analyse a Sport (15 min)Choose ONE: (1) Sprinting — what forces accelerate the runner? What is the optimal stride length? (2) Swimming — why does the body position affect speed so much? (3) Cycling — why do cyclists crouch low? (4) Throwing a basketball — why is release angle important? Analyse using physics principles.Analyse sport
    Present Analysis (5 min)3–4 students present sport analysisShare

    Success Criteria Check: Applies physics principles to analyse at least one sport, identifying relevant forces, energy transformations, or motion characteristics.

    Lesson 3: Engineering Analysis

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Design Activity)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Engineering Design Process (10 min)"Engineering applies physics to solve real problems. Steps: (1) Define problem. (2) Research constraints. (3) Generate concepts. (4) Develop solution. (5) Test and iterate. (6) Evaluate."Learn process
    Design Challenge (25 min)"Design a mousetrap-powered car that travels the farthest distance. Key physics: The spring stores elastic PE when you cock the trap. When released, elastic PE → kinetic energy of the car. Friction at the axle, rolling resistance, air drag all reduce the distance. How do you maximise distance? (Lightweight car, low friction axle, large wheels for low rolling resistance, aerodynamic shape.)"Design car
    Test and Measure (10 min)Build mousetrap cars. Measure: mass, distance traveled, time traveled. Calculate: initial KE from spring, efficiency.Test
    Evaluate (5 min)"What worked? What would you change? How did physics principles help you design a better car?"Evaluate

    Differentiation: EAL students draw design. Gifted students: calculate theoretical maximum distance from stored spring energy and compare to actual results.

    Success Criteria Check: Designs and builds a mousetrap car using physics principles, measures performance, and evaluates against theoretical expectations.

    Lesson 3: Hot Task Preparation
