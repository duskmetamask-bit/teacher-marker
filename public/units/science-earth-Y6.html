AC9S6U01 — Explain how scientific knowledge has developed based on the evidence of changes in Earth over geological time and human activity

Mentor Text/Context: Geology, natural disasters, climate science, environmental science

Writing Focus: Evidence-based scientific explanation, cause and effect, evaluating scientific claims, research-based argumentation

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning about Earth's geological history, plate tectonics, and extreme Earth events — using evidence to understand how Earth's surface has changed and why natural disasters occur.

Success Criteria:

  • I can explain the theory of plate tectonics and how it explains Earth's features and changes.
  • I can describe how Earth's surface has changed over billions of years, using evidence.
  • I can explain the causes and effects of extreme Earth events: earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, tsunamis.
  • I can use evidence (fossils, rock formations, ice cores, geological data) to support scientific explanations.
  • I can evaluate scientific claims about Earth's history and climate change.

    WEEK 1 — INTRODUCTION TO EARTH'S HISTORY

    Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Hook (10 min)"Earth is 4.54 billion years old. This is almost impossible to comprehend. But we know this from evidence. Today you will explain what evidence scientists use to understand Earth's history — and whether human activity can cause lasting changes to our planet."Think
    Task (45 min)Cold Task: "What evidence do scientists use to understand Earth's past? And is human activity causing changes to Earth that are permanent?" Write a response with at least 3 pieces of evidence and a reasoned argument. Year 6: 300–450 words. Focus: Evidence-based reasoning, scientific understanding.Write

    Differentiation: Key evidence cards available with examples.

    Lesson 2: The Evidence of Time (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (20 min)"How do we know Earth's history? EVIDENCE. 1. FOSSIL RECORD — Shows past life, extinction events, environmental conditions. 2. ROCK STRATA (layers) — Deeper layers = older (superposition). Shows sequence of events. 3. ICE CORES — Bubbles of ancient atmosphere trapped in Antarctic ice — show temperature and CO2 levels going back 800,000 years. 4. RADIOACTIVE DATING — Measuring decay of radioactive elements in rocks to determine absolute age. 5. ROCK AND MINERAL COMPOSITION — Same rock formations on different continents show they were once joined. 6. BIOLOGICAL EVIDENCE — Similar fossils on continents now separated by oceans."Learn evidence
    You Do (30 min)"Sort evidence by type: For each piece of evidence (fossils, ice cores, rock layers, radioactive dating), write: What type of information does it give? (Past life, past climate, age of rocks, past geography) How far back does it go? What is one limitation? Complete a table."Sort and evaluate
    Share (5 min)Which piece of evidence do you think is most powerful? Why?Discuss

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify at least 4 types of evidence for Earth's history and explain what each shows.

    Lesson 3: How Do We Know? (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"Scientific knowledge develops over time as new evidence emerges. OLD idea: Continents are fixed in place. NEW evidence: Same fossils on opposite sides of oceans → continents must have moved. NEW theory: Plate tectonics (developed in 1960s after evidence accumulated). This is how science works — ideas are tested against evidence and refined."Learn
    We Do (30 min)"Timeline: Create a timeline of key discoveries about Earth's history. Include: Wegener's continental drift theory (1912) / Discovery of mid-ocean ridges (1950s) / Plate tectonic theory confirmed (1960s) / Ice core data (1990s-present) / Evidence of Anthropocene (recent). For each: What was the evidence? What did it change about our understanding?"Create timeline
    You Do (10 min)Write: "Scientific knowledge about Earth's history changes when..."Write

    WEEK 2 — PLATE TECTONICS

    Lesson 1: The Earth's Structure (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (20 min)"Earth's structure: 1. INNER CORE — Solid iron and nickel, extremely hot (5400°C), despite heat it is solid due to extreme pressure. 2. OUTER CORE — Liquid iron and nickel, creates Earth's magnetic field (which is why compasses point north). 3. MANTLE — Semi-solid rock that flows very slowly (convection currents). This flow causes tectonic plates to move. 4. CRUST — Thin outer layer. Two types: OCEANIC crust (thin, dense, basalt) and CONTINENTAL crust (thick, less dense, granite). We live on the crust."Learn structure
    You Do (30 min)"Draw a cross-section of Earth's layers. Label: Inner core, Outer core, Mantle, Crust. Write one fact about each. Include relative thickness (crust is very thin relative to the whole Earth — like the skin on an apple). Colour code: Inner core (yellow/orange — hottest), Outer core (orange), Mantle (red/orange), Crust (brown — thinnest layer)."Draw
    Share (5 min)Why is the crust like the skin on an apple?Explain

    Lesson 2: How Plates Move (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"The LITHOSPHERE = crust + upper mantle. It is broken into TECTONIC PLATES — like a cracked eggshell floating on the mantle. There are 7 major plates and many minor ones. They MOVE because of CONVECTION CURRENTS in the mantle — hot material rises, cool material sinks, creating circular flow that drags the plates. The plates move at about 2–15cm per year — slow but continuous."Learn
    We Do (25 min)"Demonstrate convection: A container of water with food colouring added to hot water and cold water. Watch: Cold (blue) sinks, hot (red) rises, creates circular flow. Draw this. Relate to mantle convection and plate movement. Also: Show how a table with a tablecloth and slowly drag the cloth — things on top move with the cloth."Demonstrate
    You Do (10 min)Write: "Convection currents in the mantle cause plate movement because..." / "If Earth's interior cooled completely, the consequence would be..."Write

    Lesson 3: What Plates Create (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)"Map the plates: Use a world map. Label and colour: 7 major plates (Pacific, North American, South American, African, Eurasian, Antarctic, Australian/Indian). Mark: Where they are moving apart, where they are moving together, where they are sliding past each other. Label: Divergent boundary, Convergent boundary, Transform boundary. Include arrows showing direction of movement. Research: Which plate moves the fastest? (Pacific plate — about 10cm/year)"Map
    Share (5 min)Which plate are we on in Australia? What direction is it moving?Share

    WEEK 3 — PLATE BOUNDARIES

    Lesson 1: Divergent Boundaries (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"DIVERGENT BOUNDARIES: Two plates move APART. Mantle material rises, melts, creates new crust. Creates: Mid-ocean ridges (underwater mountain ranges), rifts (valleys) on land, volcanic activity. Example: The Mid-Atlantic Ridge — Iceland sits on it. The African Rift Valley — Africa is splitting. When it splits completely, a new ocean will form in millions of years."Learn divergent
    You Do (35 min)"Draw and label: A divergent boundary cross-section. Show: Two plates moving apart, magma rising, new crust forming, mid-ocean ridge. Write: "Divergent boundaries create new crust because..." Also: Find on a map: Mid-Atlantic Ridge, East African Rift, Reykjanes Ridge.Draw and find

    Lesson 2: Convergent Boundaries (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"CONVERGENT BOUNDARIES: Two plates move TOGETHER. Three types: 1. OCEANIC + OCEANIC → One subducts under the other, creates deep ocean trenches + volcanic island arcs. Example: Japan (Mariana Trench + volcanic islands). 2. OCEANIC + CONTINENTAL → Oceanic subducts (it is denser), creates coastal mountain ranges + volcanic activity. Example: Andes Mountains (Nazca plate under South American plate). 3. CONTINENTAL + CONTINENTAL → Neither subducts, they crumple and uplift, creates massive mountain ranges. Example: Himalayas (India plate hitting Eurasian plate)."Learn convergent
    We Do (30 min)"Create 3 diagrams: One for each type of convergent boundary. Label: Which plates, which subducts (if any), what is created (trench, volcanic mountains, continental mountains). Write 3 sentences comparing the three types — which is most violent? Which creates the tallest mountains? Which is most common?"Create diagrams
    You Do (10 min)Write: "The Himalayas are forming because..."Write

    Lesson 3: Transform Boundaries (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"TRANSFORM BOUNDARIES: Two plates SLIDE PAST EACH OTHER. No new crust created, none destroyed. Creates: Fault lines, earthquakes (the most common and dangerous events at transform boundaries). Example: San Andreas Fault in California — Pacific plate sliding northwest relative to North American plate. This produces regular earthquakes."Learn transform
    You Do (40 min)"Research: San Andreas Fault — how does it work? How many earthquakes in the last 50 years? What is the risk of a major earthquake? What preparations exist? Write a 1-page report: Include a diagram of the fault showing plate movement direction."Research
    Share (5 min)What makes transform boundaries especially dangerous for people living near them?Discuss

    WEEK 4 — EARTH'S CHANGING SURFACE

    Lesson 1: Pangaea and Continental Drift (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (20 min)"Alfred Wegener (1912) proposed CONTINENTAL DRIFT — all continents were once joined as one supercontinent called PANGAEA, and have been slowly moving apart ever since. EVIDENCE: Coastlines of Africa and South America look like a jigsaw puzzle. Same fossil (Mesosaurus — small aquatic reptile) found on both continents. Same rock formations (ancient mountain range) in Africa and South America. Same climate evidence (glaciation marks in now-tropical areas). BUT: Wegener could not explain HOW they moved — this prevented acceptance until the 1960s when plate tectonics provided the mechanism."Learn drift
    You Do (30 min)"Pangaea puzzle: Given outlines of continents (Pangaea configuration), cut out and reassemble to show how they fit. Then given outlines of CURRENT positions, see how much they have moved in 200 million years. Calculate: If continents move at average 5cm/year, how far have they moved in 200 million years? (5 × 200,000,000 × 100 = 10,000km — which roughly matches actual movement). Write calculation and conclusion."Calculate
    Share (5 min)Why wasn't Wegener's theory accepted when he first proposed it? (Couldn't explain mechanism) What changed in the 1960s?Discuss

    Lesson 2: Mountain Building (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"Mountains form in several ways: 1. FOLD MOUNTAINS — Convergent continental plates crumple and uplift. Himalayas (India + Eurasia), Alps, Andes. 2. VOLCANIC MOUNTAINS — Magma erupts and piles up. Mt Fuji (Japan), Mt St Helens (USA), Mount Etna (Italy). 3. FAULT-BLOCK MOUNTAINS — Crust breaks and blocks uplifted. Sierra Nevada (USA). 4. DOME MOUNTAINS — Magma pushes up from below, then erodes. Black Hills (USA)."Learn
    We Do (25 min)"Sort: 10 mountain ranges (Himalayas, Andes, Rockies, Alps, Mt Everest, Mt Fuji, Kilimanjaro, Sierra Nevada, Great Dividing Range, Transantarctic Mountains). Sort into: Fold / Volcanic / Fault-block / Dome. For each: Write the type and the plate boundary context (convergent, etc.) that created it."Sort
    You Do (10 min)Write: "Mountains are useful to scientists because they show..."Write

    Lesson 3: Ocean Formation (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)"How the Atlantic Ocean formed: 200 million years ago, Pangaea began to split where the Atlantic is now. Divergent boundary = magma upwelling, new oceanic crust formed. As plates continued moving apart, the ocean grew wider. The ocean floor is relatively young (oldest is about 180 million years old — compared to Earth at 4.54 billion). The Pacific is much older and is shrinking as oceanic plates subduct under continental plates." Write: "The Atlantic Ocean is growing because..." / "The Pacific Ocean is shrinking because..." Draw: Pangaea → Jurassic → Present, showing how the Atlantic grew.Write and draw
    Share (5 min)How does knowing the age of the ocean floor support the theory of plate tectonics?Discuss

    WEEK 5 — EXTREME EARTH EVENTS

    Lesson 1: Earthquakes (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"EARTHQUAKES: Sudden shaking of the ground caused by stress that has built up in tectonic plates. When stress releases (at a FAULT LINE), seismic waves travel through Earth, causing the ground to shake. MEASUREMENT: Richter scale (magnitude — energy released, logarithmic) and Mercalli scale (intensity — damage caused, varies by location). Magnitude 3 = barely felt. 6 = causing damage. 9+ = catastrophic. The 2011 Japan earthquake was magnitude 9.1."Learn
    You Do (35 min)"Research: Choose a major earthquake in the last 50 years. Research: Where? When? Magnitude? What happened? What was the impact (deaths, damage, tsunami)? How did people respond? What have they done to prepare for the next one? Create a one-page report with a map showing the location."Research
    Share (5 min)What is the relationship between plate boundaries and major earthquakes?Explain

    Lesson 2: Volcanic Eruptions (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"VOLCANIC ERUPTIONS: Magma (underground) or lava (surface) erupts from a volcano. Most occur at convergent boundaries (subduction zones) or divergent boundaries (mid-ocean ridges). HOTSPOTS (magma plumes from deep mantle) create volcanic islands in the middle of plates — Hawaiian Islands are a hotspot chain. TYPES of eruptions: Effusive (gentle flow, like Hawaii) vs Explosive (violent, like Mt Vesuvius or Mt St Helens). Products: Lava flows, ash clouds, pyroclastic flows (fast-moving, deadly clouds of hot gas and rock), lahars (mudflows)."Learn
    We Do (25 min)"Compare two volcanic events: Mt Pinatubo (Philippines, 1991 — explosive, global cooling effect) vs Kilauea (Hawaii, ongoing — effusive, predictable). For each: Type of eruption / Plate context / What was produced / Human impact / Long-term effects. Create a comparison table."Compare
    You Do (10 min)Write: "Despite the dangers, people continue to live near volcanoes because..."Write

    Lesson 3: Tsunamis (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"TSUNAMIS: Waves caused by underwater earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, or landslides displacing large amounts of water. In the open ocean, waves are small (1m) and fast (700km/h). When they reach shallow water near coast, they slow down but grow in height (up to 30m). Warning signs: Earthquake, unusual ocean withdrawal, loud roar. Japan 2011: Magnitude 9.1 earthquake → tsunami up to 40m high → 18,000 deaths."Learn
    You Do (40 min)"Create a tsunami safety guide: For a coastal community in Australia (e.g., the east coast or northern coast). Include: What to do BEFORE (know your evacuation route, identify high ground). What to do DURING (evacuate immediately, don't wait). What to do AFTER (help others, avoid damaged buildings, expect aftershocks). Include a map of a real coastal town with marked evacuation routes and high ground."Create
    Share (5 min)Why can't we prevent tsunamis but we can prepare for them?Discuss

    WEEK 6 — CLIMATE EVIDENCE

    Lesson 1: Ice Cores and Past Climates (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (20 min)"ICE CORES: Cores of Antarctic ice drilled out, showing layers going back 800,000 years. Each layer = one year of snowfall. Bubbles in the ice contain trapped AIR from that time — we can measure CO2 and methane levels from ancient atmospheres. Key findings: CO2 levels and temperature have moved together (when CO2 high, temperature high). There have been ice ages and warm periods in a cycle of roughly 100,000 years. CURRENT CO2 levels (420ppm) are higher than at ANY time in the last 800,000 years — this is unprecedented."Learn
    You Do (30 min)"Draw a graph: X-axis = Time (800,000 years ago to present). Y-axis = CO2 levels (180ppm to 420ppm). Plot approximate data from ice cores: The pattern shows cyclical ice ages. Mark: The current CO2 level (far above anything in the record). Write: 'The ice core data shows that current CO2 levels are...' "Graph
    Share (5 min)Why are ice cores considered such reliable evidence?Explain

    Lesson 2: Climate Change Evidence (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"EVIDENCE of current climate change: 1. Global temperature rise (1.1°C increase since 1900). 2. Arctic ice melting (summer ice extent decreasing 13% per decade). 3. Sea level rise (20cm since 1900, accelerating). 4. Ocean warming (90% of extra heat absorbed by oceans). 5. Changing animal migration and flowering patterns. These are measured, not opinion. The question is: WHY?"Learn
    We Do (30 min)"Evaluate: Natural causes vs human causes. Natural: Solar cycles, volcanic eruptions, Earth's orbit changes (Milankovitch cycles). Human: Burning fossil fuels (CO2), deforestation (less CO2 absorption), agriculture (methane). Create a evidence comparison: For each cause, write: What is the evidence FOR? What is the evidence AGAINST? How strong is the evidence?"Evaluate
    You Do (10 min)Write: "Based on the evidence, I conclude that current climate change is primarily caused by ___ because..."Write

    Lesson 3: Natural vs Human Causes (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)"Argument writing: 'The evidence strongly supports the conclusion that human activity is the primary cause of current global warming.' Write a 200-word argument FOR this claim, using at least 4 pieces of evidence from the lesson (temperature records, CO2 levels, ice cores, melting ice). Then write a 100-word counter-argument: What might someone who disagrees say? Is their argument as well-supported by evidence?"Write argument
    Share (5 min)Present your argument. What was the strongest piece of evidence you used?Share

    WEEK 7 — INVESTIGATION

    Lesson 1: Earthquake Research (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Investigation

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)"You are an earthquake engineer. Design a building for a region prone to earthquakes. Research: What type of building survives earthquakes best? (Flexible structures, base isolators, deep foundations, symmetric designs). Apply your research: Create a building design drawing. Label features that make it earthquake-resistant. Write: Why each feature helps. Explain how plate tectonics causes the earthquakes that this building must survive."Design

    Lesson 2: Plate Tectonics Model (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Investigation

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)"Create a working model: 1. Use a container with a flexible base (like a cake tin with a removable bottom). 2. Draw a "plate boundary" in the middle — a line that you can push up or pull apart. 3. Show divergent: Push up in the middle, show "magma" (red water/coloured ice) pushing up. 4. Show convergent: Push two sides together, show one subducting under the other. 5. Show transform: Push two sides in opposite directions. Document with labelled photos/drawings. Write: "My model shows that plate movement causes... because..."Build and document

    Lesson 3: Debate — Natural or Human? (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Debate

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (50 min)"Debate: 'Human activity is not the primary cause of current climate change.' Assign teams: PRO (climate change is primarily human-caused) vs CON (natural causes are primary). Both sides must use evidence. Rules: Each team presents 3 arguments. Opposing team responds with evidence-based counter. Class votes on most convincing argument. Write: "My position after the debate is... because..."Debate
