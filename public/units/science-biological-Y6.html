Unit Content

Evolution, Adaptation and Survival — Biological Sciences Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 6

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum Science v9 — Biological Sciences

Unit Big Idea: Life on Earth has changed over vast timescales through evolution by natural selection. The diversity of life we see today reflects millions of years of adaptation to changing environments.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Research how one species has changed over time and explain what caused those changes

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Analyse the evolutionary history and adaptations of one Australian species and predict how it might evolve in the future

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to explain the theory of evolution by natural selection, describe how adaptations arise over generations, analyse evidence for evolution (fossils, DNA, comparative anatomy), evaluate how species respond to environmental pressures, and predict future evolutionary trajectories.

Success Criteria:

  • I can explain natural selection as the mechanism of evolution.
  • I can describe structural and behavioural adaptations in terms of evolutionary advantage.
  • I can interpret evidence for evolution from fossils, comparative anatomy, and DNA.
  • I can analyse how environmental pressures drive evolutionary change.
  • I can predict how a species might evolve under different selective pressures.
  • I can use science words: evolution, natural selection, adaptation, fitness, speciation, fossil, mutation, genetic variation, survival of the fittest, convergent evolution.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: Evolution and Change

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Species Change Research

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (10 min)Show images: modern horse vs prehistoric horse (Mesohippus), dinosaur vs modern bird, Homo habilis vs Homo sapiens. "These species CHANGED over time. How? Why?"Observe
    Task (30 min)Cold Task: "Research how ONE species has changed over time. It could be: the horse, the elephant, humans, the wolf/dog, wheat, or another species. What did the species look like millions of years ago? What does it look like today? What changes occurred? Why did these changes happen? (Write 4–5 sentences.)"Research
    Share (10 min)3–4 students share their speciesShare

    Success Criteria Check: Student presents a species change over time with some explanation of causes.

    Lesson 2: What Is Evolution?

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Engage

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Evolution? (15 min)"EVOLUTION = change in species over time. More specifically, change in the GENETIC COMPOSITION of a population across generations. Small changes accumulate over long periods → new species emerge. Evolution is the best-supported scientific theory in biology — backed by evidence from fossils, genetics, anatomy, and direct observation."Learn
    Darwin and Wallace (15 min)"Charles Darwin (1809–1882) developed the theory of evolution by natural selection after observing finches on the Galapagos Islands. Alfred Russel Wallace independently developed the same theory. Darwin published 'On the Origin of Species' in 1859."Learn
    Key Vocabulary (15 min)EVOLUTION: change in species over time. SPECIES: group of organisms that can breed together and produce fertile offspring. POPULATION: all members of a species in a given area. FITNESS: ability to survive and reproduce in a specific environment.Learn
    Write (5 min)"Evolution is change in species over time, driven by natural selection."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Defines evolution and correctly uses key vocabulary.

    Lesson 3: Darwin's Observations

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Engage

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Darwin's Voyage (15 min)"Darwin sailed on HMS Beagle (1831–1836). He observed: (1) Species vary within populations — no two individuals are identical. (2) More offspring are produced than survive — competition for resources. (3) Resources are limited — not all offspring survive. (4) Survival is not random — some individuals survive because they are better suited to their environment."Learn
    Galapagos Finches (15 min)"13 species of finches on Galapagos Islands. Each species has a different beak shape suited to its food source. Darwin noted: finches on islands with hard seeds had thick beaks. Finches with soft fruit had slender beaks. Beak differences matched food available. This suggested that finches adapted to local conditions over generations."Learn
    Connect to Theory (10 min)"Darwin's observations + understanding of inheritance → Theory of Evolution by Natural Selection. The key insight: individuals with favourable variations are MORE LIKELY to survive and reproduce, passing those variations to offspring."Learn
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"What 4 observations led Darwin to develop his theory?"Written response

    Success Criteria Check: Describes Darwin's 4 key observations that led to the theory of natural selection.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Natural Selection

    Lesson 1: The Mechanism of Natural Selection

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Natural Selection — The Steps (20 min)"NATURAL SELECTION: (1) POPULATION has genetic variation — individuals differ. (2) MORE OFFSPRING produced than environment can support → COMPETITION for resources. (3) INDIVIDUALS with advantageous traits are MORE LIKELY to survive and reproduce (survival of the fittest). (4) Those advantageous traits are PASSED TO OFFSPRING. (5) Over generations, advantageous traits become more common. If populations become separated, new species may form (speciation)."Learn
    Example — Peppered Moth (15 min)"During the Industrial Revolution in England, pollution darkened tree trunks (soot). Lighter moths were more visible to predators → died more often. Darker moths survived better → dark phenotype increased in frequency. This was DIRECT OBSERVATION of natural selection happening in real time!"Learn
    Modern Example — Antibiotic Resistance (10 min)"Bacteria: random MUTATIONS cause some bacteria to be antibiotic-resistant. When antibiotics are used, non-resistant bacteria die. Resistant bacteria survive → reproduce → antibiotic-resistant population grows. MRSA (methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus) is a major human health problem."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Natural selection acts on genetic variation. Individuals with advantageous traits survive and reproduce more successfully."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly explains the 4 steps of natural selection with at least 1 example.

    Lesson 2: Evidence from the Past — Fossils

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explore

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    How Fossils Form (15 min)"FOSSILS = preserved remains or traces of ancient organisms. FORMATION: organism dies → quickly buried by sediment → soft parts decay → hard parts replaced by minerals (petrification) → sedimentary rock forms over millions of years. TYPES: body fossils (bones, shells), trace fossils (footprints, burrows), cast/mould fossils."Learn
    Fossil Record (15 min)"The fossil record shows organisms in order of their age in rock layers (law of superposition). SIMPLE organisms in oldest layers → increasingly complex organisms in younger layers. TRANSITIONAL FORMS: Tiktaalik (fish with limbs — link to tetrapods), Archaeopteryx (dinosaur with feathers — link to birds), Australopithecus (ape to human link)."Learn
    Investigate the Fossil Record (15 min)Use fossil images/cards: trilobite, ammonite, Mesohippus (early horse), Paraceratherium (giant hornless rhino). Place in order from oldest to youngest. Note: What traits appeared? What traits disappeared? How did size change?Investigate
    Write (5 min)"The fossil record provides evidence that organisms have changed over time."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly orders fossils from oldest to youngest and identifies at least 2 changes shown in the fossil record.

    Lesson 3: Natural Selection Simulation

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explore

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Simulation Setup (15 min)"We will simulate natural selection. You are predators. These 'prey' are coloured dots/shapes on a background. The background will change (natural selection pressure). Predators that catch more prey have more 'offspring' (points)."Set up
    Conduct Simulation (20 min)Round 1: Light background, mixed coloured prey. Predators 'eat' prey they can see. Count survivors. Round 2: Darker background. More of which colour survive? Discuss: Why did the population change?Simulate
    Analysis (10 min)"What happened to the prey population over rounds? Which trait became more common? Why? How is this like natural selection?"Analyse
    Write (5 min)"Natural selection leads to changes in population traits when environmental pressures favour certain variants."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Explains the simulation as a model of natural selection and identifies how selective pressure changed the population.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLAIN: Evidence for Evolution

    Lesson 1: Fossil Evidence

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Strengths and Limitations of Fossils (15 min)"FOSSIL EVIDENCE: Strengths: direct evidence of past life, shows transitional forms, dates rock layers. Limitations: fossilisation is rare (soft-bodied organisms rarely preserved), fossils only show morphology, not behaviour/genetics, fossil record is incomplete (gaps)."Learn
    Horse Evolution (15 min)"Hhorse evolution: Eohippus (Hyracotherium) — small, forest-dwelling, 55 million years ago (Mya). Mesohippus — slightly larger, 40-30 Mya. Merychippus — larger, grassland, 17-11 Mya. Equus (modern horse) — large, single toe (hoof), 4 Mya to present. Shows: size increase, toe reduction, diet change (browser to grazer)."Learn
    Whale Evolution (15 min)"WHALE EVOLUTION: Pakicetus (50 Mya) — land mammal, closest relative to whales. Ambulocetus — 'walking whale,' amphibious. Basilosaurus — fully aquatic, tiny hind limbs remain. Modern whales — no hind limbs, flippers, flukes. All stages found as fossils, confirming whale ancestry from land mammals."Learn
    Research (5 min)Research one transitional fossil form.Research

    Success Criteria Check: Analyses horse evolution as evidence for evolution and identifies at least 2 transitional features.

    Lesson 2: Comparative Anatomy

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Homologous Structures (15 min)"HOMOLOGOUS STRUCTURES: body parts in different species that have the SAME evolutionary origin (shared ancestry) but may have DIFFERENT functions. Examples: forelimb bones — human arm, bat wing, whale flipper, horse leg. Same bones (humerus, radius, ulna, carpals, metacarpals, phalanges) but modified for different functions. This is EVIDENCE OF COMMON DESCENT."Learn
    Analogous Structures (10 min)"ANALOGOUS STRUCTURES: body parts that have SIMILAR function but DIFFERENT evolutionary origins. Examples: bat wing and bee wing (both fly, but different origins), fish and dolphin bodies (both swim, but fish fins vs mammalian flippers). These show CONVERGENT EVOLUTION — similar environments selecting for similar solutions."Learn
    Vestigial Structures (10 min)"VESTIGIAL STRUCTURES: reduced or functionless remnants of structures that were functional in ancestors. Human: appendix (digestive), wisdom teeth (dietary), tailbone. Whale: tiny pelvic bones (remnants of land mammal hind limbs). These show evolutionary history."Learn
    Comparison Activity (15 min)Identify: whale flipper, bat wing, human arm — homologous or analogous? Ostrich wing, penguin wing, bee wing? Human appendix?Identify

    Success Criteria Check: Distinguishes homologous, analogous, and vestigial structures and provides at least 2 examples of each.

    Lesson 3: DNA Evidence

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    DNA as Evidence (15 min)"DNA provides powerful evidence for evolution. ALL life shares the same genetic code (DNA → RNA → protein). This universal code suggests common ancestry. The MORE SIMILAR the DNA sequences between two species, the MORE RECENTLY they shared a common ancestor."Learn
    Molecular Clock (10 min)"MOLECULAR CLOCK: mutations accumulate in DNA at roughly constant rates over time. By comparing DNA differences between species, scientists can estimate when they diverged. Human and chimpanzee DNA differ by ~1.2%. This small difference suggests common ancestry ~6-7 million years ago."Learn
    Cytochrome c Analysis (15 min)"Cytochrome c (protein involved in cellular respiration) is found in nearly all organisms. Amino acid sequence in human cytochrome c vs: chimpanzee (0 differences), horse (12 differences), yeast (45 differences). The more differences, the more evolutionary distance."Learn
    Australian DNA Example (10 min)"DNA analysis of Australian Aboriginal people shows they share ancestry with people from Africa ~50,000-70,000 years ago. Aboriginal Australians have been in Australia longer than any other continuous human population outside Africa."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Explains how DNA similarities provide evidence for common ancestry and uses molecular clock concepts.

    WEEK 4 — ELABORATE: Adaptation Over Time

    Lesson 1: Structural Adaptations

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Camouflage Evolution (15 min)"CAMOUFLAGE: structural adaptation that helps organisms blend with environment. TYPES: CRYPTIC COLORATION (match background — stick insects, leaf insects). MIMICRY (look like something dangerous — harmless hoverfly mimics wasp). DISRUPTIVE COLORATION (breaks up outline — zebra stripes). PREDATOR CAMOUFLAGE (great white shark — countershading, dark on top, light below)."Learn
    Predator Adaptations (15 min)"PREDATOR structural adaptations: CHEETAH — lightweight skeleton, enlarged adrenal glands, semi-retractable claws for traction, large nostrils for oxygen intake. OWLS — asymmetric ear placement for pinpointing sound, forward-facing eyes for depth perception, silent flight feathers."Learn
    Prey Adaptations (10 min)"PREY structural adaptations: GAZELLES — lightweight, large lungs, stotting behaviour (leaping high to signal fitness). ARMADILLOS — shell of bony plates (osteoderms). PORCUPINES — quills (modified hairs) released easily.Learn
    Design Organism (10 min)"Design an organism for a specific environment. What structural adaptations would it have? Explain the evolutionary advantage of each adaptation."Design

    Success Criteria Check: Identifies at least 4 structural adaptations and explains their evolutionary advantage.

    Lesson 2: Behavioural Adaptations

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Innate vs Learned Behaviours (15 min)"Behavioural adaptations can be INNATE (instinct — genetically programmed) or LEARNED (modified by experience). Innate: spider webs, bird migration, newborn mammal suckling. Learned: primates using tools, dolphins learning hunting techniques. Innate behaviours are encoded by genes and evolved through natural selection."Learn
    Migration (15 min)"MIGRATION: seasonal movement of populations. EVOLUTIONARY ADVANTAGE: access seasonal food resources, avoid harsh conditions, reach breeding grounds. Arctic tern: longest migration (pole to pole). Monarch butterfly: multi-generational migration (Canada to Mexico). Grey whale: 20,000km round trip."Learn
    Social Behaviour (15 min)"SOCIAL BEHAVIOURS: MEERKAT sentinel duty — one watches while others forage, increased survival for group. WOLF packs — cooperative hunting, shared care of young. AUSTRALIAN MAGPIE LARK: cooperative breeding (offspring from previous years help raise new young)."Learn
    Research (5 min)Research one behavioural adaptation and its evolutionary origin.Research

    Success Criteria Check: Distinguishes innate from learned behaviours and explains the evolutionary advantage of at least 2 behavioural adaptations.

    Lesson 3: Coevolution

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Coevolution? (15 min)"COEVOLUTION: when two species EVOLVE TOGETHER in response to each other. Each species exerts selective pressure on the other, driving reciprocal adaptations. Example: predator-prey coevolution — cheetah speed increases → gazelle speed increases → cheetah evolves better speed → gazelle evolves better agility."Learn
    Plant-Pollinator Coevolution (15 min)"FLOWERS and their pollinators coevolve. LONG-TONGUED MOTH — pollinates long-tubed flowers. If flower tube lengthens → moths with longer tongues survive better → moth tongue lengthens further. DARWIN'S HAWK MOTH predicted (long tongue predicted before moth was discovered) — confirmed 40 years later. AUSTRALIAN: banksia and honeyeater birds — curved beaks matched curved flowers."Learn
    Predator-Prey Coevolution (15 min)"BATESIAN MIMICRY: harmless species mimics dangerous one (hoverfly mimics wasp). MÜLLERIAN MIMICRY: two dangerous species evolve similar warning colours (poison dart frogs). VENOM vs ANTIVENOM: snakes evolve more potent venom → prey evolve resistance → snakes evolve stronger venom.Learn
    Write (5 min)"Coevolution occurs when two species influence each other's evolution."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Explains coevolution with at least 2 examples and correctly identifies Batesian from Müllerian mimicry.

    WEEK 5 — ELABORATE: Australian Evolution

    Lesson 1: Australian Biogeography

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Continental Drift (15 min)"Australia was once part of Gondwana (supercontinent with Africa, South America, Antarctica, India). ~45 million years ago, Australia separated and drifted north. This isolation led to UNIQUE EVOLUTION — Australian organisms evolved separately from other continents. Result: many endemic (found only in Australia) species."Learn
    Australian Distinctiveness (15 min)"Australia: 80% of mammals, 90% of reptiles, 90% of fish are ENDEMIC (found nowhere else). Marsupials dominate (instead of placental mammals elsewhere). Monotremes only found in Australia and New Guinea. Flowering plants: unique families (banksia, eucalyptus)."Learn
    Why Isolated Evolution? (15 min)"ISOLATION: physical separation → gene flow prevented → populations evolve independently. CLIMATE: varied climates, from tropical to temperate to arid → diverse selection pressures. OLD LANDSCAPES: ancient, stable landscapes → some species (honey possum, Wollemi pine) are living fossils."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Australia's geographic isolation led to unique evolutionary outcomes."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Explains how Australian geographic isolation led to unique evolution.

    Lesson 2: Unique Australian Adaptations

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Marsupial Adaptations (15 min)"MARSUPIAL adaptations: POUCH (marsupium) — protects developing young, allows extended lactation. KANGAROO: unique locomotion (pentapedal walking using tail, bipedal hopping). Large elastic tendons in legs store and release energy. Koorinya (water-holding — desert kangaroos can survive without free water). Koala: highly specialised diet (eucalypt leaves, low nutrition), large caecum for fermentation."Learn
    Echidna Adaptations (10 min)"ECHIDNA: spines (modified hairs) for defence. Electroreception (5000 electroreceptors in snout — detect earthworms). No teeth — uses tongue and keratinous pads. Cloaca (single opening for excretion and reproduction — reptile/marsupial characteristic). Represents ancient lineage."Learn
    Plant Adaptations (15 min)"EUCALYPT: waxy leaves (reduce transpiration), shed bark (fire management), oil glands (deterrent to insects). BANKSIA: woody follicles protect seeds, require fire to open and release seeds. XANTHORRHOEA (grass tree): fire-resistant trunk, new leaves grow from protected centre after fire. STURT'S DESERT PEA: bright red flowers attract birds, deep taproot."Learn
    Research (10 min)Research one uniquely Australian adaptation and present its evolutionary advantage.Research

    Success Criteria Check: Researches and explains evolutionary advantage of at least 3 uniquely Australian adaptations.

    Lesson 3: Evolution of Australian Marsupials

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Marsupial vs Placental Diversification (15 min)"When Australia separated from Gondwana, marsupials were the dominant mammals. In isolation, marsupials DIVERSIFIED to fill ecological niches that placental mammals filled on other continents. EXAMPLE: WOLF → THYLACINE (Tasmanian tiger — extinct 1936). FLYING SQUIRREL → SUGAR GLIDER. ANTELOPE → RED KANGAROO (convergent evolution — similar body forms in different mammal groups)."Learn
    Thylacine Case Study (15 min)"THYLACINE: looked like a wolf (convergent), but was a marsupial (pouch, not placental). Reasons for extinction: hunting by settlers, competition with dingoes, disease. LAST KNOWN SPECIMEN died in Hobart Zoo in 1936. Film footage shows thylacines walking. The thylacine demonstrates: convergent evolution AND human-caused extinction."Learn
    Ongoing Evolution (15 min)"Evolution continues today. Examples: cane toads spreading across Australia → populations show changes (larger bodies, longer legs, faster dispersal). Quolls adapting to urban environments. Plants evolving herbicide resistance."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Marsupial diversification shows evolution filling ecological niches over time."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Analyses marsupial diversification as evidence for evolution and correctly identifies at least 1 example of convergent evolution.

    WEEK 6 — EXPLAIN: Speciation and Extinction

    Lesson 1: New Species

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is a Species? (15 min)"BIOLOGICAL SPECIES CONCEPT: species = populations that can breed together and produce FERTILE offspring. If two populations cannot interbreed → separate species. HYBRIDS: sometimes two species breed (lion × tiger = liger). But ligers are usually sterile → confirms lion and tiger are separate species."Learn
    Speciation — How New Species Form (15 min)"SPECIATION: (1) POPULATION SEPARATION — geographic barrier (mountain, ocean, desert) divides population. (2) DIFFERENTIAL SELECTION — different environments apply different selection pressures. (3) GENETIC DIVERGENCE — mutations and selection accumulate, gene flow remains low. (4) REPRODUCTIVE ISOLATION — populations can no longer interbreed successfully. (5) NEW SPECIES — complete separation. ALLOPATRIC SPECIATION: caused by geographic separation."Learn
    Ring Species (10 min)"RING SPECIES: demonstrate speciation in action. Greenish warbler: populations around a mountain ring can all interbreed with neighbours. But end populations cannot interbreed — showing speciation in progress."Learn
    Write (10 min)"Speciation occurs when populations become reproductively isolated and accumulate genetic differences."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Explains the process of speciation including geographic isolation, genetic divergence, and reproductive isolation.

    Lesson 2: Extinction

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Types of Extinction (15 min)"EXTINCTION: when all members of a species die. BACKGROUND EXTINCTION: low level, always occurring (~1-5 species per year, naturally). MASS EXTINCTION: rapid loss of many species (5 in Earth's history). PERMIAN EXTINCTION (250 Mya): 96% of marine species lost. CRETACEOUS (66 Mya): dinosaurs (except birds) extinct."Learn
    Causes of Extinction (15 min)"CAUSES: habitat loss (most common), introduced species, overhunting/fishing, pollution, climate change, disease. SPECIES VULNERABILITY: species with small populations, specialised diets, slow reproduction are most vulnerable. FLORIDA PANTHER: reduced to ~30 individuals, inbreeding caused genetic problems."Learn
    Australian Extinctions (15 min)"AUSTRALIA: 60+ mammal species extinct since European settlement (highest mammal extinction rate globally). THYLACINE (1936), Pig-footed bandicoot, Desert rat kangaroo. Causes: habitat loss, introduced predators (foxes, cats), introduced herbivores (rabbits compete for food)."Learn
    Extinction vs Evolution (5 min)"Extinction is the opposite of speciation. Species that cannot adapt → go extinct. Extinction clears ecological niches → new species can evolve to fill them."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Explains the difference between background and mass extinction and identifies at least 2 causes of extinction.

    Lesson 3: Human Impact on Evolution

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Artificial Selection (15 min)"ARTIFICIAL SELECTION: humans deliberately breed organisms with desired traits. DOMESTICATION: wolves → dogs (over 15,000 years). AUROCHS (wild cattle) → modern cattle breeds. WILD MUSTARD → cabbage, broccoli, cauliflower, kale (all same species!). This shows evolution can be directed by selection pressure."Learn
    Human-Driven Evolution (15 min)"HUMAN IMPACT causing evolutionary change: ANTIBIOTIC RESISTANCE (bacteria), PESTICIDE RESISTANCE (insects), HERBICIDE RESISTANCE (weeds). FISHING DOWN: fisheries preferentially catch largest fish → fish evolving smaller body size. URBAN EVOLUTION: some birds, insects, plants adapting to urban environments (noisy environments → some birds sing at higher frequencies)."Learn
    Conservation Implications (15 min)"CONSERVE genetic diversity — prevents inbreeding. MAINTAIN habitat corridors — allows gene flow between populations. BREEDING programs — maintain genetic variation. Remove selective pressures where possible."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Human activities can accelerate or reverse evolutionary processes."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Evaluates human impact on evolution with at least 3 examples and proposes conservation strategies.
