Mathematics Unit Plan — Addition and Subtraction

Foundation (Pre-Primary) | 8 Weeks | Australian Curriculum v9

Mentor Text: Pete the Cat and His Four Groovy Buttons by Eric Litwin
A picture book about a cat losing buttons one by one — perfect concrete hook for subtraction as "taking away" and addition as "combining."

UNIT AT A GLANCE

**Year Level**Foundation (Pre-Primary / Kindergarten)
**Strand**Number
**Duration**8 weeks × 3 lessons/week = 24 lessons
**Model**5E Instructional Model
**Pedagogical Frame**Gradual Release: I Do → We Do → You Do
**Mentor Text***Pete the Cat and His Four Groovy Buttons* — Eric Litwin
**Big Ideas**Addition means combining two groups to find the total; subtraction means taking away from a group to find what remains; counting is the foundation of both operations
**Driving Question***What happens when we put things together, and what happens when we take things away?*

    • BIG UNDERSTANDINGS

    Students will understand that:

  • Counting tells us how many — we count on from the bigger number when adding
  • Addition means putting groups together — the total is bigger than either part
  • Subtraction means taking away — the result is smaller than what we started with
  • The same situation can be described as addition OR subtraction depending on the question
  • Equal groups mean each group has the same number — sharing equally
  • Numbers can be broken into parts and recombined (part-part-whole)

    • ESSENTIAL QUESTIONS

  • What happens when we put things together?
  • What happens when we take things away?
  • How many are there altogether?
  • How many are left?
  • What's the same and different about adding and subtracting?

    • VOCABULARY — TIERED

    Tier 1 (everyday)

    add, take away, plus, minus, altogether, total, how many, count, together, left, more, fewer, same

    Tier 2 (academic)

    combine, separate, compare, count on, count back, equals, altogether, groups, parts, whole

    Tier 3 (mathematical)

    addition, subtraction, number sentence, equation, sum, difference, total, collection, subitise, partition

    COLD TASK (Administered: Week 1, Lesson 1)

    Purpose: Establish baseline understanding before teaching begins. Students attempt the task with teacher support if needed (oral response acceptable for Foundation). Teachers do NOT correct or scaffold. Observe and annotate.

    Cold Task / Hot Task (IDENTICAL — used twice)

    Name: _________________________ Date: _____________ Year: _______

    Part A — Counting

  • Count the dots. How many? (Array of 7 dots)

    • ____________

  • Count the objects. How many? (7 familiar objects)

    • ____________

    Part B — Combining (Addition)

  • There are 2 red buttons and 1 blue button on the table. How many buttons altogether?

    • Draw a picture. Write the number.

    ____________ buttons altogether

  • Pete has 3 buttons. He finds 1 more. How many does he have now?

    • Draw a picture. Write the number.

    ____________ buttons

    Part C — Taking Away (Subtraction)

  • There are 4 counters. I take 1 away. How many are left?

    • Draw circles to show. Write the number.

    ____________ left

  • Pete had 4 buttons. He lost 1. How many are left?

    • Draw a picture. Write the number.

    ____________ left

    Part D — Comparing

  • I have 3 pencils. Sam has 5 pencils. Who has more?

    • Circle the answer: Sam / Me

    How many more? ____________

    Part E — Equal Groups

  • Share these 6 dots equally between 2 circles. Draw them.

    • ⬤ ⬤ ⬤ ⬤ ⬤ ⬤

    Each circle has: ____________

    Cold Task is NOT marked as right/wrong in front of students. Return unmarked. Use teacher annotations only.

    HOT TASK (Administered: Week 8, Lesson 24)

    Identical to Cold Task — same 8 questions, same format.

    Students receive back their Cold Task alongside their Hot Task at the end of Week 8. They share: "What did you know in Week 1? What do you know now?"

    A–E RUBRIC — Addition and Subtraction (Foundation)

    Aligned to: AC9MFN01, AC9MFN02, AC9MFN03, AC9MFN04, AC9MFN05

    Criterion 1: Counting and Number Recognition (to 20)

    GradeDescriptor
    **A**Consistently and confidently counts collections to 20 using efficient strategies; accurately names numbers out of sequence to 20; uses subitising for small collections; independently checks their own counting
    **B**Counts reliably to 20 with some prompting; able to name most numbers to 20; uses counting strategies but may lose track beyond 10
    **C**Counts to at least 10 reliably with one-to-one correspondence; names numbers to 10 with some support; begins to count beyond 10 with scaffolding
    **D**Counts to 5 or beyond with support; relies on finger counting; inconsistently matches one object to one number name
    **E**Counts to 3 or fewer with significant support; does not yet demonstrate reliable one-to-one correspondence

    Criterion 2: Addition — Combining Collections

    GradeDescriptor
    **A**Uses counting-on strategy confidently to combine two collections within 10; represents addition with drawings and number sentences; explains that addition makes the total bigger
    **B**Combines collections within 10 using counting strategies; attempts number sentences with occasional errors; connects addition to combining
    **C**Adds within 10 using concrete materials; counts all objects (count from 1) for both groups; begins to use number sentences with support
    **D**Adds within 5 with concrete support; counts all each time without counting on; needs physical objects to attempt any addition
    **E**Unable to combine collections; requires significant support to understand "altogether"

    Criterion 3: Subtraction — Taking Away

    GradeDescriptor
    **A**Uses counting-back strategy confidently to subtract within 10; represents subtraction with drawings and number sentences; explains that subtraction makes the total smaller
    **B**Subtracts within 10 using counting-back or concrete materials; attempts number sentences with minor errors; connects subtraction to "taking away"
    **C**Subtracts within 10 using concrete materials; counts all remaining objects; begins to use number sentences with support
    **D**Subtracts within 5 with physical objects; does not yet count back reliably; needs full scaffolding for subtraction language
    **E**Unable to take away; does not yet demonstrate understanding of subtraction

    Criterion 4: Part-Part-Whole and Equal Sharing

    GradeDescriptor
    **A**Independently partitions small collections and describes parts; successfully shares objects equally between 2 groups; explains that equal parts make a whole
    **B**Partitions collections with minor errors; shares equally between 2 groups with occasional remainder; begins to use part-part-whole language
    **C**With teacher guidance, splits collections into two groups; attempts equal sharing but may have unequal groups; begins to recognise that equal parts relate to the whole
    **D**Splits a collection of 10 or fewer with significant support; sharing attempts result in unequal groups; does not yet connect parts to whole
    **E**Unable to partition or share equally; does not demonstrate part-part-whole understanding

    Criterion 5: Comparing Collections

    GradeDescriptor
    **A**Accurately compares two collections using matching or counting strategies; uses "more," "fewer," and "same" correctly; determines "how many more" using an efficient strategy
    **B**Compares two collections with minor errors; uses more/fewer language correctly; attempts "how many more" with concrete materials
    **C**Compares two collections of up to 10 objects using matching or counting; uses comparison language with scaffolding; "how many more" attempted with support
    **D**Compares two collections with significant support; may confuse more/fewer; unable to determine "how many more" without full scaffolding
    **E**Unable to compare collections; does not demonstrate understanding of "more," "fewer," or "how many more"

    Criterion 6: Mathematical Reasoning and Communication

    GradeDescriptor
    **A**Uses mathematical words (add, take away, altogether) confidently in speech; draws pictures that represent problems accurately; explains their thinking verbally
    **B**Uses mathematical words with some confidence; drawings generally represent the problem; attempts to explain thinking
    **C**Uses some mathematical language with prompting; drawings show the problem situation; limited verbal explanation
    **D**Relies on everyday words; drawings may not accurately reflect the problem; minimal verbal explanation
    **E**Does not yet communicate mathematically; relies entirely on adult to interpret meaning

    SUCCESS CRITERIA — STUDENT-FACING

    (Displayed on classroom wall throughout unit)

    I can:

  • [ ] Count objects to 20
  • [ ] Put two groups together and tell how many altogether (addition)
  • [ ] Take away from a group and tell how many are left (subtraction)
  • [ ] Draw a picture to show an addition or subtraction problem
  • [ ] Share objects equally between two groups
  • [ ] Tell which group has more and how many more
  • [ ] Write a number sentence (e.g. 3 + 2 = 5)

    • DIFFERENTIATION FRAMEWORK

    EAL/D Students

  • Pre-teach key vocabulary with real objects before each lesson (buttons, counters, blocks)
  • Sentence frames using physical objects: "I have ___ buttons. I add ___ buttons. Now I have ___ buttons."
  • Bilingual anchor charts with pictures and home language labels
  • Pair with a supportive partner during We Do activities
  • Accept oral explanation in place of written recording
  • Allow additional processing time (minimum 20 seconds wait time after questions)
  • Reduce written output — focus on physical demonstration and verbal explanation
  • Use hand gestures: thumbs up for "adds," thumbs down for "takes away"

    • Students Identified as Gifted/Advanced

  • Explore addition and subtraction within 15 (beyond the unit scope)
  • Investigate: "What happens if you add two numbers in a different order? Does it still equal the same?"
  • Challenge: Can you show the same problem using addition AND subtraction?
  • Problem-posing: students create their own Pete the Cat style stories with different numbers
  • Investigate doubles (e.g. 3+3, 4+4) — introduction to multiplication

    • Students Not Yet at Expected Level (NEP)

  • Consolidate counting to 5 before moving to counting to 10
  • Use physical objects (counters, buttons) for every addition and subtraction activity — do not move to pictures too early
  • Focus on combining two groups of 1–3 objects; subtraction of 1–2 objects
  • Focus on one concept per lesson — addition OR subtraction, not both
  • Reduced number range: work within 5 until fluent, then extend to 10
  • Pre-lesson micro-session (5 minutes, pull-aside): counting practice with objects
  • Peer support pairing with a student who has mastered the concept
  • Modified success criteria with teacher: "I can count to ___", "I can add ___ and ___"

    LESSON 1.1 — Cold Task and Launch

    Duration: 40 minutes

    Type: Engage / Assessment Launch

    Resources: Cold Task copies, Pete the Cat and His Four Groovy Buttons (book), large buttons or counters, coloured pencils

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning to show what I already know about counting, adding, and taking away (Cold Task)
  • I am learning that this unit is about putting things together and taking things away

    • Success Criteria

  • I can attempt all parts of the Cold Task
  • I can listen to Pete the Cat and talk about what happened

    • Lesson Sequence

    ENGAGE — Hook (10 min)

    Teacher Action:

    "Before we start, I have a story about a very cool cat named Pete. Pete loves buttons. Let's find out what happens!"
  • Read Pete the Cat and His Four Groovy Buttons aloud. Do not stop to teach — let the story land.
  • After reading: "Turn and talk — what happened to Pete's buttons? Did he have more or fewer at the end?"
  • Take 3–4 shares. Accept all responses. Do not correct.
    • "That's what this whole unit is about. By the end of 8 weeks, you'll be able to explain exactly what happened using numbers."

    COLD TASK (20 min)

    Teacher Action:

    "Now I'm going to ask you to show me what you already know. This is NOT a test — I just want to see your thinking."
  • Distribute Cold Task. Students work independently.
  • For Foundation: oral response is acceptable if child cannot record.
  • Teacher circulates, observing only. Note: who counts from 1 each time; who counts on; who can write numerals.
  • Collect all Cold Tasks.

    • Student Activity:

  • Complete Cold Task independently (all 8 questions)
  • Students who finish early: draw Pete the Cat with buttons

    • DEBRIEF (5 min)

    Teacher Action:

    "How did that feel? Some of you may have found some tricky — and that's okay!"
  • Normalise: "Some of you counted everything from one. Some of you already knew how to add. We're going to learn how to do all of this really well."
  • Preview the 8 weeks.

    • Homework / Reflection: None. Cold Task is teacher-only.

    Teacher Notes

  • After lesson: sort Cold Tasks into 4 piles — emerging, approaching, at level, extending
  • Use to inform flexible groupings for Week 2 onwards
  • Note any student who already uses counting-on or writes number sentences → flag for gifted extension

    • LESSON 1.2 — Combining Groups: What Is Addition?

    Duration: 40 minutes

    Type: Concept Introduction (I Do → We Do)

    Resources: Large buttons or counters, Pete the Cat pictures (projected), whiteboard, individual whiteboards

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning that addition means putting groups together to find the total
  • I am learning that when we add, the total is bigger than each individual group

    • Success Criteria

  • I can put two groups of objects together and count the total
  • I can explain what "altogether" means

    • I DO (12 min)

    Teacher Action:

    "Let's go back to Pete. At the start, Pete has 4 buttons. But wait — in the story, he finds more buttons! Let's say Pete finds 1 more button."

    Hold up 4 large counters on the document camera.

    "Here are Pete's 4 buttons. He finds 1 more. What should I do with these?"
    "I'm going to PUT THEM TOGETHER. That means ADDING."

    Combine the groups. Count aloud: "1, 2, 3, 4, 5."

    "Pete has 5 buttons altogether. 4 plus 1 equals 5."

    Write on board with children counting along:

    ```

    4 + 1 = 5

    ```

    "The plus sign (+) means 'add'. The equals sign (=) means 'the same as'. So 4 plus 1 is the same as 5."
    "When we ADD, we make the number BIGGER."

    WE DO (18 min)

    Teacher Action:

    "Now you're going to be the button collectors. Take your counters."

    Round 1: "Put 2 counters in one group and 1 counter in another group. Push them together. How many altogether?"

    Round 2: "Put 3 counters in one group and 2 counters in another. How many altogether?"

    Round 3: "Put 1 counter and 4 counters together. How many?"

    After each round:

    "Tell your partner: how many did you have altogether?"

    Teacher explicitly uses the language:

    "ALT together. How many ALTOGETHER?"

    YOU DO (10 min)

    Students work independently:

  • Draw 2 circles. Draw 3 more circles. How many altogether?
  • Draw 4 stars. Draw 1 more star. How many altogether?
  • "When we add, does the number get bigger or smaller?" Circle: BIGGER / SMALLER

    • Differentiation

  • EAL/D: Vocabulary cards on desk (add, plus, altogether, bigger). Physical counters always available.
  • NEP: Work with groups of 1, 2, 3 only. Question 1 only. Physical counters throughout.
  • Gifted: Questions 1-3 + "Can you write the number sentence for your picture?"

    • Lesson Close

    "Thumbs up/middle/down — how confident do you feel about putting groups together?"

    LESSON 1.3 — Counting On and Writing Number Sentences

    Duration: 40 minutes

    Type: Concept Introduction + Guided Practice

    Resources: Number line to 10 (display), counters, individual whiteboards

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning to count on from the larger group when adding
  • I am learning to write an addition number sentence (e.g. 3 + 2 = 5)

    • Success Criteria

  • I can count on from the bigger number
  • I can write an addition number sentence

    • I DO (12 min)

    "I have 3 buttons in one hand and 2 buttons in the other. I want to find how many altogether. I COULD count all of them from 1... OR..."
    "I can start with the BIGGER number and count ON."

    Hold up 3 fingers on one hand, 2 on the other.

    "3 and 2. The bigger number is 3. I hold up 3 fingers. Then I count ON 2 more: 4, 5."
    "3 + 2 = 5. Same answer — but faster!"
    "This is called COUNTING ON. Start with the big number. Count on the small number."

    WE DO (18 min)

    Number line activity:

    Use a number line to 10 on the floor or desk.

    "I'm going to add 4 + 2. I start my counter on 4. Then I JUMP forward 2 jumps. Where do I land?"

    Students do the same on desk number lines with counters.

    Partner activity — each student gets a number card (e.g. "3 + 2"):

  • Partner A has the larger group; Partner B has the smaller group
  • Use counters to show, then write the number sentence
  • Practice counting on: "I started with ___ and counted on ___"

    • Whiteboard practice:

  • Teacher holds up fingers for both groups; students write the number sentence and hold up

    • YOU DO (10 min)

  • "I have 4 marbles. I get 3 more. How many altogether?" Draw and write the number sentence.
  • "I have 5 pencils. I get 2 more. How many altogether?" Draw and write.
  • Circle the strategy you'd use: COUNT ALL FROM 1 / COUNT ON FROM BIGGER NUMBER
  • EXTENSION: "What is 6 + 1? What is 1 + 6? Are they the same?"

    • Differentiation

  • EAL/D: Number line always on desk; finger counting is encouraged; sentence frame: "I started with ___ and counted on ___."
  • NEP: Focus on counting on from 1–5; all students must physically count on using the number line
  • Gifted: Extension question + investigate: "Does 2+3 equal 3+2?"

    LESSON 2.1 — Taking Away: What Is Subtraction?

    Duration: 40 minutes

    Type: Explore

    Resources: Large buttons/counters, Pete the Cat projected images, whiteboard

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning that subtraction means taking away from a group
  • I am learning that when we subtract, the result is smaller than what we started with

    • Success Criteria

  • I can take objects away from a group and count what is left
  • I can explain what "left" or "remain" means in subtraction

    • ENGAGE — Mentor Text Hook (5 min)

    Project the page of Pete's buttons.

    "Pete started with 4 buttons. He POPPED one off. What happened to the number of buttons?"

    Take responses.

    "When we take away, the number gets SMALLER. That's called SUBTRACTION."

    I DO (12 min)

    "I have 5 buttons. I take 1 away."

    Hold up 5 counters. Physically remove 1.

    "I started with 5. I TOOK AWAY 1. How many are left?"

    Count the remaining: "1, 2, 3, 4."

    "5 take away 1 equals 4."

    Write on board:

    ```

    5 - 1 = 4

    ```

    "The minus sign (−) means 'take away'. So 5 minus 1 is the same as 4."
    "When we SUBTRACT, the number gets SMALLER."

    Demonstrate with 4-1, 3-1, 5-2. Each time confirm: started with ___, took away ___, left ___.

    WE DO (18 min)

    Button taking-away activity:

    Round 1 — "Start with 4 buttons. Take away 1. How many left?"

    Students use counters. Share answers verbally.

    Round 2 — "Start with 5 buttons. Take away 2. How many left?"

    Round 3 — "Start with 6 buttons. Take away 1. How many left?"

    Discussion:

    "When we take away, do we get a BIGGER or SMALLER number?"

    Key concept: When we take away, the number always gets smaller.

    YOU DO (10 min)

  • Start with 4 counters. Take away 1. How many left? Draw and write.
  • Start with 5 counters. Take away 2. How many left? Draw and write.
  • "When we take away, does the number get BIGGER or SMALLER?" Circle the answer.
  • EXTENSION: "Start with 6. Take away 1. How many? What about starting with 6 and taking away 2 — is it the same or different?"

    • Differentiation

  • EAL/D: Vocabulary cards (take away, minus, left, subtract); physical counters always required
  • NEP: Work within 3-5; only take away 1; use physical taking-away action
  • Gifted: Questions 1-3 + "Can you show 5-2 two different ways?"

    • LESSON 2.2 — Counting Back on the Number Line

    Duration: 40 minutes

    Type: Explore

    Resources: Number lines (display + student copies), counters, whiteboard

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning to count back when subtracting
  • I am learning to use a number line to solve subtraction problems

    • Success Criteria

  • I can count back 1, 2, or 3 on a number line
  • I can solve 5 - 2 by counting back

    • I DO (12 min)

    Draw a number line 0-10 on board.

    "Here's another way to think about subtraction — JUMPING BACKWARDS on the number line."
    "I want to do 5 − 2. I start at 5. Then I jump BACK 2. Where do I land?"

    Jump on the number line: 5... 4 (back 1), 3 (back 2). Land on 3.

    "5 take away 2 equals 3. The answer to a subtraction problem is called the DIFFERENCE."

    Do another: 7 - 1 (start at 7, jump back 1 → 6)

    "This is COUNTING BACK. Instead of counting all the objects, we use the number line."

    WE DO (18 min)

    Students use their desk number lines.

    Guided practice:

    "Show me 4 - 1. Start your counter on 4. Jump back 1. Where are you?"
    "Show me 6 - 2. Start your counter on 6. Jump back 2. Where are you?"
    "Show me 8 - 1. Start your counter on 8. Jump back 1. Where are you?"

    Partner activity:

  • Partner A gives a subtraction problem (within 10)
  • Partner B solves it on the number line
  • Check together, then swap

    • YOU DO (10 min)

  • Use the number line to solve: a) 6 - 1 = ___ b) 7 - 2 = ___ c) 5 - 1 = ___
  • Draw a number line. Show 8 - 3 = 5 by drawing the jumps.
  • "My friend starts at 9 and jumps back 2. Where do they land? Show me."
  • EXTENSION: "What is 9 - 1? What is 10 - 1? Can you find a pattern?"

    • Differentiation

  • EAL/D: Pre-drawn number line on desk; keep the number line visible at all times
  • NEP: Work within 5 only; count back 1 only to start
  • Gifted: Extension + can they solve 9 - 3? What strategy did they use?

    • LESSON 2.3 — Addition and Subtraction: Same Story, Two Ways

    Duration: 40 minutes

    Type: Guided Practice

    Resources: Counters, picture books (Pete the Cat), books

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning that the same situation can be shown as addition or subtraction
  • I am learning to choose when to add and when to subtract

    • Success Criteria

  • I can solve problems using addition and problems using subtraction
  • I can tell the difference between "altogether" and "left"

    • I DO (10 min)

    "Here's something interesting. Look at this story:"
    "Pete has 3 buttons. He finds 2 more. How many does he have?"
    "Now look at THIS story:"
    "Pete has 5 buttons. He loses 2. How many does he have?"
    "Same numbers — 5, 2, 3 — but different stories. One is ADDITION. One is SUBTRACTION."
    "How do you know which one to use? Look at the question:"
  • "How many ALTOGETHER?" → ADD
  • "How many LEFT?" → SUBTRACT

    • Write on board:

    ```

    "altogether" = ADD (put together)

    "left" = SUBTRACT (take away)

    ```

    WE DO (20 min)

    Story sort activity:

    Teacher reads scenarios. Students hold up:

  • + card (addition) if it says "altogether"
  • − card (subtraction) if it says "left"

    • Scenarios:

  • Sam has 4 apples. She gets 2 more. How many altogether? (ADD)
  • Tom has 6 cars. He gives 1 to his friend. How many left? (SUBTRACT)
  • There are 5 birds on a tree. 2 fly away. How many left? (SUBTRACT)
  • I have 3 pencils. My mum gives me 4 more. How many altogether? (ADD)

    • After sort: students physically act out each scenario with counters, then write the number sentence.

    YOU DO (10 min)

  • "Mia has 5 flowers. She gives 2 to her mum. How many does she have left?" Draw and solve.
  • "There are 4 dogs. 3 more dogs come. How many dogs altogether?" Draw and solve.
  • Circle the operation: "altogether" → ADD / SUBTRACT; "left" → ADD / SUBTRACT
  • EXTENSION: "Can you make up a story using the numbers 6, 2, and 8?"

    • Differentiation

  • EAL/D: Operation cards (+ and −) on desk; reference chart visible
  • NEP: Focus on scenarios with small numbers (1-5); physical counters for all
  • Gifted: Extension + "Write both an addition and subtraction story using the same numbers."

    LESSON 3.1 — Counting On Strategy

    Duration: 40 minutes

    Type: Explore

    Resources: Number lines, counters, dominoes, individual whiteboards

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning to count on from the larger number instead of counting all
  • I am learning that this is a faster way to add

    • Success Criteria

  • I can count on from the larger number (e.g. for 3+4, start at 4 and count on 3)
  • I can explain why we start from the bigger number

    • I DO (12 min)

    "Let's say I have 6 buttons and 2 more. I could count all: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8. Eight buttons."
    "OR — I can start with the BIGGER number. Bigger number: 6. Smaller number: 2."
    "I hold up 6 in my head. Then I count ON 2 more: 7, 8."

  • Gifted: Extension + "What happens if you add 0 to a number? Does it change?"

    LESSON 8.1 — Addition and Subtraction Gallery

    Duration: 40 minutes

    Type: Review / Celebration

    Resources: Student work samples, chart paper, coloured pens

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning to celebrate my adding and subtracting knowledge
  • I am making connections between all the things I've learned

    • GALLERY WALK (20 min)

    Students' best work samples displayed around the room.

    Students walk the gallery with sticky dots:

  • GREEN dot: "Something I did well"
  • ORANGE dot: "Something I want to learn more about"

    • CONSOLIDATION GAMES (15 min)

    Students rotate through 3 mini-games (5 min each):

  • Make 10 Memory: match pairs that make 10
  • Button Story: create and solve own addition/subtraction story
  • Number Line Challenge: solve subtraction on the number line

    • REFLECTION (5 min)

    "Without looking at your books, tell your partner three things you know now that you didn't know in Week 1."

    LESSON 8.2 — Connecting It All

    Duration: 40 minutes

    Type: Synthesis

    Resources: Concept map template, whiteboards, Pete the Cat

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning to see how all the addition and subtraction ideas connect
  • I am learning to explain the big ideas in my own words

    • WHOLE CLASS CONCEPT MAP (20 min)

    Build class concept map with "Addition and Subtraction" in the centre.

    Key connections:

  • Addition ↔ Combining ↔ "altogether"
  • Subtraction ↔ Taking away ↔ "left"
  • Counting on ↔ Counting back ↔ Number line
  • Part-part-whole ↔ Fact families
  • Comparing ↔ "how many more"
  • Equal groups ↔ Sharing equally

    • PERSONAL CONCEPT MAP (15 min)

    Students create their own concept map with at least 4 key terms and at least 3 examples.

    PETE THE CAT FINAL STORY (5 min)

    "Let's solve one final Pete story. At the start, Pete had 4 buttons. The doorbell rang and he found 3 more. How many does he have now? But then he POPPED 2 more! How many are left?"

    Students solve, share, and celebrate.

    LESSON 8.3 — Hot Task + Cold → Hot Comparison + Celebration

    Duration: 45 minutes

    Type: Summative Assessment + Reflection

    Resources: Hot Task copies, Cold Task copies (returned), coloured pens, sticky notes

    Learning Intentions

  • I am demonstrating what I know about adding and subtracting
  • I am celebrating my growth from Week 1 to Week 8

    • Success Criteria

  • I can complete the Hot Task using everything I've learned
  • I can identify at least 2 ways I've grown

    • HOT TASK (20 min)

    Distribute Hot Task (identical to Cold Task).

    Students work independently.

    Teacher circulates.

    Collect all Hot Tasks.

    COLD → HOT COMPARISON (15 min)

    Return Cold Tasks alongside completed Hot Tasks.

    Students compare, side by side:

  • CIRCLE in green: answers I got right in both
  • CIRCLE in blue: things I got wrong in Week 1 but got right today
  • CIRCLE in orange: things that are still tricky for me

    • Students write on sticky notes:

  • "Before Week 1, I thought..."
  • "Now I know..."
  • "I'm most proud of..."

    • CELEBRATION (10 min)

    Whole class share — each student shares ONE "I'm most proud of" statement.

    Display both tasks: "Look How Far We've Come"

    Read Pete the Cat and His Four Groovy Buttons one final time. Celebrate!

    Teacher Assessment Note

    Collect all Hot Tasks. Mark against the A–E rubric (6 criteria). Compare to Cold Task notes. Document growth for each student. Use to inform planning for Year 1 mathematics.
