Unit Content

Forces and Motion — Physical Sciences Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 4

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum Science v9 — Physical Sciences

Unit Big Idea: Forces are pushes and pulls that can cause objects to move, stop, speed up, slow down, or change direction. All forces act between objects, either through direct contact or at a distance.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Design and conduct an experiment to measure how much force is needed to move different objects

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Design a system that uses multiple forces to solve a real-world problem

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to investigate contact and non-contact forces, describe how forces affect the motion of objects, measure and compare forces, and explain how forces are used in everyday technologies.

Success Criteria:

  • I can identify and describe contact forces (push, pull, friction, normal force) and non-contact forces (gravity, magnetic, electrostatic).
  • I can measure force using a Newton meter and record data in tables and graphs.
  • I can explain how forces change an object's speed, direction, or shape.
  • I can investigate the effects of friction on motion.
  • I can describe how balanced and unbalanced forces affect motion.
  • I can apply force concepts to real-world technologies and inventions.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: What Are Forces?

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Design a Force Experiment

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Hold a ball. "What would happen if I let go? GRAVITY would pull it down. What would happen if I threw it? The ball would fly through the air. What made it move? A FORCE!"Observe and wonder
    Task (35 min)Cold Task: "Design an experiment to find out how much FORCE is needed to move different objects. What will you measure? What objects will you test? How will you make your experiment fair? What do you predict will happen? Draw your experiment setup and write 3–4 sentences explaining your method."Design experiment
    Share (10 min)3–4 students share their experimental designsShare

    Differentiation: EAL students draw their experiment with teacher support. NEP students: design experiment for 2 objects instead of 4.

    Success Criteria Check: Student designs an experiment to measure force with a fair test method and prediction.

    Lesson 2: Forces All Around

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Engage (Concept Introduction)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What is a Force? (15 min)"A FORCE is a PUSH or a PULL. Forces can make things: START moving, STOP moving, go FASTER, go SLOWER, CHANGE DIRECTION, or CHANGE SHAPE. Forces are measured in NEWTONS (N), named after Sir Isaac Newton."Learn force definition
    Force Walk (15 min)Walk around the school/classroom. Identify forces acting on objects: book on desk (gravity pulls down, desk pushes up), door being opened (push), magnet on fridge (pull), wind blowing leaves (push), car driving (engine push, friction slow). Record at least 8 forces.Conduct force walk
    Describe the Forces (15 min)"For each force, describe: WHAT is pushing/pulling? WHAT is being pushed/pulled? WHAT direction is the force? HOW BIG is the force (estimate in Newtons if you can)?"Describe forces
    Write (5 min)"A force is a push or a pull that can change how something moves." Write in science books.Write definition

    Success Criteria Check: Identifies at least 8 forces in the environment and describes the direction and effect of each.

    Lesson 3: Contact vs Non-Contact Forces

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Engage (Categorisation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Two Types of Forces (10 min)"CONTACT FORCES: touching to apply force — push, pull, friction, air resistance, normal force. NON-CONTACT FORCES: acting at a distance — gravity, magnetism, electrostatic force."Learn two types
    Sort Activity (20 min)Students receive scenario cards. Sort into CONTACT vs NON-CONTACT: Book on table (contact), magnet attracting paper clips (non-contact), ball rolling down hill (contact/gravity), Earth pulling moon (non-contact/gravity), foot pushing bicycle pedal (contact), two balloons repelling each other after rubbing on hair (non-contact/electrostatic).Sort force cards
    Investigation — Contact vs Distance (15 min)Test: (1) Push a book across a desk (contact). (2) Hold a magnet near a paper clip without touching (non-contact — the paper clip jumps to the magnet). Record observations.Test both force types
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Give one example of a contact force and one example of a non-contact force."Written response

    Differentiation: EAL students draw and sort cards physically. Gifted students: research whether light is a force (photons exert radiation pressure).

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly sorts at least 8 forces as contact or non-contact and explains the difference.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Measuring Forces

    Lesson 1: Newton Meters

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is a Newton? (10 min)"A NEWTON (N) is the unit we use to measure FORCE. One Newton is roughly the force of a small apple's weight. A heavy book might weigh about 10N. A person might weigh 300–700N."Learn Newton unit
    Using a Newton Meter (15 min)Demonstrate how to use a Newton meter (spring scale): attach object, let it hang, read the scale when the object is stationary (weight force = gravity pulling down). Practice measuring: pencil case, book, school bag, chair.Practice measuring
    Measure Force to Move Objects (15 min)Students attach Newton meter to various objects: book, chair, desk, box, toy car. Pull slowly until the object just starts to move. Read the Newton meter at the moment of movement — this is the FORCE NEEDED TO OVERCOME FRICTION and start motion. Record in table.Measure forces
    Record and Compare (10 min)Create a bar graph: OBJECT vs FORCE NEEDED TO MOVE (N). Which object needed the most force? Which needed the least? Why?Graph results

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly uses a Newton meter to measure force, records at least 6 measurements, and creates a graph comparing forces.

    Lesson 2: Force and Motion

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Forces Change Motion (10 min)"Forces can CHANGE how fast something moves. More force = faster acceleration. More mass = harder to move. This is Newton's Second Law: F = m × a (force = mass × acceleration). For the same force, a heavier object accelerates less."Learn Newton's Second Law
    Mass vs Force Investigation (15 min)Test: Apply the SAME force (Newton meter reading) to different masses: tennis ball vs basketball vs bowling ball. How far does each travel? Record. Heavier objects accelerate less for the same applied force.Test mass and acceleration
    Data Analysis (15 min)"What happened when you applied the same force to different masses? Why? (More mass = more inertia = harder to change motion.) What happened when you applied different forces to the same mass? Why?"Analyse data
    Write (10 min)"When the same force is applied to objects of different mass, the lighter object accelerates more. Force, mass, and acceleration are related."Write conclusion

    Success Criteria Check: Investigates the relationship between force, mass, and acceleration and draws a conclusion consistent with Newton's Second Law.

    Lesson 3: Balanced and Unbalanced Forces

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Balanced Forces (10 min)"When forces are BALANCED, they are EQUAL and OPPOSITE, so they CANCEL OUT. The object doesn't change its motion — it stays still or keeps moving at the same speed in the same direction." Example: book on desk — gravity pulls down (5N), desk pushes up (5N) = balanced.Learn balanced forces
    Unbalanced Forces (10 min)"When forces are UNBALANCED, one force is greater than the other, so motion CHANGES. The object accelerates in the direction of the greater force." Example: pushing a stationary car — your push is greater than friction, so the car moves forward.Learn unbalanced forces
    Tug-of-War Demonstration (15 min)Conduct a class tug-of-war: Equal teams = balanced (no movement). Unequal teams = unbalanced (team with more force wins). Discuss: all the pushes and pulls are contact forces applied through the rope.Demonstrate tug-of-war
    Write the Rules (10 min)"BALANCED FORCES: equal magnitude, opposite direction → no change in motion. UNBALANCED FORCES: unequal magnitude → object accelerates in direction of greater force."Write rules

    Success Criteria Check: Distinguishes between balanced and unbalanced forces and correctly identifies whether forces on an object are balanced or unbalanced.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLAIN: Friction

    Lesson 1: What Is Friction?

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Causes Friction? (15 min)"FRICTION is a CONTACT FORCE that OPPOSES motion. It is caused by microscopic bumps on surfaces rubbing against each other. Even smooth-looking surfaces have tiny bumps when viewed under a microscope." Show microscopic images of smooth glass vs rough sandpaper.Learn friction cause
    Types of Friction (15 min)"Three types: STATIC FRICTION — keeps object still when you try to push it (the friction you must OVERCOME to start moving). SLIDING FRICTION — opposes sliding motion (sled sliding down a hill). FLUID FRICTION / DRAG — opposes motion through air or water (hand out car window, swimmer through water)."Learn types
    Friction Sort Activity (10 min)Scenario cards: car tires on road (sliding), plane through air (drag), pushing a heavy box (static → sliding), swimming (drag), book on tilted shelf (static), parachute falling (drag). Sort into static/sliding/drag.Sort scenarios
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Why do we put oil in car engines?" (Reduce friction between moving metal parts.)Written response

    Success Criteria Check: Identifies the three types of friction and correctly classifies at least 5 real-world examples.

    Lesson 2: Friction Investigation

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Fair Test Design (10 min)"To investigate friction fairly, what must we keep the SAME? (Same object, same surface area, same force applied.) What will we CHANGE? (The surface type.)"Design fair test
    Friction Surface Test (25 min)Test how far different objects slide on different surfaces: wooden desk, carpet, tile, sandpaper, plastic mat, ice. Method: same object (wooden block), same initial force (Newton meter pull to 2N mark, then release), measure distance slid. Record in table.Test surfaces
    Graph Results (10 min)Create bar graph: SURFACE vs DISTANCE SLID. Which surface had the MOST friction (shortest distance)? Which had the LEAST (longest distance)? Order from most to least friction.Graph and order
    Real-World Application (5 min)"Why do basketball courts have polished wood (low friction — ball bounces well)? Why do car tires have tread (increase friction — better grip)? Why do we use ice and snow chains on car tires?"Discuss

    Success Criteria Check: Conducts a fair test of friction on at least 5 surfaces, records data, graphs results, and draws conclusions.

    Lesson 3: Reducing and Increasing Friction

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Ways to Reduce Friction (15 min)"LUBRICANTS — oil, grease, wax reduce friction between moving parts. STREAMLINING — shaping objects to glide through air/fluid with less drag. BALL BEARINGS — tiny steel balls inside wheels that roll instead of slide, reducing friction dramatically. WHEELS — convert sliding friction to rolling friction (much less force needed)."Learn friction reduction
    Ways to Increase Friction (15 min)"TREAD patterns on shoes and tyres — more grip. Rough surfaces — sandpaper increases friction. Brakes — friction pads press against wheel to slow/stop. Non-slip mats — rough rubber increases friction."Learn friction increase
    Design a Solution (15 min)"Problem: a toy car keeps sliding off a wooden ramp. Design a solution to INCREASE FRICTION so the car doesn't slide. You can use: rubber bands, sandpaper, fabric, tape, cardboard. Test your solution and measure the distance the car travels before sliding off."Design solution
    Test and Share (5 min)Test all designs. Which solution worked best? How much did it reduce the distance traveled (increased friction)?Test and share

    Differentiation: EAL students draw friction solutions. Gifted students: research magnetic levitation (maglev) trains — how do they eliminate friction entirely using magnetic forces?

    Success Criteria Check: Designs and tests a friction-increasing solution, measures results, and explains why the solution works.

    WEEK 4 — ELABORATE: Gravity and Weight

    Lesson 1: Gravity

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Gravity? (15 min)"GRAVITY is a NON-CONTACT force that PULLS objects toward each other. The more MASS an object has, the stronger its gravitational pull. Earth has a HUGE mass, so it pulls everything toward its centre — this is why things fall DOWN, not sideways or up." Demonstrate: drop objects — all fall at the same rate (ignoring air resistance).Learn gravity
    Gravity Everywhere (10 min)"Gravity is NOT just on Earth. The Moon has gravity — it's why astronauts on the Moon bounced (they weighed less, about 1/6 of Earth weight). The Sun has gravity — it holds all planets in orbit. Gravity exists EVERYWHERE in the universe."Learn universal gravity
    Gravity Investigation (15 min)Students investigate: (1) Does a heavier ball fall faster? Drop two balls of different masses simultaneously — they hit the ground at the same time. (2) Does surface area affect fall speed? Crumple paper vs flat paper — flat paper falls slower (more air resistance). Paper ball falls at same speed as ball.Investigate gravity
    Write (10 min)"Gravity is a non-contact force that pulls objects toward each other. Earth's gravity pulls all objects toward its centre. All objects near Earth accelerate at the same rate due to gravity (ignoring air resistance)."Write definition

    Success Criteria Check: Explains gravity as a non-contact force and demonstrates that all objects accelerate at the same rate due to gravity (in a vacuum).

    Lesson 2: Weight vs Mass

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Mass vs Weight (15 min)"MASS is the amount of MATTER in an object. Measured in KILOGRAMS (kg). Mass is ALWAYS THE SAME — on Earth, the Moon, or in space. WEIGHT is the FORCE of gravity pulling on mass. Measured in NEWTONS (N). Weight CHANGES depending on the gravitational field strength."Learn mass vs weight
    Calculate Weight (15 min)"On Earth, gravity is approximately 10 N/kg. So: 1kg object = 10N weight. 10kg object = 100N weight. On the Moon, gravity is 1.6 N/kg, so 10kg object = 16N weight. On Jupiter, gravity is 25 N/kg, so 10kg object = 250N weight." Calculate: What would you weigh on the Moon? On Jupiter? On Mars?Calculate weights
    Mass vs Weight Measurement (10 min)Demonstrate: A spring scale (Newton meter) measures WEIGHT (force). A balance scale measures MASS (by comparing to known masses). Show both methods on the same object.Compare measurements
    Record (5 min)"MASS: amount of matter, measured in kg, same everywhere. WEIGHT: gravitational force, measured in N, changes with location."Write comparison

    Success Criteria Check: Distinguishes between mass (constant, kg) and weight (changes with gravity, N) and calculates weight on different planets.

    Lesson 3: Gravity in Our Solar System

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Solar System Gravity Walk (15 min)"Gravity keeps planets in orbit around the Sun. Closer planets orbit FASTER (Mercury 88 days vs Earth 365 days vs Neptune 165 years). This is because gravity is stronger closer to the Sun." Students walk in circles representing orbits — inner students walk fast, outer students walk slow.Model orbits
    Why Planets Stay in Orbit (15 min)"Why don't planets fly off into space? GRAVITY pulls them toward the Sun. Why don't they crash into the Sun? They have HORIZONTAL velocity — they are moving sideways so fast that they keep missing the Sun. This is ORBITAL MOTION — gravity pulls in, velocity keeps them moving sideways."Learn orbital motion
    Gravity on Different Planets (15 min)Students research: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune, Moon. For each: surface gravity (N/kg), what a 30kg person would weigh, how long a year is. Create a table.Research planets
    Draw (5 min)Draw the solar system with orbital paths, showing planets moving faster when closer to the Sun.Draw solar system

    Differentiation: EAL students create a visual comparison chart. Gifted students: research how gravity assists (slingshot maneuvers) are used to accelerate spacecraft.

    Success Criteria Check: Researches gravity on multiple solar system bodies and correctly calculates weight differences using N/kg values.

    WEEK 5 — ELABORATE: Magnetic and Electrostatic Forces

    Lesson 1: Magnetism

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Magnetism? (15 min)"MAGNETISM is a NON-CONTACT force that PULLS or REPELS certain materials (iron, nickel, cobalt, and some alloys). Magnets have two POLES: NORTH and SOUTH. LIKE POLES REPEL (push apart). UNLIKE POLES ATTRACT (pull together)." Demonstrate with bar magnets.Learn magnetism
    Magnetic Field Investigation (15 min)Place a bar magnet under paper. Sprinkle iron filings on top. The filings arrange along invisible FIELD LINES — they show where the magnetic force is strongest. Draw the pattern. The field lines go from North to South outside the magnet.Investigate magnetic fields
    What Can Magnets Attract? (10 min)Test materials: steel paper clip, aluminium foil, copper coin, rubber eraser, plastic comb, wooden pencil, stainless steel spoon, glass marble. Which are attracted to a magnet? (Iron, nickel, cobalt — ferromagnetic materials.)Test materials
    Real-World Magnets (10 min)"Where do we use magnets? fridge magnets, speakers, electric motors, MRI machines, maglev trains, compasses."Learn applications

    Success Criteria Check: Investigates magnetic fields using iron filings, explains attraction and repulsion between poles, and identifies ferromagnetic materials.

    Lesson 2: Electrostatic Forces

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Static Electricity? (15 min)"All matter is made of atoms with POSITIVE protons, NEGATIVE electrons, and neutral neutrons. Normally, objects have equal positive and negative charges — they are NEUTRAL. When objects rub together, electrons can TRANSFER — one object gains electrons (becomes NEGATIVE), the other loses electrons (becomes POSITIVE). This is STATIC ELECTRICITY." Demonstrate: rub balloon on hair — balloon sticks to wall (attracted to neutral surface due to charge separation).Learn static electricity
    Attraction and Repulsion (15 min)"Like charges REPEL (two negative balloons push apart). Unlike charges ATTRACT (negative balloon pulls on positive wall). This is similar to magnets — like poles repel, unlike poles attract. But electrostatic forces act on charges, not poles."Learn charge interaction
    Electrostatic Investigation (15 min)Rub two balloons on hair. Hold by strings. (1) Bring balloons together — they repel (both negative). (2) Bring rubbed balloon near paper scraps — they attract (induced positive charge in paper). (3) Bring near a stream of water from a tap — water bends toward balloon (water is polar, attracted to charge).Test static electricity
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Why does a balloon rubbed on hair stick to the ceiling?"Written response

    Success Criteria Check: Demonstrates static electricity through at least 3 experiments, explains charge transfer and attraction/repulsion.

    Lesson 3: Non-Contact Force Applications

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Technology Using Non-Contact Forces (15 min)MAGNETIC: Maglev trains (float on magnetic field, no friction). Electric motors (magnets + electricity = motion). Speakers (electricity → magnetic field → vibration). COMPASS (Earth's magnetic field aligns needle). ELECTROSTATIC: Photocopiers (static electricity attracts toner to paper). Air fresheners (static charge spreads fragrance particles). Lightning (massive electrostatic discharge between clouds and ground).Learn technologies
    Compare Magnetic vs Electrostatic (10 min)Similarities: Both have attraction and repulsion. Both can act at a distance. Both involve fields. Differences: Magnets affect specific materials (iron). Static electricity affects any object with charge. Magnetic poles always come in pairs. Charges can be isolated (+ or −).Compare forces
    Design a Device (20 min)"Design a device that uses a non-contact force to solve a problem. Examples: a non-contact tool to pick up metal objects, a system to separate recyclable materials, an anti-static device for sensitive electronics. Draw your design, explain the force involved, and predict how well it will work."Design device
    Share (5 min)3–4 students share designsShare

    Differentiation: EAL students draw device with labels. Gifted students: research magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) in detail — how does it use magnetic fields to create images of the human body?

    Success Criteria Check: Designs a device using non-contact forces and correctly explains the force mechanism.

    WEEK 6 — EXPLAIN: Force and Technology

    Lesson 1: Simple Machines

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Six Simple Machines (15 min)"Humans invented SIMPLE MACHINES to make work EASIER by multiplying force: LEVER — a rigid bar that pivots on a fulcrum (crowbar, seesaw, scissors). WHEEL AND AXLE — wheel attached to a central axle (car, doorknob, screwdriver). PULLEY — wheel with a rope to change direction of force (flagpole, crane). INCLINED PLANE — a sloped surface (ramp, stairs, ladder). WEDGE — two inclined planes joined (knife, axe, nail). SCREW — an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder (jar lid, drill bit, light bulb)."Learn six machines
    Identify Simple Machines (15 min)Walk around school identifying: (1) screwdriver = wheel and axle + lever. (2) stairs = inclined plane. (3) scissors = two levers + wedge. (4) flagpole = pulley. (5) doorstop = wedge. Record at least 8 examples.Identify machines
    Mechanical Advantage (10 min)"A lever can make work easier by providing MECHANICAL ADVANTAGE. If the load is closer to the fulcrum than the effort, you need LESS force but must push a longer distance. This is how a crowbar lifts a heavy lid with small effort." Demonstrate.Learn mechanical advantage
    Build a Lever (10 min)Use a ruler (lever), pencil (fulcrum), and different masses. Find the balance point. Test: if you push down on the long end with small force, can you lift a heavy object on the short end?Build and test lever

    Success Criteria Check: Identifies all six simple machines in the environment and explains mechanical advantage using a lever example.

    Lesson 2: Complex Machines

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Compound Machines (10 min)"COMPLEX MACHINES are two or more simple machines combined. A bicycle: wheel and axle (wheels) + pulley (chain mechanism) + levers (handlebars). A car: wheel and axle + lever (windshield wiper) + inclined plane (wheel threads) + many more. A scissors: two levers + two wedges."Learn complex machines
    Analyse a Complex Machine (20 min)Students disassemble (or study diagrams of): a wind-up toy, a can opener, or a pair of nail clippers. Identify all the simple machines inside. Draw the machine and label each simple machine component.Analyse machine
    Force Analysis (10 min)"When simple machines are combined, the mechanical advantages multiply. A bicycle gear system (pulley-like) lets a cyclist apply small force over a long distance → rear wheel applies larger force over shorter distance → bike goes fast."Learn force multiplication
    Design Challenge (10 min)"Design a simple machine to lift a heavy bucket from a well. You can use: lever, pulley, wheel and axle, inclined plane. Draw your design, label the simple machines, and explain how it makes lifting easier."Design machine

    Success Criteria Check: Identifies at least 4 simple machines in a complex machine and designs a solution using simple machines.

    Lesson 3: Engineering Challenge

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Design Activity)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Engineering Process (10 min)"Engineers follow a process: (1) DEFINE the problem. (2) RESEARCH constraints. (3) DESIGN a solution. (4) BUILD a prototype. (5) TEST and improve."Learn process
    Challenge: Move a Heavy Object (20 min)"Your challenge: design and build a machine to move a 500g weight from one side of the room to the other. You have: cardboard tubes, rubber bands, pencils, string, tape, scissors. Your machine must use at least TWO simple machines. Time limit: 30 minutes."Build machine
    Test Day (15 min)Test all machines. Measure: How far did the weight travel? How much force was applied? Was the machine successful? Record results.Test machines
    Reflection (5 min)"What was challenging? What would you improve? What simple machines did you use?"Reflect

    Differentiation: EAL students build and verbally explain. Gifted students: add a constraint — machine must lift the weight at least 10cm off the ground.

    Success Criteria Check: Designs and builds a machine using at least two simple machines and tests it successfully.

(4) Explain how each force works in your system. (5) Identify one weakness and how to fix it."Understand task

    Lesson 3: Celebration

    DIFFERENTIATION NOTES

    EAL Learners

  • Visual Word Wall: Picture-word pairs for all key vocabulary.
  • Partner Support: Paired during investigations.
  • Sentence Starters: "The force acting on the object is..." / "When I increased force, the object..."

    • Gifted Learners

  • Newton's Third Law Investigation: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. Investigate: when you push on a wall, the wall pushes back on you with equal force. Why doesn't the wall move?
  • Research: Design a mission to Mars — what forces must the spacecraft overcome? How do engineers minimise fuel while countering gravitational forces?
