Unit Content

Living Things Have Features — Biological Sciences Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 1

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum Science v9 — Biological Sciences

Unit Big Idea: All living things have external features that help them live in their environment. Different living things have different features suited to where they live.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Draw an animal and label at least 3 external body features

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Describe how two animals are suited to their environment and explain how their features help them survive

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to identify and describe the external features of living things, explain how these features help animals survive in their environment, compare features of different animals, and investigate how animals use their features for specific needs.

Success Criteria:

  • I can identify and describe external features of animals (body parts, coverings, movement).
  • I can explain how an animal's features help it survive in its environment.
  • I can compare two animals by their external features.
  • I can group animals based on their features (e.g., birds have feathers and wings).
  • I can use science words: features, survive, environment, external, adapted.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: Animal Features

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Draw and Label Animal Features

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Show images of 5 different animals: dog, fish, bird, frog, butterfly. "What is the same about these? What is different?"Observe
    Task (30 min)Cold Task: "Choose one animal to draw. Draw the animal showing its OUTSIDE BODY FEATURES (external features). Label at least 3 parts: eyes, ears, legs, tail, wings, scales, fur, feathers — or whatever body parts your animal has. Year 1: 3 labels. Year 2: 5 labels + 1 sentence about what the animal uses its features for."Draw and label
    Share (5 min)3–4 students share drawings and labelsShare

    Differentiation: EAL students draw and point to labels. NEP students: draw and label 2 features.

    Success Criteria Check: Student draws an animal and labels at least 3 external body features.

    Lesson 2: Amazing Animal Bodies

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Observation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Feature Focus (15 min)"Animals have AMAZING body features. The CHEETAH has long legs for running fast. The OWL has big eyes for seeing at night. The ELEPHANT has big ears for hearing and staying cool. The EAGLE has sharp claws (talons) for catching food. The DOLPHIN has a blowhole for breathing air underwater."Learn
    Feature Scavenger Hunt (15 min)Students receive animal cards or images. For each animal: find and record: NUMBER OF LEGS, TYPE OF COVERING, SPECIAL FEATURES (wings?, fins?, horns?). Record in table.Hunt
    Share Findings (10 min)"What was the most interesting feature you found? Why was it interesting?"Share

    Success Criteria Check: Records external features of at least 6 animals with accurate observations.

    Lesson 3: What Features Do We See?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Vocabulary Building)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Word Wall Introduction (10 min)"Today we start our word wall. EXTERNAL FEATURES = the outside parts of a living thing we can see. SURVIVE = stay alive. ENVIRONMENT = the place where a living thing lives. ADAPTED = having features that help a living thing survive."Introduce vocabulary
    Sort Animals by Features (15 min)Sort animal cards by: number of legs (2, 4, 6, 8, 0), type of covering (fur, scales, feathers, shell), has wings (yes/no), lives in water (yes/no). Record sorts.Sort
    Write (10 min)"An external feature is something we can see on the OUTSIDE of an animal. External features help animals survive in their environment." Write in science books with a drawn example.Write definition
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Name one external feature and explain what it helps an animal do."Written response

    Differentiation: EAL students draw and label. Gifted students: research one unusual animal feature and present to class.

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly uses vocabulary (external features, environment, survive) and sorts animals by at least 2 features.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Body Parts and Jobs

    Lesson 1: Eyes, Ears, Noses, Mouths

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Sensory Features (15 min)"Animals have SENSES — ways to learn about their world. EYES for SEEING. EARS for HEARING. NOSES for SMELLING. MOUTHS/TEETH for EATING. Some animals have amazing senses: a BLOODHOUND can smell footprints 2 days old. An OWL can see in near-darkness. A BUTTERFLY tastes with its FEET."Learn senses
    Feature-Job Matching (15 min)Animals: owl, dog, shark, butterfly, rabbit. Features: sharp forward-facing eyes (predator), ears that rotate (hear prey), nose that smells blood (shark), feet that taste (butterfly), big ears for cooling (rabbit in desert). Match each animal to its special sensory feature.Match
    Observe Ours (5 min)"What do YOUR features let you do? Can you hear as well as a dog? Can you see as well as an owl at night?"Discuss
    Record (5 min)Draw an animal and label its sensory features. Write what each helps the animal do.Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly matches at least 4 animals to their sensory features and explains what each helps the animal do.

    Lesson 2: Legs, Wings, Fins

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Movement Features (15 min)"Animals move to find food, escape danger, and find shelter. LEGS for WALKING/RUNNING/JUMPING. WINGS for FLYING. FINS for SWIMMING. TAILS for BALANCE and SWIMMING. SNAKES slither using their whole body."Learn
    Sort by Movement (15 min)Animal cards: cheetah, eagle, dolphin, kangaroo, snake, penguin, bat, octopus. Sort: WALK/RUN, FLY, SWIM, JUMP/HOP, SLITHER. Record.Sort
    Investigate: How Does Your Animal Move? (10 min)Students choose one animal and research: How does it move? What features help it move? Draw and label movement features.Research
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Name one feature that helps an animal swim. Name one feature that helps an animal fly."Written response

    Success Criteria Check: Sorts at least 6 animals by how they move and correctly identifies movement features.

    Lesson 3: Coverings That Protect

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Why Do Animals Have Different Coverings? (15 min)"FUR/HAIR: keeps mammals warm, protects skin. SCALES: protect fish and reptiles, help them swim smoothly. FEATHERS: keep birds warm AND help them fly, waterproof in some birds. SHELLS (turtle, snail): hard protection from predators. EXOSKELETON (insect, crab): hard outside skeleton for protection and muscle attachment."Learn
    Test coverings (10 min)Pass around: fur piece, fish scale, feather, shell, insect specimen (image or preserved). Feel each. "How does this covering protect the animal?"Feel and learn
    Covering Sort (10 min)Sort animal cards by covering: MAMMAL (fur/hair), BIRD (feathers), FISH (scales), REPTILE (dry scales), AMPHIBIAN (moist skin), INSECT (exoskeleton).Sort
    Draw (5 min)Draw an animal with its covering. Label the covering and write what it protects from.Draw

    Differentiation: EAL students draw coverings. Gifted students: research why some animals have bright colours (warning colours — poison dart frogs are brightly coloured to warn predators they are poisonous).

    Success Criteria Check: Identifies coverings for at least 5 animal groups and explains the protective function of each.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLORE: Animal Groups

    Lesson 1: Mammals and Birds

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Mammals (15 min)"MAMMALS are animals that: have FUR or HAIR, are born alive (not from eggs — mostly), drink MILK from their mothers when babies, are WARM-BLOODED (stay the same temperature). Examples: dogs, cats, whales, bats, horses, humans." Mammary glands (make milk) → Mammals.Learn mammals
    Birds (15 min)"BIRDS are animals that: have FEATHERS and WINGS, lay EGGS with hard shells, are WARM-BLOODED, most can FLY (some can't — penguins, ostriches). Beak shape tells you what birds eat: sharp beak = meat eater, wide flat beak = seed eater, long thin beak = nectar eater."Learn birds
    Compare Mammals and Birds (10 min)Venn diagram: MAMMAL ←→ BIRD. Same: warm-blooded, have backbones, breathe air. Different: mammals have fur/hair, birds have feathers. Mammals drink milk, birds don't. Birds lay eggs, most mammals don't.Compare
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Name 3 things that make a mammal a mammal."Written response

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly identifies at least 5 mammals and 5 birds and explains the key features of each group.

    Lesson 2: Fish and Reptiles

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Fish (15 min)"FISH: have SCALES, breathe through GILLS (take oxygen from water), most lay eggs in water, are COLD-BLOODED (temperature changes with water), have FINS for swimming, live in water their whole life. Examples: goldfish, sharks, tuna, clownfish."Learn fish
    Reptiles (15 min)"REPTILES: have DRY SCALES (different from fish scales — they protect from drying out), breathe air with LUNGS (cannot breathe underwater), most lay eggs on land, are COLD-BLOODED (bask in sun to warm up), have LEGS (except snakes). Examples: snakes, lizards, turtles, crocodiles."Learn reptiles
    Compare Fish and Reptiles (10 min)Fish vs Reptiles Venn. Same: cold-blooded, have scales, lay eggs (mostly). Different: fish have gills (water breathing), reptiles have lungs (air breathing). Fish live in water, reptiles mostly on land. Fish have fins, reptiles have legs (mostly).Compare

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly identifies the key differences between fish (gills, fins, scales) and reptiles (lungs, dry scales, land-based).

    Lesson 3: Insects and Amphibians

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Insects (15 min)"INSECTS: have 6 LEGS (3 pairs), most have 2 or 4 WINGS, body in 3 parts (head, thorax, abdomen), OUTSIDE SKELETON (exoskeleton) that protects them, many have compound eyes (made of thousands of tiny lenses). Examples: ants, bees, butterflies, beetles, flies. The BIGGEST group of animals on Earth!"Learn insects
    Amphibians (15 min)"AMPHIBIANS: live in WATER and on LAND (amphi = both), have MOIST/SLIMY SKIN (breathes through skin!), lay eggs in water (spawn), undergo METAMORPHOSIS (tadpole → froglet → frog). COLD-BLOODED. Examples: frogs, toads, salamanders, newts."Learn amphibians
    Comparison Sort (10 min)Animal cards from all four groups: mammal, bird, fish, reptile, insect, amphibian. Sort into groups. Record at least 2 features for each group.Sort

    Differentiation: EAL students sort with teacher support. Gifted students: research metamorphosis in detail (complete vs incomplete metamorphosis).

    Success Criteria Check: Sorts animals into all 6 groups (mammal, bird, fish, reptile, insect, amphibian) and identifies at least 2 key features for each group.

    WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Features Suit Environments

    Lesson 1: Where Do Animals Live?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Environments (10 min)"An ENVIRONMENT is the natural home of a living thing. OCEAN: saltwater, deep, cold at bottom. FOREST: trees, lots of plants, sheltered. DESERT: hot days, cold nights, very little water. GRASSLAND: open plains, lots of grass, few trees. POLAR: ice and snow, very cold. RAINFOREST: very wet, very hot, many different species."Learn environments
    Match Animal to Environment (15 min)Animal-environment cards: polar bear → Arctic/Antarctic. Camel → desert. Dolphin → ocean. Koala → Australian eucalyptus forest. Flamingo → lake/wetland. Eagle → mountains/open sky. For each: WHY does this animal live HERE? What features help it?Match
    Write (10 min)"Animals live in environments where their features help them survive. A polar bear has white fur (camouflage in snow) and a thick layer of fat (warmth). These features help it survive in the cold Arctic."Write
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Name one feature that helps an animal survive in its environment."Written response

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly matches at least 5 animals to their environment and explains how at least 3 animals' features suit their environment.

    Lesson 2: Forest and Ocean Animals

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Comparison)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Forest Animals (15 min)"FOREST environments: lots of trees, plants, shade, moisture. Animals: monkeys swing through trees (prehensile tails, arms for grabbing), owls hunt at night (big eyes for low light), deer have camouflage spots, frogs have bright colours to warn predators (some are poisonous). TREES provide FOOD and SHELTER."Learn forest
    Ocean Animals (15 min)"OCEAN environments: saltwater, currents, different depths. Animals: sharks have streamlined bodies (swim fast), gills (breathe underwater), colouration (dark on top, light underneath = camouflage). Dolphins have blowholes (breathe air while swimming), echolocation (find prey using sound). Sea turtles have flippers for swimming, must surface to breathe air."Learn ocean
    Compare Forest vs Ocean (10 min)Venn diagram: FOREST ANIMALS ←→ OCEAN ANIMALS. Same: need food, water (in their form), reproduce. Different: one lives in trees/land, one in water. Different breathing. Different movement (fly/climb vs swim).Compare

    Success Criteria Check: Compares forest and ocean animals, identifying at least 2 features suited to each environment.

    Lesson 3: Desert and Australian Animals

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Desert Animals (15 min)"DESERT environments: very hot in day, very cold at night, very little water. Animals: CAMEL — fat stored in hump (energy), thick eyelids protect from sand, can go days without water. KANGAROO RAT — nocturnal (active at night when cool), produces very concentrated urine to save water. SNAKE — slithers to escape heat, sheds skin. FENNEC FOX — huge ears release body heat."Learn desert
    Australian Animals (15 min)"Australia has unique animals found nowhere else: KOALA — pouch for baby, strong sense of smell to find eucalyptus leaves, no tail (sit on branches). PLATYPUS — egg-laying mammal (monotreme), duckbill, electroreception to find prey underwater. EMU — large bird that can't fly, runs fast, drinks very little water. CROCODILE — ambush predator, lives in rivers, can go months without eating."Learn Australian
    Australian Environment Match (10 min)Animals: koala, platypus, emu, crocodile, kangaroo, goanna. Match to Australian environment: outback, river, forest, desert. For each: 1 feature that helps it survive in Australia.Match

    Differentiation: EAL students match with teacher support. Gifted students: research 2 more unique Australian animals (wombat, echidna, cassowary) and their adaptations.

    Success Criteria Check: Identifies features of at least 5 Australian animals and explains how each feature helps survival.

    WEEK 5 — ELABORATE: How Animals Use Features

    Lesson 1: Finding Food

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Food-Finding Features (15 min)"Animals have features to FIND and EAT FOOD. TEETH: sharp fangs (carnivores tear meat), flat molars (herbivores grind plants). BEAKS: sharp hooked (eagle tears meat), long thin (hummingbird drinks nectar), wide flat (duck filters water/plants). CLAWS/TALONS: grab and tear prey. Long sticky tongue (frog catches flies)."Learn
    Beak Investigation (15 min)Show images or replicas of bird beaks: hooked (hawk), cone (seed eater), long thin (nectar feeder), wide flat (duck). Match beak to food type: meat, seeds, nectar, water plants. "The shape of the beak tells you what the bird eats!"Investigate beaks
    Design a Beak (10 min)"Design a bird beak for eating MEAT / SEEDS / FRUIT. Draw the bird and its beak. Label the beak shape and explain why this shape is good for this food."Design
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Why does a duck have a wide flat beak?"Written response

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly matches at least 4 beak types to food sources and explains the relationship between beak shape and diet.

    Lesson 2: Staying Safe

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Defence Features (15 min)"Animals have features to STAY SAFE from predators. CAMOUFLAGE: blend into environment (stick insect looks like a twig, chameleon changes colour to match surroundings). SHELLS/ARMOUR: hard covering (turtle pulls into shell, armadillo rolls into ball). HORNS/SPINES: sharp weapons (porcupine has quills, hedgehog has spines). WARNING COLOURS: bright colours warn predators of poison (poison dart frog). SPEED: run away fast (gazelle from cheetah)."Learn defences
    Camouflage Hunt (10 min)Hide toy animals in the classroom/garden. Some camouflaged, some bright. Students find camouflaged animals. "Why was this hard to find? What colour/shape helped it hide?"Hunt
    Sort Defences (10 min)Animal cards: chameleon, turtle, porcupine, skunk, octopus, monarch butterfly. Sort into: CAMOUFLAGE, ARMOUR, WARNING, SPINES/QUILLS, SPEED.Sort
    Draw (5 min)Draw an animal using camouflage. Where is it hiding?Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly categorises at least 5 animals' defence features.

    Lesson 3: Moving Around

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Movement Investigation (15 min)"How animals move depends on WHERE they live and WHY they need to move. OCEAN: fish use fins and body undulation, dolphins leap out of water (porpoising) to save energy. LAND: legs for running, jumping, walking. TREES: monkeys swing (prehensile tail), squirrels leap, woodpeckers climb up tree trunks. SKY: wings for flying, gliding (flying squirrel has skin membrane between legs)."Learn
    Movement Sort (15 min)Animal cards: penguin, kangaroo, flying fox, salmon, monkey, cheetah, snake. Sort: SWIM, RUN, HOP, FLY, GLIDE, CLIMB, SLITHER. For each: what features help this movement?Sort
    Write (10 min)"Animals move in different ways. The feature they use depends on where they live and what they need to do." Write and illustrate one example.Write

    Differentiation: EAL students draw movement. Gifted students: research how the cheetah's spine works like a spring to make it the fastest land animal.

    Success Criteria Check: Sorts at least 6 animals by movement type and identifies features that enable each type of movement.

    WEEK 6 — ELABORATE: Plants Have Features Too

    Lesson 1: Plant Features

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Plant External Features (15 min)"Plants have EXTERNAL FEATURES too! ROOTS: anchor plant, absorb water from soil. STEM/TRUNK: holds plant up, carries water from roots to leaves. LEAVES: catch sunlight to make food (photosynthesis). FLOWERS: make seeds for new plants. SEEDS: baby plants inside a protective coat. FRUIT: protects seeds and helps spread them."Learn
    Plant Part Investigation (10 min)Bring in real plants or use garden plants. Students observe and identify: roots (if visible), stem, leaves, flowers (if present). Draw and label the plant.Investigate
    Compare Two Plants (10 min)Compare: grass (narrow leaves, fibrous roots, grows low) vs dandelion (broad leaves, taproot, grows tall). Same: both have roots, stems, leaves. Different: leaf shape, root type, height.Compare
    Write (5 min)"Plants have external features too: roots, stems, leaves, flowers, seeds."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Identifies at least 4 external plant features (roots, stem, leaves, flowers/seeds) and explains what each does.

    Lesson 2: How Plants Use Their Features

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    How Plants Use Features for Survival (15 min)"ROOTS: absorb water and anchor plant. Some roots store food (carrot = taproot with stored energy). STEM: carries water from roots UP to leaves. Some stems store water (cactus). LEAVES: make food using sunlight (photosynthesis). Leaf shape helps in different environments: cactus spines (reduce water loss), broad rainforest leaves (catch maximum light). FLOWERS: attract pollinators (bees, birds) with colour and nectar. Seeds inside fruit are spread by animals eating fruit."Learn
    Plant Adaptation Sort (15 min)Plants: cactus, water lily, eucalypt, sunflower. Features: spines instead of leaves (cactus), air spaces in stem (water lily for floating), waxy leaves (eucalypt — reduces water loss), large flower (sunflower — attracts many pollinators).Sort
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Name one way a plant uses its features to survive."Written response

    Success Criteria Check: Identifies at least 3 plant features and explains how each helps the plant survive.

    Lesson 3: Comparing Plants

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Comparison)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Compare Plants by Features (15 min)Compare two plants: succulent (cactus) vs rainforest plant. Succulent: thick fleshy stem (stores water), spines (reduce water loss), shallow roots (collect rain quickly). Rainforest: broad thin leaves (catch maximum light in shaded forest floor), shallow widespread roots (compete for space and light).Compare
    Plant Adaptation Research (15 min)Choose a plant from a specific environment: desert (cactus), ocean (kelp/seagrass), Australian bush (wattle/eucalypt), wetland (reed). Research: What features help it survive in its environment?Research
    Present (10 min)3–4 students present their plant researchShare

    Differentiation: EAL students draw plant adaptations. Gifted students: research carnivorous plants (Venus flytrap) — how do their features help them survive in nutrient-poor environments?

    Success Criteria Check: Researches at least one plant adaptation and presents how its features suit its environment.

  • Partner Support: Paired during all activities.

    • Students with NEPs

  • Reduced Sorting: Sort 5 items instead of 10.
  • Oral Option: Verbal responses accepted.
  • Partner Support: Work with supportive partner.
  • Extra Time: Additional time for all tasks.
