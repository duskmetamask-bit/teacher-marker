Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to write a persuasive letter that tells someone what we want, why we want it, and why they should say yes — using reasons, examples, and a respectful tone.

Success Criteria:

  • I can write a letter with a greeting, my argument, and a sign-off.
  • I can give TWO or more good reasons why someone should agree with me.
  • I can include an example or evidence to support my reasons.
  • I can write in a respectful tone — even when persuading.
  • I can convince the reader that my request is reasonable.

    WEEK 1 — INTRODUCTION TO PERSUASION

    Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)"Think about something you really want — maybe a pet, a toy, a special outing, or something to do. Who is the person who can say yes or no to you? Today you will write to them."Think
    Task (30 min)Cold Task: Write a letter to someone who can give you something you want. Tell them what you want and why they should say yes. Year 2: 50–100 words. Format: letter.Independent writing

    Differentiation: Letter template available. Sentence starters for opening and closing.

    Lesson 2: I Wanna Iguana (I Do)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (15 min)Read *I Wanna Iguana* aloud. This book is structured as a series of notes/letters between a child (Alex) and his mother, arguing for an iguana. It's perfect for Year 2 persuasion. Notice: Alex gives REASONS, his mother has COUNTER-ARGUMENTS, Alex RESPONDS to her concerns.Listen and analyze
    Discuss (10 min)"What reasons does Alex give for wanting an iguana?" He says: They don't need much space / He'll feed it himself / It's educational / He already has a cat. "What does his mother say back?" She worries about the mess, the cost, who will care for it. "How does Alex respond?" He addresses her concerns one by one.Analyze argument structure
    Share (10 min)"How is this different from just saying 'I want it'? Alex is having a conversation through letters. He is LISTENING to the other person's concerns and answering them."Note technique

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify the argument structure: claim → reasons → response to counter-argument.

    Lesson 3: What Makes a Good Argument? (We Do)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)What makes Alex's argument effective? 1. He gives REASONS (not just "I want it"). 2. He listens to CONCERNS and answers them. 3. He makes it about the OTHER person too — "it won't be much trouble."Identify techniques
    We Do (15 min)Class builds a checklist of good argument rules: SAY WHAT YOU WANT / GIVE REASONS / LISTEN TO WORRIES / ANSWER WORRIES / BE POLITEBuild checklist
    You Do (10 min)Think: What do YOU want? Who would say yes or no? What might their concerns be? Write notes.Plan

    WEEK 2 — LETTERS AND STRUCTURE

    Lesson 1: Parts of a Letter (I Do)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Show a letter template on the board. Parts: DATE / GREETING (Dear...) / BODY (the argument) / CLOSING (Love, or Yours sincerely) / NAME. Each part has a job.Identify parts
    Mini-Lesson (10 min)Model writing the greeting and opening: "Dear Mum, I know you might be worried about getting a pet, but I have thought about it carefully and I want to explain why I think a cat would be wonderful."Observe letter opening
    We Do (10 min)Together, write the greeting and opening for a class example letterWrite together
    You Do (5 min)Write the greeting and opening for your own letterWrite opening

    Lesson 2: Writing the Opening (We Do)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"The opening tells the reader what you want. It should be clear and direct — like Alex saying 'I wanna iguana.'"Note
    We Do (15 min)Write 3 different openings for a class example. Choose the best one. Discuss why the others weren't as good.Practice openings
    You Do (10 min)Write the opening sentence for your letter: State clearly and respectfully what you want.Write opening

    Lesson 3: The Body of the Letter (I Do → You Do)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"The body of the letter is where you give your REASONS. Each reason should be in its own paragraph. Start with your best reason."Learn structure
    You Do (25 min)Write the body of your letter. Include at least 2 reasons. For each reason: STATE IT + EXPLAIN IT + GIVE AN EXAMPLE.Write body

    WEEK 3 — REASONS AND EVIDENCE

    Lesson 1: Strong Reasons (I Do)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (10 min)Re-read *I Wanna Iguana*. "What are Alex's reasons? Are they STRONG?" A strong reason: Is TRUE (the person can't easily argue with it) / Is SPECIFIC (not vague) / CONNECTS to what the listener cares about (maybe they care about money, or cleanliness, or your learning).Analyze reasons
    Mini-Lesson (10 min)Weak reasons vs strong reasons. WEAK: "Because I want it" / "Because it would be cool." STRONG: "Because it would teach me responsibility" / "Because it is quiet and small and won't take up much space."Compare
    You Do (15 min)Improve your reasons. Replace any weak reasons with stronger ones. Underline your strongest reason.Strengthen

    Lesson 2: Adding Examples (We Do)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"A reason is better with an EXAMPLE. If you say 'I'll look after it,' give an example of HOW." Alex says: "I'll feed it every day and clean its cage on Saturdays." This is a specific example, not just a promise.Understand examples
    We Do (15 min)Class practices: For each reason, add an example. "I'll be responsible for it." → Example: "I already feed the dog every morning without being asked."Practice
    You Do (10 min)Add an example to your strongest reason.Add example

    Lesson 3: Counter-Arguments (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"In I Wanna Iguana, the mother has concerns. Alex ANSWERS them. This is a powerful technique — if you can show you understand the other person's worry, and you have an answer, they are more likely to agree."Note technique
    We Do (15 min)Brainstorm: What might the person you're writing to worry about? Make a list. Then, for each worry, write a response.Counter-argument practice
    You Do (10 min)Add one counter-argument response to your letter: "I know you might be worried about _________, but I will _________."Add response

    WEEK 4 — DRAFTING YOUR LETTER

    Lesson 1: Planning Your Letter (We Do)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Planning

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (5 min)Show the letter planning structure: GREETING → OPENING (what I want) → REASON 1 with example → REASON 2 with example → COUNTER-ARGUMENT RESPONSE → CLOSINGShow structure
    We Do (15 min)Complete a planning sheet for your letter: What are your 2 reasons? What example for each? What worry will you address?Fill planning sheet
    You Do (15 min)Begin drafting — use your planning sheet to guide your writing.Draft

    Lesson 2: Writing the Draft (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Write the full draft of your letter. Use the planning sheet. Check: Have I said what I want? Have I given 2 reasons? Have I responded to a concern? Is it polite?Write full draft
    Share (5 min)2 students share their opening linesShare

    Lesson 3: Checking for Sense (We Do)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Editing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (5 min)Checklist: Does my letter make sense? Can someone read it once and understand exactly what I want?Read checklist
    We Do (15 min)Swap letters with a partner. Partner reads and checks: Is it clear? Are the reasons strong? Is it polite? Write 2 stars and a wish.Peer check
    You Do (15 min)Revise your letter based on feedback.Revise

    WEEK 5 — REVISION AND IMPROVEMENT

    Lesson 1: Self-Check (You Do)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Editing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (5 min)Self-checklist: Letter format? 2+ reasons with examples? Counter-argument addressed? Polite tone? Finger spaces and neat handwriting?Read checklist
    You Do (30 min)Edit your letter. Highlight any changes in different colours. Check all items on the self-checklist.Self-edit

    Lesson 2: Peer Feedback (We Do)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Peer Feedback

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (35 min)Peer conferences: Read your letter to your partner. Partner says: "I was convinced by..." and "I was not fully convinced by..." Give specific suggestions for improvement.Peer conference

    Lesson 3: Revising (You Do)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Revision

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (30 min)Revise your letter based on peer feedback. Make it the best it can be. Add more detail if needed.Revise
    Share (5 min)Share the most improved section of your letterShare

    WEEK 6 — THE FINAL LETTER

    Lesson 1: Good Copy (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Publishing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (40 min)Write or type a clean final copy of your letter. Proper letter format. Neat handwriting. Leave space for decoration if you want.Write final copy

    Lesson 2: Presentation (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Art

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (40 min)Add a border, decoration or illustration to your letter. Make it look like something someone would be proud to send.Decorate

    Lesson 3: Practice Reading (We Do)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Oral

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (35 min)Practice reading your letter out loud to a partner. Does it sound right when spoken? Fix anything that sounds awkward.Practice reading

    WEEK 7 — PRESENTING YOUR CASE

    Lesson 1: Reading Your Letter Out Loud (You Do)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Presentation

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (30 min)Present your letter to a small group (3–4 students). Read clearly and with feeling. Group members say what they found convincing.Present
    Feedback (5 min)After each presentation, discuss: Was the argument convincing? What made it work?Feedback

    Lesson 2: The Big Presentation (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Presentation

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (40 min)Each student presents their letter to the whole class. Teacher (or special guest) plays the role of the person being persuaded. After each, say: "I was convinced because..." or "I still have a concern about..."Present

    Lesson 3: Receiving Feedback (We Do)

    Time: 35 minutes | Type: Feedback

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (35 min)After all presentations, reflect: What made a letter convincing? What didn't work? Create a class list of what we learned about persuasion.Reflect

    WEEK 8 — HOT TASK AND REFLECTION

    Lesson 1: Hot Task (Assessment)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Hook (5 min)"Think about the letter you wrote in Week 1. Think about what you have learned about persuasion. Can you write something more convincing now?"Reflect
    Task (35 min)SAME TASK: Write a letter to someone who can give you something you want. Tell them what you want and why they should say yes. Year 2: 50–100 words. Focus: Clear claim, 2+ reasons with examples, response to counter-argument, respectful tone.Independent writing

    Compare Cold Task → Hot Task using rubric. Note growth in argument structure and persuasiveness.

    Lesson 2: Comparing Our Work (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Analysis

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (20 min)Compare Cold Task vs Hot Task — what improved? Look for: Stronger reasons / Better examples / Counter-argument addressed / More polished toneCompare
    Share (20 min)Students share specific improvementsCelebrate

    Lesson 3: Reflection and Celebration (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Reflection

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Reflection (25 min)Self-assess: What did you do well? What will you work on next time? Rate yourself against success criteria (1–4). Write one new goal for next persuasive writing task.Self-assess
    Celebration (15 min)Read favourite excerpts from letters. Celebrate the class's growth in persuasion. Display all letters.Celebrate

    ADAPTATIONS

    EAL Students

  • Allow home language planning
  • Sentence starters for letter format
  • Visual prompt for greeting and closing
  • Pre-teach: reason, argument, convince, counter-argument
  • Allow oral presentation with teacher scribe for letter
  • Provide counter-argument sentence starters

    • Gifted Students

  • Write to a real audience about a real request
  • Add a third reason and counter-argument response
  • Write from the RECEIVER's perspective — a letter responding to Alex's request
  • Research real examples of children who successfully persuaded adults

    • Students with Additional Needs

  • Provide sentence starters for each paragraph
  • Reduce to one strong reason + counter-response
  • Allow typed letter if handwriting is difficult
  • One-on-one support during writing time
  • Extra time for all tasks
  • Allow presentation to teacher only

    • ASSESSMENT NOTES

    Formative Assessment (Ongoing)

  • Reason lists with examples
  • Draft letters
  • Peer feedback sessions
  • Self-checklists
  • Teacher conferences (Weeks 3, 5, 7)

    • Summative Assessment

  • Cold Task → Hot Task comparison (same rubric, measure growth)
  • Final letter assessed with rubric
  • Presentation observation

    • Tracking Progress

    Use the comparison template to document:

  • Cold Task score → Hot Task score
  • Reason quality and quantity
  • Counter-argument handling
  • Next writing goal
