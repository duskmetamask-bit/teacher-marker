Grammar and Punctuation Unit — Year 4

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 4

Curriculum Link: AC9E4LY01, AC9E4LY02, AC9E4LY03, AC9E4SP01, AC9E4SP02

Focus: Paragraph structure, cohesion, adverbial and noun phrase manipulation, standard English, advanced punctuation

Mentor Text: Wonder by R.J. Palacio (2012)

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Write with paragraph structure and cohesive devices

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Same task, measure growth

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to construct coherent paragraphs with topic sentences and supporting details, use a range of cohesive devices, manipulate noun and adverbial phrases for precision, and apply advanced punctuation conventions.

Success Criteria:

  • I can write cohesive paragraphs with a clear topic sentence and supporting details.
  • I can use a range of cohesive devices (conjunctions, pronouns, adverbials, lexical chains) to link ideas within and across sentences.
  • I can expand noun phrases using pre-modifiers and post-modifiers.
  • I can construct and deconstruct complex sentences with multiple clauses.
  • I can use dashes, colons, and semi-colons accurately.
  • I can use standard English forms for person and number agreement.

    WEEK 1 — PARAGRAPH STRUCTURE

    Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)"Wonder tells the story of Auggie, a boy with a facial difference who starts school for the first time. Today you will show your grammar skills through writing about Auggie's world."Engage
    Read (20 min)Read Chapters 1–3 of *Wonder* aloud. Notice Palacio's clear paragraph structure — each paragraph has a clear focus.Listen
    Task (25 min)Cold Task (same as Hot Task): Write 3 paragraphs about Auggie. Each paragraph must have a clear topic sentence, 3+ supporting detail sentences, and a concluding sentence. Use cohesive devices to link ideas. Include expanded noun phrases. Year 4: 250–400 words.Independent paragraph writing

    Differentiation: Paragraph template with topic sentence starters. Cohesive device word bank.

    Lesson 2: Topic Sentences (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Mini-Lesson (20 min)Topic sentence = the FIRST sentence of a paragraph that tells the reader what the whole paragraph is about. It should be GENERAL (not too specific) but CLEAR (tell the main idea). "Auggie was nervous about starting school." vs "Auggie was nervous about starting school because he looked different from the other children." The first is a topic sentence. The second is too specific — it tells too much. Model with examples from *Wonder*.Learn
    Activity (20 min)Identify topic sentences in paragraphs from Chapter 4. Is the first sentence general enough? Does it tell the main idea? If not, which sentence is the topic sentence? Do 5 paragraphs.Practice
    You Do (10 min)Write 4 topic sentences about Auggie. Check: Is it general? Does it tell the main idea?Written practice

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify and write effective topic sentences.

    Lesson 3: Supporting Details (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Guided Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Supporting details = the evidence, examples, facts, and descriptions that prove the topic sentence. "Auggie was nervous" (topic) → "His hands were sweaty. His heart was pounding. He kept adjusting his astronaut helmet." (details). Each detail supports the topic sentence.Learn
    We Do (30 min)Build a paragraph together: Topic sentence: "Auggie felt different from the other children." Add details: "He was the only one who wore a space helmet indoors. He had never been to a real school before. Everyone stared at his face." Check: Does each detail support the topic? Do 3 paragraphs together.Group construction
    You Do (10 min)Write one complete paragraph (topic + 3 details + concluding sentence) about Auggie.Independent writing

    WEEK 2 — COHESION

    Lesson 1: Linking Ideas (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)Cohesive devices = words and phrases that LINK ideas within and across paragraphs. Types: CONJUNCTIONS (and, but, because, although, however), ADVERBIALS (firstly, however, in addition, finally), PRONOUN REFERENCE (he, she, it, they — referring back), LEXICAL CHAINS (same word or synonym repeated). Palacio uses all of these to create flow.Learn
    Activity (25 min)Add cohesive devices to a paragraph: "Auggie walked into school. Everyone looked at him." → "Auggie walked into school. IN CONTRAST, everyone looked at him with curiosity and pity." Add: firstly, however, in addition, another, finally, on the other hand. Do 8 additions.Practice
    You Do (10 min)Write a paragraph about Auggie's first day. Use at least 4 cohesive devices. Underline each one and label its type.Written practice

    Lesson 2: Pronoun Reference (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Pronoun reference = making sure pronouns clearly refer to the right noun. "Auggie saw Jack Will. He looked surprised." — Who looked surprised? Auggie or Jack Will? Clear reference: "Auggie saw Jack Will. Auggie looked surprised." Dahl uses "he" to refer to the most recently mentioned character — this can be ambiguous. Show the importance of clear reference.Learn
    We Do (30 min)Find ambiguous pronouns in passages from *Wonder*. Rewrite with clear reference. Create a "clarity check" for each paragraph: Can you identify what every pronoun refers to? Do 6 examples.Guided correction
    You Do (10 min)Write 4 sentences with clear pronoun reference. Check: Can you trace every pronoun back to its noun?Written practice

    Lesson 3: Lexical Chains (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)Write a paragraph about Auggie's family. Use lexical chains: Repeat key words or use synonyms to create cohesion. Topic: Auggie and Via → Use family words (brother, sister, parents), use names and pronouns strategically, use synonyms (school/educational setting, face/appearance). Track your lexical chains. Underline the word families you are using.Paragraph writing

    WEEK 3 — NOUN PHRASES

    Lesson 1: Expanding Nouns (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)Noun phrase = noun + its modifiers. Simple: "the boy." Expanded: "the quiet, thoughtful ten-year-old boy who wore an astronaut helmet." PRE-MODIFIERS (before the noun): adjectives, determiners, nouns as adjectives. POST-MODIFIERS (after the noun): relative clauses, prepositional phrases. Palacio: "the enormously compelling story of a boy who finds friendship."Learn
    Activity (25 min)Expand noun phrases step by step: "Auggie" → "Auggie Pullman" → "ten-year-old Auggie Pullman" → "ten-year-old Auggie Pullman who was born with a facial difference." Each addition adds information. Do 8 expansions.Practice
    You Do (10 min)Expand these nouns: "the teacher" "the school" "the friends" "the dog" — add pre-modifiers and post-modifiers to make them specific.Written practice

    Lesson 2: Pre and Post Modifiers (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Pre-modifiers: adjectives, nouns, determiners. "the brave young boy." Post-modifiers: relative clauses, prepositional phrases. "the boy who never gave up." Mix both for precision: "the brave young boy who never gave up and always smiled."Learn
    We Do (30 min)Build rich noun phrases together: Start with "the lesson." Add pre-modifiers: "the interesting science lesson." Add post-modifiers: "the interesting science lesson about the solar system." Add more: "the interesting year-5 science lesson about the solar system that Mr. Tushman taught." Each addition is justified.Group building
    You Do (10 min)Build 4 rich noun phrases about *Wonder* characters. Label pre-modifiers and post-modifiers.Written practice

    Lesson 3: Precise Nouns (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)Write a character description of Auggie using at least 6 expanded noun phrases. Each noun phrase should be rich and specific. Underline all pre-modifiers and post-modifiers. Check: Does each addition add useful information?Descriptive writing

    WEEK 4 — ADVERBIAL MANIPULATION

    Lesson 1: Fronting Adverbials (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)Fronting adverbials for variety and emphasis: "The bell rang." → "Finally, the bell rang." "Auggie walked in." → "Nervously, Auggie walked in." "He was scared." → "In that moment, he was scared." Fronting creates sentence variety and emphasis. Palacio uses this technique for effect.Learn
    Activity (25 min)Front adverbials in sentences: "The children stared." → "At school, the children stared." "Auggie felt alone." → "Inside, Auggie felt alone." "The year started." → "In September, the year started." Do 10 transformations.Practice
    You Do (10 min)Write 4 sentences with fronted adverbials. Underline the fronted adverbial.Written practice

    Lesson 2: Sentence Structure Variety (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Sentence structures: 1. Subject-verb-object (simple). 2. Fronted adverbial + subject-verb. 3. Subject + adverbial phrase mid-sentence. 4. Compound sentences with conjunctions. Varying these creates rhythm and interest. Palacio varies sentence length and structure throughout *Wonder*.Learn
    We Do (30 min)Rewrite a flat paragraph using varied sentence structures. Start with: "Auggie went to school. He was scared. The children stared at him. He sat at his desk. He felt alone." Rewrite using fronted adverbials, compound sentences, mid-sentence adverbials. Compare the two versions — which is more engaging?Guided revision
    You Do (10 min)Write a varied paragraph about Auggie's first week. Use at least 3 different sentence structures.Written practice

    Lesson 3: Adverbial Chains (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)Write a scene about Auggie meeting his classmates. Use adverbial chains: "Later, on the first day, in the classroom, Auggie sat nervously at his desk." Multiple adverbials at different positions. Use at least 6 adverbial phrases across the paragraph. Vary their positions.Scene writing

    WEEK 5 — COMPLEX SENTENCE CONSTRUCTION

    Lesson 1: Multiple Clauses (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)Multiple subordinate clauses: A sentence can have MORE THAN ONE subordinate clause. "When Auggie arrived at school, everyone stared at him because he looked different." TIME CLAUSE + REASON CLAUSE. "Although Auggie was nervous, he walked into school because he wanted to make friends." CONTRAST CLAUSE + TIME CLAUSE. Dahl and Palacio both use multiple subordinate clauses for complexity.Learn
    Activity (25 min)Build sentences with multiple subordinate clauses: Start: "The children laughed..." Add a time clause: "The children laughed when Auggie walked in." Add a reason clause: "The children laughed when Auggie walked in because they were surprised by his appearance." Do 8 multi-clause constructions.Practice
    You Do (10 min)Write 4 sentences with multiple subordinate clauses. Use different subordinator combinations.Written practice

    Lesson 2: Relative Clause Embedding (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Embedding relative clauses inside sentences: "Auggie was a boy." "Auggie had a special face." → "Auggie, who had a special face, was a brave boy." The relative clause is embedded between commas. Can also be non-restrictive: "Mr. Tushman, who ran the school, was kind." vs restrictive: "The teacher who was new arrived."Learn
    We Do (30 min)Embed relative clauses into sentences: "Auggie walked in." "He was ten years old" → "Auggie, who was ten years old, walked in." "The other children were staring. They had never seen anyone like Auggie." → "The other children, who had never seen anyone like Auggie, were staring." Do 8 embeddings.Guided embedding
    You Do (10 min)Write 4 sentences with embedded relative clauses. Circle the relative clause.Written practice

    Lesson 3: Sentence Openers (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)Write a scene about Auggie's first day. Use varied sentence openers: Subordinate clause opener ("When Auggie arrived..."), adverbial opener ("Nervously, Auggie..."), -ED opening ("Surprised by the noise, Auggie..."), -ING opening ("Seeing all the faces, Auggie felt..."). Use at least 6 different sentence openers.Scene writing

    WEEK 6 — ADVANCED PUNCTUATION

    Lesson 1: Dashes (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)DASH = creates emphasis or pause. TWO TYPES: EN DASH (–) for ranges and connections: "Pages 10–15." "August–September." EM DASH (—) for sudden breaks or emphasis: "Auggie knew one thing—he was different." "Everyone stared—at his face especially." The em dash creates a dramatic pause or explanation. Palacio uses dashes for effect.Learn
    Activity (25 min)Add dashes to sentences for effect: "Auggie had one thought in his mind he would survive this day." → "Auggie had one thought in his mind—he would survive this day." "The children didn't know what to say they just stared." → "The children didn't know what to say—they just stared." Do 10 additions.Practice
    You Do (10 min)Write 4 sentences using dashes for emphasis or dramatic breaks.Written practice

    Lesson 2: Colons (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)COLON = introduces what follows. Use for: LIST (Auggie needed three things: his backpack, his courage, and his smile.), EXPLANATION (Auggie had one fear: being ordinary.), QUOTATION (Auggie's mother said: "You are a wonder."). Dahl uses colons in his lists and explanations.Learn
    We Do (30 min)Add colons to sentences: "Auggie packed three items his helmet, his courage, and his hope." → "Auggie packed three items: his helmet, his courage, and his hope." "He was different in one way he was the only kid with a facial difference." → "He was different in one way: he was the only kid with a facial difference." Do 8 examples.Guided practice
    You Do (10 min)Write 4 sentences using colons for lists, explanations, and quotations.Written practice

    Lesson 3: Semi-Colons (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Mini-Lesson (10 min)SEMI-COLON = joins two related complete sentences. "Auggie was nervous; he was also brave." "The children laughed; Auggie cried." Use when sentences are closely related in meaning but can stand alone. Do NOT use for lists (use colons). Do NOT use when one sentence is just a fragment.Learn
    You Do (40 min)Write 8 sentences using semi-colons correctly. Write 4 sentences where you could use a semi-colon but DON'T (explain why a full stop is better). Check each one.Written practice

    WEEK 7 — APPLYING GRAMMAR

    Lesson 1: Wonder-Style Sentences (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Guided Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (50 min)Write Palacio-style sentences about Auggie. Include: paragraph structure with topic sentence, cohesive devices, expanded noun phrases, fronted adverbials, multiple subordinate clauses, embedded relative clauses, dashes, colons, semi-colons. Check each sentence with a partner.Integrated writing

    Lesson 2: Story Writing (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)Write a scene from Auggie's perspective about his first day. At least 3 paragraphs. Include all grammar skills: cohesive devices, expanded noun phrases, varied sentence structures, advanced punctuation.Full scene writing

    Lesson 3: Editing (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Guided Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (25 min)Editing checklist: Paragraph structure clear? Cohesive devices link ideas? Noun phrases expanded? Sentence variety achieved? Dashes/colons/semi-colons correct? Swap and mark.Peer editing
    You Do (25 min)Edit your scene using the checklist. Rewrite the best sentence.Self-editing
