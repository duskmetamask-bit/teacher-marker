Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to write an information report about a person who solved a problem through innovation — using research skills, structured sections, and precise factual language.

Success Criteria:

  • I can research and gather facts about an inventor and their invention.
  • I can structure an information report with: Introduction, Background, The Innovation, Impact, Conclusion.
  • I can write with factual, precise language — no opinions, just facts.
  • I can include diagrams, timelines, or data to support my report.
  • I can cite sources and show my research process.
  • I can write an engaging introduction that hooks the reader.

    WEEK 1 — INTRODUCTION TO THE BOY WHO HARNESSED THE WIND

    Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)"William Kamkwamba was a 14-year-old boy in Malawi, Africa when a drought destroyed his village's crops. Most people gave up. But William went to the library, read about windmills, and built one that brought electricity and water to his village. He solved a problem. Today YOU will write about someone who solved a problem through innovation."Think
    Task (40 min)Cold Task: Write an information report about an inventor or innovator who solved a problem. Year 5: 300–450 words. Focus: Can you gather facts and write a structured report?Independent writing

    Differentiation: Inventor list available for students who need a starting point. Sentence starters.

    Lesson 2: William's Story (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (20 min)Read *The Boy Who Harnessed the Wind* — selected chapters (Introduction, the drought, the library, building the windmill). Kamkwamba's voice is vivid, personal, and inspiring. His language is simple but the story is powerful. Note how he structures his experience as a problem-solution narrative.Analyze
    Discuss (15 min)"What problem did William face? What did he do about it? What happened because of his invention?" Problem → Research → Innovation → Impact. "What makes his story powerful as an informative text?" He provides facts (the drought, the details of the windmill) but also emotional truth.Analyze structure
    Share (5 min)"Who is another innovator or inventor who solved a problem?"Share

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify the problem-solution-impact structure in Kamkwamba's story.

    Lesson 3: What Is an Information Report? (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"An INFORMATION REPORT teaches the reader facts about a topic. Unlike a story, it does not have a plot. It has SECTIONS that cover different aspects of the topic."Learn
    We Do (20 min)Examine the structure of Kamkwamba's story as an information report: INTRODUCTION (who is this person?) / BACKGROUND (the problem) / THE INNOVATION (the solution) / IMPACT (what changed) / CONCLUSION (why does it matter). Make an anchor chart.Analyze structure
    You Do (10 min)Think: Who will YOU write about? Write their name and the problem they solved.Select topic

    WEEK 2 — RESEARCH SKILLS

    Lesson 1: Finding Information (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"To write a great information report, you need to RESEARCH. Sources: Books, articles, websites, interviews, documentaries. Each source has strengths and weaknesses."Learn sources
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)What to research for your inventor: Who are they? When and where did they live? What problem did they face? What did they do? How does the invention work? What was the impact? Is it still used today?Note research areas
    You Do (15 min)Begin research: Find basic facts about your inventor. Record in a research organiser. Note your sources.Begin research

    Lesson 2: Evaluating Sources (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"Not all sources are equal. RELIABLE sources: Books from publishers / Academic articles / Trusted websites (museums, universities) / Expert authors. UNRELIABLE: Random websites, social media posts, unverified claims."Learn reliability
    We Do (20 min)Evaluate 3 sources together: Is this reliable? Why or why not? Check: Who wrote it? When was it written? Is it peer-reviewed or editor-reviewed? Is the information supported by evidence?Evaluate sources
    You Do (10 min)Check your own sources. Are they reliable? Mark each source: Reliable / Uncertain / Not reliable. Replace any unreliable sources.Check sources

    Lesson 3: Gathering Your Research (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Complete your research organiser: BACKGROUND (who, where, when) / THE PROBLEM (what was the challenge?) / THE SOLUTION (how did they solve it?) / HOW IT WORKS (what did the invention do?) / IMPACT (how did it change things?) / SOURCES (at least 3).Complete research
    Share (5 min)Share one interesting fact from your researchShare

    WEEK 3 — STRUCTURE OF AN INFORMATION REPORT

    Lesson 1: The Report Architecture (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)The INFORMATION REPORT sections: 1. INTRODUCTION — Who is this person and why are they important? (Hook the reader). 2. BACKGROUND — What was the context? The problem? The world they lived in? 3. THE INNOVATION — What did they do? How does it work? This is the HEART of the report. 4. IMPACT — What changed? How many people were affected? Is it still used? 5. CONCLUSION — Why does this matter? What can we learn?Note structure
    You Do (25 min)Plan your report: Write a heading for each of the 5 sections. Under each heading, write 2–3 bullet points of what you will include.Plan sections

    Lesson 2: Writing the Introduction (I Do → You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"The introduction is the FIRST impression. It must hook the reader immediately. Options: Start with a surprising fact / Start with a question / Start with a powerful quote / Start with the inventor's most impressive achievement."Note options
    You Do (30 min)Write the INTRODUCTION for your report. Try 2 openings. Choose the one that hooks the reader most effectively.Write introduction

    Lesson 3: Background Section (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Write the BACKGROUND section: When and where did your inventor live? What was the world like? What problem needed solving? Make the reader understand WHY this invention mattered.Write background
    Share (5 min)Share your best background sentenceShare

    WEEK 4 — THE INNOVATION

    Lesson 1: How the Invention Works (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"This section explains HOW the invention works — without jargon. Explain it so a 10-year-old could understand it." Show: Kamkwamba's windmill description — he explains gears, blades, electrical wires in simple, clear language. This is technical writing at its best.Note
    You Do (25 min)Write: How does your inventor's solution work? Explain it step by step. Use simple language. Add a diagram if it helps.Write innovation section

    Lesson 2: Writing the Innovation Section (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Write the full INNOVATION section: The steps of what they did, the process they followed, the key features of the invention. Use technical terms sparingly and explain them.Write section
    Share (5 min)Share the clearest explanation from your sectionShare

    Lesson 3: Diagrams and Data (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Art + Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (15 min)"Diagrams and data support your writing. A simple diagram of the invention helps readers understand. Data — numbers, statistics, measurements — makes the innovation concrete."Note
    You Do (25 min)Create a simple labelled diagram of your invention. Write a caption that adds one key piece of information from your innovation section.Create diagram

    WEEK 5 — IMPACT AND EVIDENCE

    Lesson 1: What Changed? (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (10 min)What changed because of Kamkwamba's windmill? The village got electricity. Water was pumped. Children could study at night. The story spread — now millions know his story. This is IMPACT.Analyze impact
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)Types of impact evidence: QUANTITATIVE (numbers — "the windmill generated 12 volts of electricity, enough to power 4 lights") / QUALITATIVE (stories of change — "families could now read at night") / SCALE (how many people affected — "the village of 700 people all benefited") / DURABILITY (is it still working? — "the original windmill still runs today").Note types
    You Do (15 min)Research: What is the impact of your inventor's work? Find quantitative data (numbers), qualitative evidence (stories), scale information (how many affected), and durability (is it still used?). Add to research organiser.Research impact

    Lesson 2: Writing the Impact Section (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Write the IMPACT section: How did this invention change things? Use your data and evidence. Mix numbers with stories. Make the reader understand the real-world effect.Write impact
    Share (5 min)Share your strongest impact factShare

    Lesson 3: Statistics and Evidence (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (20 min)As a class, examine how Kamkwamba presents statistics in the book — as narrative, not just numbers. "When the drought came, families who had survived for generations were destroyed" — this is impact described, not just a statistic. Practice: Rewrite a statistic as a narrative description.Practice
    You Do (20 min)Take one of your statistics and rewrite it as narrative impact. Add it to your report.Rewrite statistics

    WEEK 6 — WRITING AND DRAFTING

    Lesson 1: Drafting the Full Report (You Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (40 min)Write the full draft: Introduction → Background → Innovation → Impact → Conclusion. Use your research and planning. Aim for 400–500 words. Follow the structure precisely.Write full draft
    Share (5 min)Share your introduction — what makes it hook the reader?Share

    Lesson 2: Peer Feedback (We Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Peer Feedback

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (45 min)Swap reports with a partner. Read each other's work. Feedback: Is the structure clear (5 sections)? Is the innovation section detailed enough to understand? Is the impact backed by evidence? Are sources cited? Write 2 stars and 1 wish.Peer feedback

    Lesson 3: Revising (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Revision

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Revise based on peer and teacher feedback. Strengthen: Introduction hook, innovation explanation, impact evidence, conclusion connection.Revise
    Share (5 min)Share the most improved section of your reportShare

    WEEK 7 — EDITING AND PUBLISHING

    Lesson 1: Self-Editing (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Editing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (5 min)Checklist: All 5 sections present? Facts are accurate? Sources cited? No opinions — only facts? Punctuation correct? Paragraphs clear?Read checklist
    You Do (35 min)Edit your report. Check facts against sources. Ensure no opinions (no "I think," no "was amazing," no "was the best" — only facts).Edit

    Lesson 2: Final Copy (You Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Publishing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (45 min)Write or type the final copy. Double line spacing. Clear section headings. Include your diagram with labels and caption. List your sources at the end (at least 3).Create final copy

    Lesson 3: Visual Elements (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Art

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (40 min)Add final visual elements: The invention diagram, any additional images, a timeline of the inventor's key moments. Make it visually professional — like a published information book.Add visuals

    WEEK 8 — HOT TASK AND REFLECTION

    Lesson 1: Hot Task (Assessment)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Hook (5 min)"Think about the report you wrote in Week 1. Think about what you learned about research, structure, factual language, and impact evidence. You are now a skilled information report writer."Reflect
    Task (45 min)SAME TASK: Write an information report about an inventor who solved a problem. Year 5: 300–450 words. Focus: 5-section structure, factual language, innovation explanation, impact evidence, sources.Independent writing

    Compare Cold Task → Hot Task using rubric. Note growth in research skills and report structure.

    Lesson 2: Comparing Our Work (We Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Analysis

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (20 min)Compare Week 1 and Week 8 reports. Look for: Structure improvement / Evidence quality / Innovation explanation / Impact writing / Source useCompare
    Share (25 min)Students share specific improvementsCelebrate

    Lesson 3: Celebration (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Celebration

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Celebration (40 min)Read excerpts from favourite reports. Display all reports. Discuss: What makes an information report powerful? What did this unit teach us about innovators and problem-solving?Celebrate

    ADAPTATIONS

    EAL Students

  • Pre-teach technical vocabulary for inventions
  • Provide research organiser with sentence starters
  • Allow home language initial research
  • Sentence starters for each section
  • One-on-one research support

    • Gifted Students

  • Compare two inventors who solved the same type of problem differently
  • Write about a contemporary innovator
  • Create a multimedia presentation (poster + written report)
  • Research the science behind the invention in more depth

    • Students with Additional Needs

  • Provide pre-researched fact sheets
  • Reduce to 3 sections (Introduction, Innovation, Impact)
  • Provide diagram to label and complete
  • Reduce word count to 200–300
  • Extra time
  • One-on-one support

    Formative Assessment (Ongoing)

  • Research organiser
  • Section drafts
  • Peer feedback
  • Teacher conferences (Weeks 3, 5, 7)

    • Summative Assessment

  • Cold Task → Hot Task comparison (same rubric, measure growth)
  • Published report assessed with rubric
