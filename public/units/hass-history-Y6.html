Unit Content

Australia and the Global Community — History Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 6

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum HASS v9 — History (Years 5–6)

Unit Big Idea: Australia's identity and future are shaped by its place in the global community — its relationships with other nations, its role in international events, and its responsibility as a global citizen.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Investigate Australia's role in one global event or relationship, and explain how it has shaped Australia's identity and position in the world

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Investigate how a significant global issue or relationship has shaped Australia and explain Australia's responsibilities as a global citizen

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to investigate Australia's place in the global community — its international relationships, its role in global events, and its responsibilities as a global citizen in an interconnected world.

Success Criteria:

  • I can describe at least 3 key international relationships Australia has.
  • I can explain how global events have shaped Australia (WWI, WWII, Cold War, 9/11).
  • I can describe Australia's response to a global challenge (climate, refugees, human rights).
  • I can explain what it means to be a global citizen and Australia's role in it.
  • I can use at least 2 types of sources to investigate global issues.
  • I can evaluate Australia's responsibilities as a global citizen.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: Australia in the World

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Australia in the World

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Show a world map with Australia's trade routes marked. "Australia is one of the most globally connected countries in the world. We export to 150+ countries, depend on Asia for trade, are allied with the USA, and are part of global agreements on climate, trade, and security. What does this tell us about Australia's place in the world?"Observe
    Introduction (5 min)"In this unit, we will investigate Australia's place in the global community. We will explore our international relationships, our role in global events, and our responsibilities as a global citizen. Understanding this helps us understand where Australia fits in the world and what kind of global citizen we should be."Listen
    Cold Task (30 min)"Choose ONE global event or relationship that has shaped Australia. Describe: What is the event/relationship? When did it happen? How has it shaped Australia? What is Australia's current position or role? Write 6–10 sentences with evidence." Year 6 expectation: structured, evidence-based, multiple perspectives, evaluation.Independent

    Differentiation: Image word bank. Sentence starters: "Australia's role in [event] was..." / "This has shaped Australia's identity by..." / "Today, Australia..." / "This raises questions about..."

    Success Criteria Check: Student identifies at least 1 global event or relationship and explains how it has shaped Australia.

    Lesson 2: Australia's Place in the World

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Hook)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australia's Geographic Position (10 min)"Australia is in the Southern Hemisphere, part of the Asia-Pacific region. We are the world's largest island and smallest continent. We are remote from Europe (traditional 'home') but close to Asia. Our nearest neighbour is Indonesia — just 200km away. This geography has always shaped our global position."Learn
    Australia's Global Role (10 min)"Australia's global role has evolved: 1901 — largely British, loyal to Empire / 1942 — Britain could not defend Australia alone (WWII crisis) / 1951 — ANZUS alliance with USA / 1970s — Whitlam ends White Australia, engages Asia / 1990s–2000s — growing engagement with China / Today — 'middle power' navigating between US and China."Learn
    Middle Power Status (10 min)"Australia is a 'MIDDLE POWER' — not a superpower but not small. We punch above our weight in diplomacy, trade, and international organisations (UN, APEC, G20). We contribute peacekeeping forces, foreign aid, and diplomatic expertise. But we also depend on larger powers for security."Learn
    Map Activity (10 min)Label a world map: USA / China / UK / Japan / Indonesia / India / Pacific Islands / New Zealand. For each: What is Australia's relationship? Write 1 word for each.Label

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify at least 5 key countries in Australia's international relationships.

    Lesson 3: Global Connections

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Concept Introduction)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    How Connected Is Australia? (10 min)"Australia is one of the most globally connected countries in the world: 30% of population born overseas / 300+ languages spoken / major export markets (China, Japan, Korea, USA) / 7.7 million Australians travel overseas annually / international students (education is 4th largest export) / large tourist industry (from Asia, UK, USA)."Learn
    Trade Connections (10 min)"Australia's trade connections: Exports: iron ore, coal, LNG, wheat, beef, wool, gold / Partners: China (largest, ~35% of exports), Japan, Korea, USA, ASEAN / Imports: machinery, electronics, vehicles, petroleum. WA is the largest exporting state — our iron ore and LNG go mostly to China."Learn
    People Connections (10 min)"Australia's people connections: Migration from 150+ countries / 1 million+ tourists annually / 700,000+ international students / 50,000+ refugees and humanitarian entrants since WWII / family reunion visas allowing citizens to bring family from overseas."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: How is Australia connected to the world? Which connection do you think is most important? Why?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 types of global connections Australia has.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: War and Australia's Identity

    Lesson 1: World War II and Australia

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    1942 — The Crisis Year (10 min)"In 1942, Australia faced its greatest threat. Japan had attacked Pearl Harbor (December 1941), was advancing through Asia, and attacked Darwin (19 February 1942) — first bombing of the Australian mainland. Singapore fell (February 1942), and 130,000 British and Australian troops were captured. Australia faced invasion risk."Learn
    Singapore's Fall (10 min)"The fall of Singapore was Australia's greatest military disaster. Australian troops, British troops, and Indian troops were captured by Japan. Most became prisoners of war. Many died building railways and in camps. This shattered the belief that Britain was invincible and could always protect Australia."Learn
    Australia Turns to America (10 min)"In 1942, Australia turned to the United States for help. General Douglas MacArthur (US) became Supreme Commander of Allied Forces in the South West Pacific. Australia's security began to depend more on the USA than Britain. This was a fundamental shift in Australia's international orientation."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: How did WWII change Australia's relationship with Britain and America? What was the significance of Singapore's fall?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 ways WWII changed Australia's international relationships.

    Lesson 2: The Cold War

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Was the Cold War? (10 min)"The Cold War (1947–1991): a period of tension between the WEST (USA and its allies) and the EAST (Soviet Union and its allies). It was called 'cold' because there was no direct large-scale military conflict between the superpowers — but there were proxy wars (Korea, Vietnam) and an arms race."Learn
    Australia's Role in the Cold War (10 min)"Australia was firmly in the Western camp: COMECON (Western alliance, ANZUS 1951) / KOREAN WAR (Australia sent troops, 1950–53) / VIETNAM WAR (Australia sent troops from 1962, peak 1966–71) / SEATO and ANZUS alliances / nuclear testing (Australian territory used for British nuclear tests, 1952–63) / suppression of communism domestically (Menzies' laws, the Communist Party being banned in 1951)."Learn
    The Vietnam War (10 min)"Vietnam was one of Australia's longest wars: 1962–1972 / 521 Australians killed / 2,300 wounded. The war was deeply controversial in Australia: anti-war protests / conscription (mandatory military service, deeply unpopular) / Vietnam veterans were often not recognised or supported. The war divided Australia."Learn
    Why Does It Matter? (10 min)"The Cold War shaped Australia's identity: we were a loyal Western ally / we were involved in conflicts that divided our society / the legacy includes nuclear issues (Uranium, nuclear weapons debate) / our alliance with the USA was cemented."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the Cold War and at least 2 ways it affected Australia.

    Lesson 3: ANZUS and Alliance Politics

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The ANZUS Treaty (10 min)"ANZUS (Australia, New Zealand, United States Security Treaty, 1951): a treaty committing each signatory to come to the aid of the others if attacked. It is the cornerstone of Australia's security alliance with the USA. For Australia, it means the USA is our primary security ally — the country that will help us if we are attacked."Learn
    The Alliance in Practice (15 min)"ANZUS in practice: US bases in Australia (Pine Gap, North West Cape — controversial) / US forces train with Australian forces / Australia supports US military operations (Iraq, Afghanistan, Syria) / the US provides a 'nuclear umbrella' (if Australia were nuclear attacked, the US would retaliate). In return, Australia provides strategic location and military support."Learn
    Is the Alliance Still Valid? (10 min)"The alliance is debated today: SUPPORTERS say we need the US for security against China / CRITICS say we are dragged into US wars that don't serve Australian interests / some argue Australia should be more independent / the question is becoming more urgent as China rises and the US faces internal challenges."Debate
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is the ANZUS alliance? How has it shaped Australia's security? Is it still in Australia's interest today?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students create a visual of the alliance structure. Gifted students: argue for or against the alliance from Australia's perspective.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the ANZUS alliance and evaluate its contemporary relevance.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: Global Events

    Lesson 1: 9/11 and Its Effects

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Happened on 11 September 2001? (10 min)"On 11 September 2001, al-Qaeda terrorists hijacked 4 commercial aircraft in the USA. Two were flown into the World Trade Center (NYC), one into the Pentagon (Washington DC), one crashed in a field (Pennsylvania). About 3,000 people died. It was the deadliest terrorist attack in history."Learn
    The Global Response (10 min)"The world responded with shock, solidarity with the USA, and a 'War on Terror.' The USA invaded Afghanistan (2001) to remove the Taliban government that harboured al-Qaeda. This started a 20-year war. Australia was one of the first countries to join — sending SAS troops and other forces."Learn
    Effects on Australia (10 min)"9/11 affected Australia: we joined the 'War on Terror' / new anti-terrorism laws in Australia / increased surveillance and security / debate about civil liberties vs security / Islamophobia and discrimination against Muslim Australians increased / it changed our relationship with the Muslim world."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What was 9/11? How did it affect Australia? What were the consequences?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe 9/11 and at least 3 of its effects on Australia.

    Lesson 2: The War on Terror

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Was the War on Terror? (10 min)"The 'War on Terror' (2001–present): a global campaign against terrorism, particularly al-Qaeda and later ISIS. It included: military invasions (Afghanistan, Iraq) / drone strikes / surveillance / counter-terrorism laws / freezing of terrorist financing. It has been controversial — critics argue it has created more terrorism than it has eliminated."Learn
    Australia's Involvement (10 min)"Australia's role: SAS and military forces in Afghanistan (2001–2021) / troops in Iraq / domestic counter-terrorism operations / intelligence sharing with allies / controversial rendition (transferring suspected terrorists to other countries for interrogation — potentially torture). Australian forces were involved in some of the most intense fighting in Afghanistan."Learn
    The Iraq War (10 min)"The Iraq War (2003–2011) was deeply controversial even among Australia's allies. The USA invaded based on the (false) claim that Iraq had weapons of mass destruction. Australia joined (2003) against strong domestic opposition. The war destabilised Iraq and contributed to the rise of ISIS."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What was the War on Terror? Was Australia's involvement justified? Why or why not?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the War on Terror and evaluate Australia's involvement.

    Lesson 3: COVID-19 and Global Health

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Pandemic and Australia (10 min)"COVID-19 (2020–2022) was the biggest global health crisis in 100 years. Australia was relatively protected early (border closures, quarantine) but faced multiple waves. We experienced: lockdowns, border closures (domestic and international) / 10,000+ deaths / economic stimulus / vaccine rollout / supply chain disruptions."Learn
    Global Connections Revealed (10 min)"COVID revealed how globally connected we are and how vulnerable that makes us: virus spread globally in days / supply chains disrupted / vaccine nationalism (rich countries hoarded vaccines) / global cooperation (COVAX — global vaccine sharing) and failure / Australia depended on global vaccine supply and global manufacturing."Learn
    What Did COVID Reveal About Australia? (10 min)"COVID revealed: Australia's relative geographic isolation is a strength / our aged care system was underprepared / our economy depends on global supply chains / Australia is a GLOBAL CITIZEN in health and trade / our relationship with China (source of early PPE) was complicated by political tensions."Learn
    Global Health Responsibility (10 min)"COVID raised questions about GLOBAL HEALTH RESPONSIBILITY: Should rich countries share vaccines faster? Should there be global systems to respond faster? Should there be rules about reporting outbreaks? Australia contributed to global vaccine sharing (COVAX) and supported Pacific nations."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe COVID-19's effects on Australia and at least 2 global health responsibility issues.

    WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Human Rights and Australia

    Lesson 1: Australia's Human Rights Journey

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Human Rights in Australia (10 min)"Australia's human rights history is complex. While Australia has been a leader in some areas (women's suffrage, workers' rights), it has also had significant failures: not granting rights to Indigenous peoples, the White Australia Policy, and ignoring refugees."Learn
    Key Human Rights Milestones (15 min)"Australia's human rights milestones: 1902 — federal women's suffrage / 1949 — all Aboriginal Australians finally get the vote (some states earlier) / 1967 — Indigenous Australians counted in census / 1984 — Sex Discrimination Act / 1986 — Human Rights Commission established / 1992 — Disability Discrimination Act / 2008 — Kevin Rudd apologised to the Stolen Generations."Learn
    What Was Missing? (10 min)"Australia does not have a comprehensive Human Rights Act. Our rights are protected in a patchwork way: through anti-discrimination laws / through the constitution (implied rights) / through international treaties Australia has signed. A proposed Human Rights Act has been debated but not implemented."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: How has Australia progressed on human rights? What gaps remain?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 human rights milestones and at least 1 remaining gap.

    Lesson 2: Indigenous Rights and Global Context

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Indigenous Rights in Global Context (10 min)"Australia's Indigenous rights issues are similar to those faced by Indigenous peoples worldwide. The UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP, 2007): sets out the rights of Indigenous peoples globally. Australia initially REFUSED to sign (2009) but eventually endorsed it in 2009 under different circumstances. The journey reflects global Indigenous rights movements."Learn
    Stolen Generations (10 min)"The STOLEN GENERATIONS: from 1910 to 1970, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children were REMOVED from their families by government authorities and placed in institutions or with non-Indigenous families. The intent was to 'assimilate' Indigenous peoples — to make them 'white.' This was done across Australia. It caused immense trauma that continues today."Learn
    The Apology (10 min)"On 13 February 2008, PM Kevin Rudd formally apologised to the Stolen Generations in Parliament. He said: 'We apologise for the laws, policies, and practices of successive Parliaments and governments that have inflicted profound grief, suffering and loss on these our fellow Australians.' This was a significant act of truth-telling."Learn
    What Hasn't Changed? (10 min)"Despite the apology, major issues remain: The median Indigenous age is 23, vs 38 for non-Indigenous / Indigenous incarceration rates are extremely high (Indigenous adults are 13x more likely to be imprisoned) / the child welfare gap / the health gap / the education gap / the life expectancy gap (~8 years lower)."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the Stolen Generations, the apology, and at least 2 ongoing issues.

    Lesson 3: Refugees and Asylum Seekers

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australia's Refugee History (10 min)"Australia has a long history of accepting refugees: post-WWII Displaced Persons (1947–1950s) / Vietnamese refugees (1970s–80s) / Cambodian refugees (1970s–80s) / Lebanese refugees (1970s–80s) / East Timorese refugees (1999) / refugees from the Middle East (2010s–present) / Ukrainian refugees (2022). Australia has been one of the most generous countries per capita in accepting refugees."Learn
    Asylum Seekers and Boat People (10 min)"ASYLUM SEEKERS (people who arrive without a visa, seeking protection) have been controversial: 1976–81 — Vietnamese 'boat people' (initially accepted as refugees, later restrictions) / 2001 — Tampa affair (Australia refused a Norwegian ship carrying asylum seekers, turning them back to sea) / 2012–2014 — 'Turn Back the Boats' policy under Gillard/Abbott / offshore processing (Manus Island, Nauru) — deeply controversial and condemned internationally."Learn
    Offshore Processing (10 min)"OFFSHORE PROCESSING: people who arrive by boat without a visa are sent to Manus Island (Papua New Guinea) or Nauru for processing. They are NOT allowed to settle in Australia even if found to be genuine refugees. This policy (from 2012 under Labor, continued under LNP) has been widely condemned: UN Human Rights Commission / medical bodies / legal community. Many have been in limbo for over a decade."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is Australia's refugee history? What are the issues with asylum seeker policy?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe Australia's refugee history and evaluate the offshore processing policy.

    WEEK 5 — EXPLAIN: Economy and Environment

    Lesson 1: Trade and Australia's Economy

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australia's Trade Position (10 min)"Australia is one of the world's most trade-dependent economies. Exports (especially minerals and energy) account for ~25% of GDP. We are the world's largest exporter of iron ore, second largest coal exporter, fourth largest LNG exporter, largest wool exporter. Our export-driven economy means international trade directly affects Australian prosperity."Learn
    Trade Tensions (15 min)"Australia's trade is increasingly influenced by GEOPOLITICS: China (largest trade partner) — but political tensions led to trade restrictions on barley, wine, beef, coal (2020–21) / USA (free trade agreement, 2020) / India (free trade agreement, 2022) / UK (free trade agreement, 2021) / the challenge of managing both the US alliance and Chinese trade."Learn
    What Is Australia Selling? (10 min)"Australia's major exports by value: Iron ore (~30% of export value, mostly to China) / Coal (thermal and coking, ~15%) / LNG (~10%) / Education (international students, ~8%) / Beef and veal (~5%) / Gold (~5%) / Wheat (~4%). WA dominates: iron ore, LNG, gold, wheat."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What does Australia trade? Who are our main trading partners? What are the challenges?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe Australia's key exports and at least 2 trade relationship challenges.

    Lesson 2: Climate Change and Global Responsibility

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australia's Climate Position (10 min)"Australia is one of the world's highest per-capita emitters of greenhouse gases. We produce about 1.3% of global emissions — small in absolute terms but large per person. At the same time, we are highly exposed to climate impacts: more bushfires, longer fire seasons, coral bleaching, drying in the south-west."Learn
    Climate Agreements (15 min)"Australia's climate policy has been inconsistent: Kyoto Protocol (1997, ratified 2007) / Paris Agreement (2016, committed to 26–28% reduction by 2030) / Net Zero by 2050 (committed 2021) / the Safeguard Mechanism (limiting big emitters) / Renewable Energy Target (42% renewables by 2030). Critics say targets are insufficient to meet 1.5°C."Learn
    The China Factor (10 min)"Australia's climate position is complicated by CHINA: China makes most of the world's solar panels and batteries / China's emissions are rising but they are also the largest investor in renewables / Australia exports coal to China (creating emissions elsewhere) / Australia and China both have interests in acting on climate but have competing political interests."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is Australia's role in global climate change? What are Australia's responsibilities? What should Australia do?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe Australia's emissions position, climate policy, and at least 2 responsibilities.

    Lesson 3: Environmental Action

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Young People and Climate Action (10 min)"Young Australians are leading climate action. School Strikes for Climate (2018–present) / Youth climate activism / Australian youth have filed legal cases against the government (Sharma v. Minister for the Environment, 2021 — lost but established the legal debate) / the voices of young people are shaping climate policy."Learn
    Business Action (10 min)"Australian businesses are taking climate action: BHP, Rio Tinto, Woodside (diversifying into renewables) / Beyond Oil and Gas Alliance (Australia joined in 2022) / hydrogen strategy / many companies setting net-zero targets / but also significant greenwashing (claiming climate action without substance)."Learn
    What Can Australia Do? (10 min)"Australia can contribute to global solutions: accelerate renewables (solar, wind, hydrogen) / protect biodiversity (one of the world's megadiverse countries) / support climate finance for developing nations / lead in green hydrogen / integrate Indigenous knowledge with modern science for land management."Learn
    My Role (10 min)"Write 2–3 specific actions you will take to address climate change. Be specific and realistic."Write

    Differentiation: EAL students create a climate action poster. Gifted students: write a climate policy proposal.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 ways Australia can contribute to global climate solutions and develop a personal action plan.

    WEEK 6 — ELABORATE: Australia's Pacific Neighbourhood

    Lesson 1: The Pacific Islands

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australia's Pacific Neighbourhood (10 min)"Australia's nearest neighbours are Pacific Island nations: Papua New Guinea / Fiji / Solomon Islands / Vanuatu / Samoa / Tonga / Kiribati / Tuvalu / Marshall Islands. These are small nations with big challenges: climate vulnerability, limited resources, complex politics, and cultural diversity."Learn
    Pacific Relations (10 min)"Australia has a complex relationship with Pacific Islands: largest aid donor (~$1.5 billion annually) / major security partner (through the Pacific Islands Forum) / labour mobility scheme (Pacific islanders work in Australia temporarily) / diplomatic presence ( embassies, high commissions) / but also seen as paternalistic, climate-insufficient, and dominant by some Pacific nations."Learn
    Pacific Challenges (10 min)"Pacific Islands face unique challenges: CLIMATE CHANGE (sea level rise, coral bleaching, extreme weather) / HEALTH (non-communicable diseases, limited health infrastructure) / ECONOMIC (limited diversification, dependence on aid and tourism) / POPULATION (some of the world's fastest-growing populations) / GEOPOLITICAL (China's increasing presence in the Pacific)."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is Australia's relationship with Pacific Island nations? What are the challenges Pacific Islands face?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe Australia's relationship with Pacific Island nations and at least 2 challenges they face.

    Lesson 2: Climate Refugees

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Pacific Islands at Risk (10 min)"Pacific Island nations are among the most climate-vulnerable in the world. Some may cease to be habitable within decades: Kiribati (low-lying atolls, freshwater contamination) / Tuvalu (highest point 4.6m, frequent flooding) / Marshall Islands (two atoll chains, sea level rise). The people of these nations may become the world's first climate refugees."Learn
    Australia's Response (10 min)"Australia's response has been INSUFFICIENT in the eyes of Pacific nations: climate policies have been criticised as inadequate to limit warming to 1.5°C / Australia initially refused to accept climate refugees / Australia offered only limited visa pathways for climate-affected Pacific neighbours / Pacific leaders have publicly criticised Australian climate policy."Learn
    Legal Questions (10 min)"There is no international legal framework for CLIMATE REFUGEES. The 1951 Refugee Convention does not cover people fleeing climate change. Some have proposed a new international agreement. Australia's obligations to accept climate refugees from the Pacific are legally and ethically ambiguous."Learn
    What Is Australia Doing? (10 min)"Australia is providing: humanitarian settlement for some Pacific communities / climate finance ($500m annually promised for Pacific climate adaptation) / Pacific support programs / but the core issue — reducing emissions to prevent the need for refugees — is contested."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is a climate refugee? How should Australia respond to Pacific climate refugees?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the situation of Pacific climate refugees and evaluate Australia's response.

    Lesson 3: Australia's Role in the Pacific

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Kind of Neighbour? (10 min)"Australia's role in the Pacific is contested: some Pacific nations see Australia as a GENEROUS neighbour (aid, trade, security) / others see us as INSUFFICIENT (not doing enough on climate, too dominant, 'big brother') / the challenge for Australia is to be a partner, not a boss."Learn
    Security in the Pacific (10 min)"Security issues: China is increasing its presence in the Pacific / Australia's security alliance with the USA creates tensions (some Pacific nations don't want to be caught in US-China rivalry) / there have been coups and instability in some Pacific nations (Solomon Islands, Fiji) / Australia is the primary security partner for most Pacific nations."Learn
    Australia's Opportunity (10 min)"Australia has an opportunity: to be a leader in climate action for the Pacific / to be a genuine partner (not just a donor) / to integrate Indigenous Australian knowledge with Pacific knowledge / to support Pacific nations' own priorities / to be honest about our own history of intervention."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What kind of neighbour should Australia be to Pacific Island nations? What should we do differently?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can evaluate Australia's role in the Pacific and propose at least 2 ways to improve the relationship.

    WEEK 7 — ELABORATE + EVALUATE: Global Citizenship

    Lesson 1: What Is a Global Citizen?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is a Global Citizen? (10 min)"A GLOBAL CITIZEN is someone who: understands they are part of a global community / cares about global issues / acts responsibly in global contexts / respects cultural diversity / understands global connections / advocates for justice globally. Global citizens think beyond their national borders."Learn
    What Does Australia Owe the World? (15 min)"As a wealthy, developed country, Australia has global responsibilities: reduce emissions (one of the highest per-capita emitters) / support global aid and development / accept refugees and migrants / respect human rights / contribute to global security / support global health (wealthy nations should support WHO, vaccine programs) / protect biodiversity (one of the world's megadiverse countries)."Learn
    Australia's Global Responsibilities (10 min)"Australia's specific global responsibilities: LEAD on climate action (can afford to, should do it first) / be a generous aid donor (currently ~0.5% of GNI, below UN target of 0.7%) / be an honest broker in geopolitics (not just follow the USA) / protect the Pacific (our neighbourhood) / be a voice for human rights / support the international rules-based order."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What does it mean to be a global citizen? What are Australia's global responsibilities?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can define global citizenship and describe at least 3 of Australia's global responsibilities.

    Lesson 2: Australia's Global Responsibilities

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Climate Responsibility (10 min)"Australia's climate responsibility is HUGE: we are among the highest per-capita emitters / we export coal and gas (which drive emissions elsewhere) / we are highly exposed to climate impacts / we have the technology and wealth to lead / our Pacific neighbours are most affected. The question is: are we doing enough?"Learn
    Refugee Responsibility (10 min)"Australia's refugee responsibility: we accept ~13,000–20,000 humanitarian entrants per year (recently increased) / we are one of the most generous per capita in some years / BUT our offshore processing of asylum seekers has been condemned internationally / our global refugee responsibility should be based on capacity, not political convenience."Learn
