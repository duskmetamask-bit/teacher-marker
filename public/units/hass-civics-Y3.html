Unit Content

Our Community and Democracy — Civics and Citizenship Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 3

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum HASS v9 — Civics and Citizenship (Years 3–4)

Unit Big Idea: Democracy means more than just voting — it means everyone having a voice in their community. Citizens can participate in democracy at the local level, and every person's participation matters.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Describe how you participate in your community and explain one way you could participate more in local democracy

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Investigate one community issue, explain how citizens can participate in the decision, and propose a specific action you could take

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to understand local democracy, identify how citizens can participate in their community, and recognise that everyone has a voice in democratic processes.

Success Criteria:

  • I can describe what democracy means and why it is important.
  • I can identify at least 3 ways citizens can participate in their community.
  • I can describe the role of local government in my community.
  • I can identify a community issue and explain how citizens can have a voice on it.
  • I can use sources to find out about local democracy.
  • I can propose a specific action a citizen could take to participate in local democracy.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: What Is Democracy?

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Our Community and Democracy

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Show two images: citizens voting in an election / a community meeting. "What do these images have in common? What do they tell us about democracy? What do you think democracy means?"Observe and discuss
    Introduction (5 min)"In this unit, we will learn about DEMOCRACY — what it means, how it works, and how YOU can participate. Democracy is not just about voting — it is about having a voice in your community. Every person matters in a democracy."Listen
    Cold Task (30 min)"Part 1: How do you participate in your community? (e.g., at school, in your neighbourhood, in your family) Part 2: Describe one way you could participate MORE in local democracy. Part 3: Write 3 sentences explaining why it is important for citizens to have a voice in their community." Year 3 expectation: 2–4 sentences, clear examples, reason given.Independent

    Differentiation: Sentence starters: "I participate in my community by..." / "One way I could participate more is..." / "It is important for citizens to have a voice because..."

    Success Criteria Check: Student describes at least 1 way they participate and explains why democratic participation matters.

    Lesson 2: Democracy Around the World

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Hook)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Democracy? (10 min)"DEMOCRACY means 'rule by the people.' In a democracy: citizens have a say in decisions / people can vote for their leaders / everyone is equal under the law / there is freedom of speech and assembly / the majority decides, but minority rights are protected."Learn
    Different Types of Democracy (10 min)"There are different types: DIRECT DEMOCRACY (citizens vote directly on every issue — used in some cantons of Switzerland) / REPRESENTATIVE DEMOCRACY (citizens vote for representatives who make decisions — used in Australia) / MONARCHY (a king or queen rules — some monarchies are also democratic, like Australia) / DICTATORSHIP (one person or group rules without citizen input — NOT democratic)."Learn
    Democracy Around the World (15 min)"Democratic countries: Australia, USA, Canada, UK, Japan, India, Germany — most of the Western world / NOT democratic: North Korea, Cuba, some countries in Africa and the Middle East / Some countries are PARTIALLY democratic (contested elections, limited freedom of speech). About 50 countries are genuinely democratic."Learn
    Why Is Democracy Important? (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: Why is democracy important? What would happen if people didn't have a say in how they were governed?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe what democracy means and name at least 2 types.

    Lesson 3: Our Local Democracy

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Concept Introduction)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Democracy Starts Locally (10 min)"Democracy starts in YOUR community. The council that decides where shops can be built, where bike paths go, and how rates are spent — THAT is democracy. Local democracy is where citizens have the most direct influence."Learn
    Our Local Area — What Council Does (15 min)"Our local council (name the specific council): manages LOCAL ROADS, FOOTPATHS, DRAINS / runs PARKS and PLAYGROUNDS / approves BUILDING APPLICATIONS / collects RUBBISH and RECYCLING / provides COMMUNITY SERVICES / runs LIBRARIES and RECREATION CENTRES. Council decisions affect our daily lives."Learn
    Who Is Our local council? (10 min)"Our council is made up of ELECTED councillors (and a mayor, who may be elected or appointed). They are voted in by local residents. They make decisions about local issues. The council employs a CEO and staff who carry out the decisions."Learn
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What does our local council do? Who makes decisions at our local council?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students draw the local council's responsibilities. Gifted students: find out the name of the mayor and their local councillors.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 responsibilities of local government and identify that councillors are elected.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Citizens and Government

    Lesson 1: What Is a Citizen?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Citizenship? (10 min)"A CITIZEN is a person who belongs to a country. In Australia, citizens have RIGHTS (what they are entitled to) and RESPONSIBILITIES (what they should do). Most people in this room are Australian citizens."Learn
    Rights of Citizens (10 min)"Australian citizens have rights: vote in elections (compulsory) / receive protection from the Australian government abroad / access to healthcare (Medicare) and education / freedom of speech and assembly / access to courts for justice / right to a passport."Learn
    How Do You Become a Citizen? (10 min)"You become an Australian citizen by: BIRTH (born in Australia to citizen parents — usually) / DESCENT (born overseas to an Australian citizen parent, can apply) / GRANT (applying for and being granted citizenship after living here). Children born in Australia to permanent residents can also become citizens."Learn
    What If You Are Not a Citizen? (10 min)"People who are not Australian citizens (permanent residents, visa holders, international students) have fewer rights: they cannot vote / cannot get an Australian passport / may be refused certain government services / may be deported if they break the law. This is one reason people apply for citizenship."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What rights does an Australian citizen have? Why might someone want to become an Australian citizen?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 rights of Australian citizens and explain at least 1 way to become a citizen.

    Lesson 2: Three Levels of Government

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Three Levels of Government (10 min)"Australia has THREE levels of government — each with different responsibilities. LOCAL (councils — roads, parks, planning) / STATE (governments of WA, NSW, etc. — schools, hospitals, police) / FEDERAL (Australian Parliament — defence, immigration, trade, tax)."Learn
    State Government (10 min)"State governments (Western Australian Government in WA): run SCHOOLS and HOSPITALS / maintain MAJOR ROADS / fund PUBLIC TRANSPORT / run POLICE and FIRE services / manage WATER and NATURAL RESOURCES / issue DRIVER'S LICENCES and REGISTER BIZ."Learn
    Federal Government (10 min)"Federal government (Australian Government): manage DEFENCE and FOREIGN AFFAIRS / make laws about IMMIGRATION and CITIZENSHIP / collect TAX and set economic policy / manage HEALTH (Medicare) and SOCIAL SECURITY / regulate TRADE between states and with other countries."Learn
    Which Level Does What? (5 min)"Sort: Which level is responsible for: local roads? / national defence? / schools? / planning approvals? / foreign affairs? / libraries? / hospitals? / rubbish collection?"Sort

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify at least 2 responsibilities of each level of government.

    Lesson 3: Local Government — Your Council

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    How Local Government Is Chosen (10 min)"LOCAL ELECTIONS: local residents vote for councillors (and mayor) / elections happen every 2–4 years (different in each state) / voting is compulsory in some states, voluntary in others / anyone over 18 who lives in the area can vote / councillors must live in the area they represent."Learn
    Your Local Councillors (10 min)"Our local councillors represent different wards (areas). They attend council meetings, vote on council decisions, and listen to community concerns. They are your LOCAL VOICE in government. The mayor chairs meetings and represents the council externally."Learn
    What Decisions Does Council Make? (15 min)"Council makes decisions about: TOWN PLANNING (where buildings can be built, what they can look like) / DEVELOPMENT APPLICATIONS (approving or rejecting new buildings) / RATES (property taxes — how much ratepayers pay) / SERVICES (rubbish, recycling, libraries, community centres) / INFRASTRUCTURE (roads, footpaths, bike paths, lighting)."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: How does local government work? Who are our local councillors? What does our council decide?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students draw the three levels of government. Gifted students: find out when the next local election is and who their current councillors are.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe how local government is elected and at least 3 things local council decides.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: Our Local Government

    Lesson 1: How Does Local Government Work?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Council Meeting (10 min)"Council makes decisions at council MEETINGS — usually open to the public. Councillors discuss issues, debate motions, and VOTE on decisions. Major decisions require a recorded vote. The council employs a CEO (Chief Executive Officer) who runs the administration day-to-day."Learn
    Council Staff (10 min)"Council has many staff: PLANNERS (assess development applications) / ENGINEERS (design roads and infrastructure) / LIBRARIANS / RECREATION STAFF / ADMINISTRATIVE STAFF / ENVIRONMENTAL STAFF. These staff implement the decisions councillors make."Learn
    How Do Decisions Get Made? (10 min)"The DECISION-MAKING PROCESS: Issue is raised (by staff, councillors, or the public) / Officers investigate and report / Councillors discuss at committee meeting / Full council votes at council meeting / Decision is recorded and actioned / If you disagree with a decision, you can APPEAL."Learn
    Source Analysis: Council Agendas (10 min)Show a copy of a council meeting agenda (available on council websites). "This is what a council meeting looks like. What do you notice? Who makes decisions? Who can attend?"Analyse

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the decision-making process at local council and identify that meetings are open to the public.

    Lesson 2: councillors — Your Local Representatives

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Do councillors Do? (10 min)"councillors: REPRESENT their local community / ATTEND council meetings and vote on decisions / LISTEN to community concerns / BRING community issues to council's attention / ADVOCATE for their ward / ENGAGE with local residents through forums, meetings, and correspondence."Learn
    How to Contact councillors (10 min)"You can contact councillors: EMAIL (all councillors have email addresses on the council website) / PHONE / LETTER / attending community meetings / FACEBOOK and social media (some use it). councillors are YOUR representatives — it is their job to listen to you!"Learn
    What Happens When You Contact councillors? (10 min)"When you contact councillors: they receive your message / they may raise it at a council meeting / they may refer it to council officers to investigate / they will often respond to you directly. Your voice DOES matter — many decisions have been changed because citizens spoke up."Learn
    Roleplay (10 min)"Roleplay: Student A is a concerned resident. Student B is a councillor. Resident: 'I am worried about the new development near our school.' Councillor: responds and explains what can be done."Practice

    Success Criteria Check: Can explain at least 2 ways to contact local councillors and describe what happens when they are contacted.

    Lesson 3: Council Decisions and You

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    How Council Decisions Affect You (10 min)"Council decisions affect your daily life: The PARK near your home — council decides whether to upgrade it / The ROAD you walk on — council decides whether to fix it / The BINS you put out — council decides the service level / The LIBRARY you visit — council decides whether to fund it. EVERY decision matters."Learn
    Development Applications (10 min)"One of the most controversial council decisions is DEVELOPMENT APPLICATIONS (DAs). When someone wants to build something new, council decides whether to approve it. Neighbours can OBJECT to developments they think are harmful. This is a key way citizens can participate."Learn
    Your Rights in the Process (10 min)"When a DA is submitted: neighbours are notified / there is a comment period / you can make a SUBMISSION (write why you support or oppose it) / the council must consider submissions before deciding. Your voice can influence the outcome."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: How do council decisions affect your life? How can you participate in council decision-making?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students draw a council decision that affects them. Gifted students: look up a development application on the local council website.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 ways council decisions affect their life and at least 1 way to participate.

    WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Having a Voice

    Lesson 1: Ways to Participate

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Many Ways to Participate (15 min)"Citizens can participate in democracy in many ways: VOTING in elections (compulsory in Australia for federal and state elections, local varies) / ATTENDING community meetings / JOINING community groups / WRITING to councillors or MPs / SIGNING PETITIONS / ATTENDING protests (peaceful assembly) / VOLUNTEERING in the community / RUNNING FOR OFFICE."Learn
    Voting and Elections (10 min)"Elections are the core of democracy. In Australia: FEDERAL elections (every 3 years) / STATE elections (every 4 years in most states) / LOCAL elections (every 2–4 years depending on state). Voting is COMPULSORY for federal and state — if you don't vote without a valid reason, you may be fined."Learn
    Why Voting Matters (10 min)"Voting matters because: your vote is equal to everyone else's / elections determine who makes decisions / if young people don't vote, politicians ignore young people's concerns / every vote counts (even in safe seats, the margin can be affected)."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What are ways to participate in democracy? Why does voting matter?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 4 ways citizens can participate in democracy and explain why participation matters.

    Lesson 2: Writing to Local Council

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    How to Write to Council (10 min)"Writing to council is a powerful tool. Structure: WHO you are / WHAT the issue is / WHY it matters / WHAT you want council to do / CONTACT details. Be polite, clear, and specific. Include your address (shows you are a local resident)."Learn
    Sample Letter (15 min)"Read a sample letter to council: 'Dear Mayor and councillors, I am a local resident at [address]. I am concerned about the proposed closure of the community garden at [location]. This garden provides... I request that council reconsider this decision and explore alternatives... Yours faithfully, [name].'"Read
    Letter-Writing Practice (10 min)"Write a letter to council about ONE issue in your community. Use the structure we learned. You don't need to send it — but practice the skill."Write
    Share (5 min)3–4 students share the issue they wrote about.Share

    Success Criteria Check: Can write a structured letter to council about a community issue.

    Lesson 3: Community Meetings

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Attending Community Meetings (10 min)"Community meetings are where citizens and decision-makers come together. Types: COUNCIL meetings (open to public) / COMMUNITY CONSULTATION (council asks for your input on specific issues) / PUBLIC SPEAKING sessions (you can speak on agenda items) / TOWN HALL meetings (community forums with politicians)."Learn
    How to Participate in Meetings (10 min)"At community meetings: you can LISTEN to what others say / you can RAISE YOUR HAND to speak / you can ASK QUESTIONS / you can MAKE SUBMISSIONS / you can simply ATTEND and show support. Presence matters — it shows decision-makers that people care."Learn
    Online Participation (10 min)"You can also participate ONLINE: council websites often have online feedback forms / social media / email campaigns / online petitions. The digital age has made participation easier."Learn
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: How can you participate in community meetings? What is one issue you might want to speak about at a community meeting?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students plan what they would say at a community meeting. Gifted students: attend a real council meeting or watch one online.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 ways to participate in community meetings and identify at least 1 issue they could speak about.

    WEEK 5 — EXPLAIN: Community Issues

    Lesson 1: Identifying Issues

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is a Community Issue? (10 min)"A COMMUNITY ISSUE is a problem or opportunity that affects people in a local area. Issues can be: INFRASTRUCTURE (roads, footpaths, lighting) / ENVIRONMENT (park upgrades, trees, biodiversity) / SERVICES (libraries, recreation, transport) / SAFETY (traffic, crime, lighting) / SOCIAL (affordable housing, loneliness, youth services)."Learn
    Issues in Our Community (15 min)"What issues do we have in our community? Brainstorm: Too many cars on local streets / Not enough bike lanes / The park needs more shade / The library closes early / Youth have nowhere to go / Footpaths are cracked / Not enough bins. Which of these is most important to YOU?"Brainstorm
    Who Is Affected? (10 min)"Every issue affects different people differently: An issue might affect ELDERLY people most / CHILDREN / PEOPLE WITH DISABILITIES / LOW-INCOME FAMILIES / YOUNG PEOPLE. Understanding WHO is affected helps us understand WHY it matters."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is one community issue? Who is affected? Why is it important?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify at least 1 community issue, describe who it affects, and explain why it matters.

    Lesson 2: Local Council and Issues

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Council's Role in Issues (10 min)"Local council can address SOME issues (roads, parks, planning) but not others (state government handles schools, hospitals, police; federal government handles taxes, defence). Knowing WHO is responsible is the first step in getting change."Learn
    Case Study: Park Upgrade (10 min)"Example: A community wants to upgrade a local park. The process: COMMUNITY identifies the issue → COUNCIL assesses feasibility → COUNCIL includes in budget → COUNCIL votes on budget → IF APPROVED, council officers plan and execute upgrade → COMMUNITY can provide feedback throughout. It takes time and citizen pressure."Learn
    Getting Council to Act (10 min)"How to get council to address an issue: WRITE to councillors / ATTEND council meetings and speak / PETITION with signatures / GET MEDIA coverage / BUILD community support / SHOW EVIDENCE of the problem (photos, data). Politicians respond to citizens who speak up."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: How can citizens get local council to address a community issue? Give an example."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 ways to get local council to address a community issue.

    Lesson 3: Taking Action Locally

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Action Planning (10 min)"Taking action locally: IDENTIFY the issue / RESEARCH the facts / GATHER evidence / BUILD support (find others who agree) / COMMUNICATE (write, attend, share) / PERSIST (change takes time) / CELEBRATE small wins."Learn
    Community Groups (10 min)"Community groups are powerful: RESIDENTS' ASSOCIATIONS (represent local residents) / ENVIRONMENTAL groups (Friends of [local park], Bike Bunbury) / PARENT groups / SPORTING clubs / FAITH groups. These groups have a collective voice that is stronger than individuals."Learn
    Successful Community Campaigns (10 min)"Examples: Save the [local tree] campaign / Bike path advocacy / No to development on [site] / Upgrade [park name] / Save [library hours]. Many local campaigns succeed — it takes community organisation and persistence."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What could you do to take action on one community issue? What would be the first step?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students plan an action visually. Gifted students: identify a specific campaign being run in their area and research how it is going.

    Success Criteria Check: Can develop a plan for taking action on a community issue with at least 3 steps.

    WEEK 6 — ELABORATE: Rights and Responsibilities

    Lesson 1: What Are Rights?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Are Rights? (10 min)"RIGHTS are entitlements — things you are OWED just because you are a person and/or a citizen. Rights are protected by law and the government must respect them. Rights include: right to vote / right to free speech / right to legal representation / right to education / right to healthcare / freedom from discrimination."Learn
    Rights in Australia (10 min)"Australia has a bill of rights? Actually NO — Australia does NOT have a comprehensive Bill of Rights. Our rights are protected in a MIXED way: through anti-discrimination laws ( Racial Discrimination Act, Sex Discrimination Act) / through the CONSTITUTION (implied rights) / through international agreements we have signed."Learn
    Australian Bills of Rights (10 min)"States and territories: Victoria and the ACT have HUMAN RIGHTS ACTS — these protect specific rights and make government accountable to the courts. Western Australia does NOT have a comprehensive Human Rights Act. This means rights in WA are less protected than in Victoria."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What are rights? Why does Australia need better protection of rights?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe what rights are and explain at least 1 gap in Australian rights protection.

    Lesson 2: What Are Responsibilities?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Are Responsibilities? (10 min)"RESPONSIBILITIES are duties — things citizens SHOULD do. Being a citizen comes with responsibilities: OBEY the law / VOTE in elections / SERVE on a jury if called / PAY taxes / PROTECT the environment / RESPECT the rights of others / PARTICIPATE in democracy."Learn
    Why Responsibilities Matter (10 min)"Responsibilities make democracy work: If people don't vote, government doesn't reflect everyone's views / If people don't serve on juries, the justice system fails / If people don't pay taxes, services (schools, hospitals) don't get funded / If people break the law, society breaks down."Learn
    Rights and Responsibilities Are Linked (10 min)"Rights and responsibilities go together: FREEDOM OF SPEECH gives you the right to express opinions, but you have the RESPONSIBILITY not to incite violence / RIGHT TO EDUCATION gives you the right to learn, but you have the RESPONSIBILITY to attend and work / RIGHT TO VOTE gives you the right to choose leaders, but you have the RESPONSIBILITY to inform yourself."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What are responsibilities? Why do they matter? What is one responsibility you have as a citizen?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 citizen responsibilities and explain why they matter.

    Lesson 3: Rights and Responsibilities in Australia

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australian Rights and Responsibilities (10 min)"Australian citizens have both RIGHTS and RESPONSIBILITIES. Our rights (partially protected): vote / speak freely / associate with others / religious freedom / legal equality. Our responsibilities: obey laws / serve on juries / vote / pay taxes / protect the environment."Learn
    What Rights Are We Missing? (15 min)"Australia does NOT have: a comprehensive Bill of Rights / explicit protection of freedom of speech / explicit protection of privacy / a written statement of rights. Some argue we need these. Others argue the current system works well enough. This is a debate in Australian civics."Debate
    Young People's Rights (10 min)"Young people have the same human rights as adults but sometimes are treated differently: under 18 cannot vote / under 18 cannot sign contracts / under 18 are subject to different laws / but you have the right to be heard on issues that affect you (UN Convention on the Rights of the Child — Australia has signed)."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What rights do young people have? What responsibilities do they have as citizens?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students create a poster of rights and responsibilities. Gifted students: debate whether Australia should adopt a Bill of Rights.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 rights and 3 responsibilities of Australian citizens and explain the rights/responsibilities balance.

    WEEK 7 — ELABORATE + EVALUATE: Community Action

    Lesson 1: Plan an Action

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Planning Community Action (15 min)"Let's PLAN community action. Steps: 1. IDENTIFY the issue clearly / 2. RESEARCH the facts / 3. DECIDE what to ask for (specific request, not vague concern) / 4. BUILD support (who else cares?) / 5. CHOOSE your method (letter, petition, meeting attendance) / 6. ACT."Learn
    What Is a Good Request? (10 min)"A good request to council is: SPECIFIC (not 'improve the park' but 'install shade sails over the playground in [park name]') / REASONABLE (something council can actually do) / EVIDENCE-BASED (show why it matters — data, quotes, photos) / TIMED (ask for something in a realistic timeframe)."Learn
    Plan Your Action (10 min)"Plan your community action: What is your issue? What do you want? What evidence supports your request? How will you communicate it?"Plan
    Share Plans (5 min)3–4 students share their action plans. Class feedback: Is the request specific? Is it reasonable?Share

    Success Criteria Check: Completes a community action plan with a specific, reasonable, evidence-based request.

    Lesson 2: Research Local Issues

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Local Issue Research (20 min)"Research a local issue using: council website (agendas, minutes, annual reports) / local newspaper / council officers (call or email) / council's social media / community group websites. Find: What is the issue? Who is affected? What has council done about it? What could citizens do?"Research
    Source Evaluation (10 min)"Evaluate your sources: Is the information ACCURATE? Who wrote it? When? Is it BIASED (one-sided)? Whose perspective is missing? Council information is useful but reflects the council's perspective — seek other perspectives too."Evaluate
    Record (10 min)Record: Issue / Facts / Council's position / Community's position / Your proposed action.Record

    Success Criteria Check: Researches a local issue using at least 2 sources and records findings.

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluation (Preparation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hot Task Preview (5 min)"For tomorrow's Hot Task: Present your community action investigation. Cover: What is the issue? Who is affected? How can citizens participate in addressing this issue? What specific action would you recommend? Use evidence from your research."Preview
    Plan (15 min)Complete a planner: Issue / Research findings / Participation options / Recommended action.Plan
    Prepare (15 min)Prepare presentation materials.Prepare
    Rehearse (5 min)Rehearse your presentation.Rehearse

    Success Criteria Check: Completes a Hot Task preparation plan.

    Lesson 3: Celebration — Our Community
