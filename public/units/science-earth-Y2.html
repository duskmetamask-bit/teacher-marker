Unit Content

Earth's Resources and Changes — Earth and Space Sciences Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 2

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum Science v9 — Earth and Space Sciences

Unit Big Idea: Earth has limited natural resources that we use and depend on. Earth's surface changes over time through natural processes and human use.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Research and describe one natural resource and how we use it

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Investigate how Earth's surface has changed in one location and evaluate how we can protect Earth's resources

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to identify Earth's natural resources, describe how they are used, explain how Earth's surface changes through natural processes and human activity, and evaluate ways to protect Earth's resources.

Success Criteria:

  • I can identify natural resources (water, soil, air, minerals, energy).
  • I can describe how humans use Earth's resources.
  • I can explain how natural processes change Earth's surface.
  • I can describe how human activities change Earth's surface.
  • I can evaluate ways to protect Earth's resources.
  • I can use science words: resource, renewable, non-renewable, erosion, weathering, sustainability, conservation.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: Earth's Resources

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Natural Resources

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Show images: water, coal, trees, iron ore, sunlight, air. "What do all these things have in COMMON? They come from EARTH. They are NATURAL RESOURCES."Observe
    Task (30 min)Cold Task: "Choose ONE natural resource (water, air, soil, rocks, plants, sunlight). Research: What is it? Where does it come from? How do we use it? Is it running out? Write 3–4 sentences and draw a picture."Research
    Share (5 min)3–4 students shareShare

    Success Criteria Check: Student presents one natural resource with description and use.

    Lesson 2: What Are Resources?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Natural Resources Defined (15 min)"NATURAL RESOURCES = materials and energy that come from the natural world without human-made changes. TYPES: WATER (rivers, oceans, rain), AIR (atmosphere), SOIL (dirt for growing), ROCKS AND MINERALS (iron ore, gold, coal), PLANTS AND ANIMALS (timber, food), ENERGY (sunlight, wind, geothermal)."Learn
    Renewable vs Non-Renewable (15 min)"RENEWABLE = replenished naturally within human timescales. Sunlight, wind, water, timber (if replanted). NON-RENEWABLE = takes millions of years to form. Coal, oil, natural gas, iron ore, gold. Once used, they are gone for a very long time."Learn
    Sort Activity (10 min)Cards: sunlight, coal, oil, water, timber, gold, wind, natural gas, fish, iron ore. Sort: RENEWABLE vs NON-RENEWABLE.Sort
    Write (5 min)"Natural resources come from Earth. Some renew quickly, some take millions of years to form."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Sorts at least 8 resources as renewable or non-renewable with explanations.

    Lesson 3: How We Use Resources

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Resource Use Web (15 min)"Resources connect to each other and to our lives. WOOD → paper, furniture, buildings. WATER → drinking, farming, industry. IRON ORE → steel → cars, buildings, tools. COAL → electricity → lights, computers."Learn
    Resource Use Survey (15 min)"Think about your morning. How many natural resources did you use before school? Water (shower), food (breakfast), timber (furniture), minerals (phone/watch), energy (electricity)." List at least 5.Survey
    Perth Context (5 min)"Perth uses: water from dams and desalination. Electricity from gas, coal, solar. Sand and limestone for building. Food from the Swan Valley and beyond."Learn
    Draw (5 min)Draw one chain: resource → product → use.Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Identifies at least 5 natural resources used in daily life.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Water

    Lesson 1: The Water Cycle

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Water Cycle Steps (15 min)"THE WATER CYCLE: EVAPORATION: sun heats water in oceans, lakes, rivers → water changes to vapour (gas) and rises. CONDENSATION: water vapour rises, cools → forms tiny water droplets → clouds. PRECIPITATION: water droplets grow heavy → fall as rain, snow, hail. COLLECTION: water collects in oceans, lakes, rivers, groundwater. REPEAT — continuously."Learn
    Model the Water Cycle (15 min)Put water in a clear plastic bag. Seal. Hang in sunlight. Observe over 30 minutes. Water evaporates, condenses on bag, droplets form, water trickles down. Draw and label.Model
    Perth Water Cycle (10 min)"Perth's water cycle: Indian Ocean (evaporation) → clouds form → rain falls on hills (dams collect) OR in the ocean. Perth's desalination plant: takes seawater, removes salt, creates drinking water."Learn
    Draw (5 min)Draw the water cycle with all 4 steps. Label each step.Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly explains all 4 stages of the water cycle.

    Lesson 2: Water in Australia

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Water Scarcity (15 min)"Australia is the DRIEST inhabited continent. Perth: Mediterranean climate — hot dry summers, mild wet winters. Most rain falls in winter (May–August). DAM capacity has decreased with population growth. Perth has built a MASSIVE DESALINATION PLANT (seawater reverse osmosis) — one of the largest in the world."Learn
    Murray-Darling Basin (10 min)"Eastern Australia's water: MURRAY-DARLING BASIN — largest river system. Used for agriculture. Too much water taken for farming → river system stressed. Environmental water releases now managed to protect river ecosystems."Learn
    Water vs Wealth (5 min)"Access to clean water is not equal worldwide. 2 billion people lack safely managed drinking water. Water is a HUMAN RIGHT but also a scarce resource."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Australia has limited freshwater. Perth uses desalination and dams to provide water."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Explains at least 2 methods of freshwater supply in Australia and identifies water scarcity issues.

    Lesson 3: Water Use and Conservation

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Water Use Investigation (15 min)"How much water do we use? Shower: 7–10L per minute. Toilet: 3–6L per flush. Taps: 6–12L per minute. Garden: 1000–2000L per hour with hose. PERTH average: 180L per person per day. Australia: among highest per capita water use in the world."Learn
    Conservation Methods (15 min)"WATER-SAVING: shorter showers, efficient appliances, drip irrigation, rainwater tanks, greywater recycling. FIX LEAKS — a dripping tap can waste 10,000L per year. Perth's Water Corporation offers rebates for water-saving devices."Learn
    Calculate Your Water Use (10 min)Students calculate: if you have a 7-minute shower, how many litres? (7L × 7 = 49L per shower). If you shower daily for a week? (49 × 7 = 343L). How could you reduce?Calculate
    Design Campaign (5 min)"Design a water conservation poster for our school."Design

    Success Criteria Check: Calculates water use and proposes at least 3 water conservation strategies.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLAIN: Soil

    Lesson 1: What Is Soil?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Soil Components (15 min)"SOIL is a MIX of ingredients: MINERAL PARTICLES (broken rocks — sand, silt, clay), ORGANIC MATTER (dead plants, animals — HUMUS), WATER (fills gaps between particles), AIR (fills pores). The ratio of sand/silt/clay determines soil type: SANDY = drains fast, poor water retention. CLAY = drains slow, holds water well. LOAM = balanced, ideal for plants."Learn
    Soil Layers (10 min)"SOIL HORIZONS (layers): TOPSOIL (A horizon): dark, rich in organic matter, most plant roots here, 5–10cm deep. SUBSOIL (B horizon): more mineral particles, less organic matter. PARENT MATERIAL (C horizon): weathered bedrock. These layers form over THOUSANDS of years."Learn
    Observe Soil (10 min)Examine soil samples with magnifying glasses. Sort particles by size. Record: texture (gritty = sandy, smooth = clay).Observe
    Draw Soil Profile (5 min)Draw the 3 soil horizons. Label each layer and what it contains.Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Identifies soil components and describes the 3 main soil layers.

    Lesson 2: How Soil Forms

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Weathering (15 min)"SOIL FORMS through WEATHERING (breakdown of rock): PHYSICAL WEATHERING: temperature changes cause rocks to crack (desert), ice expands in cracks (freeze-thaw), plant roots grow into cracks. CHEMICAL WEATHERING: rain (slightly acidic) dissolves some minerals, oxygen causes rusting (iron oxide). Biological: roots produce acids, earthworm burrows break up soil."Learn
    Soil Formation Rate (10 min)"SOIL FORMS VERY SLOWLY: ~1cm of topsoil forms in 100–1000 years depending on climate. Topsoil loss through erosion is MUCH FASTER. This means soil is effectively NON-RENEWABLE on human timescales!"Learn
    Erosion Experiment (10 min)Demonstrate: bare soil vs soil with grass cover vs soil with mulch. Pour water slowly over each. Observe runoff. Which soil eroded most? Which held water best?Test
    Write (5 min)"Soil takes hundreds of years to form but can be lost quickly through erosion."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Explains how soil forms through weathering and why soil is considered a non-renewable resource.

    Lesson 3: Soil and Living Things

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Soil Ecosystems (15 min)"SOIL is ALIVE with organisms: EARTHWORMS: aerate soil, recycle nutrients (Darwin called them 'intestines of the earth'). BACTERIA: decompose organic matter, fix nitrogen. FUNGI: decompose tough plant material, form symbiotic relationships with 90% of plant roots (mycorrhizae). INSECTS: beetles, ants burrow and mix soil. These organisms create healthy, fertile soil."Learn
    Soil and Plants (10 min)"PLANTS NEED SOIL for: ANCHORAGE (roots hold plant), WATER RETENTION (soil holds water for roots), NUTRIENTS (plants absorb minerals from soil), GAS EXCHANGE (air in soil pores for roots to breathe). Without soil, most land plants cannot grow."Learn
    Compost Investigation (10 min)"COMPOSTING: accelerates natural decomposition. Food scraps + garden waste → compost (rich in humus). Adds nutrients to soil, reduces waste." Observe compost if available.Investigate
    Design (5 min)"Design a plan to protect soil in our school garden."Design

    Success Criteria Check: Explains how soil organisms contribute to soil fertility and plant growth.

    WEEK 4 — ELABORATE: Rocks and Minerals

    Lesson 1: Types of Rocks

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Three Rock Types (15 min)"IGNEOUS: formed from cooled molten rock (magma/lava). Examples: granite (slow cooling underground, large crystals), basalt (fast cooling lava, fine crystals). SEDIMENTARY: formed from compressed and cemented sediments. Examples: sandstone (sand grains cemented), limestone (from shells/skeletons), coal (from compressed ancient plants). METAMORPHIC: changed by heat and pressure. Examples: marble (limestone changed), slate (mudstone changed)."Learn
    Rock Cycle (10 min)"The ROCK CYCLE: igneous rocks → weathering/erosion → sediments → sedimentary rocks → heat and pressure → metamorphic rocks → melting → magma → igneous rocks (repeat). Rocks transform from one type to another over millions of years!"Learn
    Perth Rocks (10 min)"Perth and WA rocks: GRANITE of the Darling Range (ancient igneous). SANDSTONE of the Perth hills. LIMESTONE along the coast. These rocks tell us about Earth's history — the oldest known rocks on Earth are 4.4 billion years old (Jack Hills, WA)!"Learn
    Sort Rocks (5 min)Examine rock samples. Sort into igneous/sedimentary/metamorphic. Justify sorting.Sort

    Success Criteria Check: Identifies all 3 rock types and describes the basic rock cycle.

    Lesson 2: How We Use Minerals

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Minerals in Everyday Life (15 min)"MINERALS = naturally occurring solid substances extracted from Earth. Uses: IRON ORE → steel → buildings, cars, tools. COAL → electricity → power. GOLD → jewellery, electronics (computers, phones). BAUXITE → aluminium → cans, foil, planes. SALT (sodium chloride) → food, de-icing roads."Learn
    Perth Mineral Use (10 min)"Perth/WA minerals: IRON ORE (Pilbara) → world's largest exporter. GOLD (Kalgoorlie — Super Pit) → Australia's largest gold mine. BAUXITE (Gove) → exported. LIQUID NATURAL GAS (LNG) → Gorgon, NW Shelf projects. These drive Western Australia's economy."Learn
    Environmental Cost (10 min)"MINING impacts: habitat destruction, water pollution, energy use, landscape scarring. But it also provides jobs and essential materials. BALANCE: rehabilitation after mining (replanting, reshaping land), stricter regulations, transition to recycling metals."Learn
    Draw (5 min)Draw one mineral and its end use.Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Identifies at least 4 mineral resources and describes their uses.

    Lesson 3: Mining in Australia

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Mining Types (15 min)"MINING METHODS: OPEN-CUT: remove surface layers, huge pits (iron ore, gold, copper). UNDERGROUND: tunnels and shafts (some gold, lead, zinc). MINING PIT: Bingham Canyon (USA) is the largest open-cut mine. WA's Super Pit (Kalgoorlie) is 3.5km × 1.5km."Learn
    Life of a Mine (15 min)"MINE LIFE CYCLE: (1) EXPLORATION — find mineral deposits. (2) APPROVAL — environmental assessment, community consultation. (3) CONSTRUCTION — build infrastructure. (4) OPERATION — extract ore, process, transport. (5) REHABILITATION — reshape land, replant native vegetation, monitor long-term."Learn
    Rehabilitation Case (10 min)"Many mining companies now rehabilitate as they go, not just at closure. Alcoa rehabilitates bauxite mines in Jarrah forest. Target: return to native forest ecosystem."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Describes mining types and explains the mine life cycle including rehabilitation.

    WEEK 5 — ELABORATE: Earth's Changing Surface

    Lesson 1: Natural Changes

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Weathering (10 min)"WEATHERING = breakdown of rocks in place (no movement). PHYSICAL: freeze-thaw cycles (water in cracks expands 9%, widens cracks). Temperature changes (rocks expand/contract unevenly, causing cracks). CHEMICAL: acid rain dissolves limestone. Biological: tree roots crack rocks."Learn
    Erosion (10 min)"EROSION = movement of broken rock material by wind, water, or ice. WIND: sand dunes migrate in deserts. WATER: rivers carve valleys, carry sediment. ICE: glaciers scrape and carry rock. GRAVITY: landslides, rockfalls."Learn
    Deposition (10 min)"DEPOSITION = dropping of eroded material in new places. Examples: river deltas (Nile, Murray-Darling), beaches (sand from river reaches coast), sand dunes, alluvial fans (mountain streams deposit at foot of hills)."Learn
    Investigate (10 min)"Perth: limestone cliffs weathered by salt air, sandy beaches eroded by waves, sand dunes at Swanbourne. Draw local examples."Investigate

    Success Criteria Check: Distinguishes between weathering, erosion, and deposition with at least 2 examples of each.

    Lesson 2: Human Changes

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Human Impact on Land (15 min)"Humans change Earth's surface faster than natural processes: DEFORESTATION: removes trees that hold soil → accelerated erosion. URBANISATION: replaces natural surfaces with concrete/asphalt → increases runoff, reduces water infiltration. MINING: creates open pits, waste dumps. AGRICULTURE: clears native vegetation, changes soil chemistry."Learn
    Compare Natural vs Human Change (15 min)Natural: slow, over millions of years. Human: can be rapid, over years or decades. Which is faster? Human change can be 10–100× faster. But natural change is continuous.Compare
    Perth Urban Growth (10 min)"Perth's urban footprint has expanded enormously since 1950. From small city to one of the most spread-out cities in the world. This has replaced native bushland, changed water catchments, increased heat island effect."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Human activities change Earth's surface faster than natural processes."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Compares natural and human rates of landscape change and provides at least 3 examples of human impact.

    Lesson 3: Weathering and Erosion Investigation

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Erosion Investigation (20 min)Test erosion by water: 3 containers: bare soil, soil with grass (or paper 'grass'), soil with mulch. Pour 500mL water over each. Measure runoff volume and sediment (mud) in runoff. Which eroded most? Which retained water best?Test
    Record Results (10 min)Complete table: SOIL TYPE — RUNOFF VOLUME — SEDIMENT AMOUNT — CONCLUSIONS.Record
    Conclusions (10 min)"Bare soil → most erosion. Vegetation/mulch → least erosion. Vegetation PROTECTS soil by: holding soil with roots, absorbing water, slowing runoff."Conclude

    Success Criteria Check: Conducts an erosion fair test, records data, and draws evidence-based conclusions.

    WEEK 6 — EXPLAIN: Protecting Earth's Resources

    Lesson 1: Conservation

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Conservation? (10 min)"CONSERVATION = protecting and managing Earth's natural resources for future generations. TYPES: PROTECTED AREAS (national parks, marine parks), RESOURCE MANAGEMENT (sustainable water use, responsible mining), SPECIES PROTECTION (preventing extinction), HABITAT RESTORATION (replanting, river repair)."Learn
    Australian Protected Areas (15 min)"Australia: 12% of land is protected in conservation reserves. WA: massive pastoral leases, but growing national park network. Marine parks: Ningaloo Reef, Great Australian Bight. Challenge: protecting enough habitat for species to survive climate change."Learn
    Indigenous Land Management (10 min)"Indigenous Australians have managed land for 65,000+ years using FIRE (controlled burning to manage vegetation, reduce wildfire risk, promote biodiversity). This is a form of conservation passed down through generations. Western science is now learning from Indigenous land management."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Conservation protects Earth's resources and ecosystems for future generations."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Explains at least 3 types of conservation and identifies at least 1 conservation initiative.

    Lesson 2: Renewable vs Non-Renewable

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Renewable Energy (15 min)"RENEWABLE ENERGY SOURCES: SOLAR: photovoltaic cells convert sunlight directly to electricity. WIND: turbines convert kinetic energy of wind to electricity. HYDRO: water flowing through turbines generates electricity (hydroelectric — provides ~7% of world electricity). GEOTHERMAL: heat from Earth's interior (hot rocks) used directly for heating or to generate electricity."Learn
    Non-Renewable Energy (10 min)"NON-RENEWABLE: COAL: burned to generate electricity (~50% of world electricity). Major source of CO₂ emissions. OIL: transportation fuel, petrochemicals. GAS: electricity generation, heating. All emit CO₂ and contribute to climate change."Learn
    Australia's Energy Mix (10 min)"Australia: coal 54%, gas 18%, renewables 23% (solar fastest growing, wind second). Perth: one of the highest per capita solar uptake in the world (rooftop solar panels). Australia has among the best renewable energy potential in the world (sun, wind)."Learn
    Debate (5 min)"Should Australia phase out coal faster? Arguments for: climate change, health costs. Arguments against: jobs, energy security, reliability."Discuss

    Success Criteria Check: Compares renewable and non-renewable energy sources with at least 2 examples of each and explains their environmental impacts.

    Lesson 3: Sustainable Living

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Sustainability? (10 min)"SUSTAINABILITY = meeting our current needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their needs. The THREE PILLARS: ENVIRONMENTAL (protect ecosystems), SOCIAL (equity, health, education), ECONOMIC (jobs, growth). All three must be balanced."Learn
    Sustainable Practices (15 min)"SUSTAINABLE LIVING: REDUCE (use less), REUSE (use again), RECYCLE (turn into new product). Examples: use reusable water bottle instead of plastic. Buy products with less packaging. Choose products made from recycled materials. Compost food scraps. Walk/bike/public transport."Learn
    School Sustainability Audit (10 min)Students audit school: paper use, plastic waste, recycling, water use, energy use. Identify 3 areas for improvement.Audit
    Design Solutions (5 min)"Design a sustainable practice for our school. How would it work? What would it save?"Design

    Success Criteria Check: Conducts a sustainability audit and proposes at least 3 evidence-based sustainable practices.
