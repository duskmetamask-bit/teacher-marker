Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to write a persuasive piece about something we believe is wrong or unjust, using evidence, logical reasoning, and sophisticated persuasive techniques to move our audience to care and to act.

Success Criteria:

  • I can argue for something I believe is wrong or unjust, with passion and precision.
  • I can use evidence (facts, statistics, examples, testimony) to support my argument.
  • I can acknowledge and address counter-arguments fairly and respectfully.
  • I can use sophisticated persuasive techniques: rhetorical questions, repetition, triplet structures, emotive language, inclusive language.
  • I can write with a strong authorial voice that is passionate but controlled.
  • I can structure a persuasive piece with a clear architecture: hook, context, argument, evidence, counter, conclusion.

    WEEK 1 — INTRODUCTION TO THE BOY IN THE STRIPED PYJAMAS

    Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)"Think about something in the world — locally or globally — that you believe is WRONG. Something that is unjust, unfair, or hurts people or the planet. Write to convince your reader that this thing is wrong and must change."Think
    Task (40 min)Cold Task: Write a persuasive piece arguing that something is wrong or unjust. You choose the topic. Year 5: 350–500 words. Focus: Can you argue passionately and clearly for something you believe in?Independent writing

    Differentiation: Topic bank available for students who need a starting point. Sentence starters for persuasive language.

    Lesson 2: What Is Injustice? (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (20 min)Read *The Boy in the Striped Pyjamas* — Chapters 1–5 (Shmuel's introduction, Bruno's confusion, the fence). Boyne writes about the Holocaust through Bruno's innocent eyes — and the power of the novel is that we know what Bruno doesn't. The reader sees the horror that Bruno cannot. This is a deliberate craft choice.Analyze text
    Discuss (15 min)"What is the injustice at the centre of this novel?" The Holocaust — the systematic murder of six million Jews and millions of others. "How does Boyne make us feel it?" Through Bruno's innocence, through Shmuel's small humanity, through the fence as symbol.Discuss craft
    Share (5 min)"What is the difference between knowing something is wrong and PERSUADING someone else it is wrong?"Note

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify at least 3 ways Boyne makes readers feel the injustice of the Holocaust.

    Lesson 3: Writing About What Is Wrong (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"The most powerful persuasion comes from genuine conviction. We are going to write about things we believe are WRONG — and we are going to argue for change."Set purpose
    We Do (20 min)Brainstorm: What is something wrong in our world? Issues: Climate change, bullying, hunger, discrimination, animal cruelty, lack of access to education, poverty, unfair treatment. Make a class list. Discuss: Which of these do you feel MOST strongly about?Discuss and select
    You Do (10 min)Decide: What will YOU argue is wrong? Write your topic in your journal. You will return to this in Week 4 when you draft your full piece.Decide topic

    WEEK 2 — EVIDENCE AND RESEARCH

    Lesson 1: Finding the Facts (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"To persuade someone something is wrong, you need FACTS. Not just feelings — evidence. Facts give your argument weight."Note
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)Types of evidence: FACTS (things that are true and verifiable) / STATISTICS (numbers that show scale) / EXAMPLES (specific cases that illustrate the problem) / TESTIMONY (quotes from people with direct experience) / EXPERT OPINIONS (people with authority who agree with you). Each type has a different strength.Note types
    You Do (15 min)For your topic: What facts do you know? What statistics might support you? Write them in your evidence folder.Collect evidence

    Lesson 2: Types of Evidence (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Show strong evidence vs weak evidence. WEAK: "Many people think climate change is bad." STRONG: "Since 1880, global average temperature has increased by approximately 1.1°C, with the rate of warming accelerating since 1980 (NASA, 2023)."Compare
    We Do (15 min)As a class, research ONE topic (e.g., the importance of reading). Find: 1 fact, 1 statistic, 1 example, 1 expert quote. Make a class evidence bank.Research together
    You Do (15 min)Add to your evidence folder: Find at least 1 strong piece of evidence for each type: fact, statistic, example.Research

    Lesson 3: Gathering Your Evidence (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Research your chosen topic. Use books, articles, reliable websites. Find: 2 facts, 1 statistic, 1 example, 1 expert quote. Record your sources.Independent research
    Share (5 min)Share one piece of strong evidence with the classShare evidence

    WEEK 3 — PERSUASIVE TECHNIQUES

    Lesson 1: Rhetorical Devices (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"RHETORIC is the art of persuasion. Skilled persuaders use specific techniques to move their audience."Note
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)Key rhetorical devices: TRIPLET — grouping three things for impact ("Government of the people, by the people, for the people"). REPETITION — repeating a key phrase for emphasis ("We shall fight on the beaches"). RHETORICAL QUESTION — a question that doesn't need an answer because the answer is obvious ("How many more must die?"). ALLITERATION — repeating starting sounds for impact. PARALLELISM — similar structure for rhythm and power.Note devices
    You Do (15 min)Identify 3 examples of rhetorical devices from a famous speech (e.g., Martin Luther King Jr's "I Have a Dream"). Label each device.Analyze speech

    Lesson 2: Triplets, Repetition, Questions (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Read The Boy in the Striped Pyjamas — find an example of a rhetorical device. Boyne uses simple language but powerful repetition. "Shmuel was a small boy, a very small boy, the smallest boy Bruno had ever seen." This is repetition for effect.Analyze
    We Do (20 min)Practice: Write a triplet about your topic. Write a rhetorical question about your topic. Write a phrase you'll repeat for emphasis.Practice
    You Do (10 min)Add 2 rhetorical devices to your argument notes.Add to notes

    Lesson 3: Emotive Language (I Do → You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"Emotive language makes people FEEL something. It creates an emotional response. Use it carefully — it is powerful but can be manipulative if overused."Note balance
    We Do (15 min)Compare: CLINICAL: "The factory released waste into the river." EMOTIVE: "The factory callously dumped poison into the river, killing fish and poisoning communities." Both are factual, but one creates emotional response. Which is more persuasive? Both can be appropriate.Compare
    You Do (15 min)Rewrite 3 sentences from your argument using emotive language. Circle the emotive words.Write emotively

    WEEK 4 — STRUCTURING YOUR ARGUMENT

    Lesson 1: The Architecture of Persuasion (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)The STRUCTURE of a persuasive piece: HOOK (first line — grabs attention) → CONTEXT (what is the issue?) → ARGUMENT (what is your position?) → EVIDENCE (your facts and examples) → COUNTER-ARGUMENT (what do opponents say? Answer it) → CONCLUSION (restate position, call to action). Show each step with examples.Note structure
    You Do (25 min)Plan your persuasive piece using the architecture. Fill in each section: Hook line, context statement, main argument, 2 pieces of evidence, one counter-argument, conclusion statement.Plan

    Lesson 2: The Counter-Argument (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"A strong argument acknowledges what others might say against it — and answers. This shows fairness, intelligence, and strength." Show counter-argument structure: "Some people say _____, but this is wrong because _____."Note technique
    We Do (20 min)For your topic: What might someone say that disagrees with you? Make a list. Then, for each disagreement, write your response.Practice counter
    You Do (10 min)Write one strong counter-argument and response for your piece.Write counter

    Lesson 3: Drafting the Opening (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (5 min)"The HOOK is the most important sentence in your piece. It must grab the reader immediately."Note
    You Do (35 min)Draft the opening of your persuasive piece: Hook + Context + Argument statement. Try 3 different hooks. Choose the best one.Draft opening

    WEEK 5 — DRAFTING AND DEVELOPMENT

    Lesson 1: Writing the Body (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Write the BODY of your persuasive piece. Follow your plan: Evidence paragraph with facts/examples → Second evidence paragraph → Counter-argument paragraph → Your response to counter.Write body
    Share (5 min)Share one paragraph for feedbackShare

    Lesson 2: Writing the Conclusion (I Do → You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"The conclusion does three things: RESTATES your argument in a new way, SUMMARISES your evidence, CALLS FOR ACTION. The conclusion should be the most memorable part — make it powerful."Note
    You Do (30 min)Write the CONCLUSION of your persuasive piece. Make it passionate and memorable. End with a call to action — what should the reader DO with this information?Write conclusion

    Lesson 3: Writers Workshop (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Peer Feedback

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (30 min)Complete your full draft. Conference with 4–5 students on key sections. Peer feedback: Is the argument clear? Is evidence strong? Is the voice passionate?Draft and conference
    Share (10 min)Read one strong paragraph from your draftShare

    WEEK 6 — REVISION AND VOICE

    Lesson 1: Revising for Impact (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Revision

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"Read your piece aloud. Where does it drag? Where does it feel flat? Where does your voice come through strongly? Fix the flat spots."Revise
    We Do (15 min)Peer swap: Read your partner's piece. Mark where you FEEL the emotion and where you feel distance. Tell your partner: "The most powerful moment was..." and "I wanted more when..."Peer response
    You Do (15 min)Revise your piece based on feedback. Focus on: Strengthening the hook, adding rhetorical devices, making the conclusion memorable.Revise

    Lesson 2: Strengthening Voice (I Do → You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Editing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Voice is what makes your writing sound like YOU — not a machine. Strong voice: Uses first person ("I believe...") / Takes a clear position / Sounds like you care deeply / Uses varied sentence lengths.Note voice
    You Do (30 min)Read your piece. Ask: Is this MY voice? Circle any sentences that sound like everyone else. Rewrite them in YOUR voice. Underline your most passionate sentences.Strengthen voice

    Lesson 3: Polishing (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Editing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Final polish: Check punctuation, spelling, paragraph structure. Ensure each paragraph has a clear purpose. Add 2 rhetorical devices if not already present. Confirm evidence is cited.Final polish
    Share (5 min)1–2 students share their most powerful sentenceShare

    WEEK 7 — FINAL DRAFT AND PRESENTATION

    Lesson 1: Final Draft (You Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Publishing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (45 min)Write or type final published version of your persuasive piece. Double line spacing. Proper formatting. Add a title and your name.Write final

    Lesson 2: Presentation Planning (We Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Oral

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (45 min)Prepare to read your piece aloud to the class. Practice: Read it 3 times. Where do you pause? Where do you raise your voice? Where do you slow down for impact? Plan your delivery. Write cue cards if needed.Prepare

    Lesson 3: Presenting Your Case (We Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Presentation

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (45 min)Each student presents their persuasive piece to the class. Class listens actively. After each, 2 students say: "The argument that moved me most was... because..." and "One piece of evidence that was most convincing was..."Present

    WEEK 8 — HOT TASK AND REFLECTION

    Lesson 1: Hot Task (Assessment)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Hook (5 min)"Think about what you argued in Week 1. Think about what you have learned about evidence, rhetoric, voice, and structure. Write something that shows your growth as a persuader."Reflect
    Task (45 min)SAME TASK: Write a persuasive piece arguing that something is wrong or unjust. Year 5: 350–500 words. Focus: Evidence, rhetorical devices, counter-argument, strong voice, memorable conclusion.Independent writing

    Compare Cold Task → Hot Task using rubric. Note growth in craft and persuasiveness.

    Lesson 2: Comparing Our Work (We Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Analysis

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (20 min)Compare Cold Task vs Hot Task — what improved? Look for: Evidence quality / Rhetorical device use / Counter-argument handling / Voice strength / StructureCompare
    Share (25 min)Students share specific growth and one thing they are most proud ofCelebrate

    Lesson 3: Reflection and Celebration (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Reflection

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Reflection (25 min)Self-assess: What did you learn about persuasion, evidence, and writing for change? What will you take forward? Rate yourself against success criteria (1–4). Write one goal for next time.Self-assess
    Celebration (15 min)Read the most powerful passage from one student's work. Discuss: What does it mean to write about injustice? What responsibility do we have as writers to speak up?Community reflection

    ADAPTATIONS

    EAL Students

  • Pre-teach social justice vocabulary (injustice, equality, discrimination, human rights)
  • Provide evidence bank for common topics
  • Sentence starters for rhetorical devices
  • Allow home language brainstorming for topic selection
  • Visual graphic organiser for structure
  • One-on-one conference for comprehension

    • Gifted Students

  • Write about a complex, less obvious injustice
  • Compare two different perspectives on the same issue
  • Research a real social justice campaign and analyze its rhetoric
  • Write a counter-argument from the opposing view, then rebut it
  • Study how Boyne's novel itself is a form of persuasion about the Holocaust

    • Students with Additional Needs

  • Reduce word count to 200–300 words
  • Provide evidence bank (pre-find facts/statistics for common topics)
  • Sentence starters for each paragraph
  • Graphic organiser for structure
  • One-on-one support during writing
  • Extra time for all tasks

  • Draft writing with peer feedback
  • Teacher conferences (Weeks 3, 5, 7)
  • Voice/self-assessment reflection

    • Summative Assessment

  • Cold Task → Hot Task comparison (same rubric, measure growth)
  • Final persuasive piece assessed with rubric
  • Presentation observation
