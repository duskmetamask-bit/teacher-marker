Unit Content

Ancient and Modern Worlds — History Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 3

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum HASS v9 — History (Years 3–4)

Unit Big Idea: The world has been shaped by people and events over thousands of years. Ancient civilisations built the foundations for the modern world, and understanding their achievements and challenges helps us understand where we came from and where we might go.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Describe one ancient civilisation and one modern event, then explain one way the past has shaped the present

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Investigate an ancient civilisation or a significant historical event, present findings, and evaluate its lasting impact on the modern world

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to investigate ancient civilisations and significant events, and to understand how the past has shaped the modern world.

Success Criteria:

  • I can describe at least 3 features of an ancient civilisation (government, economy, culture, geography, achievements).
  • I can explain how ancient civilisations have influenced the modern world.
  • I can sequence key events in history using a timeline.
  • I can use at least 2 types of sources (archaeological, written, visual) to investigate the past.
  • I can describe the significance of at least 1 ancient achievement.
  • I can explain at least 1 way the past shapes the present.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: What Is the Ancient World?

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — The Ancient World and Modern Life

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Show an image of the Pyramids of Giza (built ~4,500 years ago) next to a modern city skyline. "This building is 4,500 years old. Your school is probably less than 100 years old. What does this tell you about time? What do you think the ancient world means?"Observe and wonder
    Introduction (5 min)"In this unit, we will journey through thousands of years of history — from ancient civilisations to the modern day. You will discover how the past has shaped the world you live in now. Ancient civilisations are not just old news — their influence is all around us."Listen
    Cold Task (30 min)"Part 1: Draw or describe ONE ancient civilisation (Egypt, China, Rome, Maya, or one you know). Part 2: Name ONE event from history that you think has shaped the world we live in today. Part 3: Write 3 sentences: How has the past shaped the present? Why is it important to study history?" Year 3 expectation: detailed, evidence-based, clear connection.Independent

    Differentiation: Image word bank of ancient civilisations. Sentence starters: "Ancient [civilisation] was known for..." / "This event shaped the present by..." / "One way the past influences today is..."

    Success Criteria Check: Student identifies at least 1 ancient civilisation and at least 1 way the past shapes the present.

    Lesson 2: Time Scales

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Hook)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Time Is Longer Than We Think (10 min)"Human history goes back MUCH further than most people think. The Earth is 4.5 billion years old. Humans have been around for about 300,000 years. Written history (what we can read) goes back about 5,000 years. Human civilisations (cities, governments, writing) started about 5,000 years ago."Learn
    Chronology Lesson (15 min)"We use TIMELINE to understand time. To understand the SCALE: if all 5,000 years of written history were compressed into 1 year: Ancient Egypt starts in January / Greece and Rome peak in July–September / The Middle Ages are September–December / Today is 31 December, 11:59 PM. We are at the VERY END of human history."Learn
    Create a Timeline (10 min)Students create a personal timeline of their life (0 → current age) and compare to the school's timeline (established year → today) and then to Australian history (1788 → today).Create
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: How many years of written history do we have? Where do we sit on the timeline of all human history?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can place themselves, their school, and Australia correctly on a timeline and understands the relative scale of history.

    Lesson 3: How Do We Know About the Ancient World?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Concept Introduction)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Sources of Ancient History (10 min)"How do we know about the ancient world? From SOURCES: WRITTEN RECORDS (scrolls, tablets, carved inscriptions — but many have been lost or destroyed) / ARCHAEOLOGY (digging up buildings, tools, pottery — the main source for ancient civilisations) / ART (paintings, sculptures, carvings show us how people lived) / ORAL TRADITIONS (stories passed down before writing)."Learn
    Archaeology in Action (15 min)"ARCHAEOLOGISTS dig carefully to find objects from the past. Objects tell stories: POTTERY (how people lived, traded) / BONES (what people ate, how they died) / BUILDINGS (what people built, how they lived) / COINS (who ruled, what they traded). The Romans threw coins into fountains as offerings — archaeologists still find them!"Learn
    Source Analysis Practice (10 min)Show images: Egyptian hieroglyphics / Greek pottery / Roman coins / Aboriginal rock art. "What does each source tell us? What can we NOT tell from each source?"Analyse
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What are two types of sources we use to find out about the ancient world? Which do you think is most useful? Why?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 2 types of sources used to study ancient history and explain how they provide information.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Ancient Egypt

    Lesson 1: The Nile Civilisation

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Egypt Is the Nile (10 min)"Ancient Egypt was one of the world's first great civilisations. It lasted for over 3,000 years! Egypt existed because of the NILE RIVER — one of the longest rivers in the world. The Nile flooded every year, depositing rich soil on the banks. This made farming possible in the middle of a desert."Learn
    Geography of Egypt (15 min)Show map of Egypt: Nile River (lifeline, runs north to Mediterranean) / Fertile river valley (green strip) / Desert (Sahara — huge expanse) / Red Sea / Mediterranean Sea. "Egypt was basically a thin green line along the Nile, surrounded by desert. The desert protected Egypt but also isolated it."Observe
    Structure of Egyptian Society (10 min)"Egyptian society had layers: PHARAOH (king/god, at the top) / NOBLES (rich, helped rule) / PRIESTS (religious leaders) / SCRIBES (writers, educated class) / FARMERS (largest group, worked the land) / SLAVES (captured in war). Society was stable for thousands of years."Learn
    Why Was Egypt So Stable? (5 min)"Egypt lasted 3,000 years because: the Nile provided reliable food / the desert provided protection from invasion / strong central government / consistent religious beliefs / agricultural surplus supported large populations."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe Egypt's geography, the importance of the Nile River, and at least 3 levels of Egyptian society.

    Lesson 2: Achievements of Ancient Egypt

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Egypt's Achievements (10 min)"Ancient Egypt achieved things that amaze us today: PYRAMIDS (massive stone tombs for pharaohs) / SPHINX (giant stone statue) / HIEROGLYPHICS (complex writing system using pictures) / MUMMIFICATION (preserving dead bodies) / CALENDAR (365 days, based on the Nile flood) / MATHEMATICS (geometry to build pyramids) / MEDICINE (basic surgery, healing practices)."Learn
    How Were the Pyramids Built? (15 min)"The Great Pyramid of Giza (built ~2560 BCE, for Pharaoh Khufu) was one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World — and the ONLY one still standing. How was it built? Thousands of workers (NOT slaves — recent archaeology shows they were paid workers) moved limestone blocks from quarries. Ramps, levers, and immense human labour. It took about 20 years and 2.3 million stone blocks."Learn
    Hieroglyphics and Writing (10 min)"Hieroglyphics: over 700 symbols representing sounds and ideas. Used on temple walls, scrolls, and tombs. The Rosetta Stone (found 1799) had the same text in hieroglyphics, demotic script, and Greek — allowing scholars to finally read hieroglyphics. Ancient Egypt's writing was deciphered using this stone."Learn
    Draw or Write (5 min)"Draw one Egyptian achievement and write 2 sentences explaining why it is important."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 achievements of Ancient Egypt and explain why at least 1 was important.

    Lesson 3: Legacy of Ancient Egypt

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Egypt's Legacy Lives On (10 min)"Ancient Egypt ended when it was conquered by Rome (30 BCE). But its legacy lives on in: our understanding of the afterlife (Egyptian beliefs influenced later religions) / calendar (365-day calendar is the basis of our calendar) / architecture (the pyramid shape is still used in modern buildings) / medical knowledge (understanding of organs, surgery) / art and storytelling (myths, gods, heroes)."Learn
    Modern Egypt vs Ancient Egypt (10 min)"Modern Egypt is a Muslim, Arabic-speaking country of 100+ million people. Cairo is a modern megacity. But the Pyramids and Sphinx are still there — the greatest physical legacy of the ancient world. Tourism from the Pyramids is still a major part of Egypt's economy."Compare
    Egypt and the Modern World (10 min)"What ancient civilisation has had the most influence on the modern world? Egypt is a strong contender: the calendar, the 7-day week, the concept of the afterlife, the word 'pharaoh,' architectural styles, and much more came from Egypt."Discuss
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is ONE way Ancient Egypt still influences the modern world? Why does this matter?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students create a visual poster of Egypt's legacy. Gifted students: research one specific Egyptian achievement and trace its influence on the modern world.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 ways Ancient Egypt influences the modern world.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: Ancient China and Rome

    Lesson 1: Ancient China

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    China — One of the World's Oldest Civilisations (10 min)"Ancient China is one of the world's oldest continuous civilisations. The Shang Dynasty (1766–1122 BCE) is the earliest Chinese dynasty with written records. Chinese civilisation has continued for over 3,500 years — the longest of any civilisation on Earth. It was largely isolated from the rest of the world by geography: mountains, deserts, and oceans."Learn
    Key Achievements (15 min)"Ancient China's achievements: WRITING (Chinese characters, used for 3,500+ years) / PAPER (invented ~105 CE, spread to the world) / PRINTING (woodblock printing, then moveable type) / GUNPOWDER (invented in China, ~850 CE) / COMPASS (magnetic, used for navigation) / SILK (unique to China, highly tradeable) / THE GREAT WALL (begun ~220 BCE, built over many centuries)."Learn
    Confucius and Chinese Values (10 min)"CONFUCIUS (551–479 BCE): philosopher whose ideas became the foundation of Chinese society. His teachings: respect for elders and family / education is essential for all / harmony in society / personal virtue matters. His ideas influenced Chinese society for 2,500+ years and still influence East Asian societies today."Learn
    Draw (5 min)"Draw one Chinese achievement and label what it is and when it was invented."Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 achievements of Ancient China and at least 1 Confucian teaching.

    Lesson 2: The Roman Empire

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Rome — From Republic to Empire (10 min)"Rome began as a small city-state in Italy around 753 BCE. It expanded to become the ROMAN REPUBLIC (government by senators and elected officials). Then it became the ROMAN EMPIRE (ruled by an emperor). At its peak (117 CE), Rome controlled the entire Mediterranean world — Britain to Mesopotamia, Rhine to Sahara. 70 million people lived under Roman rule."Learn
    Roman Achievements (15 min)"Rome's achievements: LAW (Roman law is the basis of most legal systems in the Western world) / GOVERNMENT (senate, republic concept, idea of citizenship) / ENGINEERING (roads, aqueducts, concrete, the Colosseum) / ARCHITECTURE (arches, domes, columns — still used today) / LANGUAGE (Latin — basis of French, Spanish, Italian, Romanian) / RELIGION (Roman gods, many adopted from Greeks; Christianity spread through the Empire)."Learn
    Roman Roads and Engineering (10 min)"Rome built 400,000 km of roads, including 80,000 km of paved highways. 'All roads lead to Rome' was literally true. Roads were built for military purposes — but they also enabled trade, communication, and cultural exchange. Roman aqueducts carried water over hundreds of kilometres — some still stand today."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is ONE achievement of Ancient Rome? How has it influenced the modern world?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 Roman achievements and explain how at least 1 has influenced the modern world.

    Lesson 3: Comparing Rome and China

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Two Giant Civilisations (10 min)"Rome and China were the two greatest civilisations of the ancient world. They existed at the same time — but they did not really know about each other (too far apart, with deserts and mountains between them). They each developed their own solutions to the problems all civilisations face."Learn
    Venn Diagram Comparison (15 min)Create a Venn diagram: RomeChina / SAME: large empire, strong government, written law, great engineering achievements, long-lasting influence / ROME UNIQUELY: Senate, republic concept, concrete, Christianity's spread / CHINA UNIQUELY: Paper, compass, gunpowder, silk, Confucian philosophy, Great Wall."Compare
    What All Civilizations Share (10 min)"ALL civilisations share: NEED FOR GOVERNMENT (someone needs to make decisions) / NEED FOR FOOD PRODUCTION (agriculture) / NEED FOR LAWS (rules for society) / NEED FOR DEFENCE (protection) / DESIRE TO RECORD IDEAS (writing) / VALUES AND BELIEFS (religion, philosophy)."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is one major similarity between Rome and China? What is one major difference? Why does this comparison matter?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students use picture-based comparison. Gifted students: compare a third civilisation (Maya, Ancient Greece).

    Success Criteria Check: Completes a Venn diagram comparing Rome and China with at least 3 items in each section.

    WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Ancient Australia and the First Australians

    Lesson 1: Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander History

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Oldest Continuous Culture on Earth (10 min)"Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples are the oldest continuous culture on Earth. They have lived in Australia for over 65,000 years. By comparison, Rome existed for about 1,200 years, Egypt for 3,000 years. Aboriginal culture is 20–50 times older than these ancient civilisations."Learn
    Geographic Distribution (10 min)"Before 1788, Aboriginal peoples lived in ALL parts of Australia — from tropical Cape York to the temperate south, from coastal Arnhem Land to the arid interior. There were over 250 distinct language groups, each with their own Country, culture, and traditions."Learn
    Sustainable Living (10 min)"For 65,000+ years, Aboriginal peoples lived SUSTAINABLY. They managed the land through: FIRE MANAGEMENT (controlled burning to promote new growth, prevent wildfires, manage vegetation) / WATER MANAGEMENT (fish traps, waterhole protection) / MARINE MANAGEMENT (sustainable fishing, no-take zones) / SPECIES MANAGEMENT (knowing which plants and animals to take and when). This was sophisticated environmental science."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What makes Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples' history unique? Why does their 65,000-year history matter?"Write

    Note: This unit should be taught with respect and ideally with support from local Aboriginal education resources.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 unique aspects of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples' history and explain why their history is significant.

    Lesson 2: 65,000 Years of Continuous Culture

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Culture That Didn't Break (10 min)"Aboriginal culture is the oldest continuous culture on Earth — 65,000 years without interruption. No other culture on Earth can make this claim. How? Through: oral tradition (stories passed down through thousands of generations) / connection to Country (cultural identity tied to land) / art (rock art showing animals and ceremonies over millennia) / spiritual practices that remained consistent."Learn
    Dreaming — Stories of the Dreaming (10 min)"The DREAMING (or Dreamtime) is the spiritual understanding of creation and the law. Dreaming stories explain how the land, people, plants, and animals came to be. They are tied to specific places — there are Dreaming stories for every part of Australia. Dreaming stories are not just 'myths' — they encode vast knowledge about the environment, seasons, and survival."Learn
    What Is Not Known (10 min)"There is still much we do NOT know about Aboriginal history before 1788 because: oral traditions were not written down before colonisation / much evidence was destroyed during colonisation / historians are still working to recover this history. This is one reason why Indigenous knowledge is so valuable — it contains what written records cannot."Learn
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What is the Dreaming? Why is it important to Aboriginal culture?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the Dreaming and explain why Aboriginal culture is considered the oldest continuous culture on Earth.

    Lesson 3: Living History — The Present in the Past

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Aboriginal History Is Living History (10 min)"Aboriginal history is not just the past — it is LIVING HISTORY. Many Aboriginal traditions, languages, and practices continue today. Cultural revivals are happening: language programs teaching Noongar, Wiradjuri, Wirripanda, and many others / cultural burning being integrated with fire management / Indigenous-led environmental programs / Indigenous art and storytelling reaching global audiences."Learn
    What Has Survived Colonisation? (15 min)"Despite 235 years of colonisation, much has survived and much is being revived: 150+ Aboriginal languages still spoken (many endangered but being revived) / cultural ceremonies and practices continue / connection to Country despite forced removals / Indigenous art as a global cultural force / Indigenous knowledge being formally recognised by science."Learn
    Respecting Living History (10 min)"We must learn Aboriginal history with RESPECT — not as a footnote or a 'before colonisation' story, but as a living, ongoing culture that has survived incredible challenges. The oldest continuous culture on Earth deserves our attention, respect, and curiosity."Understand
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is one thing about Aboriginal history that surprised you? How does this change how you think about Australian history?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students draw one survival and one revival. Gifted students: investigate a specific Aboriginal language revival program.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 examples of how Aboriginal culture has survived and revived since colonisation.

    WEEK 5 — EXPLAIN: Australia's Colonial Past

    Lesson 1: Arrival of the British

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australia Before 1788 (10 min)"Before 1788, Australia was inhabited solely by Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples — who had lived here for over 65,000 years. They had sophisticated societies, complex governance, and sustainable land management. The British did not understand this — they called the land 'Terra Nullius' (empty land), which was legally and factually incorrect."Learn
    The First Fleet (10 min)"On 26 January 1788, the First Fleet arrived in Botany Bay (then moved to Sydney Cove). It brought: 1,000+ convicts (mostly for minor crimes like stealing bread) / 300+ marines and officers / limited supplies. The colony was established because Britain needed somewhere to send convicts after losing the American colonies."Learn
    What Colonisation Meant (10 min)"For Aboriginal peoples, colonisation meant: DISPLACEMENT from Country / VIOLENCE (frontier conflicts, massacres — still being documented by historians) / DISEASES (smallpox, measles, influenza killed many) / LOSS OF LANGUAGE AND CULTURE (forced removals, children taken) / BREAKING OF LAW AND GOVERNANCE (cultural practices disrupted). This is a history that Australia is still grappling with."Learn
    Source Analysis: First Fleet Diary (10 min)Show an excerpt from a First Fleet diary (Arthur Bowes Smyth, 1788): 'The natives appear to be an indolent race...' Analyse: What does this source say? What does it NOT say? Whose perspective is shown? Whose perspective is missing?Analyse

    Note: Handle colonisation with historical accuracy and sensitivity.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 effects of colonisation on Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples.

    Lesson 2: Early Colony Life

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Life in Early Sydney (10 min)"Early Sydney was very different from today: poor sanitation, limited food, harsh climate / convicts worked for the government (building, farming) / punishments were harsh / the colony depended on supply ships from Britain (took 8 months to arrive) / many died from disease, starvation, and conflict."Learn
    Growth of the Colony (15 min)"The colony survived and grew: farming expanded / more free settlers arrived (not just convicts) / other colonies were established (Hobart 1804, Brisbane 1824, Perth 1829) / Sydney became a city / pastoral expansion pushed into Aboriginal lands (causing conflict)."Learn
    Convict vs Free Australia (10 min)"Australia's early population was mostly convicts and soldiers. By the 1850s, free settlers were the majority. Many of Australia's major institutions (banks, newspapers, universities) were founded by people who were once convicts. Australia's egalitarian (everyone-is-equal) culture partly comes from this unusual founding story."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What was life like in the early Sydney colony? How did the colony change over time?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 aspects of early colony life and at least 1 way the colony changed over time.

    Lesson 3: Gold Rush and Migration

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Australian Gold Rush (10 min)"In 1851, gold was discovered in NSW and Victoria. This transformed Australia. The gold rush brought: 600,000+ migrants (from Europe, China, USA, and elsewhere) / population tripled in 10 years / new cities and towns built / wealth that funded schools, railways, and infrastructure / Chinese migration (25% of the population by 1860 — causing anti-Chinese tension)."Learn
    The Gold Fields (15 min)"Gold fields were chaotic, dangerous, and exciting. Diggers (miners) from all over the world came. Victoria's gold fields: Ballarat, Bendigo. Rules were made by the diggers themselves (democratic governance). Tensions between British miners and Chinese miners led to riots (Lambing Flat, 1861). The White Australia Policy (1901) was partly a response to these tensions."Learn
    Modern Australia Takes Shape (10 min)"After the gold rush: Australia became one of the world's wealthiest countries per capita / the population was much more diverse / cities grew / democracy expanded (secret ballot, voting rights) / Federation became possible (uniting the colonies into one nation)."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What was the gold rush? How did it change Australia?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the Australian gold rush and at least 2 ways it changed Australia.

    WEEK 6 — ELABORATE: Significant Events and People

    Lesson 1: Federation

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Federation? (10 min)"FEDERATION means joining together. On 1 January 1901, the 6 British colonies (NSW, VIC, QLD, SA, WA, TAS) joined together to become one country — the COMMONWEALTH OF AUSTRALIA. Each colony became a STATE. Federation was a BIG DEAL — it required lots of negotiation, compromise, and vision."Learn
    Why Did Australia Federate? (10 min)"Reasons for Federation: COMMON DEFENCE (a united country is easier to defend) / ECONOMY (free trade between colonies) / SHARED CONCERNS (Chinese immigration, labour movements) / BRITISH IMPERIAL CONNECTIONS / TRAVELLING SHOWMEN (they wanted one set of laws across Australia). The main debate was about the role of Indigenous Australians — they were NOT included in the vote to federate."Learn
    Key Figures (10 min)"Key figures in Federation: SIR HENRY PARKES (called the 'Father of Federation', NSW Premier, pushed for federation over decades) / EDMUND BARTON (first Prime Minister) / ALFRED DEAKIN (important early PM). These figures were mostly white British men — many other voices were not heard."Learn
    Our Federated Australia (10 min)"Federation created modern Australia: one currency (dollar) / one constitution / shared citizenship / national government. BUT: it also entrenched some problems (the Constitution allowed states to make decisions about Indigenous peoples) / the new nation began with a White Australia Policy (restricted non-European migration)."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe what Federation is, when it happened, and at least 1 reason it occurred.

    Lesson 2: World War I and Its Effects

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Why World War I? (10 min)"World War I (1914–1918) was one of the deadliest conflicts in human history — 17 million dead. It started because of: COMPLEX ALLIANCES (countries promised to defend each other) / NATIONALISM (pride in one's nation) / IMPERIAL COMPETITION (countries competing for colonies) / MILITARISM (arms race) / ASSASSINATION of Archduke Franz Ferdinand (trigger)."Learn
    Australia in WWI (15 min)"Australia, as part of the British Empire, joined WWI. 416,000 Australians served overseas (out of a population of 5 million). 60,000 died. Famous battles: Gallipoli (1915 — ANZAC legend born here) / the Western Front (France, devastating losses) / Passchendaele (Belgium). The ANZAC legend: the idea that Australian soldiers were brave, tough, egalitarian — this became central to Australian identity."Learn
    Effects on Australia (10 min)"WWI's effects: 60,000 dead, 152,000 wounded / grief that affected every family / economic disruption / women's suffrage (women gained the right to vote in federal elections partly due to WWI — they took on men's work) / changed Australia's relationship with Britain (the 'imperial loyalty' began to fade) / the Treaty of Versailles (1919) — blamed Germany."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What was World War I? How did it affect Australia?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe WWI and at least 2 of its effects on Australia.

    Lesson 3: Social Change in Australia

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Social Change Is Normal (10 min)"Australia has changed dramatically over 120 years. Some changes were peaceful (rights movements, policy reforms). Some were painful (forced removal policies, economic upheaval). Understanding how and why Australia changed helps us understand what changes are still needed."Learn
    Key Social Changes in Australian History (15 min)"1902 — Women gain right to vote in federal elections / 1967 — Indigenous Australians counted in census and Commonwealth laws (referendum, 90%+ YES vote) / 1973 — End of the White Australia Policy (Gough Whitlam) / 1992 — Native Title recognised (Mabo decision) / 2023 — Indigenous Voice to Parliament referendum (not passed but the conversation continues)."Learn
    Why Does Social Change Happen? (10 min)"Social change happens because: PEOPLE PUSH FOR IT (activists, community members) / CRISIS REVEALS INJUSTICE (the pandemic revealed inequalities) / LEADERSHIP CHANGES (new governments, new ideas) / INTERNATIONAL PRESSURE (global human rights movements) / ECONOMIC SHIFTS (changing who has power). Change is never automatic — someone has to fight for it."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is one social change in Australian history? Who fought for it? Why did it matter?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students create a timeline of social change. Gifted students: research one specific social movement in Australia.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 1 social change in Australian history, explain who pushed for it, and evaluate its impact.

    WEEK 7 — ELABORATE + EVALUATE: The Past and Present

    Lesson 1: Legacy of the Past

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Past Lives in the Present (10 min)"Every society carries its past with it. Things from the past that are still important today are called LEGACIES. The language you speak, the laws you follow, the borders of your country, the food you eat — all are legacies of history."Learn
    Legacy Mapping (15 min)"Map the legacies of history: ROMAN LAW → most Western legal systems / EGYPTIAN CALENDAR → our 365-day calendar / CHINESE PRINTING → books and newspapers / ABORIGINAL FIRE MANAGEMENT → modern land management / BRITISH PARLIAMENT → Australian government / GREEK DEMOCRACY → democratic ideals."Learn

    DIFFERENTIATION NOTES

    EAL Learners

  • Visual Word Wall: civilisation, legacy, dynasty, empire, colony, federation, archaeology, source, timeline, continuity, change, perspective.
  • Sentence Starters: "Ancient [civilisation] was important because..." / "This event changed Australia by..." / "One perspective on this event was..."
  • Home Culture Research: Students may investigate their family's country of origin in the context of ancient history.
  • Partner Support: Paired for research activities.

    • Gifted Learners

  • Research Extension: Investigate the Maya or Ancient Greece in more depth.
  • Comparative Essay: Write a 5-paragraph essay comparing two ancient civilisations.
  • Modern Legacy Analysis: Trace the specific influence of one ancient civilisation on a specific modern technology or practice.

    • Students with NEPs

  • Reduced Written Expectation: 4–6 sentences for written tasks.
  • Oral Option: Students may present verbally.
  • Extra Time: Additional time for all tasks.
  • Word Banks: Provided for all written tasks.
  • Scaffolded Templates: Timeline template, Venn diagram template, investigation template.
