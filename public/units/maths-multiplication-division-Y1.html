UNIT AT A GLANCE

**Year Level**Year 1
**Strand**Number
**Duration**8 weeks × 3 lessons/week = 24 lessons
**Model**5E Instructional Model
**Pedagogical Frame**Gradual Release: I Do → We Do → You Do
**Mentor Text***The Very Hungry Caterpillar* — Eric Carle
**Big Ideas**Multiplication is repeated addition and making equal groups; division is sharing equally; the 2×, 5×, and 10× tables are foundational; multiplication and division are inverse operations
**Driving Question***What happens when we add the same number many times, and what happens when we share equally?*

BIG UNDERSTANDINGS

Students will understand that:

  • Multiplication is adding the same number repeatedly (2+2+2 = 3×2)
  • Multiplication can be shown as equal groups
  • Division is the inverse of multiplication
  • The 2×, 5×, and 10× tables are learnable
  • Arrays show multiplication visually
  • Groups of and sharing are both multiplication and division

    • VOCABULARY

    Tier 2 (academic)

    groups of, arrays, rows, columns, repeated addition, equal groups, share equally, double, triple, skip-count, fact family, inverse

    Tier 3 (mathematical)

    multiplication, division, product, quotient, factor, multiple, commutative property

    A–E RUBRIC — Multiplication and Division (Year 1)

    Criterion 1: Skip-Counting and Number Patterns

    GradeDescriptor
    **A**Skip-counts by 2s, 5s, 10s from 0 reliably; connects patterns to multiplication; extends patterns confidently
    **B**Skip-counts by 2s, 5s, 10s reliably; beginning to connect to multiplication
    **C**Skip-counts by 2s and 5s with some support; beginning to use patterns
    **D**Skip-counts by 2s with physical support; needs objects
    **E**Unable to skip-count; counts by ones

    Criterion 2: Multiplication — Repeated Addition and Groups

    GradeDescriptor
    **A**Writes multiplication as repeated addition fluently; creates equal groups from a given multiplication; solves multiplication word problems using 2×, 5×, 10×
    **B**Writes multiplication as repeated addition reliably; solves problems using 2× and 5×; beginning ×10
    **C**With support, writes multiplication as repeated addition; solves 2× and 5× problems; beginning ×10
    **D**Uses repeated addition with physical objects; multiplication notation with scaffolding
    **E**Unable to write multiplication; significant support required

    Criterion 3: The 2×, 5×, and 10× Tables

    GradeDescriptor
    **A**Recalls ×2, ×5, ×10 facts instantly and in any order; explains patterns in each table; connects ×5 to clock (minutes) and ×2 to doubling
    **B**Recalls most ×2, ×5, ×10 facts; occasional errors; explains patterns
    **C**Recalls ×2, ×5, ×10 with some prompting; uses skip-counting as backup strategy
    **D**Recalls ×2 facts with skip-counting support; ×5 and ×10 require objects
    **E**Unable to recall multiplication facts; relies entirely on counting

    Criterion 4: Division — Sharing

    GradeDescriptor
    **A**Shares collections equally between 2, 3, 5 groups; uses multiplication to check; solves sharing word problems
    **B**Shares between 2 and 5 reliably; attempts 3 and 4 groups; beginning to use multiplication to check
    **C**Shares between 2 and 5 with scaffolding; uses skip-counting or repeated addition to solve
    **D**Shares between 2 groups with significant support; objects required
    **E**Unable to share equally; significant support required

    Criterion 5: Division — Grouping (How Many Groups?)

    GradeDescriptor
    **A**Finds how many groups of a given size fit into a collection; relates grouping to multiplication; uses the inverse fact to check
    **B**Finds groups reliably for ×2 and ×5; beginning to relate to multiplication
    **C**Finds groups with skip-counting and objects; beginning to relate to multiplication facts
    **D**Finds groups of 2 using skip-counting with support
    **E**Unable to find groups; does not connect to multiplication

    Criterion 6: Arrays and Commutativity

    GradeDescriptor
    **A**Creates arrays with given rows and columns; uses skip-counting or known facts to find totals; explains that 2×5 = 5×2
    **B**Creates arrays reliably; beginning to use known facts to find totals
    **C**Creates arrays with scaffolding; counts all or skip-counts to find total
    **D**Creates arrays with significant support; counts all
    **E**Unable to create arrays

    LESSON 1.2 — Multiplication as Repeated Addition

    Duration: 50 minutes

    Type: Concept Introduction

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning that multiplication is adding the same number many times
  • I am learning to write repeated addition as multiplication

    • Success Criteria

  • I can write 4+4+4 as 3×4
  • I can explain that multiplication is faster than repeated addition

    • I DO (15 min)

    "The caterpillar eats 4 apples on Monday, 4 on Tuesday, 4 on Wednesday. How many apples?"
    "4 + 4 + 4 = 12. That's adding 4 THREE times."
    "That's the same as 3 GROUPS OF 4. We write: 3 × 4 = 12."
    "3 × 4 means: 3 groups of 4. Or: add 4 three times."
    "Multiplication is FASTER than repeated addition!"

    WE DO (20 min)

    Write as multiplication:

  • 5 + 5 + 5 = 3 × 5 = 15
  • 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 = 4 × 2 = 8
  • 10 + 10 + 10 = 3 × 10 = 30
  • 3 + 3 + 3 + 3 + 3 = 5 × 3 = 15

    • YOU DO (15 min)

  • 6 + 6 = 2 × 6 = ___
  • 4 + 4 + 4 + 4 + 4 = 5 × 4 = ___
  • Write 3 × 5 as repeated addition: ___ + ___ + ___ = ___
  • EXTENSION: "3×4 and 4×3 — are they the same? Test with counters."

    Duration: 50 minutes

    Type: Explore

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning the 2× multiplication table
  • I am learning that 2×n is the same as double n

    • Success Criteria

  • 7 × 2 = ___ 9 × 2 = ___ 6 × 2 = ___
  • "What is double 8?"
  • EXTENSION: "If 2×7 = 14, what is 2×8?"

    LESSON 4.1 — Division as Sharing

    Duration: 50 minutes

    Type: Explain

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning that division means sharing equally
  • I am learning that 12 ÷ 3 = 4 means 12 shared between 3 = 4 each

    • Success Criteria

  • I can solve 12 ÷ 3 = 4 using sharing
  • I can relate division to multiplication

    • I DO (15 min)

    "Division is the OPPOSITE of multiplication."
    "12 ÷ 3 = 4. This means: 12 shared equally between 3 people = 4 each."
    "12 ÷ 4 = 3. This means: 12 shared equally between 4 people = 3 each."
    "Related facts: 3 × 4 = 12 and 4 × 3 = 12. And: 12 ÷ 3 = 4 and 12 ÷ 4 = 3."
    "The FACT FAMILY: 3, 4, 12."

    WE DO (20 min)

    Share and solve:

  • 10 ÷ 2 = ___ (share 10 between 2)
  • 15 ÷ 3 = ___ (share 15 between 3)
  • 20 ÷ 5 = ___
  • 12 ÷ 4 = ___
  • 18 ÷ 2 = ___

    • YOU DO (15 min)

  • 14 ÷ 2 = ___ 20 ÷ 4 = ___ 25 ÷ 5 = ___
  • "Share 15 stickers between 3 friends. How many each?"
  • "For 20 ÷ 5 = 4, what multiplication fact is related?"
  • EXTENSION: "Write the fact family for 2, 6, 12."

    • LESSON 4.2 — Division as Grouping

    Duration: 50 minutes

    Type: Explain

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning that division can also mean "how many groups of a size fit in?"
  • I am learning to solve 12 ÷ 3 by asking "how many 3s in 12?"

    • Success Criteria

  • I can solve "how many 3s in 12?" = 12 ÷ 3 = 4

    • I DO (15 min)

    "12 ÷ 3 can also mean: how many groups of 3 fit in 12?"
    "Skip-count by 3: 3, 6, 9, 12. That's 4 groups of 3. So 12 ÷ 3 = 4."
    "15 ÷ 5: skip-count by 5: 5, 10, 15. That's 3 groups of 5. So 15 ÷ 5 = 3."

    WE DO (20 min)

    How many groups?

  • How many 2s in 14? 14 ÷ 2 = ___ (2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, 14 → 7)
  • How many 5s in 20? 20 ÷ 5 = ___ (5, 10, 15, 20 → 4)
  • How many 3s in 15? 15 ÷ 3 = ___ (3, 6, 9, 12, 15 → 5)
  • How many 2s in 20? 20 ÷ 2 = ___

    • YOU DO (15 min)

  • How many 2s in 18? 18 ÷ 2 = ___
  • How many 5s in 30? 30 ÷ 5 = ___
  • "How many 10s in 40? 40 ÷ 10 = ___. How do you know?"
  • EXTENSION: "How many 2s in 30? How many 5s in 30?"

    LESSON 5.1 — Fact Families

    Duration: 50 minutes

    Type: Explain

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning that multiplication and division are inverse operations
  • I am learning the fact family for 3, 5, 15

    • Success Criteria

  • I can write all 4 facts in a fact family

    • I DO (15 min)

    "3 × 5 = 15, 5 × 3 = 15, 15 ÷ 3 = 5, 15 ÷ 5 = 3. Same three numbers. That's the FACT FAMILY."
    "If you know 3 × 5 = 15, you know ALL FOUR facts."

    YOU DO (15 min)

  • Write the fact family for: 4, 2, 8
  • Write the fact family for: 5, 5, 25
  • Write the fact family for: 3, 6, 18
  • EXTENSION: "For 10 × 2 = 20, write the full fact family."

    • LESSON 5.2 — Using Inverse to Check

    Duration: 50 minutes

    Type: Explain

    I DO (12 min)

    "You can CHECK a division answer using multiplication."
    "18 ÷ 3 = 6. Check: 6 × 3 = 18. ✓"

    YOU DO (15 min)

  • 20 ÷ 5 = 4. Check: 4 × 5 = ___ ✓
  • 16 ÷ 2 = 8. Check: __ × __ = __ ✓
  • 30 ÷ 10 = 3. Check: __ × __ = __ ✓
  • EXTENSION: "If 7 × 2 = 14, what is 14 ÷ 2?"

    • LESSON 6.2 — Commutativity

    Duration: 50 minutes

    Type: Elaborate

    I DO (12 min)

    "2 × 5 = 10 and 5 × 2 = 10. The ORDER doesn't matter for multiplication."
    "This is the COMMUTATIVE PROPERTY: a × b = b × a."
    "Division is NOT commutative: 10 ÷ 2 = 5 but 10 ÷ 5 = 2. Different answers!"

    YOU DO (15 min)

  • Prove: 3 × 5 = 5 × 3 (draw arrays)
  • "Is 20 ÷ 4 = 20 ÷ 5? YES / NO. Explain."
  • EXTENSION: "Is subtraction commutative? Test: 10 - 2 vs 2 - 10."
