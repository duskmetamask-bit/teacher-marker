Mentor Text/Context: Environmental science, physical sciences, ecological balance

Writing Focus: Scientific explanations, cause and effect, cycle diagrams, evidence-based reasoning

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning about Earth's major cycles — the water cycle, rock cycle, and carbon cycle — and how matter is conserved and transformed through these natural processes.

Success Criteria:

  • I can explain the water cycle and describe each stage: evaporation, condensation, precipitation, collection.
  • I can describe the rock cycle and explain how rocks change between igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic forms.
  • I can explain the carbon cycle and understand why carbon cycling is important for life on Earth.
  • I can use evidence to explain how matter is conserved in these cycles — it changes form but doesn't disappear.
  • I can create accurate diagrams and written explanations of these cycles.

    WEEK 1 — INTRODUCTION TO EARTH'S CYCLES

    Lesson 1: What Is a Cycle? (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Hook (10 min)"A CYCLE is something that goes round and round — it keeps going and never really ends. In nature, matter cycles through different forms and places. Water evaporates, forms clouds, falls as rain, flows to the ocean, evaporates again. Rock is melted, cools, erodes, forms new rock, melts again. Carbon moves from the atmosphere to plants to animals to the ground, back to the atmosphere."Understand cycles
    I Do (15 min)"Cycles are important because they show CONSERVATION OF MATTER — matter doesn't appear from nowhere or disappear to nothing. It changes form. Water doesn't disappear when it evaporates — it becomes invisible water vapour. Carbon in a tree doesn't vanish when the tree dies — it goes into the soil."Learn conservation
    You Do (25 min)"Draw a simple cycle diagram of something you know: The day cycle (morning → midday → afternoon → evening → night → morning) OR the water cycle you already know OR the cycle of how you get ready for school. Circle the parts that repeat. Label the type of cycle."Draw
    Share (5 min)Show your cycle diagram. What makes it a cycle rather than a line?Share

    Success Criteria Check: Can explain what a cycle is and why cycles demonstrate conservation of matter.

    Lesson 2: Conservation of Matter (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"Conservation of matter: In a closed system, matter cannot be created or destroyed — it can only change form. In nature, the Earth is essentially a closed system for matter (some small gains from meteors, small losses to space). So the total amount of matter on Earth stays the same — it just moves and changes."Learn conservation
    We Do (25 min)"Demonstration: Put water in a sealed plastic bag. Weigh it. Observe as you place it in sunlight (evaporation). Weigh again (same mass — water is now water vapour inside the bag). Put in cool place (condensation). Weigh again (same). Pour out — mass decreases (now we open the system). But before we poured, mass was constant." Weigh, observe, record.Experiment
    You Do (10 min)Write: "The water cycle conserves matter because..." / "If matter was not conserved, one possible consequence would be..."Write

    Lesson 3: Why Cycles Matter (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Investigation

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (45 min)"Research: What happens if a cycle is disrupted? Choose ONE: Water cycle disrupted by deforestation → less condensation → drought. Rock cycle disrupted by too much mining → habitat loss. Carbon cycle disrupted by too much CO2 → climate change. Research: What happens? What causes the disruption? What are the consequences? Create a poster with text and diagrams."Research
    Share (10 min)Present your poster. What cycle disruption concerned you most?Present

    WEEK 2 — THE WATER CYCLE

    Lesson 1: Evaporation and Condensation (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (20 min)"The WATER CYCLE: 1. EVAPORATION — Liquid water (from oceans, lakes, rivers, plants) is heated by the sun and changes to water VAPOUR (a gas — invisible). This rises into the atmosphere. 2. CONDENSATION — As water vapour rises, it cools. Cool air can't hold as much water vapour. The vapour condenses into tiny water droplets, forming CLOUDS. This is why clouds form — water changes from gas to liquid droplets."Learn evaporation/condensation
    You Do (30 min)"Demonstration: Hot water + cold plate above it. Watch condensation form. Record observations. Also: Watch ice being placed over warm water — condensation forms on the cold surface. Draw and label what you observed."Observe
    Share (5 min)Where have you seen evaporation happen in real life? Where have you seen condensation?Share

    Lesson 2: Precipitation and Collection (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"3. PRECIPITATION — When cloud droplets combine and become too heavy, they fall as rain (liquid) or snow/sleet/hail (solid). This returns water to the ground. 4. COLLECTION/RUNOFF — Water flows over land (runoff) into rivers and oceans, or soaks into ground (groundwater). Then the cycle starts again — evaporation from these water bodies."Learn
    We Do (25 min)"Water cycle walk: Mark 4 stations (evaporation, condensation, precipitation, collection) around the school or classroom. At each station, students act out the process and say the key facts. After, write the 4 stages in order with one fact about each."Act
    You Do (15 min)Write: For each stage, write: "At the ___ stage, water ___ (what happens) because ___ (why it happens)." Complete for all 4 stages.Write

    Lesson 3: The Complete Cycle (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)"Create a complete water cycle diagram: Draw a labelled diagram showing all 4 stages with arrows showing direction of flow. Include: Sun (energy source), clouds, rain, rivers, ocean, mountains, trees (transpiration). Label: Evaporation, Condensation, Precipitation, Collection/Runoff. Below the diagram, write a paragraph explaining the cycle."Create diagram
    Share (5 min)Show your diagram. Check: Is the direction of flow correct? Are all 4 stages labelled?Check

    WEEK 3 — DEEP DIVE: WATER IN AUSTRALIA

    Lesson 1: Australia's Water Systems (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (20 min)"Australia is the driest inhabited continent. Our water systems: Major rivers (Murray-Darling Basin — supplies 40% of our agricultural water), coastal rivers, underground water (aquifers). Most rain falls on the east coast (tropical). The interior is arid. Water scarcity is a major issue."Learn
    You Do (30 min)"Map: Draw a map of Australia. Mark: Areas of high rainfall (east coast, far north). Areas of low rainfall (interior, western coast). The Murray-Darling Basin (Victoria, NSW, Queensland). The major rivers: Murray, Darling, Murrumbidgee. The major cities and where their water comes from."Map
    Share (5 min)Why do you think most Australians live on the east coast? (Water availability)Discuss

    Lesson 2: Drought and Flood (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"Australia has extreme climate events. DROUGHT: Extended periods of low rainfall. The Murray-Darling has experienced severe droughts (Millennium Drought 2001–2009). Impact: Low water in dams, crop failures, livestock deaths, water restrictions. FLOOD: Heavy rainfall filling dams, causing rivers to overflow. Can be sudden (flash floods) or extended (river floods). Both are part of the water cycle at extremes."Learn
    We Do (30 min)"Case study: The Millennium Drought and the 2022 floods. Research: What happened? What was the impact? How did people adapt? How does this relate to the water cycle?" Create a comparison table: Drought vs Flood — Causes / Water cycle stage affected / Impact / Response.Compare
    You Do (10 min)Write: "In a drought, less evaporation means less ___ which can lead to ___."Write

    Lesson 3: Water Management (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)"Australia's water management: Desalination plants (Perth, Adelaide, Sydney) — turn salt water into fresh water using energy. Dams and reservoirs — store water from wet periods for dry periods. Water recycling — treated wastewater used for agriculture or drinking (indirect potable reuse). Water restrictions — reduce use during drought. Research one of these in detail. Write a 1-page report: How does it work? What are the pros and cons? How does it relate to the water cycle?"Research
    Share (5 min)Which water management strategy do you think is most important for Australia's future?Discuss

    WEEK 4 — THE ROCK CYCLE

    Lesson 1: The Three Rock Types (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (20 min)"The three rock types: 1. IGNEOUS — Formed from molten rock (magma/lava) that cools and solidifies. If cooled slowly (underground) = coarse texture (granite). If cooled quickly (surface) = fine texture (basalt). 2. SEDIMENTARY — Formed from layers of sediment (sand, mud, shells) compressed over millions of years. Often has visible layers (sandstone, limestone). May contain fossils. 3. METAMORPHIC — Formed when existing rocks are changed by HEAT and PRESSURE (but not melted). Limestone → marble. Shale → slate.Learn
    You Do (30 min)Examine rock samples: Granite (igneous), Basalt (igneous), Sandstone (sedimentary), Limestone (sedimentary), Marble (metamorphic), Slate (metamorphic). For each: Describe texture, appearance, possible origin. Write which type each is with reasoning.Examine
    Share (5 min)How can you tell a sedimentary rock from an igneous rock?Explain

    Lesson 2: Changing Between Types (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"The rock cycle shows how rocks change between types: Igneous rock (from cooling magma) → Weathering/Erosion (broken into sediment) → Sedimentary rock → Heat and Pressure → Metamorphic rock → Melting → Magma → Cool → Igneous rock again. The cycle can start at any point and go in any direction."Learn cycle
    We Do (30 min)"Trace a rock through the cycle: You find a granite rock (igneous). It gets weathered (wind, rain, temperature changes break it into small pieces). These pieces are carried by rivers and deposited in layers. Over millions of years, they compress into sandstone (sedimentary). Heat and pressure from Earth's movements change it into quartzite (metamorphic). If it melts again, it becomes magma — and the cycle continues. Write this story."Trace
    You Do (10 min)Write: "The rock cycle conserves matter because..." / "If Earth's interior cooled completely, the rock cycle would stop because..."Write

    Lesson 3: The Complete Rock Cycle (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)"Create a rock cycle diagram: Draw a circle with arrows showing all possible pathways between the three rock types and magma. Label: Igneous (2 ways), Sedimentary, Metamorphic, Magma. On each arrow, write the PROCESS that causes the change (cooling, heat and pressure, melting, erosion, deposition, compression). Then write a paragraph explaining the cycle."Create diagram
    Share (5 min)Check: Are all possible pathways shown? Are the processes correct?Check

    WEEK 5 — THE CARBON CYCLE

    Lesson 1: Carbon and Life (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (20 min)"CARBON is the element of life. All living things are made of carbon-based molecules — proteins, fats, carbohydrates. Carbon is the backbone of organic chemistry. Carbon cycles through living things and the environment. The carbon cycle shows how carbon moves between: Atmosphere (CO2) / Plants (photosynthesis) / Animals (respiration, eating) / Soil (decomposition, fossil fuels) / Ocean (dissolved CO2, shells)."Learn carbon
    You Do (30 min)"Photosynthesis experiment (if possible): Plant in sealed bag with water, exposed to light. Observe: The plant takes in CO2 from the air and releases O2. Water appears on inside of bag (transpiration). Draw and label what happens. Write: 'Photosynthesis uses ___ and produces ___.' "Experiment
    Share (5 min)Why is carbon called "the element of life"?Explain

    Lesson 2: Carbon Stores and Transfers (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"CARBON STORES (where carbon is kept): Atmosphere (CO2, ~415 ppm), Plants and Animals (living carbon), Soil (dead organic matter, carbon locked in dirt), Oceans (dissolved CO2, marine organisms' shells), Fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas — ancient carbon from millions of years ago). CARBON TRANSFERS (how carbon moves): Photosynthesis (atmosphere → plant), Respiration (plant/animal → atmosphere), Decomposition (dead organism → soil → atmosphere eventually), Combustion (burning fossil fuels → CO2 to atmosphere)."Learn
    We Do (30 min)"Create a carbon cycle diagram: Draw and label: Atmosphere (CO2), Plants (photosynthesis arrow), Animals (respiration arrow), Ocean (absorption arrow), Soil (decomposition arrow), Fossil fuels (combustion arrow). Show arrows in both directions where appropriate (e.g., ocean absorbs CO2 and releases it). Colour code: Blue for ocean, Green for plants, Brown for soil."Create diagram
    You Do (10 min)Write: "Carbon is conserved in the carbon cycle because..." / "If there were no decomposers (bacteria, fungi), the carbon cycle would be affected because..."Write

    Lesson 3: The Carbon Budget (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (45 min)"Carbon budget: The Earth has a natural carbon budget — sources (adding CO2 to atmosphere) and sinks (removing CO2). Natural sources: Respiration, decomposition, volcanic eruptions. Natural sinks: Photosynthesis, ocean absorption. HUMAN additions: Burning fossil fuels (adding CO2) / Deforestation (less photosynthesis = less CO2 removed). Research current data: How much CO2 do humans add vs natural processes? How much does Australia contribute? Write a 1-page report."Research
    Share (10 min)Present your key finding about carbon. What surprised you most?Share

    WEEK 6 — CONNECTING THE CYCLES

    Lesson 1: How Cycles Interconnect (I Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (20 min)"The cycles are CONNECTED. Water cycle + Carbon cycle: Plants absorb water (water cycle) AND carbon (carbon cycle) through photosynthesis. When plants transpire (release water), they also release oxygen. Water + Rock cycle: Water causes weathering of rocks (erosion part of rock cycle). Water carries sediment to form sedimentary rocks. Carbon + Rock cycle: Fossil fuels (carbon store) are formed from ancient organic matter (dead plants/animals) → compressed into coal/oil/gas. Ocean (carbon store) has calcium carbonate shells → limestone (sedimentary rock)."Learn connections
    You Do (30 min)"Draw a concept map: In the centre, write 'Earth's Cycles'. Draw 3 circles: Water cycle, Rock cycle, Carbon cycle. Draw lines between them where they connect. At each connection, write HOW they connect. Colour code the connections."Create map
    Share (5 min)Which cycle is most connected to the others? Why?Discuss

    Lesson 2: Human Impact (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (35 min)"Human impact on cycles: Deforestation → disrupts water cycle (less transpiration, less cloud formation) AND carbon cycle (less CO2 absorbed). Mining → disrupts rock cycle (removes rock faster than natural processes) AND water cycle (contamination). Burning fossil fuels → adds CO2 to atmosphere (carbon cycle) → ocean absorbs more CO2 → becomes more acidic → affects marine organisms with calcium carbonate shells (rock cycle connection). These are interconnected impacts." Research: Choose one human impact and trace its effects through all three cycles. Write: "Deforestation affects the water cycle by... it affects the carbon cycle by... it affects the rock cycle by..."Research
    You Do (10 min)Write: "The most important thing humans can do to protect Earth's cycles is ___ because..."Write

    Lesson 3: Climate Connections (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)"Climate change is caused by disrupting the carbon cycle (adding CO2). This affects the water cycle (more evaporation, more intense rainfall, changed patterns). This affects the rock cycle (more weathering, more erosion in some areas). Connect all three: Rising temperatures (energy in the system) → More evaporation (water cycle) → More CO2 in atmosphere (carbon cycle) → More warming (feedback loop). Draw and label a diagram showing this feedback loop. Write an explanation of 150 words."Create
    Share (5 min)Present your feedback loop diagram. What is the most important connection you found?Present

    WEEK 7 — INVESTIGATION

    Lesson 1: Water Cycle Model (We Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Investigation

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)"Build a model water cycle: Use a large glass jar, a smaller cup inside it, plastic wrap over the top. Put water in the bottom of the jar (representing ocean). Place the small cup inside (representing land). Cover with plastic wrap, seal edges. Place in sunlight (or under a lamp). Observe over 30 minutes: Condensation on the plastic wrap (evaporation + condensation). Water droplets forming and dripping into the cup (precipitation). Measure the water collected. Draw and label your model. Write: 'My model demonstrates the water cycle by showing... The most successful part was...' "Build and observe

    Lesson 2: Rock Cycle Observation (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Investigation

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)"Observation: Grow crystals (optional — can use Epsom salts or sugar). OR examine a large piece of pumice (volcanic rock — igneous with air holes from fast cooling). OR examine a sedimentary rock with visible layers. OR examine a metamorphic rock with banding. For your sample: Describe its appearance in detail. Identify which type of rock it is. Explain HOW it formed using rock cycle knowledge. Draw and label the rock. Write a paragraph explaining the rock cycle process that created your rock."Observe and write

    Lesson 3: Research Presentation (You Do)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Presentation

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (50 min)"Choose one topic: The water cycle in the Australian outback / How coral reefs relate to the carbon cycle and ocean chemistry / How mountains form and break down (rock cycle in action) / The connection between volcanoes and the carbon cycle / How an ice age affects all three cycles. Research and prepare a 3-minute oral presentation with a diagram. Include: What is happening? How does it relate to the cycles? What is the evidence?"Research and present

    ADAPTATIONS

    EAL Students

  • Visual cycle diagrams with labels and arrows
  • Word wall: evaporation, condensation, precipitation, erosion, deposition, metamorphic, photosynthesis, respiration
  • Sentence starters for cycle descriptions: "First, ___. Then, ___. Finally, ___."
  • Colour-coded diagrams (use same colours for same processes across all three cycles)
  • Home language resources for key vocabulary if available

    • Gifted Students

  • Research the carbon cycle in relation to ocean acidification and coral bleaching
  • Investigate deep time carbon cycles (how carbon moved between atmosphere and rocks over billions of years)
  • Explore how the rock cycle relates to plate tectonics
  • Research biochar or carbon capture technology as solutions

    • Students with Additional Needs

  • Focus on one cycle in depth (water cycle) before introducing others
  • Provide pre-labelled diagram templates to fill in
  • Reduce to key vocabulary: evaporation, condensation, rain, rock types
  • One-on-one support for written explanations
  • Extra time
  • Allow oral presentation instead of written for some tasks
