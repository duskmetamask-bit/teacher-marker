Learning Intentions

We are learning to write a persuasive text that presents a clear viewpoint, supports it with structured arguments and evidence, and uses rhetorical devices to persuade the reader.

Identify the author's viewpoint and bias in a persuasive text
Structure a persuasive text: hook, thesis, three arguments, counterargument, conclusion
Use rhetorical devices: rule of three, repetition, emotive language, expert opinion, analogy, rhetorical questions
Analyse how persuasive texts work — in ads, speeches, articles, and visual texts
Write my own persuasive piece (essay, letter, speech, or editorial) with a clear position and supporting evidence
Rhetorical Devices — Key Reference

Six devices students will identify and use throughout the unit

Rule of Three

Things come in threes — they're memorable and satisfying. "Life, liberty and the pursuit of happiness." "They came. They built. They took."

Repetition

Repeating a word or phrase for emphasis. Martin Luther King Jr.: "I have a dream... I have a dream... I have a dream."

Emotive Language

Words that make the reader FEEL something. "Families devastated, communities shattered, workers abandoned without hope."

Expert Opinion

"According to Dr. Jane Smith, a child psychologist..." — citing authorities to strengthen credibility.

Analogy

Comparing something complex to something familiar. "Writing a persuasive essay is like building a house."

Rhetorical Questions

Questions asked for effect, not expecting an answer. "Isn't it time we stopped pretending?" "How many more children must suffer?"

Week 1 — Introduction to Persuasive Writing

Foundation Week
Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)
40 min Assessment
Hook (5 min) Show three images: clean playground vs. littered one, student reading vs. playing, school garden vs. bare concrete. Ask: Which would you prefer? How could you convince someone to agree?
Task (35 min) Cold Task — same prompt as Hot Task (Week 8): Think about something at school that should be changed. Write a persuasive piece (letter, essay, or speech) arguing why this change should happen. Year 5: 250–350 words. Year 6: 300–450 words.
Differentiation: Word bank, sentence starters, and planning web available for EAL/writing support students. Gifted: may write from the perspective of someone opposing the change.
Lesson 2: What is Persuasion? (I Do)
35 min Explicit
Hook (5 min) Say: "Everyone should have a pet." Ask: Do you agree? Disagree? How could I convince you?
Mini-Lesson (15 min) Define: Persuasion = writing or speaking to convince someone to believe or do something. Compare: Persuasion vs. Informative vs. Entertaining. Show three short texts — students identify the purpose.
Analysis (10 min) Identify persuasion in everyday life: TV ads, signs, petitions, speeches, reviews. Why do people try to persuade us?
Share (5 min) Students share one example of persuasion they saw or heard recently.
Lesson 3: Finding the Viewpoint (We Do)
35 min Guided
I Do (10 min) Show a short advertisement. Ask: What does this ad want me to believe? What viewpoint is presented? Whose viewpoint might be missing? Model finding the VIEWPOINT = what side the writer takes.
We Do (15 min) Show two contrasting news headlines about the same event. Create a T-chart: Viewpoint A vs. Viewpoint B. Discuss: How can two writers see the same event so differently?
You Do (10 min) Think: What is YOUR viewpoint on something important to you? Write 2–3 sentences stating your position on ONE thing you care about (homework, screen time, school uniform).

Week 2 — The Rabbits: Perspective and Bias

Mentor Text Week
Lesson 1: Whose Voice is Heard? (I Do)
40 min Explicit
Read + Think (20 min) Read The Rabbits aloud (all pages). Allow time to absorb illustrations. Ask: What do you see? What do you notice? What do you feel?
Mini-Lesson (10 min) Discuss: The Rabbits is about colonisation — told from the perspective of the native animals (bilbies, bandicoots, emus). Ask: Whose viewpoint is presented? What side are Marsden and Tan on? How can you tell?
Analysis (10 min) Find evidence: How does the language make you feel about the rabbits? How are the rabbits shown visually? Create a LIST: things the rabbits do vs. things the native animals do.
Lesson 2: Whose Voice is Absent? (We Do)
40 min Guided
I Do (10 min) Re-read selected pages. Ask: We hear from the native animals. But do we hear from the rabbits? What would the rabbits' side sound like? What is ABSENT from this story?
We Do (15 min) The concept of BIAS = presenting one side while leaving out the other. When an author chooses a perspective, they also choose what NOT to show. If the rabbits told this story, what would change?
You Do (15 min) Write a SHORT paragraph (5–8 sentences) from the rabbits' perspective. Pretend you are one of the rabbit soldiers. What would you say to justify what you are doing? (Exercise in understanding another viewpoint — not an endorsement.)
Lesson 3: What Argument Does the Book Make? (I Do → We Do)
40 min Explicit
I Do (10 min) THESIS = the main argument a writer is trying to prove. Pose: What is Marsden and Tan's thesis in The Rabbits? Model: "The book argues that colonisation was destructive and unfair." / "The book argues that the native animals lost everything."
We Do (15 min) Students work in pairs to identify ONE clear argument the book makes. Write it as a thesis statement. Share with class. Discuss: Is there more than one possible thesis?
You Do (15 min) Think about a topic you feel strongly about. Write your own thesis statement — one sentence that clearly states your position on this issue. (We'll return to this thesis throughout the unit.)

Week 3 — Text Structure: The Persuasive Essay

Structure Week
Lesson 1: Hook and Thesis (I Do)
35 min Explicit
Hook Example (10 min) Show four opening lines — which grabs you? "Many people think homework is good." vs. "Imagine coming home after a seven-hour school day and being told you still have two more hours of work to do." vs. "Homework is a topic that divides opinion." vs. "Homework should be abolished."
Mini-Lesson (15 min) Structure: HOOK → THESIS → ARGUMENT 1 → ARGUMENT 2 → ARGUMENT 3 → COUNTERARGUMENT → CONCLUSION. Thesis must be ARGUEABLE (someone could disagree). Model thesis: "Too much homework harms wellbeing" — argueable. "Homework exists" — NOT argueable.
You Do (10 min) Take the thesis you wrote in Week 2 Lesson 3. Refine it so it is argueable. Write a HOOK — a question, surprising fact, vivid image, or bold statement.
Lesson 2: The Three Arguments (I Do → We Do)
40 min Guided
I Do (10 min) Why THREE arguments? Three is the RULE OF THREE — the most persuasive number. Each argument needs: REASON + EVIDENCE/EXAMPLE. Model: Thesis "School uniforms should be required." Arg 1: creates equality (can't be judged by expensive brands). Arg 2: saves time. Arg 3: builds school pride.
We Do (15 min) Together, plan three arguments for a sample thesis: "Primary school students should not have homework." Choose the three strongest. Write one as a full argument with REASON + EVIDENCE.
You Do (15 min) Return to your thesis. List as many potential arguments as you can (aim for 5–6). Choose the three strongest. Write each one as a heading in your planning document.
Lesson 3: Counterargument and Conclusion (We Do → You Do)
40 min Guided
I Do (10 min) COUNTERARGUMENT = acknowledging the other side before rebutting it. Without: "My view is right and anyone who disagrees is wrong." With: "Some people believe X. However, on closer examination, Y is actually true because..." Why powerful? Shows you've considered both sides — makes YOU more credible.
Mini-Lesson (10 min) CONCLUSION: Don't repeat your thesis. Elevate it. Techniques: Return to your hook / Make a broader statement / Call to action. BAD: "So that's why I think this." GOOD: "If we want students to thrive, not just survive, we must act now."
You Do (20 min) Plan your counterargument (what would someone who disagrees say?) and your conclusion strategy. Complete full outline: Hook / Thesis / Argument 1 / Argument 2 / Argument 3 / Counterargument + Rebuttal / Conclusion.

Week 4 — Rhetorical Devices

Craft Week
Lesson 1: Rule of Three + Repetition (I Do)
35 min Explicit
Hook (5 min) Say: "Read to write, write to read, revise to rise." Ask: What do you notice? Why does it stick?
Mini-Lesson (15 min) Rule of Three: Things come in threes — memorable, satisfying, persuasive. Examples: "Life, liberty and the pursuit of happiness." "Stop, look, and listen." Find in The Rabbits: "They came. They built. They took." Repetition: MLK: "I have a dream... I have a dream... I have a dream."
You Do (15 min) Find two examples of rule of three in ads, speeches, or songs. Write three sentences using rule of three for your persuasive piece. Write one paragraph using repetition for emphasis.
Lesson 2: Emotive Language + Expert Opinion (I Do → We Do)
40 min Guided
I Do (10 min) Emotive Language = words that make the reader FEEL. "The factory closed and people lost their jobs." vs. "The factory closed, leaving families devastated, communities shattered, and hundreds of workers abandoned without hope." Same facts — very different impact. In The Rabbits: "screamed," "burned," "lost," "terrified."
Expert Opinion (10 min) Expert Opinion = citing authorities to strengthen argument. "According to Dr. Jane Smith, a child psychologist..." Why it works: moves from opinion to evidence.
We Do (10 min) Students find one example of expert opinion in a news article or advertisement. Share and discuss: Is this expert credible?
You Do (10 min) Write one paragraph using at least three emotive words. Write one sentence using expert opinion (can be fictional — "According to researchers...").
Lesson 3: Analogy + Rhetorical Questions (I Do → We Do → You Do)
40 min Guided
I Do (10 min) Analogy = comparing something complex to something simpler. "Writing a persuasive essay is like building a house — you need a solid foundation (thesis), strong walls (arguments), and a roof to seal it in (conclusion)." MLK: "The arc of the moral universe is long, but it bends toward justice."
Rhetorical Questions (10 min) Rhetorical Question = asked for effect, not expecting an answer. "Isn't it time we stopped pretending homework helps learning?" "How many more children must suffer before we act?" Find examples in The Rabbits or ads.
You Do (15 min) Write two analogies for your persuasive piece. Write one rhetorical question for your introduction and one for your conclusion.

Week 5 — Analysing Real Persuasive Texts

Analysis Week
Lesson 1: Advertisements (We Do)
40 min Guided
I Do (10 min) Show 3–4 advertisements. For each: What is the HOOK? What is the THESIS (what claim)? What RHETORICAL DEVICES? Model full analysis of one ad.
We Do (15 min) In pairs, analyse a different ad each. Framework: HOOK / THESIS / DEVICES (rule of three, emotive language, expert opinion, analogy, rhetorical question) / COUNTERARGUMENT (is there one?) / TARGET AUDIENCE.
You Do (15 min) Choose your favourite ad from the session. Write a paragraph explaining what makes it effective — or ineffective. Which devices does it use? Who is it trying to persuade?
Lesson 2: Speeches (I Do → We Do)
40 min Guided
I Do (15 min) Play or read excerpt from a famous speech (Greta Thunberg, Malala Yousafzai, or MLK Jr. — age-appropriate). Analyse: What is the thesis? Find: rule of three, emotional moment, rhetorical question, analogy, expert/evidence language.
We Do (15 min) Watch a 2-minute advertisement or short speech. Students complete full analysis framework: Hook / Thesis / Argument 1 / Argument 2 / Argument 3 / Counterargument / Conclusion / Devices / Target audience.
You Do (10 min) Think: What speech would YOU give? What is one issue in your school or community you feel strongly about? Write a one-sentence thesis for that speech.
Lesson 3: News Articles and Opinion Pieces (You Do)
40 min Independent
Mini-Lesson (5 min) NEWS article = presents facts. OPINION PIECE = presents a viewpoint and tries to persuade. Both appear in newspapers. Discuss: news article about a sports match vs. editorial about school funding.
You Do (30 min) Students read a provided opinion article (teacher-selected, age-appropriate). Complete full analysis: VIEWPOINT / THESIS / THREE ARGUMENTS / COUNTERARGUMENT / DEVICES USED / BIAS — whose viewpoint is missing?
Share (5 min) 2–3 students share one insight from their article analysis.

Week 6 — Planning and Drafting

Draft Week
Lesson 1: Plan Your Persuasive Piece (We Do → You Do)
40 min Guided
Mini-Lesson (10 min) Return to your outline from Week 3 Lesson 3. Today we refine and add detail. For EACH of your three arguments: Add a FACT or EXAMPLE. Add EMOTIVE LANGUAGE. Add a RHETORICAL DEVICE. Add EXPERT OPINION if relevant.
We Do (10 min) Model refining Argument 1 for a sample thesis on the board. Show how to expand a bare argument into a full, persuasive paragraph.
You Do (20 min) Complete a full detailed plan: Hook (with device) / Thesis (clearly stated) / Argument 1 (reason + evidence + emotive language + device) / Argument 2 / Argument 3 / Counterargument + rebuttal / Conclusion (strategy chosen).
Lesson 2: Draft — Introduction and Arguments (You Do)
40 min Independent
You Do (35 min) Using your detailed plan, write the DRAFT: INTRODUCTION (hook + thesis) / ARGUMENT 1 / ARGUMENT 2 / ARGUMENT 3. Year 5: 250–350 words. Year 6: 300–450 words. This is a ROUGH DRAFT — focus on getting ideas down.
Share (5 min) 2–3 students share their introduction for feedback.
Differentiation: EAL students may write introduction and one argument only. Gifted students: add a fourth argument or write a second persuasive piece from the opposing viewpoint.
Lesson 3: Draft — Counterargument and Conclusion (You Do)
40 min Independent
You Do (35 min) Complete draft: COUNTERARGUMENT + REBUTTAL / CONCLUSION. Read full draft aloud to yourself. Does it sound persuasive? Does the argument flow?
Peer Feedback (5 min) Swap with a partner. Partner reads draft and answers: Does the HOOK grab you? Is the THESIS clear? Are the THREE ARGUMENTS convincing? Is the COUNTERARGUMENT fair?

Week 7 — Revising and Editing

Polish Week
Lesson 1: Revision — Strengthen Arguments (We Do → You Do)
40 min Guided
Mini-Lesson (10 min) Revision checklist: Have I used at least one rhetorical device in each argument? Have I included emotive language? Rule of three at least once? Expert opinion or fact/evidence? Acknowledged the counterargument fairly? Model improving one weak argument paragraph.
You Do (25 min) Read draft carefully. Highlight each rhetorical device in a different colour (yellow = rule of three, pink = emotive language, blue = expert opinion, green = analogy, orange = rhetorical question). Add devices where missing. Strengthen weak arguments.
Share (5 min) One student shares a revised paragraph for class feedback.
Lesson 2: Editing — Grammar and Vocabulary (You Do)
40 min Independent
Mini-Lesson (10 min) Editing checklist: Capital letters and full stops / Commas in long sentences / Question marks for rhetorical questions / No run-on sentences / Varied sentence starters / Precise vocabulary (replace "good," "bad," "big," "said").
You Do (30 min) Edit full draft using checklist. Use a different coloured pen for each type of correction. Read it aloud — if you stumble, there is likely an error.
Lesson 3: Publishing and Presentation (You Do)
40 min Independent
You Do (35 min) Produce FINAL PUBLISHED VERSION. Options: Neat handwritten / Word processed / Illustrated with images that support your argument. Include a REFERENCES list if you used facts or expert opinions.
Share (5 min) Briefly share: One thing I am proud of in my final piece.

Week 8 — Hot Task and Celebration

Assessment Week
Lesson 1: Hot Task (Assessment)
40 min Assessment
Hook (5 min) Briefly revisit The Rabbits: "We learned to identify viewpoint and bias. We learned to structure an argument. We learned to use rhetorical devices. Today we write."
Task (35 min) SAME TASK AS COLD TASK: Write a persuasive piece arguing whether a change should be made at school. You may use the same topic as Cold Task OR choose a new one. Year 5: 250–350 words. Year 6: 300–450 words.
Lesson 2: Comparing Our Work (We Do)
40 min Guided
We Do (20 min) Compare a Cold Task → Hot Task sample from the same student (with permission). What improved? What changed? Annotate differences on the rubric. Where did rhetorical devices appear? Where did structure improve?
Share (20 min) Students share one specific improvement they noticed in their own writing from Cold Task to Hot Task. What will they carry forward into their next persuasive writing piece?
Lesson 3: Celebration (We Do)
40 min Guided
Celebration (30 min) Read a selection of Hot Task persuasive pieces aloud. Acknowledge individual improvements — specific mentions of growth in structure, devices, voice. Display pieces. Vote on: Most Convincing Argument / Best Hook / Best Use of Rhetorical Devices.
Reflection (10 min) Self-reflection: What is ONE thing I will do differently in my next persuasive writing piece? Write it down. Share with a partner.

AC v9 Cross-Curriculum Priorities

Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Histories and Cultures: The Rabbits is a key text for exploring colonisation from First Nations perspectives. The unit explicitly discusses whose voice is heard and whose is absent — building critical literacy around perspective and bias in historical narratives.

Ethical Understanding: Students engage with the ethics of persuasion, distinguishing between legitimate persuasion and manipulation. The counterargument component builds intellectual empathy.

Intercultural Understanding: By writing from the rabbits' perspective (Week 2 Lesson 2), students develop perspective-taking skills and engage with the experiences of colonised peoples.

EAL/D Students

  • Sentence starter bank for each structural component (hook, thesis, argument, counterargument, conclusion)
  • Allow oral rehearsal of arguments before writing — record and transcribe
  • Word bank: persuasive verbs (argue, claim, demand, urge), transition words (however, although, consequently), emotive vocabulary
  • Provide a partially completed outline to reduce cognitive load
  • Allow home language for initial brainstorming and planning
  • One-on-one conference before each writing session
  • Model annotated persuasive essay as reference
  • Extra time for all tasks; allow typing instead of handwriting

Gifted / Advanced Students

  • Write a counterargument FIRST (from opposing viewpoint), then rebut it
  • Write the same piece in TWO different genres (e.g., speech AND editorial) — analyse what changes
  • Analyse a real political speech or debate — identify all devices and rate effectiveness
  • Write a rebuttal to a published opinion piece
  • Explore the difference between persuasion and manipulation — ethical boundaries
  • Design a poster or advertisement embodying the same argument

Students with NEPs

  • Adjust word count expectations accordingly
  • Provide fully structured graphic organiser with sentence starters for each paragraph
  • Allow oral recording or transcription of ideas
  • Break each lesson into shorter writing blocks (2 × 15 min with a break)
  • Provide a peer writing buddy for drafting sessions
  • Allow text-to-speech for editing
  • Simplify success criteria to: (1) I have a clear thesis, (2) I have three arguments, (3) I have used at least two rhetorical devices
  • Extra time for all writing and assessment tasks

Assessment Summary

Formative Exit tickets, drafts and planning documents (Week 4), teacher conferences during drafting (Week 6), peer feedback using analysis framework (Week 6 Lesson 3)
Summative Cold Task (Week 1) → Hot Task (Week 8) compared using same rubric; published persuasive piece assessed with full rubric; self-reflection with goal-setting
