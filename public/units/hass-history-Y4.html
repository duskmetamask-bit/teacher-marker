Unit Content

Australia: Past, Present, and Future — History Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 4

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum HASS v9 — History (Years 3–4)

Unit Big Idea: Australia's history is complex — it is both the story of ancient Indigenous civilisations and a relatively new colonial nation. Understanding the full depth of Australian history, including its challenges and achievements, helps us understand who we are and who we might become.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Investigate a significant period or event in Australian history and explain its impact on the present

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Investigate a significant period or event in Australian history, evaluate its impact, and explain how it shapes the Australia of today

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to investigate significant periods, events, and people in Australian history, and to evaluate their impact on the Australia of today.

Success Criteria:

  • I can describe at least 3 significant periods or events in Australian history.
  • I can explain at least 3 ways Australian history has shaped the Australia of today.
  • I can describe the perspectives of different groups of people in Australian history.
  • I can use sources (photos, documents, objects) to investigate history.
  • I can sequence key events in Australian history on a timeline.
  • I can evaluate the significance of a historical event or period.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: Australia's Deep History

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Significant Australian History

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Show an image of the Aboriginal flag alongside the Australian flag. "Both flags fly together. Why? What story does this tell about Australia? What do you know about Australia's history?"Observe and wonder
    Introduction (5 min)"In this unit, we will explore Australian history — from 65,000 years ago to today. We will discover that Australia's history is complex, sometimes difficult, and always significant. Understanding our history helps us understand who we are."Listen
    Cold Task (30 min)"Choose ONE significant period or event in Australian history (from images provided or your knowledge). Describe: What happened? When and where? Who was involved? Why did it happen? What impact did it have on Australia today? Write 5–8 sentences with evidence." Year 4 expectation: detailed, evidence-based, clear impact on present.Independent

    Differentiation: Image word bank of Australian history events. Sentence starters: "This event was significant because..." / "It impacted Australia by..." / "Today, we can see this in..."

    Success Criteria Check: Student identifies at least 1 significant period or event and describes its impact on the present.

    Lesson 2: Before 1788

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Hook)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australia's Deep Past (10 min)"Australia had a history for 65,000 years BEFORE British colonisation. Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples had built complex societies, languages, and cultures. When the British arrived in 1788, they did not find an 'empty land' — they found a land that was already deeply known and cared for."Learn
    What Was Australia Like Before 1788? (15 min)"Before 1788, Aboriginal peoples: covered all of Australia / had over 250 language groups / had complex governance systems and laws / managed the land sustainably for 65,000 years / had trade networks spanning the continent / had deep spiritual and cultural lives. This was one of the most sophisticated societies on Earth."Learn
    Living Histories (10 min)"Aboriginal history is LIVING HISTORY — it is not just the past. Many Aboriginal traditions, languages, and practices continue today. Cultural sites, stories, and knowledge are still active. The oldest continuous culture on Earth is still alive."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What was Australia like before 1788? Why is this history important?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 aspects of Australia before 1788 and explain why this history matters.

    Lesson 3: Living Histories — The Present in the Past

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Concept Introduction)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australia's Two Histories (10 min)"Australia has TWO deep histories: 65,000+ years of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander history / 235 years of post-colonisation history (since 1788). Both are Australian history. Both matter. Too often, only the second history is taught — we need to know both."Learn
    What Has Survived? (15 min)"Despite colonisation, much of Aboriginal culture has survived: 150+ languages still spoken / rock art sites across Australia / Indigenous land management integrated with modern science / Indigenous art as a global cultural force / cultural ceremonies still practised / connection to Country maintained despite forced removals."Learn
    Truth-Telling (10 min)"TRUTH-TELLING means honestly acknowledging the full history — including the difficult parts. This is not about blame. It is about understanding the truth of what happened so we can understand who we are today. Truth-telling is part of healing and moving forward."Understand
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: Why do we need to know Australia's full history, including both Aboriginal and post-colonisation history?"Write

    Note: Teach with respect. Recommend Aboriginal Education Officer support.

    Success Criteria Check: Can explain why it is important to know both Aboriginal and post-colonisation history.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Colonial Australia

    Lesson 1: First Contact

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Happened at First Contact? (10 min)"FIRST CONTACT between Aboriginal peoples and British settlers was complex and traumatic. The British arrived with no understanding of the sophisticated societies that existed. They declared the land 'Terra Nullius' — empty land. This was legally and factually wrong."Learn
    Effects of Contact (15 min)"First contact had devastating effects: NEW DISEASES (smallpox, measles, influenza — killed up to 90% of some Aboriginal communities) / VIOLENCE (frontier conflicts, massacres — documented but not yet fully counted) / DISPLACEMENT (people pushed off Country) / LOSS OF LANGUAGE AND CULTURE (children taken, cultural practices suppressed). These effects continue today."Learn
    Different Experiences (10 min)"Different Aboriginal communities had different contact experiences: some communities had sustained peaceful contact / some had immediate violent conflict / some had trade relationships first / some maintained their distance. No single story fits all of Australia."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What happened at first contact between Aboriginal peoples and British settlers? What were the effects?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 effects of first contact on Aboriginal peoples.

    Lesson 2: Early Colonies

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Sydney, Hobart, Brisbane, Perth (10 min)"Britain established colonies at different times: Sydney (1788) / Hobart (1804) / Brisbane (1824) / Perth (1829). Western Australia was founded separately from Swan River Colony (1829) by James Stirling. Each colony had different challenges — Perth was the most isolated."Learn
    Convict vs Free (10 min)"Early Australia was mostly a penal colony. Convicts were sent for crimes (often minor — theft of food). Over time, free settlers arrived. By the 1850s, free settlers outnumbered convicts. But Australia's convict origins shaped our culture: egalitarian (anti-authority), pragmatic, self-reliant."Learn
    Convict Life (10 min)"Convict life was harsh but sometimes surprising: convicts could earn money, buy property, and marry / emancipated convicts (ticket-of-leave) became respected community members / some convicts were better educated than their overseers / the 'convict stain' faded quickly — many of Australia's founders were former convicts."Learn
    Convict Architecture (10 min)"Convict architecture still stands in Australia: Hyde Park Barracks (Sydney) / Port Arthur (Tasmania) / Fremantle Prison (WA). These buildings tell us about convict life — the design, the materials, the purpose. What do these buildings say about convict society?"Examine

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 aspects of early colony life in Australia.

    Lesson 3: Gold and Growth

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Gold Rush Transforms Australia (10 min)"Gold discovered in 1851 (Bathurst, NSW; Ballarat and Bendigo, VIC) was one of the most transformative events in Australian history. In 10 years, Australia's population tripled. New cities and towns were built. Australia became one of the world's richest countries per capita."Learn
    Who Came? (15 min)"The gold rush brought people from everywhere: Britain, Europe, China, USA, New Zealand. By 1861, 25% of Victoria's population was Chinese. This diversity caused tension — the White Australia Policy (1901) was partly a response. Chinese people contributed enormously to Australian development despite facing discrimination."Learn
    Chinese Australians (10 min)"Chinese Australians have been part of Australia since the gold rush. They established businesses, built railways, worked in market gardens. Their contribution to Australia is enormous and often under-acknowledged. The White Australia Policy discriminated against them for 70 years."Learn
    Draw a Gold Rush Scene (5 min)"Draw a gold field scene. Label at least 3 things you see in the scene."Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the gold rush and at least 2 ways it changed Australia.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: Federation and Nationhood

    Lesson 1: The Road to Federation

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Why Did Colonies Unite? (10 min)"By the 1890s, Australia's 6 British colonies were considering uniting into one nation. Why? Defence (a united country is easier to defend) / Economic reasons (free trade between colonies) / National pride / Pressure from labour movements / A desire to be seen as a mature, self-governing nation. But there were also debates about Indigenous inclusion."Learn
    The Federation Process (15 min)"The road to Federation: 1891 — First draft constitution rejected / 1897–98 — Second constitutional convention, national debate / 1899 — Votes in each colony / 1 January 1901 — Australian nation born at Centennial Park, Sydney. Sir Henry Parkes (NSW) called 'Father of Federation' — he pushed for it for 30 years."Learn
    The Constitution (10 min)"Australia's Constitution (1901): established federal government / powers divided between states and federal government / original constitution EXCLUDED Indigenous Australians from counting in the census. This was changed in 1967."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is Federation? How did it happen? Who were the key figures?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe Federation and at least 1 reason why it occurred.

    Lesson 2: Life in the New Nation

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Early 20th Century Australia (10 min)"In the early 1900s, Australia was: a young, optimistic nation / predominantly British in population and culture / agrarian (wool and wheat were major exports) / politically progressive in some areas (secret ballot, compulsory voting, women's suffrage) / but deeply discriminatory in others (White Australia Policy, Indigenous exclusion)."Learn
    The White Australia Policy (15 min)"The WHITE AUSTRALIA POLICY (1901–1973): restricted non-European immigration. Implemented through the Immigration Restriction Act 1901 (known as the 'White Australia' law). Tests for potential migrants (written in any language except European ones). It was designed to keep Australia 'British.' It was discriminatory and has been formally apologised for."Learn
    Women's Suffrage (10 min)"Australia led the world in women's suffrage. South Australia (1894) and Western Australia (1899) were among the first places in the world where women could vote. By 1902, women could vote in federal elections. Australia was the second nation in the world (after New Zealand) to grant women's suffrage."Learn
    What Does This Tell Us? (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What does early 20th century Australia tell us about our values? What was good? What was problematic?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 aspects of early 20th century Australia including the White Australia Policy.

    Lesson 3: The ANZAC Legend

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is the ANZAC Legend? (10 min)"ANZAC Day (25 April) commemorates Australian and New Zealand Army Corps soldiers who fought at Gallipoli in 1915. The 'ANZAC legend' is the idea that Australian soldiers were uniquely brave, tough, egalitarian, and resilient. This legend became central to Australian national identity."Learn
    The Gallipoli Campaign (15 min)"Gallipoli (1915): Allied forces (British, French, Australian, New Zealand, Indian) tried to force the Ottoman Empire out of WWI by capturing Constantinople (Istanbul). It was a military failure — but Australian soldiers distinguished themselves at Lone Pine and the Nek. 8,700 Australians died at Gallipoli."Learn
    Why Does It Matter? (10 min)"The ANZAC legend matters because: it was Australia's first major engagement as a nation / it created a sense of national identity / the 'digger' soldier became an Australian archetype / it is a story of sacrifice / it created institutions (RSL,Returned and Services League) that still serve veterans."Learn
    The Controversy (5 min)"Some historians argue the ANZAC legend has been OVERUSED — that it promotes militarism, ignores the horror of war, and is used for political purposes. Others argue it is a legitimate story of courage and sacrifice. Both views deserve consideration."Discuss

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the ANZAC legend, at least 2 facts about Gallipoli, and explain why it is significant.

    WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Social Movements

    Lesson 1: Women's Suffrage

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Fight for Women's Voting Rights (10 min)"In the 1800s, women in Australia were not allowed to vote. Suffragists (women and men who campaigned for the vote) used: petitions, speeches, marches, civil disobedience (hunger strikes, chaining themselves to fences). They faced ridicule, arrest, and abuse."Learn
    Australian Suffrage Timeline (15 min)"South Australia: women gained the vote in 1894 (federal 1902) / Western Australia: women gained the vote in 1899 (federal 1902) / Australia overall: federal suffrage in 1902 / New Zealand (1893) was first in the world, then South Australia. Australia led the world in women's political rights."Learn
    Key Figures (10 min)"Key figures: MARY LEE (SA, suffragist, founded the Women's Suffrage Society) / CLARICE EDER (WA, suffragist, journalist) / ADA EVANS (NSW, suffragist). Many others — often ordinary women who organised extraordinary campaigns."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What was the women's suffrage movement? How did Australian women win the vote? Why does it matter?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the women's suffrage movement in Australia and at least 2 key facts about its achievement.

    Lesson 2: Indigenous Rights

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    1967 — The Referendum (10 min)"In 1967, Australians voted in a REPUBLICAN REFERENDUM (a vote to change the Constitution). The question: Should Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples be counted in the census and should the federal government make laws about them? 90.77% voted YES — one of the highest 'yes' votes in Australian history."Learn
    What Changed (10 min)"1967 changed: Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples were counted in the census / the federal government could now make laws specifically for Indigenous peoples / it was a symbolic moment of recognition / it enabled the Aboriginal Loans Commission (1960s) and land rights frameworks (1970s). It was not perfect — it did not grant rights directly, but it was a crucial step."Learn
    Ongoing Struggles (10 min)"1967 did NOT fix everything: the Stolen Generations (children removed from families 1910–1970) were not addressed / land rights were still needed / Indigenous health, education, and incarceration gaps were not fixed / the referendum was one step in a longer journey."Learn
    Key Figures (10 min)"Key figures in Indigenous rights: CHARLES PERKINS (Aboriginal activist, civil rights leader, first Indigenous university graduate) / EDDIE MABO (land rights hero — Mabo decision 1992 recognised Native Title) / CHARLOTTE RING (activist for Indigenous women's rights). Many others — elders, community members, activists."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the 1967 referendum and at least 2 ways Indigenous rights have been fought for.

    Lesson 3: Migration and Multiculturalism

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australia's Migration History (10 min)"Australia's migration history: 1788 — predominantly British / 1850s gold rush — more diverse / 1901 — White Australia Policy / 1945–1970s — Post-WWII immigration from Europe (Displaced Persons from WWII, Italian, Greek, Dutch, German) / 1970s — end of White Australia Policy / 1980s onwards — Asian migration, humanitarian refugees / Today — most culturally diverse in Australian history."Learn
    The White Australia Policy Ends (10 min)"The Whitlam government (1972–1975) abolished the White Australia Policy: Gough Whitlam (Labor PM) / 1973: racistselection criteria changed / 1975: Racial Discrimination Act (made it illegal to discriminate on basis of race). This was a profound shift in Australian identity."Learn
    Multiculturalism (10 min)"MULTICULTURALISM: the official recognition and celebration of Australia's cultural diversity. The 1970s onwards: ethnic broadcasting (SBS TV began 1980) / multicultural policy / recognition of Indigenous cultures / acceptance of diverse religions, foods, languages, and traditions. Today: 30% of Australians born overseas / 300+ languages spoken."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: How has Australia's migration history changed? What is multiculturalism? Why does it matter?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students write about their own family's migration story. Gifted students: research one specific migration wave.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 ways Australia's migration history has changed and explain why multiculturalism matters.

    WEEK 5 — EXPLAIN: Australia and the World

    Lesson 1: World War I's Impact

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    WWI and Australia (10 min)"Australia joined WWI as part of the British Empire (1914–1918). 416,000 Australians served (out of 5 million population). 60,000 died. 152,000 were wounded. Australia had one of the highest casualty rates per capita of any participating nation."Learn
    Key Battles (15 min)"Gallipoli (1915): 8,700 deaths / The Western Front (France, 1916–1918): 52,000 deaths / Passchendaele (Belgium, 1917): 5,250 deaths in one battle. The Western Front was one of the most brutal campaigns in human history — trenches, machine guns, artillery."Learn
    Social Impact (10 min)"WWI impacted Australia: grief in every community (every family affected) / changed relationship with Britain (loyalty questioned) / women's suffrage strengthened (women took on men's roles) / changed the economy (post-war reconstruction) / created the Returned and Services League (RSL) / the ANZAC legend."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: How did WWI affect Australia? What was the human cost?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe WWI and at least 2 of its impacts on Australia.

    Lesson 2: World War II and Post-War

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    WWII and Australia (10 min)"WWII (1939–1945) changed Australia fundamentally. Japan attacked Darwin (1942) — first time Australian mainland was bombed. The threat of invasion forced Australia to rethink its security. Australia turned to the United States (ANZUS alliance, 1951) as its primary security partner — a break from full dependence on Britain."Learn
    The Post-War Immigration Boom (10 min)"Post-WWII, Australia launched a massive immigration program: Displaced Persons (refugees from Europe, 1947–1950s) / Assisted passage scheme (paying for people to come to Australia) / Over 7 million migrants arrived 1945–1985 / European migration (Italian, Greek, Dutch, German, Polish) / By 1970s, Asian migration was beginning."Learn
    Why the Immigration Program? (10 min)"Australia launched mass immigration for: POPULATION (Australia was sparsely populated, vulnerable to being absorbed by larger neighbours) / ECONOMIC GROWTH (more workers, more consumers) / DEFENCE (more people, harder to invade) / It was also partly about 'populate or perish' — a phrase used at the time."Learn
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: Why did Australia launch a big post-war immigration program? How did it change Australia?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the post-WWII immigration program and at least 2 ways it changed Australia.

    Lesson 3: Australia in the Asia-Pacific

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australia's Changing Relationships (10 min)"Australia's relationship with Asia has transformed: Historically: Britain was 'home,' Asia was 'distant and strange' / 1940s–1970s: Cold War — Asia seen as a threat / 1970s onwards: engagement with Asia through trade (China, Japan, Korea) and diplomacy / Today: our biggest trade partner (China) and our most important strategic relationship (USA) are both in our region."Learn
    Key Relationships (15 min)"Australia's key relationships: CHINA (largest trade partner, but complex political tensions) / USA (primary security ally, ANZUS) / JAPAN (major trade partner, historical reconciliation) / INDONESIA (nearest neighbour, complex relationship) / INDIA (growing trade and strategic partnership) / PACIFIC ISLAND nations (climate vulnerability, aid relationships). These relationships shape Australian foreign policy."Learn
    The Pacific (10 min)"Australia's Pacific neighbourhood: small island nations that are among the most climate-vulnerable in the world / Tuvalu, Kiribati, Fiji — sea level rise threatens their very existence / Australia is the largest aid donor to the Pacific / Australia's climate policy is a significant issue in Pacific relations."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: How has Australia's relationship with Asia and the Pacific changed? Why does it matter?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 of Australia's key international relationships and explain how they have changed.

    WEEK 6 — ELABORATE: Significant Australians

    Lesson 1: Political Leaders

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Key Australian Political Leaders (15 min)"Significant political leaders: EDMUND BARTON (first PM, helped write the Constitution) / ALFRED DEAKIN (second PM, 'the golden age') / BENNY (BennyMaui) (Māori leader at Gallipoli — more complex narrative needed) / GOUGH WHITLAM (transformed Australian culture — ended White Australia, created Medicare, independent foreign policy) / PAUL KEATING (pushed reconciliation, Asia engagement) / JOHN HOWARD (longest-serving PM, 1996–2007) / KEVIN RUDD (apologised to the Stolen Generations, 2008)."Learn
    What Makes a Leader Significant? (10 min)"Leaders are significant when they: make major changes (Whitlam's changes were profound) / lead at crucial moments (Howard during the 1996 gun reforms after Port Arthur) / represent their people well / leave a lasting legacy (institutions, laws, changed norms). Not all leaders are remembered positively."Learn
    Leadership Debate (10 min)"Not all significant leaders are admired. Some leaders made decisions that were harmful to some groups. We must evaluate leaders critically — their achievements AND their failures."Discuss
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What makes a political leader significant? Give one example of a leader and what they achieved."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 significant Australian political leaders and evaluate their significance.

    Lesson 2: Changemakers

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australian Changemakers (15 min)"CHANGEMAKERS: people who made things better by challenging the status quo. BONNIE CLEMENT (WA woman who founded the 'Coals to Newcastle' scheme for working women's holidays) / CHARLES PERKINS (civil rights — walked into 'the Roses' shop in 1965, challenged discrimination, fought for Indigenous rights) / STELLA NORFORTH (established Australian Women in Agriculture) / FIONA STANLEY (researcher, child health, HIV research)."Learn
    Contemporary Changemakers (10 min)"Today's changemakers: TANYA HOSCH (Indigenous legal rights advocate) / MARLENA KATISCH (climate activist, school striker) / DR. MIRIAM ROSLY (advocate for Muslim women in Australia) / DR. JACQUI MATEKEREI (Pacific climate champion). Changemakers are all around us."Learn
    What Changemakers Do (10 min)"Changemakers: identify injustice / speak up / take action / build coalitions / persist despite opposition / create new norms. Changemakers are often not in positions of formal power — they are ordinary people who refuse to accept injustice."Learn
    Draw (5 min)"Draw or describe one Australian changemaker and explain what they changed."Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 Australian changemakers and explain what they changed.

    Lesson 3: Local Heroes

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Local Heroes Matter (10 min)"Not all significant Australians are famous nationally. In every community, there are LOCAL HEROES — people who changed things in their own place. These are often the people who make the most difference in everyday lives."Learn
    Types of Local Heroes (15 min)"Local heroes: local activists who changed council policy / sports coaches who gave opportunities to disadvantaged youth / teachers who changed the lives of students / volunteers who built community resources / health workers who served their community / Indigenous elders who preserved culture."Learn
    Research Local History (10 min)"We are going to find local heroes in our area. Using local resources (council archives, library, historical society, Trove), find one person or event that is significant in our LOCAL history. It may not be famous nationally, but it matters HERE."Research
    Present (5 min)"Share one thing you found about our local history."Share

    Differentiation: EAL students draw a local hero or significant local site. Gifted students: create a local history guide.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 1 significant local hero or event in their community's history.

    WEEK 7 — ELABORATE + EVALUATE: Legacy Investigation

    Lesson 1: Legacy of the Past

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Legacies Do We Live With? (15 min)"Every period of Australian history has left us LEGACIES — things that are still here because of what happened before. GOOD legacies: women's right to vote, rule of law, multiculturalism / CHALLENGING legacies: Indigenous disadvantage (roots in dispossession), the gap between rich and poor, environmental degradation."Learn
    Legacy Mapping (15 min)"Map the legacies of Australian history: British legal and political system / English language / ANZAC legend / White Australia Policy's legacy in racism / Indigenous knowledge of land management / Post-war immigration's diversity."Learn
    What Legacy Do We Choose? (10 min)"We don't simply inherit legacies — we can choose what to celebrate and what to change. Truth-telling about the past does not mean rejecting it. It means understanding it fully so we can build a better future."Understand
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is one legacy of Australian history in your life today? Is this a legacy worth keeping or changing?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 legacies of Australian history and evaluate whether they are worth keeping.

    Lesson 2: Investigate a Period or Event

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Choose Your Investigation (5 min)"Choose one significant period or event from Australian history to investigate in depth. Options: Pre-1788 Indigenous history / First contact / Gold rush / Federation / WWI / 1967 referendum / Post-war migration / Another of your choice."Choose
    Investigation (25 min)Students investigate using provided sources and their own research: What happened? When and where? Who was involved? Why did it happen? What were different perspectives? What is its lasting impact?Investigate
    Record (10 min)Students record: Key facts / Sources used / Perspectives examined / Legacy today.Record

    Success Criteria Check: Completes an investigation of a significant period or event using at least 2 sources and at least 2 perspectives.

    Lesson 3: Prepare Hot Task

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Preparation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hot Task Preview (5 min)"For tomorrow's Hot Task: Present your investigation. Cover: What is your period or event? What happened? Who was involved? Why did it happen? What were different perspectives? What is its lasting impact on Australia today? Use at least 2 sources."Preview
    Plan and Prepare (30 min)Students plan and prepare their presentations using their investigation notes.Plan
    Rehearse (5 min)"Rehearse telling your investigation to someone. Can you explain the lasting impact?"Rehearse

    Differentiation: EAL students may present with visual support. Gifted students: present on a topic not covered in class.

    Success Criteria Check: Completes a Hot Task preparation plan.

    WEEK 8 — EVALUATE: Hot Task and Reflection

    Lesson 1: Hot Task — Historical Investigation Presentation

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Summative Assessment

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Set Up (5 min)"Today is your Hot Task. Present your investigation of a significant period or event in Australian history. You have 5 minutes. Use your sources. Explain the lasting impact."Listen
    Presentations (35 min)Students present their investigations. Teacher uses checklist: Historical accuracy? / Sources used? / Perspectives examined? / Legacy explained?Present

    Assessment Note: Use rubric to assess. Compare to Cold Task for growth analysis.

    Lesson 2: Cold Task vs Hot Task — Growth Reflection

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Self-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Compare (15 min)Place Cold Task next to Hot Task. "Use a green texta to mark 3 things you did BETTER. Use a red texta to mark 1 thing to improve."Compare
    Growth Statement (10 min)"I have grown as an Australian history investigator because..." Write 4–6 sentences.Write
    Share (10 min)Volunteers share one insight about Australian history.Share

    DIFFERENTIATION NOTES

    EAL Learners

  • Home Culture Research: Students may investigate their family's connection to Australian history (migration, settlement).
  • Visual Presentations: Focus on visual timelines, maps, and images.
  • Partner Support: Paired for research activities.
  • Cultural Sensitivity: Respect all perspectives on Australian history.

    • Gifted Learners

  • Research Extension: Investigate a specific period in depth (e.g., the Stolen Generations, one gold field, the 1967 referendum).
  • Comparative Essay: Write a comparative essay on two periods of Australian history.
  • Local History Project: Create a full local history guide.

    • Students with NEPs

  • Reduced Written Expectation: 5–7 sentences for written tasks.
  • Oral Option: Students may present verbally.
  • Extra Time: Additional time for all tasks.
  • Word Banks: Provided for all written tasks.
  • Scaffolded Templates: Timeline template, investigation template.
