Unit Content

Our Land, Our Future — Geography Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 4

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum HASS v9 — Geography (Years 3–4)

Unit Big Idea: The world faces significant environmental challenges that require understanding, action, and global cooperation. Young people can make a difference by understanding these challenges and taking action in their own places.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Investigate an environmental challenge in two places and explain how people are responding

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Investigate and evaluate a local environmental challenge, propose solutions, and explain how connections between places can help address the challenge

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to investigate environmental challenges facing places around the world and in Australia, and to propose and evaluate solutions that consider sustainability and global cooperation.

Success Criteria:

  • I can describe at least 2 significant environmental challenges facing places today (climate change, biodiversity loss, water scarcity, deforestation).
  • I can explain how and why environmental challenges affect different places differently.
  • I can evaluate at least 2 solutions to environmental challenges and assess their sustainability.
  • I can describe how places are connected and how global cooperation can address environmental challenges.
  • I can take action in my local place to contribute to environmental solutions.
  • I can use sources (data, maps, reports, photos) to investigate environmental issues.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: Our Planet Under Pressure

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Environmental Challenges

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Show two contrasting images: one pristine natural environment vs one showing environmental damage (e.g., coral bleaching, deforestation, flood aftermath). "What do you notice? What is happening to our planet? What questions do you have?"Observe and wonder
    Introduction (5 min)"In this unit, we will investigate environmental challenges facing our planet. We will look at how places are under pressure, how people are responding, and what we — as young people — can do about it. This is one of the most important topics of our time."Listen
    Cold Task (30 min)"Choose two places facing environmental challenges (from images provided or your knowledge). For each: (1) Name the environmental challenge. (2) Describe how it is affecting the place. (3) Explain how people in the place are responding. (4) Write 2 sentences: What should people do differently? Is the response sustainable?" Year 4 expectation: 6–10 sentences, clear environmental focus, evaluation of responses.Independent

    Differentiation: Image word bank of environmental challenges. Sentence starters: "The environmental challenge in [place] is..." / "People are responding by..." / "This response is [sustainable/unsustainable] because..." / "A better solution would be..."

    Success Criteria Check: Student identifies at least 2 places with environmental challenges and describes at least 1 response.

    Lesson 2: Environmental Challenges of Our Time

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Hook)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Big Five Challenges (15 min)"Scientists and world leaders have identified 5 major environmental challenges facing our planet: (1) CLIMATE CHANGE — rising temperatures, more extreme weather / (2) BIODIVERSITY LOSS — species extinction faster than any time in history / (3) WATER SCARCITY — clean water running out in many places / (4) DEFORESTATION — forests cut down faster than they regrow / (5) POLLUTION — plastic in oceans, chemicals in soil and water."Listen
    Challenge Cards (15 min)Distribute challenge cards (one per pair). Each card has: name of challenge, brief description, 2–3 example places affected, key statistic. Pairs share their challenge with the class.Explore cards
    Which Affects Us? (10 min)"Which of these 5 challenges affect Australia? Which affect our local area? Which affect places far from us? Are some challenges connected to each other?"Discuss local relevance
    Inquiry Questions (5 min)"Write your 3 inquiry questions for this unit. Example: 'How does climate change affect the Great Barrier Reef?' 'What can I do about plastic pollution?' 'How does deforestation in the Amazon affect ME in Australia?'"Write inquiry questions

    Success Criteria Check: Can name all 5 major environmental challenges and identify at least 2 places affected by at least 1 challenge.

    Lesson 3: Exploring Data — How Do We Know?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Concept Introduction)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Data Is Evidence (10 min)"Scientists use DATA — numbers and measurements — to understand environmental challenges. Temperature records, species population counts, water levels, deforestation satellite data — all data helps us understand what is happening."Listen
    Data Exploration (15 min)Show real data sets: Global temperature anomaly graph (1880–2024, showing warming trend) / Australian species extinction list / Great Barrier Reef coral bleaching data / WA water usage data. For each: What does the data show? What is the trend over time?Interpret data
    Reading Graphs (10 min)"Line graphs show trends over time. Bar graphs compare things. Pie charts show proportions. What type of graph is each? What does the data tell us?"Learn graph types
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Write 2 sentences about what ONE of the data sets tells us about an environmental challenge."Write

    Differentiation: EAL students interpret data through image cards. Gifted students: find and bring an environmental data source for the class.

    Success Criteria Check: Can read a line graph showing environmental data and describe what the trend shows.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Climate Change and Its Effects

    Lesson 1: Understanding Climate Change

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Weather vs Climate (Again) (5 min)"Remember: weather is day-to-day conditions. Climate is the usual pattern over long time. Climate CHANGE means the PATTERN is shifting — the baseline is moving."Review
    The Greenhouse Effect (15 min)"Our atmosphere has gases (CO2, methane) that trap heat from the sun — like a blanket. This is natural and keeps Earth warm enough for life. But since the Industrial Revolution (1760s), humans have added MORE gases by burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas). More gases → more heat trapped → warming Earth."Understand mechanism
    Evidence (10 min)Show data: CO2 levels (280 ppm pre-industrial to 420+ ppm today) / global temperature rise (~1.1°C above pre-industrial) / sea level rise (ice melting, thermal expansion) / Arctic sea ice decline (satellite data). "Scientists have measured all of this. The evidence is clear."Observe data
    Why Should Australia Care? (10 min)"Australia is warming faster than the global average. We already see: more extreme heat events, longer fire seasons, coral bleaching, drying in the south-west. WA's climate has already shifted."Local relevance

    Success Criteria Check: Can explain the greenhouse effect in simple terms and name at least 2 pieces of evidence for climate change.

    Lesson 2: Effects on Places Around the World

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Effects Are Everywhere (10 min)"Climate change affects EVERY place on Earth differently. Warmer places get even hotter. Cold places lose ice. Wet places get more rain (and floods). Dry places get even drier (and more drought)."Listen
    Case Studies (20 min)Show effects in 4 places: PACIFIC ISLANDS (Tuvalu, Kiribati — sea level rising, islands flooding, people becoming climate refugees) / ARCTIC (ice melting, polar bears losing habitat, permafrost thawing) / SAHARA EXPANDING (Sahel region — desert spreading south, drought, crop failure) / AUSTRALIA (Great Barrier Reef bleaching, longer fire seasons, drying south-west, more cyclones in north).Observe and note
    The Most Vulnerable Suffer Most (5 min)"Who suffers most from climate change? Poorer countries that contributed least to the problem. Pacific Island nations produce almost no CO2 but face disappearing. This is a global JUSTICE issue."Understand justice dimension
    Climate Impact Map (5 min)Students create a simple impact map: place → effect → who is affected.Map

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 effects of climate change in different places and explain who is most affected.

    Lesson 3: Climate Change in Our Local Place

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    WA Is Already Changing (10 min)"Western Australia has already experienced measurable climate change. The south-west has warmed by ~1°C since 1970. Rainfall has decreased by ~20% in the south-west. Fire seasons are longer. The Indian Ocean is warming, affecting marine life."Listen
    Local Evidence (15 min)Show data: Perth dam levels (declining over decades) / bushfire frequency and severity in WA / Great Western Woodlands health data / marine heatwave events off WA coast. "This is not distant — it is happening HERE."Observe
    What It Means for Us (10 min)"What does this mean for WA? Less water in dams → more desalination dependency / More fire danger → more bushfire risk / Ocean warming → fish migration (some species moving south) / Less rain → impacts on farming (Wheat Belt already drying)."Discuss
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: How has climate change already affected our local area? What might it mean for the future?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students draw climate effects on the local area. Gifted students: create a climate impact report for a local area.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 effects of climate change that are already occurring in WA.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: Biodiversity Under Threat

    Lesson 1: What is Biodiversity?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Biodiversity Defined (10 min)"Biodiversity is the variety of ALL life on Earth — the DIFFERENT species of plants, animals, fungi, and microorganisms, the GENETIC differences within species, and the ECOSYSTEMS they form. A healthy ecosystem has high biodiversity."Listen
    Why Biodiversity Matters (10 min)"Biodiversity provides: FOOD (crops, livestock, fish) / MEDICINE (many medicines come from nature) / CLEAN AIR AND WATER (ecosystems filter our air and water) / POLLINATION (bees and insects pollinate our crops) / FLOOD AND EROSION CONTROL (wetlands, mangroves). Biodiversity is our LIFE SUPPORT SYSTEM."Understand importance
    Web of Life (15 min)"All species are connected — like a web. Remove one strand and the whole web weakens. If bees disappear, many crops fail. If apex predators disappear, prey species overpopulate and destroy vegetation. EVERYTHING is connected."Understand interconnection
    Biodiversity Hotspots (5 min)"Biodiversity hotspots are places with huge species variety AND high threat of destruction. Australia is one of them — 87% of our mammals are found nowhere else. The Amazon, Madagascar, and Cape Floristic Region (South Africa) are others."Note

    Success Criteria Check: Can define biodiversity and explain at least 2 reasons why it is important.

    Lesson 2: Australian Species Crisis

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australia Has a Problem (10 min)"Australia has the highest rate of MAMMAL extinction in the world. Since European arrival (1788), we have lost over 100 species — more than any other country. We also have one of the highest rates of plant and animal extinction overall."Listen
    Why Are Species Disappearing? (15 min)5 main pressures: (1) HABITAT LOSS — clearing for farming, cities, mining / (2) INTRODUCED SPECIES — rabbits, foxes, cats, pigs destroy habitat and prey on native animals / (3) CLIMATE CHANGE — changing rainfall, more fires / (4) POLLUTION — chemicals, plastic / (5) OVERHARVESTING — fishing too much, logging too fast.Learn pressures
    Case Study: The Thylacine (5 min)"The thylacine (Tasmanian tiger) is our most famous extinction. Hunted, lost habitat to farming, introduced diseases. Last known living thylacine died in Hobart Zoo in 1936. It is GONE. This happened within 100 years of European arrival."Note extinction
    Species We Must Save Now (10 min)Show images: Koala (threatened by habitat loss and climate) / Black Cockatoo (habitat loss, food scarcity) / Maui's dolphin (New Zealand, critically endangered, < 60 remain) / Leadbeater's Possum (Victoria, critically endangered). "These are the species we are fighting to save right now."Observe

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 3 reasons why species in Australia are disappearing and at least 1 threatened species.

    Lesson 3: Protecting Species — What Can Be Done?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Protection Strategies (15 min)"What can be done? (1) PROTECTED AREAS — national parks and marine parks / (2) HABITAT RESTORATION — replanting native vegetation / (3) INTRODUCED SPECIES CONTROL — baiting programs for foxes and cats / (4) BREEDING PROGRAMS — captive breeding for critically endangered species / (5) CORRIDORS — connecting fragmented habitats so animals can move."Learn
    Case Study: Bilby Recovery (10 min)"The bilby was almost extinct by the 1990s. Programs that saved it: feral animal control (cats and rabbits), habitat protection, breeding programs, reintroduction to predator-free areas. It is still threatened but RECOVERING. This is proof that conservation works when we act."Understand success
    WA Examples (10 min)"In WA: Western Shield ( Dept of Biodiversity, Conservation and Attractions) runs one of the world's largest feral animal control programs / Chuditch (western quoll) recovery program / Noongar bird monitoring and land management. Indigenous knowledge integrated with Western science."Learn local
    What Can We Do? (5 min)"Write 2–3 things that can be done to protect local species in our area."Write

    Differentiation: EAL students create a species protection poster. Gifted students: research one specific species recovery program in detail.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 conservation strategies and explain at least 1 success story.

    WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Water Scarcity and Management

    Lesson 1: Global Water Crisis

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Water Is Life (10 min)"Water is essential for all life — humans, animals, and plants. But only 3% of Earth's water is fresh water, and most of THAT is frozen in ice caps. Less than 1% is accessible for use. Water scarcity — not having enough clean fresh water — affects over 2 billion people."Listen
    Water Scarcity Map (10 min)Show global water stress map: HIGH STRESS: Middle East (Saudi Arabia, Yemen, Jordan), North Africa (Libya, Egypt), India, parts of China / MODERATE: Southern Europe, parts of USA, Australia / LOW: Canada, Russia, parts of South America, parts of Africa. "Water-stressed areas are where demand exceeds supply."Observe
    Why Water Scarcity Happens (10 min)"Water scarcity is caused by: POPULATION GROWTH (more people = more demand) / CLIMATE CHANGE (drier conditions in some regions) / POLLUTION (contaminating fresh water sources) / POOR MANAGEMENT (wasting water, leaky infrastructure) / AGRICULTURAL USE (70% of all fresh water is used for farming)."Learn causes
    Write (10 min)"Write 3–4 sentences: What is causing water scarcity in places like the Middle East? How is it affecting people there?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 causes of global water scarcity and identify regions with high water stress.

    Lesson 2: Water in Australia

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australia Is the Driest Inhabited Continent (10 min)"Australia is the driest inhabited continent. We have low average rainfall, high evaporation (due to heat), highly variable rainfall (droughts and floods), and our rivers are relatively small. This makes water management critical."Listen
    Water Sources in WA (15 min)"In Perth and south-west WA: DAM WATER (harvested rainfall, now declining) / GROUNDWATER (underground water, used extensively) / DESALINATION ( seawater, 3 plants in WA including Perth's Southern Seawater Desalination Plant, produces 45% of Perth's water) / WATER RECYCLING (indirect and direct). Our water security is diverse."Learn
    Water Wise in WA (10 min)"Western Australia has been proactive: Water Corporation's waterwise programs / 40+ litres per person per day target / rainwater tanks / verge gardens / Waterwise councils program. Perth is one of the few Australian cities that avoided severe water restrictions despite drying trend."Learn
    Compare to Other Cities (5 min)"Cape Town, South Africa nearly ran out of water in 2018 — 'Day Zero' almost happened. Melbourne has had prolonged water restrictions. Perth managed better. Why? Diversified sources and waterwise behaviour."Compare

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 2 of WA's water sources and explain at least 1 reason why WA's water management is relatively strong.

    Lesson 3: Indigenous Water Knowledge

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Indigenous Water Knowledge Is Ancient (10 min)"Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples have managed water for over 65,000 years. They developed sophisticated understanding of: seasonal water cycles / underground water sources / fish and water plant management / flood and drought cycles."Listen
    Examples of Indigenous Water Management (15 min)Show: Aboriginal fish traps (stone structures in rivers that guided fish into controlled areas, used sustainably) / Fire management affecting waterway health / Scarred trees showing water levels and cultural sites / Connection between cultural burning and waterway regeneration.Observe
    Integration with Modern Science (10 min)"Modern science is now recognising what Indigenous peoples have known for millennia. Indigenous water monitoring programs are now used alongside Western science in many parts of Australia. The West Australian Kimberley's Indigenous water monitoring program is a world-class example."Understand integration
    Respectful Reflection (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What does Indigenous water knowledge teach us about managing water sustainably?"Write

    Note: This lesson should be taught with respect. Recommend consultation with local Aboriginal education support.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 examples of Indigenous water management practices and explain why they are valuable.

    WEEK 5 — EXPLAIN: Land Degradation and Conservation

    Lesson 1: Land Under Pressure

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What is Land Degradation? (10 min)"Land degradation is when land gets worse at supporting life. Types: SOIL EROSION (topsoil blown or washed away) / SALINITY (underground salt rising due to clearing trees) / COMPACTION (soil squashed by heavy machinery) / CONTAMINATION (chemicals, oil, heavy metals) / DESERTIFICATION (land turning to desert due to misuse)."Listen
    Causes of Land Degradation (15 min)"Land is degraded by: DEFORESTATION (trees hold soil together — remove them and soil erodes) / OVERGRAZING (too many animals eat all vegetation, soil exposed) / INTENSIVE FARMING (monocultures deplete specific nutrients) / URBAN SPrawl (concrete replaces soil) / CLIMATE CHANGE (more fires, more drought)."Learn causes
    Global Extent (10 min)"The UN estimates that 40% of all agricultural land is moderately to highly degraded. In Australia: 57% of land shows some degree of degradation. Salinity affects 3–5 million hectares in NSW alone. The equivalent of one cricket pitch of soil is lost every 2 minutes in Australia."Observe data
    Why Should We Care? (5 min)"We care because: soil takes 100s to 1000s of years to form / food security depends on healthy soil / land degradation releases carbon stored in soil (worsening climate change) / degraded land costs farmers billions in lost productivity."Understand

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 3 types of land degradation and at least 2 causes.

    Lesson 2: Australian Land Challenges

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Australian Context (10 min)"Australia has unique land challenges: ancient, weathered, nutrient-poor soils / fragile ecosystems / high fire risk / much of the continent is already arid or semi-arid / introduced species (rabbits, goats, camels) damage land."Listen
    Case Study: The Wheat Belt (15 min)"The WA Wheat Belt was originally diverse native vegetation. Since clearing for farming: (1) SALINITY — clearing native deep-rooted plants allowed water tables to rise, bringing salt up into the root zone / (2) EROSION — exposed topsoil blown away / (3) BIODIVERSITY LOSS — 90%+ of original vegetation cleared / (4) ACIDIFICATION — soils becoming too acidic for crops. This is one of Australia's most significant land degradation stories."Observe
    Salinity in Focus (10 min)Show images: salt-affected farmland in WA / salt-tolerant crops being trialled / revegetation programs. "Solutions: deep-rooted native perennials, salt-tolerant crops, agroforestry, preventing further clearing."Learn
    What Can Be Done? (5 min)"Write 2–3 sentences: What should farmers do to prevent further land degradation in the Wheat Belt?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 land degradation issues in the WA Wheat Belt and at least 1 solution.

    Lesson 3: Restoration and Conservation

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Land Restoration Is Possible (10 min)"Land restoration is one of the most hopeful environmental actions. Degraded land CAN recover if we: stop the source of degradation / add organic matter / replant native species / manage water properly. The Great Barrier Reef shows us that restoration takes TIME — decades of consistent effort."Understand hope
    Restoration Case Studies (15 min)Show: GREEN WAVES (WA environmental not-for-profit — large-scale Revegetation in Wheat Belt, biodiversity corridors) / Savanna Burning (Indigenous-led fire management in Arnhem Land — reduces late dry season wildfires, slows碳 release, creates Indigenous jobs) / Yellowstone Wolf Reintroduction (USA — reintroducing apex predator improved river ecosystems, reduced erosion).Observe
    WA Examples (10 min)"In WA: Gondwana Link (restoring connectivity across south-west WA landscape) / WHEB (Western Environmental Health — rehabilitation programs) / Carbon farming (revegetation for carbon credits, creates economic incentive for restoration) / Mine site rehabilitation (mining companies legally required to rehabilitate, though standards vary)."Learn local
    My Role (5 min)"Write 1 thing you could do to help prevent land degradation or support restoration in your local area."Write

    Differentiation: EAL students draw a restoration action. Gifted students: investigate one specific restoration program in detail.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 land restoration approaches and explain at least 1 reason why restoration gives hope.

    WEEK 6 — ELABORATE: People and Environmental Change

    Lesson 1: Environmental Movements

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Young People Are Leading (10 min)"Environmental movements are groups of people who work together to protect the environment. Young people around the world are at the forefront of environmental action. School Strikes for Climate — led by young people globally — has been one of the largest social movements in history."Listen
    Key Movements and Figures (15 min)Show: Greta Thunberg (Sweden — started School Strike for Climate, 2018, inspired global youth movement) / Dr. Jane Goodall (primatologist — 60+ years working for chimp conservation) / David Attenborough (naturalist — 'Blue Planet' raised awareness of ocean plastic) / Traditional owners and Indigenous activists globally for land rights and environmental protection.Observe
    Australian Environmental Milestones (10 min)"In Australia: Franklin River blockade (Tasmania, 1983) — stopped a dam / Sagebrush rebellion (not Australia but global parallel) / Marine park creation (Great Barrier Reef Marine Park, 1975) / Native Title (Mabo decision, 1992) / Anti-fracking movements in WA (Kimberley, Lock the Gate campaign) / School Strikes in Perth."Learn local
    What Can We Do? (5 min)"What is one environmental issue you care about? What could you do about it?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 2 environmental movements or figures and describe 1 action they took.

    Lesson 2: First Australians — Caring for Country

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Indigenous Environmental Leadership (10 min)"Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples are the world's oldest continuous environmental managers. Their knowledge of Country is profound and is now recognised as essential for environmental protection in Australia."Listen
    Caring for Country in Practice (15 min)Show programs: Indigenous Rangers (Aboriginal people employed as rangers to manage Country — combine traditional knowledge with modern science — over 1,100 ranger groups in Australia) / Indigenous Protected Areas (IPAs — traditional land managed for conservation, now 44 million hectares) / Joint management of national parks (Traditional Owners as partners, e.g., Uluru-Kata Tjuta).Observe
    Cultural Burning (10 min)"Cultural burning is Aboriginal fire management — lighting small, carefully timed fires in early dry season. This: reduces fuel loads (prevents catastrophic fires) / promotes biodiversity (many species need fire to regenerate) / protects cultural sites / creates food sources. Western science is now studying and adopting cultural burning practices."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: How does Indigenous knowledge contribute to environmental protection in Australia?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 ways Indigenous peoples contribute to environmental protection and explain why this knowledge is valuable.

    Lesson 3: Local Environmental Action

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Local Action Matters (10 min)"Environmental change happens one place at a time. Local actions add up. Every piece of vegetation protected, every piece of litter removed, every tree planted — all matter. YOU can make a difference."Understand
    Local Environmental Issues (15 min)"What environmental issues do we have in Perth/WA? (1) Biodiversity loss in the Wheat Belt / (2) Marine debris on our coasts / (3) Urban heat island effect (Perth is warming faster than surrounding areas) / (4) Groundwater depletion / (5) Feral animals in sensitive areas."Identify local
    What Can We Do? (10 min)"Actions we can take: Participate in Tree Day / Clean Up Australia Day / School verge/nature strip planting / Feral animal awareness / Monitor local waterways / Write to local MP about environmental issues / Make sustainable choices."Learn
    Action Planning (5 min)"Write 2–3 specific actions you will take in the next month to help the local environment."Plan

    Differentiation: EAL students draw their planned action. Gifted students: create a full community action proposal.

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify at least 2 local environmental issues and plan at least 2 specific actions to address them.

    WEEK 7 — ELABORATE + EVALUATE: Solutions and Action

    Lesson 1: Solutions Toolkit

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Solutions Framework (15 min)"When evaluating solutions to environmental challenges, use this TOOLKIT: (1) Does it address the ROOT CAUSE or just the symptoms? (2) Is it SUSTAINABLE long-term? (3) Does it consider SOCIAL JUSTICE — fair for all people? (4) Does it work at SCALE — can it grow to make a big difference? (5) What are the COSTS and who pays?"Learn
    Evaluate Solutions (15 min)Show and evaluate: Electric vehicles (reduces emissions, but battery production has environmental cost, electricity must be clean too) / Renewable energy (solar/wind — sustainable long-term, but intermittency challenges, land use) / Meat reduction (reduces methane and water use, but cultural/economic barriers) / Carbon offset schemes (controversial — some are genuine, some are not).Evaluate
    Best Solutions (10 min)"The best solutions: are systemic (change the system, not just individual behaviour) / are just (fair to everyone) / are long-term (look decades ahead) / are multiple (many actions together, not one silver bullet). There is no single solution — it is all of us, doing many things."Conclude
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Apply the toolkit to ONE solution from today's lesson. Evaluate it against the 5 criteria."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can apply the solutions toolkit to evaluate at least 1 proposed solution.

    Lesson 2: Local Environmental Investigation

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Local Investigation Brief (5 min)"Today you will investigate a local environmental challenge — something happening in Perth or WA. You will research: What is the challenge? Where is it happening? Why is it happening? Who is affected? What solutions are proposed? Is the solution sustainable?"Prepare
    Investigation Time (20 min)Students investigate their chosen local issue using provided sources. Options: Urban heat in Perth / Marine debris on WA coast / Groundwater depletion in Perth / Feral cats in WA / Swan-Canning River system health / Fire risk in Perth hills / Local biodiversity loss in Perth.Investigate
    Process Findings (10 min)"Organise your findings: CHALLENGE / LOCATION / CAUSE / EFFECTS / SOLUTIONS / SUSTAINABILITY ASSESSMENT."Process
    Note-Taking (5 min)Students take structured notes for Hot Task preparation.Note

    Success Criteria Check: Has investigated a local environmental challenge with structured notes covering all 6 elements.

    Lesson 3: Action Plans

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    From Understanding to Action (10 min)"Understanding environmental challenges is not enough — we must ACT. An ACTION PLAN has: (1) The issue / (2) What I will do specifically / (3) When I will do it / (4) Who else is involved / (5) How I will measure success / (6) What I need to make it happen."Learn
    Create Action Plans (20 min)Students create individual action plans based on their local investigation. Format: short written plan or poster with 6 sections.Create
    Share Action Plans (10 min)3–4 students share their action plans with the class. Class gives feedback: "Is it specific? Is it achievable? Is it measurable?"Share

    Differentiation: EAL students create a visual action plan. Gifted students: create a group action plan for a class or school environmental initiative.

    Success Criteria Check: Creates an action plan with all 6 required elements for a local environmental issue.

    WEEK 8 — EVALUATE: Hot Task and Reflection

    DIFFERENTIATION NOTES

    EAL Learners

  • Visual Word Wall: climate change, biodiversity, sustainability, conservation, degradation, scarcity, mitigation, adaptation, carbon, ecosystem, species, renewable, non-renewable.
  • Sentence Starters: "The environmental challenge is..." / "This challenge is caused by..." / "People are responding by..." / "A sustainable solution would be..." / "The connection between this place and another is..."
  • Image-Based Research: Students use image-based sources rather than text-heavy sources where possible.
  • Partner Support: Paired for research and discussion activities.

    • Gifted Learners

  • Research Extension: Investigate a specific environmental policy (e.g., Australian carbon credit scheme, Great Barrier Reef 2050 Plan).
  • Policy Proposal: Write a formal proposal to a local council about a specific environmental action.
  • Comparative Study: Compare environmental challenges in two countries in similar situations.

    • Students with NEPs

  • Reduced Written Expectation: 4–6 sentences on written tasks.
  • Word Banks: Provided for all written tasks.
  • Oral Option: Students may present verbally.
  • Extra Time: Additional time for all tasks.
  • Scaffolded Templates: Action plan template, solutions toolkit template.

    • ASSESSMENT NOTES

    Formative Assessment (Ongoing)

  • Cold Task review (Week 1)
  • Data interpretation check (Week 1)
  • Exit tickets (Weeks 2, 3, 4, 5)

  • Action plans (Week 7)

  • Cold Task (Week 1, Lesson 1): Pre-assessment. Establishes baseline.
  • Hot Task (Week 8, Lesson 1): Post-assessment. Assessed against rubric.
