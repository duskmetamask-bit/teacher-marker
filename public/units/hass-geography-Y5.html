Unit Content

Global Connections — Geography Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 5

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum HASS v9 — Geography (Years 5–6)

Unit Big Idea: Our world is deeply interconnected through trade, migration, information, and culture. These connections shape how places develop, how resources are distributed, and how global challenges are shared. Understanding connections helps us understand our place in the world.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Investigate two places and explain how they are connected to other places through trade, migration, or communication

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Investigate how a significant global challenge connects multiple places and explain how cooperation between places could address it

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to investigate how places are connected through trade, migration, communication, and shared challenges, and to evaluate how these connections affect people's lives and wellbeing.

Success Criteria:

  • I can describe at least 3 types of connections between places (trade, migration, communication, culture, environment).
  • I can explain how trade networks distribute resources and shape economies.
  • I can describe how migration shapes both places of origin and destination.
  • I can explain how communication technology has changed connections between places.
  • I can investigate a global challenge and evaluate how connections between places affect responses.
  • I can use sources (data, maps, reports, statistics) to investigate global connections.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: A Connected World

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Connections Between Places

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Show a world map highlighting a smartphone supply chain: minerals from the DRC (cobalt), manufacturing in China, design in California, sales worldwide. "This phone is connected to the whole world. Everything is connected."Observe and wonder
    Introduction (5 min)"In this unit, you will investigate global connections — how places are linked through trade, migration, communication, and shared challenges. These connections shape our daily lives even when we don't see them."Listen
    Cold Task (30 min)"Choose two places. Describe at least 2 ways they are connected to OTHER places (trade, migration, communication, culture). For each connection: WHO is involved? WHAT is being exchanged? HOW does it move? Write 4–6 sentences explaining how these connections affect the places." Year 5 expectation: detailed, multiple connection types, evidence-based.Independent

    Differentiation: Image word bank. Sentence starters: "This place is connected to [place] through..." / "The connection involves..." / "This affects the places by..." / "The connection works because..."

    Success Criteria Check: Student identifies at least 2 places and at least 2 types of connections between them.

    Lesson 2: The Web of Connection

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Hook)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    We Are All Connected (10 min)"The world is more connected than ever in human history. Every place is part of a network — no place is truly isolated. Your clothes were made somewhere else. Your food was grown and processed somewhere else. Your favourite app was built somewhere else. This is not neutral — it shapes everything."Listen
    The Four Connection Types (15 min)Four types of connections: (1) TRADE — goods and resources move between places / (2) MIGRATION — people move between places / (3) COMMUNICATION — information moves instantly / (4) CULTURE — ideas, beliefs, and practices spread between places. All four operate simultaneously and overlap.Learn
    Connection Mapping Activity (10 min)Students map their own personal connections: Where were your clothes made? Where does your food come from? Does your family have origins elsewhere? Who do you communicate with globally? Teacher draws a live web on the board connected to students.Map personal connections
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What is one way our local community is connected to the world? How does this connection affect us?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify at least 3 types of connections and give at least 1 personal example of global connection.

    Lesson 3: Mapping Connections

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Concept Introduction)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students

Iron ore: Australia → China (large arrow) / Coffee: Brazil → USA → global (medium arrows) / Crude oil: Middle East → Asia, Europe, USA (massive arrows). "Thicker arrow = more trade. Direction shows flow. This is a FLOW MAP."Learn
    Create a Flow Map (10 min)Students create a simple flow map showing one connection they learned about in Lesson 1. They use arrows to show direction and thickness to show volume.Create
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Draw a flow map showing one global connection involving Australia. Label: What is flowing? Where from? Where to? Which direction?"Draw

    Differentiation: EAL students draw a simplified flow map with teacher support. Gifted students: create a multi-flow map showing multiple goods traded with Australia.

    Success Criteria Check: Can create a simple flow map showing one global trade connection involving Australia.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Trade Networks

    Lesson 1: How Trade Works

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Why Do Places Trade? (10 min)"Places trade because: no place has EVERYTHING / some places are BETTER at producing certain things / specialisation increases efficiency / comparative advantage (even if not the best, it is cheaper for you to produce what you are relatively better at). Trade makes all places better off than if they tried to be self-sufficient."Learn
    Trade Vocabulary (10 min)"EXPORTS: goods a place SELLS to others / IMPORTS: goods a place BUYS from others / TRADE BALANCE: exports minus imports / TARIFF: a tax on imported goods / FREE TRADE: trade without restrictions / PROTECTIONISM: using tariffs to protect local industries."Learn
    Australia's Trade Profile (10 min)"Australia is a major global trader. Top exports: iron ore (world's largest exporter, ~60% to China) / coal (2nd largest exporter) / LNG (LNG: liquefied natural gas, 4th largest) / beef, lamb, wheat / wool / gold. Top imports: machinery / electronics / vehicles / petroleum / consumer goods. We are a RESOURCE ECONOMY — we sell what comes from the ground."Observe
    Trade Partners (10 min)"Australia's top trade partners: CHINA (our biggest by far — both exports and imports) / JAPAN / SOUTH KOREA / USA / ASEAN countries. China is our largest because: proximity (geography), growing middle class (demand), manufacturing capacity."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Can define exports and imports and name at least 2 of Australia's top exports and trade partners.

    Lesson 2: Global Supply Chains

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What is a Supply Chain? (10 min)"A supply chain is the sequence of processes involved in producing and distributing a product. Most products we buy have COMPLEX supply chains — multiple countries, multiple companies, multiple stages. Few things are made start-to-finish in one place."Learn
    Smartphone Supply Chain (15 min)"The smartphone is the most complex supply chain in history: RARE EARTH MINERALS (DRC, Philippines, China — used in screens and batteries) / MINING AND REFINING (China refines 85% of rare earth minerals) / DESIGN (California, USA — Apple, Samsung) / MANUFACTURING (Shenzhen, China — Foxconn factories with 200,000+ workers) / ASSEMBLY (components from 50+ countries assembled in China) / DISTRIBUTION (ships, planes, retail) / RETAIL (stores in every country). One phone = the whole world."Understand
    Supply Chain Risks (10 min)"Supply chains have RISKS: GEOGRAPHIC CONCENTRATION (most rare earth refining in China — political risk) / LABOUR ISSUES (Foxconn worker conditions — exposed in 2010) / ENVIRONMENTAL (mining damage in DRC) / TRANSPORT (COVID-19 showed: ships blocked = supply chain crisis) / GEOPOLITICAL (US-China trade war → tariffs → prices rise)."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: How does a supply chain connect places? What are 2 risks in global supply chains?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 stages in a supply chain and identify at least 2 risks.

    Lesson 3: Trade and Power

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Trade Is Not Neutral (10 min)"Trade is not just about economics — it is about POWER. Countries and companies with more power get better deals. Historical trade patterns often reflect colonial history — some countries are still caught in patterns of exporting raw materials (like ore) and importing finished products (like electronics). This keeps them less wealthy."Understand
    The Resource Curse (10 min)"The RESOURCE CURSE: countries with lots of natural resources (oil, gold, diamonds) often have LOWER economic growth than countries without them. Why? Because: prices are volatile / governments rely on resource revenue, neglect other industries / inequality if wealth not distributed / 'Dutch Disease' (resource wealth makes other exports uncompetitive). Australia largely avoided this because we diversified."Learn
    Power in Supply Chains (10 min)"In supply chains, POWER is held by those with unique resources or unique capabilities. China has power over rare earth refining. Apple has power over design and brand (captures most profits). Foxconn has less power (competes with other manufacturers). Countries negotiate TRADE AGREEMENTS to get better access."Understand
    Trade Agreements (10 min)"Trade agreements are deals between countries to reduce trade barriers. Examples: ASEAN Free Trade Area / RCEP (Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership — includes Australia, China, Japan, Korea) / USMCA (US-Mexico-Canada) / EU Single Market. Agreements aim to make trade fairer and easier."Learn

    Differentiation: EAL students create a visual power map of one supply chain. Gifted students: research one specific trade agreement and present findings.

    Success Criteria Check: Can explain the resource curse and describe at least 1 example of power dynamics in trade.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: Migration and People

    Lesson 1: Why People Move

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Why Do People Migrate? (15 min)"People move for many reasons — we call these PUSH and PULL factors. PUSH FACTORS (make you want to leave): war/conflict, persecution, poverty, lack of jobs, natural disasters, climate change effects. PULL FACTORS (attract you to a new place): safety, better jobs, education, family connections, political freedom, better healthcare."Learn
    Types of Migration (10 min)"MIGRATION types: (1) INTERNAL — moving within a country (rural to city) / (2) INTERNATIONAL — moving between countries / (3) PERMANENT — to live permanently / (4) TEMPORARY — work visa, student visa / (5) FORCED — refugees, asylum seekers / (6) VOLUNTARY — by choice."Learn
    Australia's Migration History (10 min)"Australia's migration story: 1788 onwards British colonists / 1850s Gold Rush — Chinese and European / 1901 White Australia Policy (restricted non-European migration until 1973) / Post-WWII: Big Australian migration program — displaced persons from Europe (1945–1970s) / 1970s onwards: multicultural Australia — Asia, Middle East, Africa, Eastern Europe. Now: highest per-capita immigration rate in the world."Learn
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What is one push factor and one pull factor that might cause someone to migrate?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 2 push and 2 pull factors and identify the type of migration for at least 1 scenario.

    Lesson 2: Effects of Migration on Places

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Effects on Destination (10 min)"Migration affects PLACES OF DESTINATION: (1) ECONOMIC: labour market (some jobs filled, wages affected) / (2) CULTURAL: food, festivals, language diversity / (3) SOCIAL: multicultural communities, sometimes social tension / (4) INFRASTRUCTURE: housing, schools, hospitals, public transport under pressure or expanded / (5) DEMOGRAPHIC: ageing populations offset by migration."Learn
    Effects on Places of Origin (10 min)"Migration also affects PLACES OF ORIGIN: (1) ECONOMIC: remittances (money migrants send home — huge for some countries) / (2) BRAIN DRAIN: educated people leave (doctors, engineers — hard to replace) / (3) CULTURAL: continued connections and cultural flows / (4) DEMOGRAPHIC: populations change (ageing, women left behind)."Learn
    Remittance Case Study (10 min)"Remittances: money migrants send to family in home countries. Global remittances: ~US$800 billion per year (larger than foreign aid!). Philippines: remittances from overseas workers are 3rd largest source of foreign income. Tonga: remittances equal 40% of GDP. For some countries, remittances > foreign investment + foreign aid combined."Learn
    Immigration and Australia Today (10 min)"Australia today: multicultural society — 30% of population born overseas / 300+ languages spoken / all major world religions practiced / immigration program: skilled workers, family reunion, humanitarian (refugees). Australia benefits from immigration economically and culturally."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 effects of migration on destination and 2 effects on origin, and explain remittances.

    Lesson 3: Migration Stories

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Migration Stories Are Human (10 min)"Behind every migration statistic is a HUMAN STORY. Migration shapes families, identities, and futures. Let's hear stories — real people's experiences of moving between places."Listen
    Story 1: Italian Migration to Australia (10 min)"Post-WWII Italian migration: 1 in 4 Australians today have Italian ancestry. 1950s–70s: many came as displaced persons or through assisted passage. Worked in factories, construction, agriculture. Many settled in specific areas (e.g., Carlton in Melbourne). Their children and grandchildren now fully part of Australian multicultural identity."Learn
    Story 2: Vietnamese Migration to Australia (10 min)"Post-1975: Vietnamese refugees after the Vietnam War. Many came by boat — controversial at the time. Settled in areas like Cabramatta (Sydney), creating vibrant Vietnamese communities. Now second generation is fully integrated — doctors, lawyers, politicians. One of Australia's most successful multicultural stories."Learn
    Migration Is Ongoing (10 min)"Migration is ongoing: Ukraine war → refugees globally / Syria → ongoing displacement / Climate refugees — Tuvalu, Kiribati — first nations facing disappearance due to sea level rise. These are today's migration stories."Understand today
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: How does migration affect the identity of both the person migrating and the places they move between?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students share their own migration story (or family story) with support. Gifted students: write a migration narrative from a specific person's perspective.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 1 migration story and explain its impact on identity and place.

    WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Communication Networks

    Lesson 1: From Cave Paintings to the Internet

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Communication Revolution (10 min)"Communication has gone through revolutions: (1) SPEECH — 100,000+ years ago — humans could share ideas across distance / (2) WRITING — ~5,000 years ago (cuneiform, hieroglyphics) — knowledge could be stored / (3) PRINTING — ~1450 Gutenberg — knowledge could be mass-produced / (4) TELEGRAPH — 1830s — near-instant communication across distances / (5) INTERNET — ~1990s — instant global digital communication. Each revolution changed everything."Learn
    The Internet Changes Everything (15 min)"The internet (commercially available from ~1990, mobile from ~2007) transformed connection: EMAIL (near-instant written communication) / SOCIAL MEDIA (sharing with masses) / VIDEO CALLS (face-to-face across the world) / STREAMING (music, film, education on demand) / CLOUD COMPUTING (all data accessible anywhere). Before: a letter took weeks. Now: a video call takes milliseconds."Learn
    How the Internet Works (10 min)"The internet is PHYSICAL: servers (computers that store websites) / undersea cables (fibre optic cables under oceans — 95% of international data travels these) / data centres (huge facilities storing all our data) / cables on land / WiFi and mobile towers (last mile). All of this is infrastructure."Learn
    Draw the Internet (5 min)"Draw a simple diagram showing how a message from Perth travels to London. Label: device → WiFi/cable → server → undersea cable → London server → recipient."Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Can trace at least 3 stages in how a message travels from Australia to another country via the internet.

    Lesson 2: The Digital World

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Digital Economy (10 min)"The DIGITAL ECONOMY: economic activities enabled by digital technologies. Examples: e-commerce (Amazon, Alibaba — online shopping) / digital services (Uber, Airbnb — platform economies) / online entertainment (Netflix, Spotify) / digital finance (online banking, cryptocurrency) / remote work (Zoom, Teams). The digital economy is now larger than many traditional economies."Learn
    Digital Giants (10 min)"Who controls the digital world? BIG TECH companies: Apple (US) / Microsoft (US) / Alphabet/Google (US) / Meta/Facebook (US) / Amazon (US) / Alibaba (China) / Tencent (China). These companies have enormous power — they control platforms billions of people use. They also control enormous data."Learn
    Data Is the New Oil (10 min)"DATA is the new most valuable resource. Every click, every search, every purchase generates data. Companies use this data to: sell advertising (targeted ads) / improve products / predict behaviour. Data privacy is a major issue — who owns your data? What can companies do with it?"Learn
    Write (10 min)"Write 3–4 sentences: How has the internet changed how places are connected? What are 2 impacts of the digital economy?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 impacts of the digital economy and name at least 1 digital giant.

    Lesson 3: Digital Divides

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Digital Divide (10 min)"The DIGITAL DIVIDE: the gap between those who have good internet access and those who don't. This gap has CONSEQUENCES: less education access (online learning) / less economic opportunity (can't work digitally) / less access to services (telehealth) / less political voice (social media activism) / social exclusion."Learn
    Who Is Affected? (10 min)"Digital divide affected by: GEOGRAPHY (rural and remote areas have less connectivity) / WEALTH (devices and data plans cost money) / EDUCATION (some people lack digital skills) / INFRASTRUCTURE (some countries have no cables or towers). In Australia: remote Indigenous communities have limited connectivity despite NBN satellite."Learn
    Australia's Digital Divide (10 min)"In WA: FIFO (fly-in fly-out) and remote workers rely on satellite / Perth and regional centres have good connectivity / remote Kimberley and Pilbara communities have limited options / Remote Indigenous communities: digital inclusion is a social justice issue."Learn local
    Solutions (10 min)"Solutions: government infrastructure (NBN, satellite for remote) / community WiFi programs / digital literacy training / subsidised devices and data / community digital hubs (libraries, community centres)."Learn

    Differentiation: EAL students create a digital divide poster. Gifted students: research the digital divide in one specific country.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the digital divide, identify at least 3 affected groups, and name at least 2 solutions.

    WEEK 5 — EXPLAIN: Culture Connections

    Lesson 1: Culture Flows Around the World

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Culture Is Not Fixed (10 min)"Culture is not isolated — it FLOWS between places through migration, trade, media, technology, and education. Every culture has always been influenced by others. The idea of 'pure' culture is a myth. Cultural exchange is natural and ancient."Listen
    How Culture Flows (15 min)"Culture spreads through: MIGRATION (people bring their culture to new places — pizza came to Australia with Italian migrants) / TRADE (silk road spread religions, foods, technologies between East and West) / MEDIA (Korean pop culture spread globally via YouTube — the Korean Wave/Hallyu) / EDUCATION (Buddhism spread from India to China via monks) / SPORT (football/soccer spread from England to the world)."Learn
    Food as Culture (10 min)"Food is one of the most visible cultural exchanges: Italian food came with Italian migrants to Australia / Vietnamese food came with Vietnamese refugees / Indian food came through British colonial connections / McDonald's and KFC are American cultural exports now found globally / Australian meat pies and Vegemite are distinctively Australian. Food tells migration stories."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What is one way culture has spread between places? Give a specific example."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 ways culture flows between places and give at least 2 specific examples.

    Lesson 2: Popular Culture

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Popular Culture? (10 min)"POPULAR CULTURE (pop culture): the culture that is widespread and commonly shared — mainstream music, movies, fashion, social media. Pop culture is spread by MASS MEDIA — TV, movies, music, internet. It is shaped by the biggest media companies."Listen
    Global Pop Culture (15 min)"Pop culture is increasingly global and US-dominated: Hollywood movies (most-watched globally) / American pop music (Billboard charts influence world) / K-Pop (Korean Wave — BTS, Blackpink — global phenomenon from ~2018) / Anime (Japanese — global fanbase) / Social media platforms (TikTok from China, Instagram from US). These create SHARED CULTURAL MOMENTS globally."Observe
    Cultural Imperialism (10 min)"Some scholars argue that global pop culture spread represents CULTURAL IMPERIALISM — one country's culture dominates others, potentially reducing local cultural diversity. American cultural products dominate globally. Some countries push back by protecting local culture (France protects French language in media, India protects local film industry)."Understand debate
    Australian Pop Culture (5 min)"Australia has its own pop culture: INXS, Kylie Minogue, Tame Impala /堆 International / The Castle (Aussie film classic) / Neighbours, Home and Away (exported globally) / AC/DC, Midnight Oil / Indigenous Australian art (now globally recognised). Australian pop culture punches above its weight globally."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Can explain the concept of cultural imperialism and give at least 1 example of a country's protection of local culture.

    Lesson 3: Cultural Preservation

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    When Culture Is Threatened (10 min)"Cultural preservation means keeping cultural practices, languages, and traditions alive. Culture is threatened when: globalisation promotes dominant cultures / younger generations adopt global pop culture over traditional culture / languages die out (a language dies every 2 weeks globally) / urbanisation displaces traditional practices / conflict destroys cultural sites."Learn
    Indigenous Cultural Preservation (10 min)"Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander cultures in Australia have shown remarkable resilience: 65,000+ years of continuous culture / language preservation (many languages being revived) / cultural burning practices / Indigenous knowledge of astronomy, medicine, environment / art as cultural expression and preservation. Many are volunteer-run community language programs."Learn
    What Can Be Done? (10 min)"Cultural preservation strategies: language programs in schools / cultural festivals (NAIDOC Week, Reconciliation events) / digital archives (recording oral histories) / cultural exchange programs / supporting Indigenous-led preservation / learning local language (Noongar language revival in WA schools)."Learn
    My Cultural Connections (10 min)"What cultural traditions, languages, or practices exist in your family? Write 2–3 sentences: What traditions does your family maintain? How might they connect to places around the world?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students write or draw about cultural traditions from their home culture. Gifted students: investigate one specific cultural preservation program.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 threats to culture and at least 2 preservation strategies.

    WEEK 6 — ELABORATE: Global Challenges

    Lesson 1: Shared Global Challenges

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Global Challenges Are Shared (10 min)"Many challenges facing the world require GLOBAL COOPERATION — no single country can solve them alone. This is because places are connected. Climate change doesn't respect borders. Pandemics spread globally. Economic crises cascade across countries. These are SHARED challenges."Listen
    The Big Five Shared Challenges (15 min)"Five shared global challenges: (1) CLIMATE CHANGE — requires global emissions reduction agreement (Paris Agreement) / (2) PANDEMICS — requires global health cooperation (WHO) / (3) INEQUALITY — wealth gap between rich and poor countries / (4) REFUGEE CRISIS — 100+ million displaced people globally / (5) CYBERSECURITY — cyber attacks cross borders, require international law."Learn
    Case Study: COVID-19 Pandemic (10 min)"COVID-19 (2020–2022) showed global connection: virus spread globally in days / supply chains disrupted (masks, PPE, vaccines) / international travel halted / vaccine nationalism (countries hoarded doses) / COVAX (global vaccine sharing program) eventually helped / global economic impacts. The pandemic showed both how connected we are AND how unequally we share burdens."Observe
    Cooperation Works (5 min)"The OZONE LAYER is an example of global cooperation working. Montreal Protocol (1987): global agreement to phase out CFCs. Ozone hole is now healing. This proves countries CAN cooperate on global challenges when there is sufficient will and scientific consensus."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 3 shared global challenges and explain why global cooperation is needed.

    Lesson 2: Climate Change as a Connector

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Climate Change Connects Everything (10 min)"Climate change is the defining connection between places. It is caused by activities in one place but affects places all over the world. Rich countries contributed most to historical emissions but poor countries suffer most. This is a global JUSTICE issue."Listen
    Cascading Effects (15 min)"Climate change effects CASCADE across systems: Rising temps → ice melts → sea level rises → coastal cities threatened → people migrate → political instability → conflict. HEAT → droughts → crop failures → food prices rise → hunger → migration. These cascades connect distant places."Learn
    Paris Agreement and Its Limits (10 min)"Paris Agreement (2015): 196 countries committed to limiting warming to 1.5–2°C above pre-industrial. Progress: INSUFFICIENT. Current policies put us on track for ~2.7°C warming. Gap between commitment and action is large. The agreement is voluntary — countries choose their own targets (NDCs — Nationally Determined Contributions)."Learn
    WA and Climate Connections (5 min)"Perth: already experiencing climate change effects (drying, more fire weather). WA resources (iron ore, LNG) are central to global emissions (exported and burned elsewhere). We contribute to the problem AND are affected by it. WA's climate strategy: Net Zero by 2050, renewable hydrogen strategy."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 cascading effects of climate change and explain at least 1 justice dimension.

    Lesson 3: Climate Migration and Refugees

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Climate Refugees (10 min)"CLIMATE REFUGEES: people forced to move due to climate change effects — rising sea levels, extreme weather, drought. Unlike refugees fleeing conflict, there is no international legal framework to protect climate refugees. They fall through the cracks."Learn
    Case Study: Pacific Island Nations (15 min)"Pacific nations most at risk: Tuvalu, Kiribati, Kiribati, Marshall Islands — sea level rising, frequent flooding, freshwater contamination, crop failure. Tuvalu has already negotiated with Australia for CONCEPT OF SLOWLY DISAPPEARING NATION — its citizens will become climate refugees by end of century. Australia has offered limited refugee-style visas. This is happening NOW."Learn
    Internal Climate Migration (10 min)"Climate migration also happens within countries: Australian scientists model: by 2050, parts of western Queensland may become uninhabitable due to heat and drought — population will need to relocate internally. This is 'managed retreat' — planned relocation away from dangerous areas."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is a climate refugee? Why is there no legal protection for them? What should be done?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the situation of climate refugees from Pacific Island nations and explain the legal gap.

    WEEK 7 — ELABORATE + EVALUATE: Wellbeing and Connection

    Lesson 1: Wellbeing and Place

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What is Wellbeing? (10 min)"WELLBEING is not just the absence of illness — it is a state of being: PHYSICAL (healthy body) / MENTAL (healthy mind) / SOCIAL (positive relationships) / ECONOMIC (adequate resources) / CULTURAL (identity and belonging) / SPIRITUAL (meaning and purpose). Wellbeing is shaped by place — where you live affects your wellbeing."Learn
    Wellbeing and Place Connection (15 min)"Place affects wellbeing: ACCESS to services (healthcare, education, shops) / SAFETY (crime rates, environmental hazards) / COMMUNITY (social connections, support networks) / ENVIRONMENT (air quality, green space, noise) / IDENTITY (belonging to a place, cultural connection) / OPPORTUNITY (jobs, education). Urban and rural places offer different wellbeing profiles."Learn
    Global Wellbeing Comparison (10 min)"World Happiness Report ranks countries by wellbeing. Nordic countries (Finland, Denmark, Iceland) consistently top the list — why? Strong social safety nets, high trust, good governance, strong community, high equality. Australia ranks in top 15 — high material living standards but challenges: mental health, inequality."Observe

    DIFFERENTIATION NOTES

    EAL Learners

  • Visual Word Wall: trade, supply chain, migration, diaspora, remittance, digital divide, cultural exchange, global citizen, wellbeing, identity, network, connection.
  • Sentence Starters: "This place is connected to..." / "The connection works by..." / "One effect of this connection is..." / "A global challenge affecting this place is..." / "As a global citizen, I will..."
  • Home Language Research: Students may investigate their family's country of origin as a place study connected to Australia.
  • Partner Support: Paired for research and discussion activities.

    • Gifted Learners

  • Research Extension: Investigate one specific global connection (e.g., the global supply chain of chocolate, coffee, or smartphones).
  • Policy Proposal: Write a formal proposal to a global organisation about addressing a specific global challenge.
  • Comparative Study: Compare global connections in two countries at different economic levels.

    • Students with NEPs

  • Reduced Written Expectation: 4–6 sentences on written tasks.
  • Word Banks: Provided for all written tasks.
  • Oral Option: Students may present verbally.
  • Extra Time: Additional time for all tasks.
  • Scaffolded Templates: Flow map template, global citizen action plan template.
