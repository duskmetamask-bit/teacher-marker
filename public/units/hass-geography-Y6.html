Unit Content

Environmental Change and Management — Geography Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 6

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum HASS v9 — Geography (Years 5–6)

Unit Big Idea: Places face environmental challenges that require understanding, management, and sometimes dramatic change. The environment is not separate from human activity — people are part of ecosystems and have the capacity to both degrade and restore them. Understanding these interactions is key to a sustainable future.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Investigate an environmental change in two places and explain how it affects the environment and people, and how people are managing it

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Investigate an environmental management issue in depth, evaluate management approaches, and propose a management plan for a specific place

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to investigate environmental changes and their effects on places, evaluate how people manage environmental challenges, and develop management plans that balance environmental, social, and economic considerations.

Success Criteria:

  • I can describe at least 3 types of environmental change affecting places (climate change, deforestation, urbanisation, pollution).
  • I can explain how and why environmental changes affect different places and communities differently.
  • I can evaluate at least 2 environmental management approaches and assess their effectiveness and sustainability.
  • I can develop a management plan for an environmental issue that balances multiple considerations.
  • I can use sources (data, maps, reports, statistics, case studies) to investigate environmental management.
  • I can communicate findings clearly and make evidence-based proposals.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: Environmental Change

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Investigating Environmental Change

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Show two images of the same place at different times: a glacier in 1900 vs 2020 / a forest in 1950 vs today / a city skyline in 1970 vs 2020. "What changed? What caused the change? How do we know?"Observe and question
    Introduction (5 min)"In this unit, you will investigate environmental change — how places are changing, why, and what people are doing about it. This is one of the most pressing issues of our time. You will learn to evaluate management approaches and develop your own management plans."Listen
    Cold Task (30 min)"Choose two places facing environmental change (from images provided or your knowledge). For each place: (1) Name the environmental change. (2) Describe how it is affecting the environment (plants, animals, land, water). (3) Describe how it is affecting people. (4) Explain how people are managing or responding to it. (5) Write 2 sentences: Is the management approach effective? What would you suggest differently?" Year 6 expectation: 8–12 sentences, evaluation of management, evidence-based.Independent

    Differentiation: Image word bank with before/after comparisons. Sentence starters: "The environmental change in [place] is..." / "This affects the environment by..." / "People are responding by..." / "The management approach is [effective/ineffective] because..." / "A better approach might be..."

    Success Criteria Check: Student identifies at least 2 places with environmental changes and describes at least 1 management response.

    Lesson 2: Types of Environmental Change

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Hook)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Five Major Types (15 min)"There are 5 major types of environmental change: (1) CLIMATE CHANGE — warming atmosphere, shifting weather, sea level rise / (2) BIODIVERSITY LOSS — species extinction, habitat destruction / (3) LAND DEGRADATION — erosion, salinity, desertification / (4) WATER POLLUTION AND SCARCITY — contamination, over-extraction / (5) URBAN AND AGRICULTURAL EXPANSION — natural areas converted to human use."Learn
    Case Studies Introduction (15 min)"Each type of change has ICONIC CASE STUDIES that illustrate it: Climate change: global coral bleaching / Biodiversity loss: thylacine extinction / Land degradation: WA Wheat Belt salinity / Water issues: Murray-Darling Basin / Urban expansion: Singapore or Hong Kong land reclamation."Note
    Which Are Most Pressing? (10 min)"Not all changes are equal everywhere. In Australia: CLIMATE CHANGE is the most pressing (warming, fires, bleaching) / BIODIVERSITY LOSS is critical (highest mammal extinction rate) / WATER SCARCITY affects the south-west / URBAN EXPANSION pressures Perth's fringe. Different places face different priorities."Discuss
    Inquiry Questions (5 min)"Write your 3 inquiry questions for this unit. Example: 'What is the most pressing environmental change affecting [place]?' 'Why did this happen?' 'How effective is the current management?' 'What should be done differently?'"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can name all 5 major types of environmental change and identify at least 2 case study examples.

    Lesson 3: Reading Environmental Data

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Concept Introduction)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Data Is Evidence (10 min)"Environmental management decisions should be evidence-based. Scientists, governments, and communities use DATA to understand problems, monitor change, and evaluate solutions. Learning to read environmental data is a core geographic skill."Listen
    Data Types (15 min)"Types of environmental data: TIME SERIES (measurements over time — e.g., temperature from 1900 to 2024) / SPATIAL (geographic distribution — e.g., a map of vegetation cover) / STATISTICAL (numerical summaries — e.g., percentage of species lost) / REMOTE SENSING (satellite data — e.g., deforestation rates from space) / QUALITATIVE (observations, interviews — e.g., Traditional Owner knowledge). Different data answers different questions."Learn
    Reading Key Environmental Graphs (15 min)Practice reading: (1) Global temperature anomaly graph (rising trend) / (2) Australian species extinction bar graph / (3) WA rainfall trend graph (declining) / (4) Global deforestation area line graph (continuing high rates). For each: What does the data show? What is the trend? What does it mean for management?Interpret data
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Choose ONE graph from today's lesson. Write 3 sentences describing what it shows and what management implication it suggests."Write

    Differentiation: EAL students interpret graphs with teacher support using image-based data cards. Gifted students: find and bring an environmental graph from a current news source.

    Success Criteria Check: Can read and interpret at least 2 types of environmental data graphs and explain their management implications.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Case Studies in Change

    Lesson 1: The Great Barrier Reef — A Case Study in Climate Change

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Great Barrier Reef — Icon of Australia (10 min)"The Great Barrier Reef (GBR) is the world's largest living structure — 2,300km of coral reef, visible from space. It is one of the most biodiverse places on Earth. 1.5 billion people depend on it. It generates $6.4 billion annually from tourism and fishing. It is UNDER THREAT."Listen
    What Is Coral Bleaching? (15 min)"Coral bleaching: when ocean temperature rises just 1–2°C above normal summer maximum, corals expel the algae (zooxanthellae) that give them colour and food. Without algae, coral bleaches (turns white) and starves. If temperatures stay high, coral dies. Recovery, if it happens, takes decades."Understand
    The Data (10 min)"Mass bleaching events: 1998 (first global event) / 2002 / 2016 (worst on record at time) / 2017 (Back-to-back with 2016 — first time) / 2020 / 2022 (most widespread bleaching on record — 91% of reefs affected) / 2024 (5th mass bleaching event in 8 years). The interval between events is SHORTENING — climate is speeding up."Observe data
    Management Approaches (5 min)"GBR management: Great Barrier Reef Marine Park Authority (GBRMPA) / marine park zoning (no-take zones) / water quality improvement (reduce land-based runoff) / Crown-of-thorns starfish control (coral-eating predator) / CLIMATE CHANGE ACTION (this is the only real solution — reducing emissions). Current status: UNESCO World Heritage 'in danger' listing (2023)."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Can explain coral bleaching, describe at least 3 mass bleaching events and their data trend, and name at least 2 management approaches.

    Lesson 2: Amazon Deforestation — A Case Study in Land Degradation

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Amazon — Lungs of the Earth (10 min)"The Amazon rainforest covers 5.5 million km² (larger than Australia). It produces 20% of the world's oxygen — hence 'lungs of the Earth.' It is the most biodiverse place on Earth (10% of all species). It stores 150–200 billion tonnes of carbon (stopped being a carbon sink in 2021/2022 — now emits carbon due to fires and degradation)."Listen
    The Deforestation Crisis (15 min)"Deforestation drivers: BEEF (cattle ranching — 80% of deforestation) / SOYA (soybeans for animal feed — industrial farming) / LOGGING (illegal and legal) / MINING (gold, minerals) / DAM CONSTRUCTION. Brazil lost 17% of original forest by 2020. Rate slowed dramatically 2004–2012 under governments that enforced protection. Then rose again under subsequent governments."Learn
    The Data (10 min)"Amazon deforestation data: 2004 peak (~27,000 km²/year) / 2012 low (~4,500 km²/year) / 2019 spike (10,100 km² — highest in 10 years) / 2022 data (11,568 km² — record high). 2022 fires: 103,000 fire hotspots in Amazon biome. This is not just an environmental issue — it is a climate, biodiversity, Indigenous rights, and economic issue."Observe data
    Solutions and Tensions (5 min)"Solutions: enforce protection laws / satellite monitoring / Indigenous land rights (Indigenous peoples are the BEST guardians of the forest) / sustainable beef and soy certification / debt-for-nature swaps. Tensions: economic pressure (agriculture is Brazil's biggest economy) vs environmental protection."Learn

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 drivers of deforestation, explain the data trends, and name at least 2 solutions with their tensions.

    Lesson 3: Urban Growth and Environmental Change

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Urban Expansion Is Transforming the World (10 min)"By 2050, 68% of the world's population will live in cities. Urban expansion consumes natural land — forests, wetlands, farmland. This causes: biodiversity loss (habitat destruction) / altered waterways (runoff increase, pollution) / urban heat island effect (cities 5–10°C warmer than surrounding areas) / agricultural land loss."Listen
    Case Study: Singapore (15 min)"Singapore: 728 km² island (smaller than Perth), 5.9 million people (denser than almost any city). Solutions: vertical city (high-rises, 85% live in high-rises) / land reclamation (has expanded significantly through land reclamation) / massive greening of city (vertical gardens, park connectors) / world-class public transport / water management (dams, NEWater recycling, desalination). Despite extreme density, Singapore maintains significant green space."Learn
    Case Study: Perth's Urban Sprawl (10 min)"Perth is one of the most sprawling cities in the world — low-density, car-dependent. Urban footprint growing faster than population. This consumes Wheat Belt farmland and remnant vegetation. Solutions: infill development (medium-density in existing suburbs) / transit-oriented development / urban growth boundaries / protecting significant ecological remnants."Learn local
    Which Model Is Better? (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: Which urban model (Singapore's vertical density vs Perth's sprawl) is more environmentally sustainable? Consider: land use, transport, energy, biodiversity."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can compare two urban development models, evaluate their environmental trade-offs, and reach a defensible conclusion.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: Managing the Environment

    Lesson 1: Environmental Management Approaches

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Three Approaches to Environmental Management (15 min)"There are 3 main approaches: (1) PROTECTION — protecting areas from human interference (national parks, marine parks). Example: banning development in a sensitive area. (2) MANAGEMENT — managing human use to minimise damage (sustainable harvesting, managed access). Example: allowing some fishing but with catch limits. (3) RESTORATION — actively repairing damaged environments. Example: replanting native vegetation after clearing."Learn
    Compare the Approaches (10 min)"Protection: cheapest and most effective if done early / Restoration: expensive and uncertain (ecosystems are complex) / Management: most common, most challenging (requires balancing competing uses). Most environmental challenges require a MIX of all three approaches."Compare
    The Precautionary Principle (10 min)"The PRECAUTIONARY PRINCIPLE: if there is a threat of serious or irreversible environmental damage, lack of full scientific certainty should not be used as a reason for postponing cost-effective measures. In plain terms: don't wait until you have perfect proof to act — the risk is too high."Understand
    Apply to Case Studies (5 min)"Apply the three approaches to the GBR: Protection: no-take zones, marine park / Management: Crown-of-thorns control, water quality improvement / Restoration: coral gardening, assisted evolution (breeding heat-tolerant corals)."Apply

    Success Criteria Check: Can explain all 3 management approaches and apply them to at least 1 case study.

    Lesson 2: Government Policy and Environmental Management

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Why Government Involvement? (10 min)"Environmental management often requires government involvement because: individual actions cannot solve global problems / market forces alone cannot protect the environment (externalities — costs borne by society, not the company) / coordination problems require rules / long-term environmental health requires laws."Learn
    Environmental Policy Tools (15 min)Government tools: (1) REGULATION (laws — e.g., Environmental Protection Act) / (2) INCENTIVES (subsidies, tax breaks for green behaviour — e.g., solar rebates) / (3) MARKET MECHANISMS (cap-and-trade, carbon pricing — e.g., Safeguard Mechanism in Australia) / (4) INFORMATION (labelling, reporting requirements — e.g., energy star ratings).Learn
    Australian Environmental Policy (10 min)"Australia's key environmental policies: EPBC Act (Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation — federal level, protects Matters of National Environmental Significance) / State Environmental Protection Acts / Water Act 2007 (Murray-Darling Basin) / Climate Change Act 2022 (net zero by 2050) / Renewable Energy Target. WA: Environmental Protection Act 1986, EPA (Environmental Protection Authority) provides advice to government."Learn
    Effectiveness of Policy (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: Which environmental policy tool (regulation, incentives, market mechanisms, information) do you think is most effective? Why?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 2 environmental policy tools and evaluate at least 1 for effectiveness.

    Lesson 3: Community Action and Environmental Management

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Community Action Matters (10 min)"Governments and corporations are important but community action is VITAL. Grassroots movements create the political will for government action, directly address local issues, and build social norms around environmental care."Listen
    Examples of Community Environmental Action (15 min)Show: Beach cleanup movements (Oceanic Society, Take 3 for the Sea — removes plastic from Australian beaches) / School climate strikes (mass youth mobilisation globally since 2018) / Transition Towns movement (community-led resilience building, localised energy and food) / Buy Nothing groups (reduce consumption) / Landcare (farmers and volunteers doing on-ground environmental work — 100,000+ members in Australia)."Observe
    Limits of Community Action (10 min)"Community action has limits: it cannot solve problems that require large infrastructure or legislation / it can be unevenly distributed (more active in affluent areas) / it sometimes lacks technical expertise / it can be co-opted by commercial interests. Community + government + business working together is most effective."Understand
    My Community Action (5 min)"Write 2–3 sentences: What is one environmental issue in our local community that community action could help address? What could a community group specifically do about it?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students create a visual poster about community environmental action. Gifted students: research one specific community environmental organisation in depth.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 examples of community environmental action and identify at least 2 limitations of community action.

    WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Indigenous Environmental Management

    Lesson 1: Traditional Ecological Knowledge

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) (10 min)"TRADITIONAL ECOLOGICAL KNOWLEDGE (TEK): the knowledge that Indigenous peoples have accumulated over thousands of years about their environment. It is: empirical (based on thousands of years of observation) / holistic (connects all elements of Country) / dynamic (changes and adapts) / transmitted orally and through practice across generations."Learn
    What TEK Includes (15 min)TEK covers: seasonal calendars (when to plant, fish, harvest) / water management (location of reliable water sources, sustainable harvesting) / fire management (cultural burning practices) / species management (knowing which species are indicators of ecosystem health) / soil management / navigation and mapping. This knowledge is being formally recognised by Western science."Learn
    Integration with Western Science (10 min)"Western science and TEK are being integrated more and more: Indigenous Monitoring Programs (Indigenous rangers collect data alongside scientists) / Joint management of national parks (Uluru, Kakadu) / Fire management (Arnhem Land, Kimberley — reduced late-season wildfires dramatically) / Marine management (Sea Country Indigenous Protected Areas)."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What is Traditional Ecological Knowledge? Why is it valuable for environmental management?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can define TEK, describe at least 3 examples of it, and explain why it is valuable for environmental management.

    Lesson 2: Caring for Country Today

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Indigenous Rangers (10 min)"Indigenous rangers: Aboriginal people employed as land and sea managers, combining traditional knowledge with modern science. Over 1,100 ranger groups in Australia. Rangers do: feral animal and weed control / fire management / marine monitoring / cultural site protection / threatened species management. Ranger programs: provide meaningful employment on Country / pass traditional knowledge to younger generations / produce tangible environmental results / strengthen cultural identity."Learn
    Indigenous Protected Areas (IPAs) (10 min)"IPAs: Indigenous-owned land managed for conservation. 44 million hectares of Australia (larger than the UK). IPA management plans are developed by Traditional Owners. IPAs often have lower fire frequency and better environmental outcomes than adjacent non-Indigenous land."Learn
    Noongar Country and Management (10 min)"The Noongar people of south-west WA: 14 clans, specific boodjar (Country). Noongar knowledge: understanding of the Swan and Canning rivers / wetland management / the seven seasons of the Noongar calendar / use of fire for regeneration / food and medicine from native plants. Noongar involvement in environmental management is growing: Swan River Trust, Perth bushland management, Western Australian Herbarium partnerships."Learn local
    Why Indigenous Management Is Effective (10 min)"Studies consistently show: Indigenous-managed lands have equal or better environmental outcomes than lands managed by governments or private owners. This is because: people who have lived in a place for millennia understand its systems deeply / there is cultural motivation to care for Country (not just economic) / knowledge has been tested and refined over thousands of years."Understand

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 Indigenous environmental management programs and explain at least 2 reasons why Indigenous management is effective.

    Lesson 3: Joint Management of National Parks

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Joint Management in Practice (15 min)"JOINT MANAGEMENT: parks and protected areas where Traditional Owners and government share management responsibility. Examples: Uluru-Kata Tjuta National Park (handed back to Anangu in 2019 after 35 years of joint management) / Kakadu (joint management since 1992) / Boodjamulla (Lawn Hill) National Park (Queensland — Gulf region). Joint management agreements include: cultural site protection / Indigenous rangers / joint decision-making."Learn
    Benefits of Joint Management (10 min)"Joint management benefits: better environmental outcomes (combining knowledge systems) / cultural preservation (sites protected, knowledge transmitted) / economic benefits (jobs, enterprises, community development) / social cohesion (shared purpose across cultures) / healing and reconciliation."Learn
    Challenges (10 min)"Joint management challenges: funding uncertainty / power imbalances (government often retains veto power) / differing worldviews about land management / community capacity / remote location logistics."Understand
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What is joint management of national parks? Why is it important?"Write

    Note: This lesson should ideally be delivered in partnership with local Aboriginal education support or an Indigenous community member.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe joint management, its benefits, and challenges, and explain at least 2 reasons why it is important.

    WEEK 5 — EXPLAIN: Urban Environmental Challenges

    Lesson 1: Urbanisation and the Environment

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Cities Are Major Environmental Challenges and Opportunities (10 min)"Cities consume 75% of global resources and produce 70% of carbon emissions. BUT cities are also where most people live — and where we can achieve efficiencies. The challenge: provide good lives for urban dwellers while reducing environmental impact. This is the urban sustainability challenge."Listen
    Urban Environmental Issues (15 min)Cities face: (1) AIR POLLUTION (vehicles, industry — causes 4.2 million deaths globally per year from outdoor air pollution) / (2) WATER POLLUTION (urban runoff, sewage) / (3) WASTE MANAGEMENT (landfill, recycling, plastic pollution) / (4) HEAT ISLAND EFFECT (cities warmer, caused by dark surfaces, lack of vegetation, heat generated by humans) / (5) BIODIVERSITY LOSS (habitat fragmentation, invasive species).Learn
    Sustainable Cities Movement (10 min)"The sustainable cities movement: compact cities (reduce sprawl, encourage walking) / green buildings (passive design, solar, green roofs) / public transport (reduce car dependency) / urban greening (tree canopy, parks, green corridors) / circular economy (reduce, reuse, recycle, recover). 30-minute city concept (Melbourne/others): everything you need within 30 minutes by public transport, walking, or cycling."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What urban environmental challenge do you think is most pressing in Australian cities? Why? What should be done about it?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 3 urban environmental challenges and explain at least 2 in detail.

    Lesson 2: Perth's Environmental Challenges

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Perth Is Unique (10 min)"Perth is the most isolated major city on Earth. Population: ~2.2 million (growing). It has: Mediterranean climate (hot dry summers, mild wet winters) / global biodiversity hotspot (south-west WA is one of 36 global biodiversity hotspots) / ancient, nutrient-poor soils / drying climate trend (since 1970s)."Learn
    Perth's Key Environmental Challenges (15 min)"Perth's challenges: (1) WATER SECURITY — rainfall declined ~20% since 1970s, dams are less reliable, desalination is backup / (2) URBAN SPRAWL — one of the most spread-out cities globally, car-dependent, consuming farmland and bushland / (3) BIODIVERSITY LOSS — south-west WA has very high endemism, clearing for development threatens critically endangered ecosystems (Banksia woodlands, Swan Coastal Plain) / (4) URBAN HEAT — Perth is warming faster than surrounding areas, urban heat island intensifying / (5) MOORSBY WETLAND DRAINAGE — historical drainage of wetlands for development."Learn
    Managing Perth's Challenges (10 min)"Responses: WA Water Strategy (diversified sources including desalination, recycled water, groundwater) / Directions 2030 (spatial plan to manage growth, urban consolidation, corridors) / Perth Peel Green Plan (protecting environmental assets) / WA Climate Policy (net zero by 2050) / Urban forest strategy (planting trees to counter urban heat)."Learn
    Local Action (5 min)"What can YOU do about Perth's environmental challenges? Write 2–3 specific actions."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 3 environmental challenges specific to Perth and at least 2 management responses.

    Lesson 3: Sustainable Cities — Case Studies

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Sustainable Cities Around the World (15 min)"Let's examine 3 sustainable city models: (1) FREIBURG, GERMANY — Europe's greenest city: solar-powered, extensive tram network, car-free Vauban district, waste recycling 70%+ / (2) SINGAPORE — green city in a dense urban environment: vertical greening, sky gardens, park connectors linking all parks, water management (4 taps strategy) / (3) CURITIBA, BRAZIL — pioneering bus rapid transit, park system that controls flooding, recycling programs."Learn
    What Makes a City Sustainable?     Apply to Perth (10 min)"What can Perth learn from these cities? Evaluate Perth against the sustainable city criteria. What are Perth's strengths? What does Perth need to improve?"Discuss
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is one thing Perth should do to become more sustainable? Why would this work in Perth's context?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 features of sustainable cities and apply them critically to Perth.

    WEEK 6 — ELABORATE: Climate Change Management

    Lesson 1: Climate Mitigation

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Climate Mitigation? (10 min)"CLIMATE MITIGATION: actions that REDUCE the severity of climate change by cutting greenhouse gas emissions. Mitigation addresses the CAUSE. Key areas: energy (transition from fossil fuels to renewables) / transport (electric vehicles, public transport, active transport) / buildings (energy efficiency, gas-free) / industry (process changes, carbon capture) / land use (reforestation, reduced deforestation)."Learn
    Renewable Energy Transition (15 min)"The global renewable energy revolution: Solar and wind are now the CHEAPEST new energy sources in most of the world. Battery storage (for when sun doesn't shine, wind doesn't blow) is falling in price rapidly. Australia: renewables reached ~35% of electricity generation in 2023,目标是 82% by 2030. WA: Synergy shutting down coal-fired power stations, large-scale solar and wind rollout, Western Power grid upgrades."Learn
    Economic and Political Dimensions (10 min)"Climate mitigation has economic and political dimensions: fossil fuel industries employ millions — transition must be JUST (provide alternative jobs) / countries that act first may face short-term economic disadvantage / 'Greenwashing' — companies claiming climate action without substance / 'Climate delay' — advocating small actions while opposing systemic change."Understand
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Write 3 sentences: What is climate mitigation? What is the most important mitigation action for Australia to take? Why?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can define climate mitigation and name at least 3 mitigation actions with at least 2 specific examples.

    Lesson 2: Climate Adaptation

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Climate Adaptation? (10 min)"CLIMATE ADAPTATION: actions that PREPARE for the effects of climate change that are now unavoidable. Even with the best mitigation, some climate change is locked in. Adaptation addresses the EFFECTS. Key areas: infrastructure (flood-resilient buildings, cooling) / water (dams, desalination, recycled water) / agriculture (drought-resistant crops) / health (heat health plans) / coastal management (protect or retreat)."Learn
    Adaptation Case Studies (15 min)Show: NETHERLANDS (flood management — world leader, living with water, Room for the River program) / DHAKA, BANGLADESH (cyclone shelters, flood-resistant housing, early warning systems — reduced deaths dramatically despite ongoing vulnerability) / AUSTRALIAN CITIES (various heatwave response plans, flood mapping, coastal vulnerability assessments).Learn
    Managed Retreat (10 min)"MANAGED RETREAT: proactively moving people and infrastructure AWAY from areas at high risk of climate impacts (coastal flooding, bushfire). In Australia: insurance companies withdrawing coverage from high-risk areas (increasing) / some local councils buying and demolishing high-risk properties / difficult conversations about which communities should receive protection and which should move."Learn
    Adaptation vs Mitigation (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What is the difference between climate mitigation and climate adaptation? Why do we need BOTH?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can explain the difference between mitigation and adaptation, give at least 2 adaptation examples, and explain why both are needed.

    Lesson 3: Local Climate Action

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Local Action Is Part of Global Change (10 min)"Local climate action adds up. Every home with solar, every community garden, every council with a climate plan — all add up.     What Is a Management Plan? (10 min)"A MANAGEMENT PLAN is a structured document that: describes the environmental issue / sets clear objectives / identifies management actions / assigns responsibilities / sets timelines / establishes monitoring and evaluation.
