Unit Content

Energy Transfer and Transformation — Physical Sciences Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 5

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum Science v9 — Physical Sciences

Unit Big Idea: Energy cannot be created or destroyed — it can only be transferred or transformed from one form to another. Understanding energy helps us design efficient systems and make sustainable choices.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Design a system that converts energy from one form to another and predict energy losses

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Design an energy-efficient solution for a real-world problem, explaining energy transfers and losses

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to investigate how energy is transferred and transformed, describe the conservation of energy, analyse energy efficiency in systems, and apply energy principles to design sustainable solutions.

Success Criteria:

  • I can explain that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred or transformed (conservation of energy).
  • I can identify and describe energy transfers in everyday systems.
  • I can investigate energy transformations — how one type of energy changes to another.
  • I can calculate energy efficiency and explain why some energy is always "lost" as heat.
  • I can design systems that minimise energy waste.
  • I can apply energy principles to evaluate real-world energy choices.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: What Is Energy?

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Design an Energy Conversion System

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Show a wind-up toy. Wind it up — energy stored in spring. Let it go — toy moves. "What happened to the energy? Where did it come from? Where did it go?"Observe and wonder
    Task (35 min)Cold Task: "Design a system that changes energy from one form to another. Examples: a bicycle (chemical from food → movement → electrical for lights), a solar calculator (light → electrical). Draw your system. Label: What ENERGY INPUT? What ENERGY OUTPUT? What happens in between? Where might some energy be 'lost'? Year 5: 1 transformation. Year 6 extension: 2+ transformations in a chain."Design energy system
    Share (10 min)3–4 students share their designs. Class votes on most interesting.Share

    Differentiation: EAL students draw their system and point to labels. NEP students: design a system with teacher support.

    Success Criteria Check: Student designs an energy conversion system with an input, output, and attempt to identify energy losses.

    Lesson 2: Forms of Energy

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Engage (Concept Introduction)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Forms of Energy (15 min)"Energy exists in many FORMS: CHEMICAL (stored in food, fuel, batteries). THERMAL/HEAT (hot objects). LIGHT (from sun, bulbs). ELECTRICAL (in wires, circuits). SOUND (from vibrations). MOVEMENT/KINETIC (moving objects). STORED/POTENTIAL (stretched spring, raised weight). NUCLEAR (in atoms). These forms can be TRANSFERRED and TRANSFORMED."Learn energy forms
    Identify the Form (15 min)Scenario cards: burning wood (chemical → heat + light), solar panel (light → electrical), speaker playing music (electrical → sound), lifting a book (chemical in muscles → gravitational potential), car driving (chemical → movement + heat), wind turbine (movement → electrical). For each: identify input and output forms.Identify energy forms
    Real-World Audit (15 min)Walk classroom/school: identify energy forms for 10 objects. Record: OBJECT — ENERGY FORM — IS IT RENEWABLE?Conduct audit
    Write (5 min)"Energy comes in many forms: chemical, thermal, light, electrical, sound, kinetic, potential, nuclear. Energy can be transformed from one form to another."Write definition

    Success Criteria Check: Identifies at least 10 energy forms in real-world contexts and distinguishes between kinetic and potential energy.

    Lesson 3: Conservation of Energy

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The Law of Conservation (15 min)"LAW OF CONSERVATION OF ENERGY: Energy cannot be created or destroyed. It can only be TRANSFERRED (moves from one place to another) or TRANSFORMED (changes from one form to another). The total amount of energy in the universe stays the SAME."Learn law
    Trace Energy (15 min)Trace energy through a coal-fired power plant: Chemical energy in coal → thermal energy when burned → kinetic energy of steam → kinetic energy of turbine → electrical energy from generator → light energy in homes. Total energy at each step = the same (minus small losses).Trace energy path
    Conservation Investigation (15 min)Roll a ball down a ramp. At top: maximum potential energy (high position). At bottom: maximum kinetic energy (fast, low position). Mid-way: half each. The TOTAL (potential + kinetic) stays constant (ignoring friction). Demonstrate with a Newton meter setup or motion sensor if available.Investigate conservation
    Write the Law (5 min)"Energy is ALWAYS conserved. It changes form but the total amount never changes."Write law

    Differentiation: EAL students draw the energy transformation chain. Gifted students: research how Einstein's E=mc² relates to the conservation law (mass can be converted to energy, and energy can create mass).

    Success Criteria Check: States the law of conservation of energy and correctly traces energy through at least one multi-step transformation.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Energy Transfers

    Lesson 1: Transferring Energy

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Energy Transfer? (10 min)"ENERGY TRANSFER is when energy MOVES from one place to another without changing form. Light from the sun transfers through space to Earth. Heat transfers from a hot stove to a pot. Electrical energy transfers through wires to a light bulb."Learn transfer
    Three Types of Transfer (10 min)"CONDUCTION — heat through solids (particle to particle). CONVECTION — heat through fluids (hot rises, cool sinks). RADIATION — heat/light through empty space (waves). Electrical transfer — through wires. Sound transfer — through air/solids/liquids."Learn three types
    Transfer Investigation (15 min)Test three heat transfer types: (1) Conduction: metal spoon in hot water — how fast does heat travel? (2) Convection: food colouring in warm water — how does it move? (3) Radiation: dark paper vs white paper under a heat lamp — which gets hotter?Test transfers
    Record (10 min)Record observations for each transfer type. Draw diagrams showing how energy moved in each case.Record observations
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"How does the heat from the sun reach Earth?"Written response

    Success Criteria Check: Investigates all three heat transfer methods, records observations, and correctly identifies each type.

    Lesson 2: Energy Transfer Chains

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Energy Chains (10 min)"An ENERGY CHAIN shows the SEQUENCE of energy transformations from start to finish. Arrow shows direction of energy flow." Example: Sun → plant → coal → power station → electrical wire → light bulb → light.Learn chains
    Build Energy Chains (20 min)Students build chains for: (1) Eating breakfast → running →: Chemical (food) → kinetic (running) → thermal (body heat). (2) Wind turbine → home: Kinetic (wind) → electrical (turbine) → light (bulb). (3) Solar phone charger: Light (sun) → electrical (panel) → chemical (battery). Draw each as a chain diagram.Build chains
    Energy Loss in Chains (15 min)"Each transformation loses some energy as HEAT (due to friction, resistance, etc.). A 100W light bulb doesn't produce 100W of light — some is lost as heat. An internal combustion engine converts 25% of chemical energy to movement, 75% is lost as heat. Energy is CONSERVED, but some becomes less useful (low-grade heat)."Learn energy loss
    Write (5 min)Draw: INPUT ENERGY → TRANSFORMER → USEFUL OUTPUT + WASTED HEAT.Draw diagram

    Success Criteria Check: Builds at least 3 energy chains and correctly identifies where energy losses occur in each.

    Lesson 3: Conductors and Insulators in Energy Transfer

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Energy Conductors (10 min)"ENERGY CONDUCTORS are materials that allow energy to transfer QUICKLY. Metals are good thermal conductors (pots, radiators) and electrical conductors (wires). Water conducts heat well ( oceans regulate climate). Air is a poor conductor (insulator — trapped air in double glazing insulates homes)."Learn conductors
    Energy Insulators (10 min)"ENERGY INSULATORS slow or prevent energy transfer. Wool, fibreglass, foam — trap air and reduce heat transfer. Plastic, rubber — prevent electrical transfer (safe handles). Double glazing, cavity walls — reduce heat transfer in buildings."Learn insulators
    Testing Insulators (20 min)Test which materials best prevent thermal energy transfer: wrap ice cubes in different materials (wool, plastic, aluminium foil, paper, cotton). After 20 minutes, measure how much ice melted. Less melted = better insulator.Test insulators
    Record Results (5 min)Graph results. Order materials from best to worst insulator.Graph and order

    Success Criteria Check: Tests at least 5 materials as thermal insulators, records results, and correctly interprets which materials are best insulators.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLAIN: Energy Transformations

    Lesson 1: What Is Transformation?

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Energy Transformation vs Transfer (15 min)"ENERGY TRANSFORMATION is when energy CHANGES FORM. Energy transfer is when energy MOVES from one place to another. Example: Solar panel: light → electrical (transformation, no movement). Electric heater: electrical → thermal (transformation). Wire carrying electricity: electrical → electrical (transfer only)."Learn distinction
    Transformation Sort (15 min)Scenario cards: light bulb (electrical → light + heat), photosynthesis (light → chemical), swinging pendulum (potential → kinetic → potential), battery (chemical → electrical), nuclear power plant (nuclear → thermal → kinetic → electrical), bouncing ball (kinetic → potential → kinetic, losing some to heat each bounce). Sort into: TRANSFORMATION ONLY, TRANSFER ONLY, BOTH.Sort scenarios
    Transformation Diagrams (15 min)Students draw at least 5 transformation diagrams: Input form → TRANSFORMER → Output form. Label the transformer (what causes the change).Draw diagrams
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"What is the difference between an energy transfer and an energy transformation?"Written response

    Success Criteria Check: Distinguishes between energy transfer and transformation and correctly categorises at least 6 real-world examples.

    Lesson 2: Common Energy Transformations

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Six Key Transformations (15 min)"Six transformations we see every day: CHEMICAL → THERMAL (burning, digestion). CHEMICAL → KINETIC (muscles moving). ELECTRICAL → LIGHT (bulbs, screens). ELECTRICAL → THERMAL (heaters, toasters). LIGHT → ELECTRICAL (solar panels). KINETIC → ELECTRICAL (dynamos, wind turbines)."Learn key transformations
    Transformation Research (20 min)Students choose 3 appliances/devices from home and research: What energy transformation happens? What is the input? What is the useful output? What is wasted? Create a table.Research appliances
    Common Devices Investigation (15 min)Test devices if possible: torch (chemical in battery → electrical → light), fan (electrical → kinetic), heater (electrical → thermal), solar calculator (light → electrical). Draw transformation diagrams for each.Test devices
    Write (5 min)"Everyday devices transform energy from one form to another. Some transformations are more efficient than others."Write conclusion

    Success Criteria Check: Researches at least 3 devices, identifies their energy transformations, and creates accurate transformation diagrams.

    Lesson 3: Energy Transformation Devices

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Power Station Analysis (15 min)Coal-fired power station transformation chain: Chemical (coal) → Thermal (combustion) → Kinetic (steam turbine) → Kinetic (generator) → Electrical (transmitted through grid). Efficiency: ~35–45% — most energy lost as waste heat.Learn power station
    Renewable Energy Systems (15 min)Solar: Light → Electrical (photovoltaic cells). Wind: Kinetic → Electrical (turbine). Hydroelectric: Kinetic (falling water) → Electrical (turbine). Biomass: Chemical (plants) → Thermal → Kinetic → Electrical. Compare efficiencies.Learn renewables
    Compare Efficiencies (10 min)Create comparison chart: COAL: 35–45% efficient. SOLAR PV: 15–22% efficient. WIND: 30–45% efficient. HYDRO: 85–95% efficient. BIOMASS: 25–35% efficient. "Why do renewable sources have such different efficiencies?"Compare
    Reflection (10 min)"Which energy source is most efficient? Which is most sustainable? Which is most available in Australia? How does understanding efficiency help us make better energy choices?"Discuss

    Differentiation: EAL students create a visual comparison chart. Gifted students: research perovskite solar cells and why they're more efficient than traditional silicon.

    Success Criteria Check: Compares efficiency of at least 4 energy systems and explains why efficiency varies.

    WEEK 4 — ELABORATE: Energy Efficiency

    Lesson 1: What Is Efficiency?

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is Efficiency? (15 min)"ENERGY EFFICIENCY = USEFUL OUTPUT ÷ TOTAL INPUT. Expressed as a percentage. If a device puts in 100J of energy and gets 25J of useful output, it is 25% efficient. The other 75J is wasted (usually as low-grade heat). We always want HIGHER efficiency = less wasted energy."Learn efficiency
    Calculate Efficiency (15 min)Examples: (1) Light bulb: 100J electrical in, 15J light out = 15% efficient. (2) LED: 100J electrical in, 85J light out = 85% efficient. (3) Car engine: 100J chemical in, 25J movement out = 25% efficient. Students calculate: 100J in, 40J out = ?%. 200J in, 50J out = ?%.Calculate efficiency
    Why Is 100% Impossible? (10 min)"The Second Law of Thermodynamics: every energy transformation produces some waste heat that disperses into the environment and cannot be recovered. This is ENTROPY — the tendency of energy to spread out and become less useful. No machine can be 100% efficient."Learn entropy
    Write (10 min)"Efficiency = useful output ÷ total input × 100. No energy transformation is 100% efficient because some energy always becomes low-grade heat. This is entropy."Write definition

    Success Criteria Check: Calculates efficiency using the formula and correctly explains why 100% efficiency is impossible.

    Lesson 2: Investigating Efficiency

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Simple Machine Efficiency (15 min)"Let's investigate the efficiency of simple machines." Set up: lever, pulley, or ramp. Measure: Input force × distance (input work) vs Output force × distance (output work). Calculate efficiency.Plan investigation
    Investigate a Lever (20 min)Test a lever: Effort (force applied) × effort distance = Input work. Load (force lifted) × load distance = Output work. Calculate: Output ÷ Input × 100 = Efficiency %. Test with different load positions.Test lever
    Compare Systems (10 min)"What made the lever more or less efficient? (Friction between pivot and bar, friction in the rope.) What would make it closer to 100%? (Lubrication, smoother surfaces.)"Analyse
    Record (5 min)Record all measurements and calculations. Draw graph: Load position vs Efficiency %.Record results

    Success Criteria Check: Conducts a fair test measuring input and output work, calculates efficiency, and draws conclusions.

    Lesson 3: Reducing Energy Waste

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Sources of Energy Waste (15 min)"Energy is wasted through: HEAT LOSS (cars, machines, light bulbs — all produce waste heat). FRICTION (engines, machines, moving parts). AIR RESISTANCE (vehicles, aircraft). ELECTRICAL RESISTANCE (wires heat up slightly). LIGHT SCATTERING (some light scatters in wrong directions). Sound (some energy lost as sound)."Learn waste sources
    Reducing Waste — Real Solutions (15 min)"Solutions: INSULATION (reduce heat loss — double glazing, cavity walls, fibreglass batts). AERODYNAMICS (streamlined shapes reduce air resistance — sports cars, aircraft). LUBRICATION (oil reduces friction in engines). LED LIGHTING (more efficient, less heat waste). HIGH-VOLTAGE TRANSMISSION (electricity transmitted at high voltage reduces current, reduces resistive heating in wires)."Learn solutions
    Design Challenge (15 min)"Design a plan to reduce energy waste in: (A) your classroom, (B) a refrigerator, or (C) a car. What specific changes would you make? How much energy would you save? Use evidence from your investigations."Design plan
    Share (5 min)3–4 students share plansShare

    Differentiation: EAL students draw and label their design. Gifted students: research passive house design and how it achieves near-zero energy consumption.

    Success Criteria Check: Designs a plan to reduce energy waste using evidence from investigations.

    WEEK 5 — ELABORATE: Renewable and Non-Renewable Energy

    Lesson 1: Types of Energy Resources

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Renewable vs Non-Renewable (15 min)"NON-RENEWABLE: finite resources that run out when depleted. Fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) — take millions of years to form, will run out within centuries. Nuclear (uranium) — limited supply. RENEWABLE: resources that replenish naturally within human timescales. Solar, wind, hydro, geothermal, tidal, biomass — available indefinitely as long as the sun shines, wind blows, water flows."Learn distinction
    Australia's Energy Mix (15 min)"Australia's energy: Coal 54%, gas 18%, renewables 23% (solar growing fastest, wind second). Australia has among the highest solar potential in the world and excellent wind resources. We are one of the largest per-capita energy users in the world."Learn Australia
    Sort Activity (10 min)Cards: coal, oil, natural gas, solar panels, wind turbines, nuclear power, hydroelectric dams, tidal energy, geothermal, biomass. Sort: Renewable or Non-Renewable? Explain reasoning.Sort cards
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Why is coal considered non-renewable?"Written response

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly sorts at least 8 energy resources as renewable or non-renewable and explains the difference.

    Lesson 2: Renewable Energy Technologies

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Solar Energy (15 min)"SOLAR PV (photovoltaic): light photons strike silicon cells → electrons released → electrical current. Each cell ~22% efficient. CSP (concentrated solar power): mirrors focus sunlight → heat water → steam → turbine → electricity. Panels on rooftops: direct conversion. Solar farms: large-scale generation."Learn solar
    Wind Energy (15 min)"Wind turbines: wind turns blades (kinetic) → shaft spins → generator produces electricity. Modern turbines: 30–45% efficiency. Offshore wind farms (in the ocean) capture stronger, more consistent winds. Onshore wind farms: more accessible but face community opposition due to noise and visual impact."Learn wind
    Hydro and Other Renewables (15 min)"HYDROELECTRIC: water stored behind dams (potential energy) → released through turbines (kinetic) → generator produces electricity. Very high efficiency (85–95%) but dam construction has environmental impacts. GEOTHERMAL: heat from Earth's interior (radioactive decay). TIDAL: tidal movement drives turbines. BIOMASS: burning plant material releases chemical energy stored from sunlight."Learn other renewables
    Compare Renewables (5 min)Table: TYPE — HOW IT WORKS — EFFICIENCY — ADVANTAGES — DISADVANTAGES — SUITABLE FOR AUSTRALIA?Complete table

    Success Criteria Check: Researches at least 3 renewable energy technologies and creates a comparison table showing how each works and its suitability.

    Lesson 3: Sustainability Challenge

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Design Activity)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Sustainability Criteria (10 min)"For energy to be SUSTAINABLE: ENVIRONMENTAL — low emissions, minimal habitat destruction. ECONOMIC — affordable to build and operate. SOCIAL — accepted by communities, safe. ENERGY SECURITY — reliable supply, not dependent on imports."Learn criteria
    Choose an Energy Scenario (15 min)"Choose ONE scenario: (A) A remote Indigenous community needs reliable electricity. (B) A coastal city wants to reduce carbon emissions. (C) An agricultural region needs to power irrigation pumps. Design an energy solution using renewable sources. Consider: Which energy source? Why? How will it be stored? What are the trade-offs?"Choose and plan
    Design Your Solution (15 min)Draw your energy system. Label: Energy source, transformation steps, storage (if needed), distribution. Calculate estimated efficiency.Design solution
    Present and Evaluate (10 min)Present solutions. Evaluate each against: environmental impact, cost, reliability. Class votes on most sustainable.Present

    Differentiation: EAL students draw their system and explain verbally. Gifted students: design a hybrid system combining 2+ renewable sources to ensure 24/7 power supply.

    Success Criteria Check: Designs a renewable energy solution using at least one renewable source and evaluates it against sustainability criteria.

    WEEK 6 — EXPLAIN: Energy in Our Lives

    Lesson 1: Household Energy

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Energy in a Typical Home (15 min)"Average Australian home energy use: Heating/cooling 40%, hot water 22%, appliances 20%, lighting 8%, cooking 5%, other 5%. The biggest energy users are HVAC (heating, ventilation, air conditioning) and hot water systems."Learn home use
    Energy Audit (20 min)Students conduct a simplified home energy audit (using a checklist): What appliances use most energy? When are they used? What could be changed to reduce energy use? Students calculate: If a 100W light bulb runs 6 hours/day, how much energy does it use? (100W × 6h = 600Wh = 0.6kWh/day.)Conduct audit
    Calculate Energy Cost (10 min)"Average Australian electricity cost: ~30c/kWh. A 100W light bulb running 6 hours/day = 0.6kWh/day × 30c = 18c/day. Over a year = $65.70. Replace with 15W LED (same light output): 0.015kW × 6h = 0.09kWh/day × 30c = 2.7c/day = $9.86/year. Saving: ~$55/year per bulb!"Calculate savings
    Write Recommendations (5 min)Write 3 recommendations for reducing home energy use with calculations.Write

    Success Criteria Check: Conducts an energy audit, calculates energy consumption and cost, and makes evidence-based recommendations.

    Lesson 2: Industrial Energy

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Industrial Energy Use (15 min)"Industries use huge amounts of energy: MINING — drilling, crushing, transporting ore (mostly diesel, some electricity). MANUFACTURING — smelting metals, processing materials (electricity, gas). FOOD — refrigeration, processing, packaging (electricity, gas). TEXTILES — dyeing, weaving, heating (gas, electricity)."Learn industry use
    Energy Intensity (10 min)"ENERGY INTENSITY = energy used per unit of output. Steel production: very high intensity — large amounts of coal/electricity per tonne. Renewable manufacturing: also energy-intensive initially, but pays back energy debt over time. Industries constantly seek to reduce energy intensity to cut costs and emissions."Learn intensity
    Case Study — Aluminium Smelting (15 min)"Aluminium is made by passing electrical current through molten alumina (Al₂O₃ extracted from bauxite). Requires enormous electricity — aluminium smelters are built near cheap power sources (hydro in Norway/Tasmania). Recycling aluminium uses 95% less energy than smelting from ore!"Learn case
    Write (10 min)"Industrial energy use is high because of the processes required. Energy efficiency improvements save both money and emissions."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Researches one industrial energy use case and explains why it requires so much energy.

    Lesson 3: Energy Futures

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australia's Energy Future (15 min)"Australia's renewable energy transition: The Australian Government has a Renewable Energy Target. More than 33% of Australia's electricity already comes from renewables. South Australia has the world's biggest battery (Tesla Powerpack) and significant wind generation. Victoria closed its last coal-fired power station. Queensland is building large solar farms."Learn transition
    Emerging Technologies (15 min)"EMERGING: GREEN HYDROGEN — use excess renewable electricity to split water → hydrogen gas → burn for heat/industry OR use in fuel cells. BATTERY STORAGE — lithium-ion battery farms store solar/wind energy for use when sun doesn't shine/wind doesn't blow. This is the key to 100% renewable grids. CARBON CAPTURE — capture CO₂ from coal plants and store underground. ELECTRIC VEHICLES — 90% efficient vs 25% for petrol cars."Learn emerging
    Debate: Energy Choices (15 min)"Should Australia invest more in nuclear energy (zero emissions, reliable, but radioactive waste)? Or should we focus entirely on renewables + storage? Students take positions and debate using evidence."Debate
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"What is the biggest barrier to a 100% renewable energy grid?"Written response

    Differentiation: EAL students take notes in structured format. Gifted students: research small modular reactors (SMRs) and their potential in Australia.

    Success Criteria Check: Evaluates at least 2 future energy technologies and presents a reasoned position on Australia's energy future.

    DIFFERENTIATION NOTES

    EAL Learners

  • Visual Word Wall: Picture-word pairs for all vocabulary.
  • Partner Support: Paired during investigations.
  • Sentence Starters: "The input energy is..." / "The output energy is..." / "The efficiency is..."

    • Gifted Learners

  • Thermodynamics Deep Dive: Explore the Second Law of Thermodynamics in depth — entropy, heat engines, Carnot efficiency.
  • Grid Design: Design a hypothetical 100% renewable energy grid for Australia. Which states connect? Where are solar/wind farms? How do you handle peak demand?

    • Students with NEPs

  • Simplified Calculations: Focus on identification of transformations, less on efficiency calculations.
  • Oral Option: Verbal presentation accepted.
  • Partner Support: Work with supportive partner.
