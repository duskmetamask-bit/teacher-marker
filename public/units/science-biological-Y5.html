Unit Content

Life Cycles and Reproduction — Biological Sciences Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 5

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum Science v9 — Biological Sciences

Unit Big Idea: All living things reproduce — creating offspring that carry genetic information from parents. Life cycles vary enormously across species, but reproduction is universal to all life.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Research and present the life cycle of two organisms and explain the differences

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Design an investigation into a reproduction strategy and explain how it maximises species survival

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to describe the reproductive processes of different organisms, compare sexual and asexual reproduction, explain how genetic information is passed from parents to offspring, evaluate reproductive strategies in different environments, and analyse the role of life cycles in species survival.

Success Criteria:

  • I can describe and compare the life cycles of at least 4 different organisms.
  • I can explain the difference between sexual and asexual reproduction.
  • I can describe how genetic information is passed from parents to offspring.
  • I can evaluate how different reproductive strategies maximise species survival.
  • I can use science words: reproduction, sexual, asexual, gametes, fertilisation, zygote, offspring, genetics, metamorphosis, dormancy.

    Lesson 2: Sexual vs Asexual Reproduction

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Engage

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Sexual Reproduction (20 min)"SEXUAL REPRODUCTION: two parents contribute GENETIC MATERIAL. Parent 1 produces GAMETES (sex cells: sperm or pollen). Parent 2 produces gametes (eggs or ovules). FERTILISATION: gametes join to form a ZYGOTE (first cell of new organism). Offspring are genetically UNIQUE — different from both parents. Combines genetic variation."Learn
    Asexual Reproduction (20 min)"ASEXUAL REPRODUCTION: ONE parent produces offspring. Offspring are GENETICALLY IDENTICAL to the parent (CLONES). Types: BINARY FISSION (bacteria, amoeba — cell divides into two). BUDDING (hydra, yeast — new organism grows from parent). SPORULATION (fungi, ferns — spores released). VEGETATIVE PROPAGATION (strawberry runners, potato eyes — new plant grows from part of parent)."Learn
    Compare (5 min)SEXUAL: 2 parents, genetically unique offspring, slower, more energy-intensive. ASEXUAL: 1 parent, genetically identical, faster, no mate needed. Which is better? Depends on environment!Compare
    Sort Activity (5 min)Items: bacterium, strawberry plant, human, fern, hydra, chicken, potato, apple tree. Sort: SEXUAL vs ASEXUAL.Sort

    Success Criteria Check: Correctly classifies at least 6 organisms as sexual or asexual reproduction and explains the difference.

    Lesson 3: Life Cycles Introduction

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Engage

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Life Cycle Diversity (15 min)"LIFE CYCLES vary enormously. SIMPLE: bacteria divide every 20 minutes. MODERATE: human — 9 months gestation, years to maturity. COMPLEX: 17-year cicada — most of life underground, emerge for weeks to reproduce. PLANTS: some annuals complete life in one season, some trees live hundreds of years."Learn
    Life Cycle Stages Investigation (20 min)Research one organism's life cycle in depth: (A) chicken, (B) frog, (C) butterfly, (D) bean plant. Create a detailed life cycle diagram with stage names, descriptions, and duration.Research
    Draw and Label (10 min)Draw your organism's life cycle with all stages. Label.Draw
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"What is the main difference between sexual and asexual reproduction?"Written response

    Success Criteria Check: Creates a detailed life cycle diagram for one organism with accurate stage descriptions.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Animal Reproduction

    Lesson 1: Mammal Reproduction

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Mammal Reproduction (20 min)"MAMMALS: internal fertilisation (inside mother's body). Embryo develops in UTERUS, receives nutrients via PLACENTA. Live birth (except monotremes). ALTRICIAL vs PRECOCIAL: Altricial (helpless, eyes closed, no fur — mice, humans). Precocial (can walk within hours — horses, kangaroos)."Learn
    Monotremes — Egg-Laying Mammals (15 min)"MONOTREMES: platypus and 4 echidna species. The ONLY egg-laying mammals. Fertilisation → shell forms around embryo → laid in burrow. Mother curls around eggs. Hatches after 10 days. Baby licks milk from mother's mammary patches (no nipples)."Learn
    Marsupials — Pouch-Bearing Mammals (10 min)"MARSUPIALS: kangaroo, koala, possum. Short gestation in uterus (weeks). Embryo finishes development in EXTERNAL POUCH (marsupium) drinking milk. At birth: tiny, hairless, blind — must crawl to pouch."Learn
    Compare Mammal Types (5 min)Table: MONOTREME vs MARSUPIAL vs PLACENTAL. Differences in: fertilisation, gestation location, development, nourishment.Compare

    Success Criteria Check: Compares monotreme, marsupial, and placental mammal reproduction and identifies key differences.

    Lesson 2: Bird and Reptile Reproduction

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Bird Reproduction (20 min)"BIRDS: internal fertilisation, hard-shelled eggs laid. MATING: cloacal kiss (male and female cloacas touch). REPRODUCTIVE TRACT: oviduct adds albumin (white) and shell. INCUBATION: parents warm eggs (species-dependent period). PARENTAL CARE: many species both parents care for chicks. Some species ( megapodes like malleefowl) leave eggs in mound, no parental care."Learn
    Reptile Reproduction (15 min)"REPTILES: mostly internal fertilisation, leathery eggs laid on land (except sea turtles — lay eggs on beaches). Some reptiles (some snakes, lizards) are OVIPAROUS (egg-laying). Some are VIVIPAROUS (live birth — some pythons, sea snakes). Temperature determines sex in some reptiles ( incubation temperature). No parental care in most."Learn
    Compare Birds and Reptiles (10 min)Similar: internal fertilisation, egg-laying. Different: bird eggs hard-shelled, reptile eggs leathery. Bird parental care extensive, reptile minimal. Both warm-blooded (birds) vs cold-blooded (most reptiles).Compare
    Write (5 min)"Birds and reptiles both lay eggs, but bird eggs have hard shells and receive extensive parental care."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Compares bird and reptile reproduction, correctly identifying similarities and differences.

    Lesson 3: Fish and Amphibian Reproduction

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Fish Reproduction (15 min)"FISH: mostly external fertilisation (female releases eggs, male releases sperm/milt simultaneously). SPAWNING: many eggs released (cod produces millions). Some fish care for eggs (mouth-brooding cichlids — dad holds eggs in mouth). Cuttlefish and some sharks: internal fertilisation, live birth."Learn
    Amphibian Reproduction (15 min)"AMPHIBIANS: external fertilisation in water. Most: female lays eggs in water, male releases sperm over them (amplexus). SPAWN: jelly-coated eggs (frogspawn). METAMORPHOSIS: tadpole → froglet → frog. Some amphibians: internal fertilisation (salamanders), some live birth (alpine salamander)."Learn
    Compare Across Vertebrates (15 min)Table comparing all vertebrate groups: where fertilisation occurs, where development occurs, parental care level.Compare
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Why do most fish and amphibians need to lay eggs in or near water?"Written response

    Differentiation: Gifted students: research the axolotl — a salamander that retains larval features (external gills) into adulthood (neoteny). Is it sexual or asexual reproduction?

    Success Criteria Check: Compares fish and amphibian reproduction, correctly identifying external fertilisation and the importance of water for egg survival.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLAIN: Invertebrate Reproduction

    Lesson 1: Insect Reproduction

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Insect Reproduction Overview (15 min)"INSECTS: internal fertilisation. Most lay eggs. COMPLETE METAMORPHOSIS: egg → larva (caterpillar — eating machine) → pupa (chrysalis — transformation) → adult (reproductive stage). INCOMPLETE METAMORPHOSIS: egg → nymph (mini adult) → adult. Some insects: live birth (aphids under good conditions)."Learn
    Honeybee Deep Dive (20 min)"HONEYBEE: CASTE SYSTEM. QUEEN: mates with multiple drones (males), stores sperm for years, lays up to 2000 eggs/day. DRONES: males, result of unfertilised eggs (parthenogenesis). WORKERS: sterile females, care for brood, build comb, forage. Queen substance (pheromone) regulates colony. If queen dies, workers raise new queen from young larva."Learn
    Interesting Facts (5 min)"Queen can lay fertilised (worker bees, new queens) or unfertilised (drones) eggs. Drones have no father — only a mother! This is called arrhenotoky (form of parthenogenesis)."Learn
    Draw Life Cycle (10 min)Draw honeybee life cycle.Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Describes honeybee reproduction including the caste system and parthenogenesis in drones.

    Lesson 2: Crustaceans and Molluscs

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Crustacean Reproduction (15 min)"CRUSTACEANS (crabs, lobsters, shrimp, barnacles): mostly internal fertilisation. Many: female carries eggs under tail (berried). Some: free-spawning (corals release eggs and sperm into water). Amphipods: males carry females on back before mating. MARSUPIAL CRUSTACEAN: some isopods (roly-polys) have brood pouch."Learn
    Mollusc Reproduction (15 min)"MOLLUSCS (snails, slugs, squid, octopus): Most are separate sexes. Some are hermaphrodites (both sexes in one individual — earthworms, some snails). EXTERNAL: many free-spawning (squid, octopus — males use hectocotylus arm to pass spermatophores). INTERNAL: most snails and slugs (simultaneous hermaphrodites — both lay eggs after mating)."Learn
    Compare Invertebrate Groups (15 min)Table: INSECTS vs CRUSTACEANS vs MOLLUSCS. Fertilisation (internal/external), parental care, metamorphosis.Compare
    Write (5 min)"Invertebrates show enormous diversity in reproductive strategies."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Compares invertebrate reproductive strategies and identifies at least 3 key differences.

    Lesson 3: Australian Invertebrates

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australian Invertebrates (15 min)"AUSTRALIAN INVERTEBRATES: (1) Witches (stick insects) — some parthenogenetic, females produce clones without mating. (2) Garden snails (introduced) — simultaneous hermaphrodites, each lays eggs after mating. (3) Christmas tree worms — broadcast spawning (release eggs and sperm into water). (4) Tarantulas — internal fertilisation, lay eggs in burrow, mother guards spiderlings."Learn
    Research Project (20 min)Choose one Australian invertebrate: (A) Christmas beetle, (B) Sydney funnel-web spider, (C) blue-ringed octopus, (D) giant Gippsland earthworm. Research and present: reproduction type, egg/offspring count, parental care, metamorphosis stages.Research
    Present (15 min)3–4 students presentPresent

    Success Criteria Check: Researches and presents reproduction of one Australian invertebrate with accurate details.

    WEEK 4 — ELABORATE: Plant Reproduction

    Lesson 1: Flowering Plant Reproduction

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Flower Parts and Reproduction (20 min)"FLOWERS = reproductive organs. MALE parts: STAMEN (anther + filament). Anther produces POLLEN (contains male gametes). FEMALE parts: PISTIL (stigma + style + ovary). Ovary contains OVULES (female gametes). POLLINATION: pollen lands on stigma. FERTILISATION: pollen tube grows down style to ovule, sperm joins egg → ZYGOTE. Ovule becomes SEED, ovary becomes FRUIT."Learn
    Pollination Mechanisms (15 min)"WIND POLLINATION: lightweight pollen, feathery stigmas (grasses, eucalypts). ANIMAL POLLINATION: nectar guides, showy petals, fragrance. Bees see UV patterns. Birds (honeyeaters) attracted to red/orange. Some flowers: male and female parts on different plants (dioecious — e.g., holly)."Learn
    Fertilisation Process (10 min)Double fertilisation in flowering plants: one sperm + egg → embryo. One sperm + two polar nuclei → endosperm (food storage). Draw process.Draw
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"What is the difference between pollination and fertilisation in plants?"Written response

    Success Criteria Check: Explains the process of pollination and fertilisation in flowering plants and identifies at least 3 plant parts involved.

    Lesson 2: Non-Flowering Plant Reproduction

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Fern Reproduction (15 min)"FERNS: do NOT produce flowers or seeds. Reproduce via SPORES on underside of fronds (leaves). SPORANGIA: clusters that produce spores. When spores land in suitable conditions → PROTHALLUS (tiny heart-shaped gametophyte). This produces GAMETES (sperm and eggs). Sperm swims to egg in water → zygote → new sporophyte (fern plant). This is ALTERATION OF GENERATIONS."Learn
    Gymnosperm Reproduction (15 min)"GYMNOSPERMS (cycads, conifers): 'naked seeds' — seeds not enclosed in fruit. CONES: male cones (produce pollen) and female cones (produce ovules). Wind pollination. CYCADS: ancient plants, male and female on separate plants (dioecious). Seeds often have bright coverings to attract birds for dispersal."Learn
    Moss and Liverwort (10 min)"MOSSES and LIVERWORTS: non-vascular plants. Reproduce via spores. Alternation of generations dominant: gametophyte is the visible plant, sporophyte is the capsule on a stalk."Learn
    Compare Plant Groups (10 min)Table: FLOWERING vs FERN vs GYMNOSPERM vs MOSS. Seeds/spores? Flowers? Vascular?Compare

    Success Criteria Check: Compares reproduction in at least 3 non-flowering plant groups and explains alternation of generations.

    Lesson 3: Asexual Reproduction in Plants

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Asexual Plant Reproduction (20 min)"PLANTS can reproduce ASEXUALLY. VEGETATIVE PROPAGATION: new plant from part of parent — genetically identical clone. RUNNERS (strawberry): horizontal stems → new plants at nodes. RHIZOMES (ginger, couch grass): underground stems → new shoots. TUBERS (potato): modified underground stems with 'eyes' → new plant. BULBS (onion, tulip): underground food storage organs → new bulb offsets. CUTTINGS: humans take stem pieces, put in soil → new plant."Learn
    Advantages and Disadvantages (10 min)"ADVANTAGES: fast, no need for pollinators, one parent can colonise area. DISADVANTAGES: all offspring genetically identical (no variation) — vulnerable to same diseases. If parent has weakness, clones have same weakness."Learn
    Investigation (15 min)Propagate a plant asexually: take a strawberry runner and plant it in a pot. OR plant a potato eye cutting. Observe and record over 3 weeks.Investigate
    Write (5 min)"Plants can reproduce asexually, producing genetically identical offspring."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Identifies at least 4 types of asexual reproduction in plants and explains the advantages/disadvantages.

    WEEK 5 — EXPLAIN: Genetics and Inheritance

    Lesson 1: What Do Offspring Inherit?

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Inheritance Overview (15 min)"OFFSPRING INHERIT genes from BOTH parents. Genes contain instructions for building and running the organism. Inherited traits: eye colour, fur colour, height potential, seed shape. ENVIRONMENT also affects traits: nutrition affects height, sunlight affects plant colour. Nature (genes) + Nurture (environment) = phenotype."Learn
    Punnett Squares (20 min)"PUNNETT SQUARE: diagram to predict offspring genotypes. If parent is Hh (heterozygous) crossed with hh (homozygous recessive): 50% Hh, 50% hh. If H = purple flowers, h = white flowers: 50% purple, 50% white offspring."Learn
    Pedigree Analysis (10 min)"PEDIGREE CHARTS: show how traits pass through generations. Useful for predicting offspring characteristics in breeding programs."Learn
    Activity (5 min)Complete Punnett square for: TT × tt (T = tall, t = short). What % tall vs short offspring?Complete

    Success Criteria Check: Uses Punnett squares to predict offspring genotypes and phenotypes for at least 2 crosses.

    Lesson 2: Genes and DNA

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explain

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    DNA Structure (15 min)"DNA = Deoxyribonucleic acid. Double helix (twisted ladder). Four bases: ADENINE, THYMINE, GUANINE, CYTOSINE (A-T, G-C). Genes = sections of DNA with instructions for making proteins. Each gene is hundreds to thousands of base pairs long. Humans: ~25,000 genes."Learn
    Chromosomes (10 min)"CHROMOSOMES: DNA wrapped around proteins. Humans: 46 chromosomes (23 pairs). One set from mum (23), one set from dad (23). Pairs: one pair determines sex (XX = female, XY = male). Other pairs determine other traits."Learn
    Alleles (10 min)"ALLELES: different versions of the same gene. DOMINANT allele (capital letter): shows if present. RECESSIVE allele (lowercase): only shows if no dominant allele present. HOMOZYGOUS (AA or aa): two same alleles. HETEROZYGOUS (Aa): one of each."Learn
    Write (15 min)"DNA contains genes which determine inherited traits. Different alleles can be dominant or recessive."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Explains the relationship between DNA, genes, chromosomes, and alleles with at least 3 examples.

    Lesson 3: Variation and Adaptation

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Genetic Variation (15 min)"GENETIC VARIATION: differences in DNA between individuals. Sources: MUTATION (random changes in DNA — rare, but source of all new variation), GENE SHUFFLING (during meiosis — crossing over mixes parental genes), SEXUAL REPRODUCTION (combines genes from two parents — major source of variation)."Learn
    Variation in Australian Animals (15 min)"Australian animals show variation: EASTERN GREY KANGAROO: coat colour varies from light grey to dark brown. TASMANIAN DEVIL: black/white fur ratio varies. Galahs: grey and pink, but intensity varies. This variation allows species to adapt to changing environments."Learn
    Adaptation and Genetics (10 min)"STRUCTURAL ADAPTATIONS: physical features coded by genes. BEHAVIOURAL ADAPTATIONS: instincts (coded by genes) + learned behaviours (not coded). Some traits are highly heritable (passed from parents). Others are modified by environment."Learn
    Design Exercise (10 min)"If you were breeding dogs, how would you increase the proportion of puppies with a desired trait? (Select parents with the trait, use Punnett squares to predict outcomes, cull off-types.)"Design

    Success Criteria Check: Explains sources of genetic variation and connects genetic variation to adaptation.

    WEEK 6 — ELABORATE: Reproductive Strategies

    Lesson 1: Trade-offs in Reproduction

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Reproductive Trade-offs (20 min)"REPRODUCTIVE EFFORT: energy invested in reproduction vs other functions. r vs K STRATEGY: r-strategists (bacteria, insects, fish): many offspring, little care, short lifespan. K-strategists (mammals, birds): few offspring, high care, long lifespan. Each strategy maximises reproductive success in different environments."Learn
    Case Studies (15 min)"COD FISH: r-strategist — millions of eggs, no parental care, most die. OSTRICH: K-strategist — few eggs, both parents incubate, 9-month care. MARSUPIAL vs PLACENTAL: both K-strategists but marsupials invest less in gestation (shorter), more in lactation (pouch).Learn
    When Each Strategy Works (10 min)"r-strategy: unstable environment, high mortality from other causes, food unpredictable. K-strategy: stable environment, strong competition, predictable resources. In stable environments, K-strategists outcompete r-strategists. In disturbed environments, r-strategists colonise first."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Organisms face trade-offs: many offspring with little care vs few offspring with lots of care."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Compares r and K reproductive strategies and explains when each is advantageous.

    Lesson 2: Australian Reproductive Strategies

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Australian Examples (20 min)"FRILLED DRAGON: 4–25 eggs, buried in soil, left alone. Female may lay multiple clutches per season. MALLEEFOWL: 1 egg per season in large mound, male tends mound for 9 months. MEGAPODES: one of the most demanding reproductive strategies — massive energy investment by male. SEAHORSES: male has POUCH, female deposits eggs in male's pouch, male fertilises and carries developing young (male pregnancy).Learn
    Investigation (15 min)Choose one uniquely Australian reproductive strategy. Research: What is the strategy? Why did it evolve? What are the advantages? What are the risks?Research
    Evaluate (15 min)"Which Australian reproductive strategy seems most effective for survival? Evaluate evidence."Evaluate

    Success Criteria Check: Researches and evaluates one Australian reproductive strategy with evidence-based conclusions.

    Lesson 3: Human Impact on Reproduction

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Elaborate

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Impact on Reproduction (15 min)"HUMAN IMPACTS: Pollution (oestrogen-mimicking chemicals affect fish and amphibian reproduction), climate change (temperature affects sex determination in turtles — warmer = more females), habitat loss (reduces breeding success), introduced predators (eat eggs and young)."Learn
    Conservation Breeding (15 min)"CONSERVATION BREEDING programs: zoos, wildlife sanctuaries. Captive breeding + reintroduction. Genetic management — maintain genetic diversity.案例: TAASMANIAN DEVIL insurance population — save from facial tumour disease."Learn
    Evaluate Conservation (15 min)"Evaluate: Is captive breeding the best strategy? Pros: protects genetically valuable individuals. Cons: captive animals may lose wild behaviours, expensive, some species don't breed well in captivity."Evaluate
    Write (5 min)"Human impacts on reproduction require conservation interventions to protect species."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Evaluates the effectiveness of conservation breeding programs as a reproductive strategy.
