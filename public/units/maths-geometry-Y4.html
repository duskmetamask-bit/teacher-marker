Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to identify, describe, classify and construct 3D objects including prisms and pyramids, and to understand line and rotational symmetry.

Success Criteria:

  • I can identify and name 3D objects: cubes, rectangular prisms, triangular prisms, cylinders, cones, pyramids.
  • I can describe the properties of 3D objects: faces, edges, vertices.
  • I can distinguish between prisms and pyramids based on their structure.
  • I can construct 3D objects using nets.
  • I can identify and create lines of symmetry and rotational symmetry.

    WEEK 1 — INTRODUCTION TO 3D OBJECTS

    Lesson 1: What Are 3D Objects? (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Hook (10 min)Show a collection of 3D objects: box, ball, pyramid toy, can, dice, cone. "These objects have THREE dimensions: LENGTH, WIDTH, and HEIGHT (or depth). They take up SPACE. 2D shapes are flat — they only have length and width. 3D objects are SOLID — they have volume."Understand 3D
    I Do (15 min)"3D objects have FACES (flat surfaces), EDGES (lines where two faces meet), and VERTICES (corners — where edges meet)." Show each on a cube. "A cube has 6 faces, 12 edges, 8 vertices."Learn components
    You Do (20 min)Sort objects into groups. What is the same about objects in each group? Name each group. Record objects.Sort
    Share (5 min)Which group has the most objects? What do those objects have in common?Share

    Success Criteria Check: Can explain the difference between 2D shapes and 3D objects.

    Lesson 2: Naming 3D Objects (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"3D objects have specific names. CUBE — 6 identical square faces. CUBOID (rectangular prism) — 6 rectangular faces. SPHERE — perfectly round, no faces. CYLINDER — 2 circular faces, 1 curved surface. CONE — 1 circular face, 1 curved surface coming to a point. PRISM — uniform cross-section. PYRAMID — triangular faces meeting at a point."Learn names
    We Do (20 min)"Match the object to its name." Show objects and names. Sort 20 objects/pictures into categories: cube, cuboid, cylinder, cone, sphere. Write the name under each.Match
    You Do (10 min)Write: Name 3 objects in the room that are: a cube, a cylinder, a sphere.Write

    Lesson 3: Everyday 3D Objects (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Investigation

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (40 min)3D object hunt: Walk around classroom and school (with permission). Find and record: 5 cubes, 5 cylinders, 3 spheres, 3 cones, 5 rectangular prisms. For each: Draw the object, write where you found it, write its name.Hunt
    Share (10 min)Which 3D object did you find most often? Why do you think that is?Discuss

    WEEK 2 — PROPERTIES OF 3D OBJECTS

    Lesson 1: Faces, Edges, Vertices (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (20 min)"Faces: flat or curved surfaces. Edges: lines where two faces meet. Vertices: points where edges meet (corners)." Use a cube and a cylinder as examples. "A cube has flat faces. A cylinder has flat circular faces AND a curved surface — but the curved surface is NOT a face. We call it a curved surface."Learn components
    You Do (25 min)Build a cube with playdough and matchsticks (or similar). Count: Faces = 6, Edges = 12, Vertices = 8. Then build a triangular prism. Count its faces, edges, vertices.Build and count
    Share (5 min)Compare: Which had more vertices — cube or triangular prism? Which had more edges?Compare

    Lesson 2: Counting and Recording (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (20 min)Build and count in groups: Rectangular prism (count faces, edges, vertices). Triangular prism. Square pyramid. Record in a table: Object / Faces / Edges / Vertices.Build and record
    You Do (25 min)Create a property chart: For each of these objects — cube, rectangular prism, triangular prism, square pyramid, triangular pyramid, cylinder, cone, sphere — write the number of faces, edges, vertices. Use objects or pictures to help.Fill chart
    Share (5 min)What patterns do you notice? (e.g., prisms have two identical ends, pyramids come to a point)Discover

    Lesson 3: Comparing Objects (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (45 min)"Compare two objects: Choose any two 3D objects from the list. Write: How are they the same? How are they different? Which has more faces? Which has more vertices? Which would roll?" Complete comparison sheet for 3 pairs.Compare
    Share (5 min)Share one interesting comparison you madeShare

    WEEK 3 — PRISMS

    Lesson 1: What Is a Prism? (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"A PRISM has: 1. Two identical ends (same shape and size). 2. The same cross-section ALL the way through. If you cut a prism in half at ANY point, you see the same shape." Show: A rectangular prism cut shows a rectangle. A triangular prism cut shows a triangle. "The name comes from the shape of the ends."Learn prism
    You Do (30 min)Sort shapes: Which are prisms? Sort 15 shapes into prisms and not prisms. For each prism: Write the shape name (rectangular prism, triangular prism) and what shape the ends are (rectangle, triangle).Sort prisms
    Share (5 min)How can you tell if something is a prism without cutting it?Explain

    Success Criteria Check: Can explain the defining property of a prism.

    Lesson 2: Rectangular Prisms (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"A RECTANGULAR PRISM: Ends are rectangles. Cross-section is always a rectangle. It has 6 faces (all rectangles), 12 edges, 8 vertices."Learn
    We Do (20 min)Find rectangular prisms in the room: books, tissue boxes, bricks, drawers. Draw 5 examples. Label each: "Rectangular prism — ends are rectangles."Find
    You Do (15 min)Build a rectangular prism using a net (template provided). Count faces, edges, vertices as you build.Build

    Lesson 3: Triangular Prisms (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"A TRIANGULAR PRISM: Ends are triangles. Cross-section is always a triangle. It has 5 faces (2 triangles + 3 rectangles), 9 edges, 6 vertices."Learn
    You Do (35 min)Build a triangular prism from a net. Then draw a diagram: Label the triangular faces, the rectangular faces, all edges and vertices. Count to verify.Build and label
    Share (5 min)Compare a rectangular prism and a triangular prism. What is the same? What is different?Compare

    WEEK 4 — PYRAMIDS

    Lesson 1: What Is a Pyramid? (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"A PYRAMID has: A BASE (one flat face, usually a polygon) and TRIANGULAR faces that all MEET AT ONE POINT (the apex/peak). The triangular faces slope up to meet at the top." Show Egyptian pyramid picture. "The base can be any shape — this gives different types of pyramids."Learn pyramid
    You Do (30 min)Sort shapes: Which are pyramids? Sort 15 shapes into pyramids and not pyramids. For each pyramid: Write what shape the base is (square pyramid, triangular pyramid).Sort
    Share (5 min)What is the difference between a prism and a pyramid?Explain

    Lesson 2: Square and Triangular Pyramids (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"SQUARE PYRAMID: Base is a square. 4 triangular faces meet at a point. 5 faces, 8 edges, 5 vertices. TRIANGULAR PYRAMID (TETRAHEDRON): Base is a triangle. 3 triangular faces meet at a point. 4 faces, 6 edges, 4 vertices."Learn
    We Do (20 min)Build a square pyramid and a triangular pyramid from nets. Count faces, edges, vertices for each.Build and count
    You Do (15 min)Draw both pyramids. Label: Apex, Base, Triangular faces (for square), Edges, Vertices.Label diagrams

    Lesson 3: Compare Prisms and Pyramids (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Practice

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (40 min)Venn diagram: Prisms vs Pyramids. In left circle: Triangular prism. In right circle: Triangular pyramid. In overlap: What both have. Below: What makes them different.Compare
    Share (10 min)Present your Venn diagram. Explain the key difference between a prism and a pyramid.Present

    WEEK 5 — CONSTRUCTION

    Lesson 1: Nets (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (20 min)"A NET is a 2D pattern that, when folded, becomes a 3D object. Different objects can have different nets. A cube has 11 different possible nets. A rectangular prism has many nets too." Show nets for: Cube, rectangular prism, triangular prism, square pyramid.Learn nets
    You Do (25 min)Cut out and fold a cube net. Identify: Which parts become the faces? Which folds become the edges? Which corners become the vertices?Fold
    Share (5 min)Could you fold a cube net the other way (inside out)? Would it still make a cube?Think

    Lesson 2: Building Models (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (40 min)Build from provided nets: Triangular prism, square pyramid. Check: Does it match the 3D object? Count faces, edges, vertices. Does the net correspond correctly?Build
    You Do (5 min)Write: "One thing I noticed when building was..."Reflect

    Lesson 3: Designing a 3D Object (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Design

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (45 min)Design challenge: Create a net for a rectangular prism (any size). Draw the net on grid paper. Cut out and fold to check it works. Revise if needed. Then build it from card.Design and build
    Share (5 min)Show your completed 3D object. Explain how you designed the net.Present

    WEEK 6 — SYMMETRY

    Lesson 1: Line Symmetry (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"A shape has LINE SYMMETRY when you can draw a line through it and the two halves match exactly — like a mirror line. The line of symmetry can be horizontal, vertical or diagonal." Show: Butterfly with vertical line of symmetry. House with horizontal. Show shapes: Square has 4 lines of symmetry. Rectangle has 2. Isosceles triangle has 1.Learn symmetry
    You Do (30 min)Find all lines of symmetry: Square (4), Rectangle (2), Equilateral triangle (3), Isosceles triangle (1), Regular pentagon (5), Circle (infinite — every line through centre). For each: Draw the shape, draw all lines of symmetry, count them.Find symmetry
    Share (5 min)Which shape had the most lines of symmetry? What is special about it?Discuss

    Lesson 2: Creating Symmetrical Shapes (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"To draw symmetrically: 1. Draw the line of symmetry. 2. Draw the shape on one side. 3. Copy/mirror the shape on the other side — point by point."Learn technique
    We Do (20 min)Draw: A symmetrical house (vertical line). A symmetrical butterfly (vertical line). A symmetrical letter (choose one). A symmetrical flag design. Each time: Draw line first, then shape on one side, then mirror to other side.Draw
    You Do (15 min)Design: Create a symmetrical pattern using 2D shapes. Use a mirror to check it is truly symmetrical. Colour it in.Design

    Lesson 3: Rotational Symmetry (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"A shape has ROTATIONAL SYMMETRY when it can be rotated (turned) and still looks the same — before completing a full 360° turn. The ORDER of rotational symmetry is how many times it matches itself in one full turn. A square: order 4 (matches every 90°). An equilateral triangle: order 3 (matches every 120°). A rectangle: order 2 (matches every 180°)."Learn
    You Do (30 min)Find order of rotational symmetry: Square (4), Rectangle (2), Parallelogram (2), Equilateral triangle (3), Regular hexagon (6), Kite (1 — only matches at 360°). For each: Turn to test, record order.Find order
    Share (5 min)What is the order of rotational symmetry of a circle? (Infinite — it matches at every angle)Discuss

    WEEK 7 — CLASSIFICATION AND PROBLEM SOLVING

    Lesson 1: Classifying 3D Objects (I Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (20 min)"We classify 3D objects by their properties: PRISM or PYRAMID? FLAT FACES or CURVED SURFACES? NUMBER of faces, edges, vertices. A classification key: Does it have curved surfaces? YES → cylinder or cone or sphere. NO → prism or pyramid. Is there a point where triangular faces meet? YES → pyramid. NO → prism. What shape is the base? This determines the specific name."Learn classification
    You Do (25 min)Create a classification key for: cube, rectangular prism, triangular prism, square pyramid, triangular pyramid, cylinder, cone, sphere. Use yes/no questions. Test your key on a partner's 3D object.Create key
    Share (5 min)Test your key — can your partner identify the object from your key? Did it work?Test

    Lesson 2: Angle Connections (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (35 min)Connect 3D objects to angles: "Where triangular faces meet, there are ANGLES at the edges and vertices. The angles of the base polygon determine the angles at the vertices." Explore: A square pyramid has angles at the apex. A triangular prism has angles at the vertices of the triangular ends. Draw cross-sections — what 2D shapes do you see? What angles are in them?Connect
    You Do (10 min)Write: "One way 3D objects connect to angles is..."Write

    Lesson 3: Real-World Application (You Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Application

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (45 min)Design challenge: "You are an architect. Design a building or structure using at least 4 different 3D objects. Draw a plan view (from above), a side view, and an isometric view. Label each 3D object used. Explain why you chose each shape for its purpose."Design
    Share (5 min)Present your design. Explain your choices.Present

    WEEK 8 — HOT TASK AND REFLECTION

    Lesson 1: Hot Task (Assessment)

    Time: 55 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Task (55 min)Part A: Name and classify 10 3D objects (prism/pyramid/other, specific name, count faces/edges/vertices). Part B: Identify lines of symmetry in 5 shapes (how many lines each). Part C: Match 6 nets to their 3D objects. Part D: Build a 3D object from a given net. Part E: Explain the difference between a prism and a pyramid in writing.Independent assessment

    Compare Cold Task → Hot Task. Note growth in classification, properties, and symmetry understanding.

    Lesson 2: Review (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Review

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (25 min)Review stations: Station 1 — Name 3D objects. Station 2 — Count properties. Station 3 — Symmetry challenge. Station 4 — Net to 3D. Rotate through all 4.Review stations
    Share (25 min)"What is one thing about 3D objects and symmetry that I will remember forever?" Write answer. Share with class.Reflect

    Lesson 3: Celebration (We Do)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Celebration

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Celebration (50 min)Create a 3D model city: In groups, design and build a city from 3D objects. Include at least 5 different 3D shapes. Present to class. Award certificates: Best Architecture, Best Teamwork, Most Creative Use of Shapes, Best Symmetry Design.Celebrate

    ADAPTATIONS

    EAL Students

  • Visual word wall: cube, prism, pyramid, face, edge, vertex, symmetry
  • 3D object manipulatives for hands-on sorting
  • Step-by-step net assembly instructions with pictures
  • Sentence starters: "A cube has ___ faces because..."
  • Home language labels for 3D object names

    • Gifted Students

  • Investigate Euler's formula: V - E + F = 2 (for polyhedra)
  • Explore why there are only 5 Platonic solids
  • Design the net for an unusual prism or pyramid
  • Investigate rotational symmetry order vs number of sides in regular polygons

    • Students with Additional Needs

  • Provide 3D object models for all classification tasks
  • Reduce to 4 main 3D objects: cube, rectangular prism, cylinder, sphere
  • Use pre-made nets with fold lines pre-scored
  • Provide counting sheets with faces, edges, vertices already labelled on image
  • Partner work for building tasks
  • Extra time for all tasks

    Formative Assessment (Ongoing)

  • Object hunts and classification recordings
  • Property chart completion
  • Net construction accuracy
  • Symmetry identification and creation
  • Teacher conferences (Weeks 2, 4, 6)

    • Summative Assessment

  • Hot Task (Week 8) — naming, properties, symmetry, nets, construction
  • 3D model city presentation
