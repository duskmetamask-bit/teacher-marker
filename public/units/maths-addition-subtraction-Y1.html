    • BIG UNDERSTANDINGS

    Students will understand that:

  • Addition and subtraction are inverse operations — they "undo" each other
  • The commutative property: adding in any order gives the same total (3+4 = 4+3)
  • Building to 10 is a powerful strategy for mental computation
  • The associative property: (2+3)+4 = 2+(3+4) — grouping doesn't change the sum
  • Different strategies work better for different problems
  • Estimation helps us check if our answer is reasonable

    • ESSENTIAL QUESTIONS

  • How do I add efficiently?
  • How do I subtract efficiently?
  • How do I know which strategy to use?
  • What happens when I add 0 to a number?
  • How do addition and subtraction relate to each other?

    • VOCABULARY — TIERED

    Tier 1 (everyday)

    add, subtract, plus, minus, altogether, total, how many left, count on, count back, more, fewer, same, bigger, smaller

    Tier 2 (academic)

    strategy, efficient, combine, separate, partition, estimate, check, count on from, near doubles, bridge to 10

    Tier 3 (mathematical)

    number sentence, equation, sum, difference, commutative property, associative property, additive inverse, mental computation, regrouping

    COLD TASK (Administered: Week 1, Lesson 1)

    Purpose: Establish baseline understanding before teaching begins. Students attempt the task independently, teachers do NOT prompt or scaffold. Collect and annotate.

    Cold Task / Hot Task (IDENTICAL — used twice)

    Name: _________________________ Date: _____________ Year: _______

    Part A — Counting

  • Count the dots. How many?

    • (Array of 14 dots)

    ____________

  • Count by 2s from 0. Write the next 5 numbers:

    • 0, 2, 4, ___, ___, ___, ___, ___

    Part B — Addition

  • There are 7 apples. I add 4 more. How many altogether?

    • Draw jumps on the number line. Write the number sentence.

    __ + __ = ___

  • 6 + 4 = ___ 8 + 2 = ___ 5 + 5 = ___

    • Part C — Subtraction

  • There are 12 stars. I take away 5. How many are left?

    • Draw your thinking. Write the number sentence.

    __ - __ = ___

  • 9 - 3 = ___ 10 - 5 = ___ 14 - 7 = ___

    • Part D — Doubles

  • Circle the doubles: 3 + 3 4 + 5 6 + 6 2 + 3
  • 4 + 4 = ___ 6 + 6 = ___ 7 + 7 = ___

    • Part E — Problem-Solving

  • Sam has 8 marbles. She gives 3 to Tom. How many does Sam have left?

    • Draw and write the number sentence.

  • There are 5 red butterflies and 4 yellow butterflies. How many butterflies altogether?

    • Part F — Number Sense

  • Write the number that makes this true: 8 + ___ = 10
  • What is 3 more than 17? ___ What is 5 less than 17? ___

    • Cold Task is NOT marked as right/wrong in front of students. Return unmarked. Use teacher annotations only.

    HOT TASK (Administered: Week 8, Lesson 24)

    Identical to Cold Task — same 12 questions, same format.

    Students receive back their Cold Task alongside their Hot Task at the end of Week 8. They annotate: "Before I thought… Now I know…"

    Aligned to: AC9M1N01, AC9M1N02, AC9M1N03, AC9M1N04, AC9M1N05

    Criterion 1: Counting and Number Sense (to 50)

    GradeDescriptor
    **A**Confidently counts to 50 using efficient strategies; orders and compares numbers to 50; accurately counts by 2s, 5s, and 10s; uses patterns to extend number sequences
    **B**Counts reliably to 50; can order numbers to 50 with minor errors; counts by 2s and 5s with some support
    **C**Counts to at least 30 reliably; begins to count beyond 30 with scaffolding; counts by 2s and 5s with visual support
    **D**Counts to 20 with support; inconsistent with counting strategies beyond 10; needs visual support for skip-counting
    **E**Counts to 10 with significant support; does not yet demonstrate reliable counting strategies

    Criterion 2: Addition — Strategies and Fluency (within 20)

    GradeDescriptor
    **A**Uses efficient mental strategies (count-on, doubles, bridge to 10) fluently; explains why certain strategies work better for different problems; applies commutative and associative properties; solves addition within 20 independently
    **B**Uses count-on and near-doubles reliably within 20; beginning to use bridge to 10 with prompting; solves most addition problems mentally
    **C**Uses counting-on and doubles within 20 with some scaffolding; begins to choose appropriate strategies; still relies on counting all for some problems
    **D**Uses counting-all strategy within 10; attempts count-on with visual support; needs concrete materials for addition within 10

    Criterion 3: Subtraction — Strategies and Fluency (within 20)

    GradeDescriptor

    **B**Uses count-back and think-addition reliably within 20; beginning to connect addition and subtraction facts; solves most subtraction problems mentally
    **C**Uses count-back with visual number line support; beginning to use think-addition with prompting; still needs concrete materials for some subtraction problems
    **D**Subtracts within 10 using physical taking-away; does not yet use count-back reliably; needs concrete materials
    **E**Unable to subtract; does not yet demonstrate understanding of subtraction as "take away"

    Criterion 4: Additive Partitioning and Flexible Strategies

    GradeDescriptor

    **B**Partitions numbers in at least two ways; uses build-to-10 strategy with minor prompting; begins to apply flexible thinking
    **C**Partitions small numbers (within 10) with teacher guidance; begins to use bridging-to-10 strategy; limited flexibility
    **D**Partitions numbers 1-5 with physical objects; unable to apply partitioning to computation

    • DIFFERENTIATION FRAMEWORK

    EAL/D Students

  • Pre-teach mathematical vocabulary with visual dictionary cards before each week
  • Sentence frames: "I know 8 + 2 = 10, so 8 + 3 = ___"
  • Bilingual anchor charts (addition/subtraction strategies in home language)
  • Pair with a supportive English-speaking partner during We Do activities
  • Accept oral explanation and drawing in place of written equations where appropriate
  • Allow additional processing time (minimum 20 seconds wait time after questions)
  • Reduce written output — focus on strategy demonstration with manipulatives
  • Use hand gestures for strategies: "jump forward" for count-on, "jump back" for count-back

    • Students Identified as Gifted/Advanced

  • Explore addition with three addends (e.g. 4 + 5 + 3 = ___)
  • Investigate the commutative property deeply: if 3+4=7, what else do you know without calculating?
  • Challenge: add and subtract within 50 using mental strategies
  • Problem-posing: students create their own Very Hungry Caterpillar style problems
  • Introduce algebraic thinking: if 7 + ___ = 12, what is the missing number? How do you know?
  • Investigate patterns: what happens when you add 0? What happens when you subtract 0?

    • Students Not Yet at Expected Level (NEP)

  • Consolidate bonds to 10 before introducing bridging-to-10 strategy
  • Use physical manipulatives (counters, number lines) for every computation activity
  • Focus on addition/subtraction within 10 until fluent, then extend to 15, then to 20
  • Pre-lesson micro-session (5 minutes, pull-aside): counting practice, number recognition
  • Peer support pairing with a student who has mastered the concept
  • Modified success criteria with teacher: focus on 3-4 key criteria rather than all 8
  • Use number lines with explicit jump markings for count-on and count-back

    LESSON 1.1 — Cold Task and Launch

    Duration: 50 minutes

    Type: Engage / Assessment Launch

    Resources: Cold Task copies, The Very Hungry Caterpillar, fruit counters, coloured pencils

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning to show what I already know about addition and subtraction (Cold Task)
  • I am learning that this unit is about strategies for adding and subtracting

    • Success Criteria

  • I can attempt all sections of the Cold Task independently
  • I can listen to The Very Hungry Caterpillar and talk about the maths

    • Lesson Sequence

    ENGAGE — Hook (10 min)

    Teacher Action:

    "This is the story of a very hungry caterpillar. Every day he eats a little bit more. Let's see what happens!"
  • Read The Very Hungry Caterpillar aloud. Do not stop to teach — let the story land.
  • After reading: "Turn and talk — what numbers did you notice? How much did the caterpillar eat each day?"
  • Take 3–4 shares. Note: who knows jump strategy, who counts all.
    • "By Week 8, you'll be able to solve problems like the caterpillar's eating sequence — efficiently!"

    COLD TASK (30 min)

    Teacher Action:

    "Now I'm going to ask you to show me what you already know. This is NOT a test — I just want to see your thinking."
  • Distribute Cold Task. Students work independently and silently.
  • Teacher circulates, observing. Note: which strategy each student uses (count all vs. count on).
  • Collect all Cold Tasks.

    • Student Activity:

  • Complete Cold Task independently (all 12 questions)

    • DEBRIEF (10 min)

    Teacher Action:

    "How did that feel? Some of you counted everything from one. Some of you counted on. Some of you knew your doubles. That's what we'll sharpen this unit."

    Homework / Reflection: None. Cold Task is teacher-only.

    Teacher Notes

  • After lesson: sort Cold Tasks into 4 piles — emerging, approaching, at level, extending
  • Note any student who uses jump/partition strategy → flag for gifted extension
  • Note any student who counts all from 1 → plan for targeted support

    • LESSON 1.2 — Addition as Combining: Jump Strategy Intro

    Duration: 50 minutes

    Type: Concept Introduction (I Do → We Do)

    Resources: Number lines (floor + student), fruit counters, The Very Hungry Caterpillar projected images, whiteboard

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning to use the jump strategy for addition (jump from the larger number)
  • I am learning that starting from the larger number makes adding faster

    • Success Criteria

  • I can use the jump strategy to add two numbers
  • I can explain why we start from the larger number

    • I DO (15 min)

    "Let's look at the caterpillar. On Monday he ate through 1 apple. On Tuesday he ate through 2 more apples. How many did he eat altogether?"
    "One way: 1, 2, 3. That's counting ALL from one."
    "Better way: START from the BIGGER number and JUMP."

    Draw number line on board.

    "Start at 1 (the first group). JUMP forward 2 (the second group): 2, 3."

    Write:

    ```

    1 + 2 = 3

    ```

    "The bigger number here is 2. Start at 2, jump 1 more: 3."
    "But wait — 1 + 2 and 2 + 1 give the SAME answer. That's the COMMUTATIVE PROPERTY — order doesn't matter for addition!"

    Demonstrate with counters and number line: 4+3 and 3+4 both = 7.

    WE DO (20 min)

    Jump strategy practice — number lines:

    "Let's solve 5 + 3 using the jump strategy."
  • Start at 5. JUMP forward 3: 6, 7, 8. Answer: 8.

    • Partner activity:

  • Partner A gives an addition problem
  • Partner B solves on number line, says answer
  • Check with counters. Swap roles.

    • Practice set: 6+3, 4+5, 7+2, 3+6, 8+1

    After each: "Did you start from the bigger or smaller number? Why?"

    YOU DO (15 min)

  • Use the number line to solve: a) 7 + 3 = ___ b) 4 + 6 = ___ c) 8 + 2 = ___
  • Draw jumps to show: 5 + 4 = 9 on the number line.
  • "Circle the bigger number first, then solve: 3 + 8 = ___"
  • EXTENSION: "Is 6 + 4 the same as 4 + 6? Prove it with your number line."

    • Differentiation

  • EAL/D: Jump strategy checklist on desk; "big number first" written as visual reminder
  • NEP: Work within 10; always use number line with jumps drawn
  • Gifted: Extension + "What happens if the numbers are equal — like 5 + 5?"

    • LESSON 1.3 — Counting On: Making It Efficient

    Duration: 50 minutes

    Type: Guided Practice

    Resources: Number lines, individual whiteboards, counters

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning to count on from the larger number without drawing every jump
  • I am learning to do addition mentally

    • Success Criteria

  • I can count on from the larger number using my fingers or in my head
  • I can solve addition problems within 20 using mental computation

    • I DO (15 min)

    "Today I want us to get FASTER. Instead of drawing every jump, let's do it in our heads."
    "7 + 5. The bigger number is 7. I HOLD 7 in my head. Then I count ON 5 using my fingers: 8, 9, 10, 11, 12."
    "7 + 5 = 12. Faster!"

    Model 8 + 4, 9 + 3, 6 + 7 — all using mental count-on.

    "When the second number is small (1, 2, 3, 4), count-on is the BEST strategy."
    "When the second number is close to 10 (8, 9), we might use a different strategy — which we'll learn next week."

    WE DO (20 min)

    Mental maths rounds:

    Teacher calls out problems. Students solve mentally and write the answer on whiteboards.

  • Round 1: count-on with second number 1-3: 8+1, 7+2, 9+3, 6+2, 5+3
  • Round 2: count-on with second number 4-5: 6+4, 7+5, 8+3, 9+4

    • After each round: "Who can explain their thinking?"

    YOU DO (15 min)

  • Solve mentally: a) 8 + 3 = ___ b) 7 + 4 = ___ c) 9 + 2 = ___
  • "For 6 + 5, should you hold 6 or 5 in your head? Why?"
  • "I hold 9 in my head and count on 4: 10, 11, 12, 13. What is the answer?"
  • EXTENSION: "What is the largest sum you can get when adding two numbers under 10? What is the smallest sum? Can both addends be the same?"

    • Differentiation

  • EAL/D: Finger-counting method explicitly taught; small number shown on fingers while holding big number in head
  • NEP: Second number only up to 3; mental maths with physical support available
  • Gifted: Extension + "What if you counted back from the larger number? Does 8+4 work that way?"

    LESSON 2.1 — Subtraction as Take Away: Count Back

    Duration: 50 minutes

    Type: Explore

    Resources: Number lines, counters, individual whiteboards

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning to subtract by counting back on a number line
  • I am learning that subtraction "undoes" addition

    • Success Criteria

  • I can count back to solve subtraction problems
  • I can explain the relationship between addition and subtraction

    • ENGAGE — Mentor Text Hook (5 min)

    Project the Friday feast page (10 items).

    "The caterpillar ate through 10 pieces of food. That's ADDITION — adding more and more. But what if the caterpillar had to GIVE BACK some food? That's SUBTRACTION."

    I DO (15 min)

    "The caterpillar ate 10 strawberries. He DIGESTS 3. How many are left?"

    Draw number line.

    "I start at 10. COUNT BACK 3: 9, 8, 7."

    Write: 10 - 3 = 7

    "10 take away 3 equals 7."
    "But here's the cool thing: 7 + 3 = 10. So 10 - 3 = 7 because 7 + 3 = 10."
    "ADDITION and SUBTRACTION are INVERSE OPERATIONS. They UNDO each other!"

    Draw the inverse on the board with a linking arrow.

    WE DO (20 min)

    Number line subtraction practice:

    Solve using count-back on number line:

  • 9 - 3
  • 10 - 4
  • 8 - 2
  • 7 - 5
  • 12 - 3

    • After each: "What is the related addition fact?"

    Build fact families:

  • 3 + 7 = 10, 7 + 3 = 10, 10 - 3 = 7, 10 - 7 = 3

    • YOU DO (15 min)

  • Use count-back to solve: a) 8 - 3 = ___ b) 10 - 5 = ___ c) 9 - 4 = ___
  • "Write the fact family for 4, 6, 10."
  • "10 - 3 = 7. What addition fact is related? How do you know?"
  • EXTENSION: "If 8 + 5 = 13, what is 13 - 5? How do you know?"

    • Differentiation

  • EAL/D: Fact family cards on desk; number line with back-jumps visually highlighted
  • NEP: Work within 10; count-back 1, 2, 3 only; fact families for bonds to 10
  • Gifted: Extension + "Can you subtract from numbers up to 20 using count-back?"

    • LESSON 2.2 — Subtraction as Difference

    Duration: 50 minutes

    Type: Explore

    Resources: Counters, number lines, comparison mat

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning that subtraction can mean "difference" — how far apart two numbers are
  • I am learning to use counting to find the difference

    • Success Criteria

  • I can find the difference between two numbers by counting on or back
  • I can explain "difference" in my own words

    • I DO (15 min)

    "Here's another way to think about subtraction. The caterpillar ate 10 pieces of food on Friday. The butterfly that came out was 1 butterfly. What's the DIFFERENCE?"
    "Difference means: how far apart are they? How many MORE?"
    "10 pieces of food, 1 butterfly. The difference is 9. 10 - 1 = 9."
    "Difference is how much BIGGER one number is than another."
    "The caterpillar was very SMALL. The food was 10 pieces. 10 - 1 = 9. That's a big difference!"
    "Compare this to: There are 7 red apples and 4 green apples. What's the difference?"

    Use matching: match 4 red to 4 green. 3 red are unmatched → difference is 3.

    10 - 7 = 3.

    "To find difference, you can COUNT ON from the smaller number to the bigger number: 4... 5, 6, 7. That's 3 more. So the difference is 3."

    WE DO (20 min)

    Difference practice:

  • 8 and 5 — what's the difference? (count on from 5 to 8 = 3)
  • 9 and 4 — what's the difference?
  • 7 and 7 — what's the difference? (they're equal!)
  • 10 and 3 — what's the difference?

    • Partner activity: pick two numbers, find the difference, explain strategy.

    YOU DO (15 min)

  • Find the difference: a) 9 and 6 b) 8 and 3 c) 7 and 7
  • "There are 8 butterflies and 5 caterpillars. What's the difference?"
  • "Is finding 'difference' the same as take-away subtraction? Explain."
  • EXTENSION: "If the difference between two numbers is 4, what could the two numbers be? Find 3 pairs."

    • Differentiation

  • EAL/D: Difference vocabulary visual; matching method explicitly modelled
  • NEP: Work with numbers to 10; difference of 1-3 only
  • Gifted: Extension + "Can you have a negative difference? When would that make sense?"

    • LESSON 2.3 — Same Story, Two Operations

    Duration: 50 minutes

    Type: Guided Practice

    Resources: Story problem cards, counters, number lines

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning that the same number facts can be used in addition AND subtraction
  • I am learning to write fact families

    • Success Criteria

  • I can write all four facts in a fact family
  • I can explain how addition and subtraction are connected

    • I DO (12 min)

    "Let's look at this fact: 4 + 5 = 9."

    Write the fact family:

    ```

    4 + 5 = 9

    5 + 4 = 9

    9 - 4 = 5

    9 - 5 = 4

    ```

    "The same three numbers — 4, 5, 9 — give us TWO addition facts and TWO subtraction facts."
    "This is why knowing your addition facts helps you with subtraction. If you know 4 + 5 = 9, you also know 9 - 5 = 4!"

    WE DO (20 min)

    Fact family game — Four-in-a-Row:

    Partner game. Grid with addition/subtraction facts. Partner A covers 4+5=9 and 9-4=5; Partner B covers 5+4=9 and 9-5=4. First to get 4 in a row wins.

    Fact family construction:

    Given the sum, write all four facts:

  • Sum = 10 (e.g. 6+4, 4+6, 10-6, 10-4)
  • Sum = 12
  • Sum = 15

    • YOU DO (15 min)

  • Write the fact family for: 7, 8, 15
  • Write the fact family for: 6, 6, 12
  • "If 8 + 3 = 11, what else do you know immediately?"
  • EXTENSION: "Can you write a fact family for 4, 4, 8? Is it different from 3, 5, 8?"

    • Differentiation

  • EAL/D: Fact family template on desk; numbers provided with visual support
  • NEP: Fact families for bonds to 10 only (4+6, 3+7, 2+8, 5+5)
  • Gifted: Extension + "Write a fact family for 0. What do you notice?"

    LESSON 3.1 — Partitioning Numbers

    Duration: 50 minutes

    Type: Explore

    Resources: Counters, part-part-whole mats, number boards

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning to partition numbers in flexible ways
  • I am learning that the same number can be split in many ways

    • Success Criteria

  • I can partition a number into two or more parts in multiple ways
  • I can use partitioning to help me add

    • I DO (15 min)

    "Here's a powerful idea: we can BREAK APART numbers."
    "8 can be broken into 5 and 3. Or 6 and 2. Or 7 and 1. Or 4 and 4. Or 8 and 0."

    Write all partitions of 8 on the board.

    "Why does this help us add? Because 6 + 8 is hard. But 6 + 4 = 10, and 8 = 4 + 4. So 6 + 8 = 6 + 4 + 4 = 10 + 4 = 14."
    "That's the BEGINNING of the BRIDGE TO 10 strategy. You make one number reach 10 first, then add the rest."

    WE DO (20 min)

    Partition challenge:

    Use counters. Find all the ways to partition:

  • 8 into two parts
  • 9 into two parts
  • 10 into two parts

    • Record in maths books.

    Partner game: "I'm thinking of a number. It can be partitioned into 3 and 5. What could the number be?" (8)

    YOU DO (15 min)

  • Partition 7 in three different ways.
  • Partition 9 in three different ways.
  • "Use partitioning to solve: 6 + 7 = ___. Hint: make the 6 up to 10."
  • EXTENSION: "Partition 12 into two parts in 5 different ways."

    • Differentiation

  • EAL/D: Partition chart on desk; concrete counters for all partitioning

  • "A tree had 7 apples. It grew 5 more. How many apples?" (JOIN → 7+5)

    • YOU DO (15 min)

  • Solve problem 3 above. Show READ-DRAW-WRITE-CHECK.

    LESSON 8.1 — Addition and Subtraction Gallery

    Duration: 50 minutes

    Type: Review / Celebration

    Resources: Student work samples, chart paper, coloured pens

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning to celebrate my addition and subtraction knowledge
  • I am making connections between all the strategies I've learned

    • GALLERY WALK (25 min)

    Students' best work samples displayed around the room.

    Students walk the gallery:

  • GREEN dot: "Something I did well"
  • ORANGE dot: "Something I want to learn more about"

    • CONSOLIDATION GAMES (20 min)

    Students rotate through 3 mini-games:

  • Bridge to 10 BINGO
  • Fact Family Match-Up
  • Strategy Challenge Cards

    • REFLECTION (5 min)

    "Without looking at your books, tell your partner three things you know now that you didn't know in Week 1."

    LESSON 8.2 — Connecting It All

    Duration: 50 minutes

    Type: Synthesis

    Resources: Concept map template, whiteboards

    Learning Intentions

  • I am learning to see how all the addition and subtraction ideas connect
  • I am learning to explain the big ideas of the unit in my own words

    • WHOLE CLASS CONCEPT MAP (25 min)

    Build class concept map with "Addition and Subtraction" in the centre.

    Key connections:

  • Jump strategy ↔ Count on strategy
  • Bridge to 10 ↔ Part-part-whole
  • Doubles ↔ Near doubles
  • Inverse operations ↔ Fact families
  • Join problems ↔ Separate problems
  • Compare problems ↔ Difference

    • PERSONAL CONCEPT MAP (20 min)

    Students create their own concept map with at least 6 key terms and at least 5 connections.

    LESSON 8.3 — Hot Task + Cold → Hot Comparison + Celebration

    Duration: 55 minutes

    Type: Summative Assessment + Reflection

    Resources: Hot Task copies, Cold Task copies (returned), coloured pens, sticky notes

    HOT TASK (30 min)

    Distribute Hot Task (identical to Cold Task from Week 1).

    Students work independently and silently.

    Collect all Hot Tasks.

    COLD → HOT COMPARISON (15 min)

    Return Cold Tasks alongside completed Hot Tasks.

    Students compare, side by side:

  • CIRCLE in green: answers correct in both
  • CIRCLE in blue: wrong in Week 1, correct now
  • CIRCLE in orange: still tricky

    • Write on sticky notes:

  • "Before Week 1, I thought..."
  • "Now I know..."
  • "I'm most proud of..."

    • CELEBRATION (10 min)

    Whole class share. Display both tasks: "Look How Far We've Come"

    Read The Very Hungry Caterpillar one final time — students solve the eating sequence mathematically.

    Teacher Assessment Note

    Collect all Hot Tasks. Mark against the A–E rubric (6 criteria). Compare to Cold Task notes. Document growth for each student. Use to inform planning for Year 2 mathematics.
