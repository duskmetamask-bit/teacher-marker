Unit Content

Active Investigations — Geography Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 3

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum HASS v9 — Geography (Years 3–4)

Unit Big Idea: The world is divided into places with different climates, resources, and populations. How people use the environment varies across places. Understanding these differences and connections helps us make sense of the global community.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Investigate two places, describe their features, and identify how people use the environment in each place

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Investigate how and why people use environments differently in two places, and evaluate environmental sustainability

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to investigate how and why people in different places use environments differently, and to evaluate the sustainability of these uses.

Success Criteria:

  • I can describe the climate, natural features, and resources of at least 2 places.
  • I can explain how and why people in different places use environments differently (farming, mining, fishing, tourism, city living).
  • I can describe the connections between places (trade, migration, communication, culture).
  • I can evaluate the sustainability of environmental use in at least 1 place.
  • I can use sources (maps, data, photos, statistics) to investigate places.
  • I can process and present geographic information clearly.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: Our World of Places

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Investigating Places

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Show two satellite images: Amazon rainforest vs Australian outback. "What do these places have in common? How are they different? What do you think living here would be like?"Observe and compare
    Introduction (5 min)"In this unit, you will become GEOGRAPHIC INVESTIGATORS. You will learn to investigate places like a geographer — asking questions, gathering evidence, and drawing conclusions about how people use environments."Listen
    Cold Task (30 min)"Choose two places on Earth (from images provided or your knowledge). For each place: (1) Describe the climate and 2 natural features. (2) Describe how people use the environment. (3) Write 2 sentences: How are the two places different in how people use them?" Year 3 expectation: 5–8 sentences, detailed labels.Independent

    Differentiation: Image word bank. Sentence starters: "People in this place use the environment by..." / "Both places are different because..." / "One place uses land for..."

    Success Criteria Check: Student produces a drawing/writing showing 2 places and at least 2 differences in how people use the environment.

    Lesson 2: World Map Deep Dive

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Hook)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Become Cartographers (10 min)"A map is a geographer's essential tool. Maps show us where places are, what surrounds them, and how they connect. Today we are going to learn to read maps critically."Listen
    Map Elements (10 min)Teacher displays a world map. Key elements: compass rose, scale, legend, latitude/longitude lines, equator, Tropics of Cancer and Capricorn, prime meridian. Model how to use each.Learn map elements
    Latitudes and Climates (10 min)"The further from the equator (latitude 0°), the colder the climate generally. Near the equator = tropical/hot. Mid-latitudes = temperate. Near the poles = cold/Arctic. This is why Brazil is hot and Canada is cold."Understand latitude-climate link
    Label the World (10 min)Students label a world map: equator, Tropics of Cancer and Capricorn, prime meridian, 5 major oceans, Australia, Antarctica, South America, Europe, Africa, Asia, North America.Label map

    Success Criteria Check: Can correctly locate the equator, both tropics, and at least 3 continents on a world map.

    Lesson 3: Climate Zones of the World

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Concept Introduction)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Climate Zones (10 min)"The world is divided into climate zones. Each zone has distinct weather patterns and affects what grows there and how people live. Understanding climate zones helps us understand why places are different."Listen
    The 5 Main Climate Zones (15 min)Show map with zones clearly marked: Tropical (near equator, hot and wet, e.g., Amazon, Congo, Indonesia) / Arid (hot and dry, e.g., Sahara, Arabian Peninsula, Australian interior) / Temperate (mild, four seasons, e.g., Sydney, Melbourne, London) / Mediterranean (hot dry summer, mild wet winter, e.g., Perth, Adelaide, Cape Town, Mediterranean Europe) / Cold/Polar (very cold, ice/snow, e.g., Antarctica, Alaska, Northern Canada, Siberia).Observe
    Climate Zone Sort (10 min)City/place cards: Singapore (tropical), Cairo (arid), London (temperate), Perth (Mediterranean), Antarctica (polar). Students sort into correct climate zones and justify why.Sort and justify
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: Which climate zone do we live in? How does our climate zone affect how we live?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students use a colour-coded climate map. Gifted students: research what climate zone Tokyo or another major city is in and justify.

    Success Criteria Check: Can correctly identify at least 3 climate zones and name an example of each.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Environment and People

    Lesson 1: Natural Environments

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What is a Natural Environment? (10 min)"A natural environment is all the elements that exist naturally in a place — landforms, water, climate, soil, plants, animals. No person created these. They were here before humans."Listen
    Types of Natural Environments (15 min)Show environments: Desert (hot arid, cacti, lizards, sparse human population) / Rainforest (hot wet, tall trees, huge biodiversity, indigenous peoples) / Arctic/Tundra (cold, permafrost, mosses, Inuit peoples) / Grassland/Savanna (mixed grass and trees, large animals, pastoralism) / Ocean/Coastal (marine ecosystems, fishing communities).Observe
    Describe the Environment (10 min)"When geographers describe environments, they look at: LAND (flat, hilly, mountainous), WATER (rivers, ocean, rainfall), CLIMATE (hot, cold, wet, dry), VEGETATION (forests, grasslands, none), ANIMALS (what lives there). Let's apply this framework."Learn framework
    Apply to Two Environments (5 min)Students choose 2 environments and write 3–5 words describing each using the framework.Apply

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 natural environments using geographic vocabulary (land, water, climate, vegetation).

    Lesson 2: How Do People Use Environments?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Environment Use Types (15 min)"People use environments for: FARMING (agriculture, livestock) / MINING (extracting resources) / FISHING (harvesting from oceans/rivers) / TOURISM (visiting natural beauty) / CITY LIVING (urban development) / CONSERVATION (protecting nature). How people use a place depends on its environment."Listen
    Environment Use Case Studies (15 min)Show: Australian wheat belt (farming on temperate plains) / Pilbara mining region (extracting iron ore in arid WA) / Great Barrier Reef (tourism and fishing, tropical marine) / Amazon rainforest (logging, farming, indigenous peoples) / Norwegian coast (fishing, tourism). For each: What environment? What use? Why there?Observe and note
    Use-Map Connection (10 min)"Farming needs flat land and water → wheat belt / Mining needs resources underground → Pilbara / Fishing needs ocean → coast / Tourism needs beauty and accessibility → reef. USE FOLLOWS ENVIRONMENT."Understand connection
    Write (5 min)"Choose one use type (farming, mining, fishing, tourism). Write 2 sentences: What environment does it need? Where in the world is this use found?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can explain at least 2 connections between environment type and human use.

    Lesson 3: Resources and Economies

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Are Resources? (10 min)"Resources are things in the environment that people use. Renewable resources can regrow (trees, solar, wind, fish stocks). Non-renewable resources cannot regrow (iron ore, oil, coal, gold). Sustainable use means not using resources faster than they can be replenished."Listen
    Resource Map of Australia (15 min)Show resource map: Iron ore (Pilbara, WA) / Coal (Queensland) / Gold (Kalgoorlie, WA; Bendigo, VIC) / Natural gas (North West Shelf, WA) / Wheat (WA Wheat Belt, NSW) / Wool (WA, NSW, VIC) / Lamb/beef (nationwide). Describe each region's economy.Observe
    WA Resources (10 min)"Western Australia is one of the world's richest resource regions. We produce: iron ore (biggest export), gold, nickel, lithium (for batteries), wheat, wool, LNG. Mining is the biggest part of our economy."Learn local
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Write 1 sentence: What is one non-renewable resource from WA? What is one renewable resource?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students use a resource picture sort. Gifted students: create an infographic of WA's top 5 exports.

    Success Criteria Check: Can distinguish between renewable and non-renewable resources with at least 1 example of each.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: Connections Between Places

    Lesson 1: Trade Connects Places

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What is Trade? (10 min)"Trade is how places share resources. Places that have lots of something trade with places that need it. Wheat from WA farms goes to Indonesia, the Philippines, and China. In return, we get goods those countries produce."Listen
    Trade Routes (15 min)Show world map with trade routes: Australian iron ore → China and Japan / Brazilian soybeans → China / Saudi oil → worldwide / Chilean copper → global electronics. "Most global trade moves by SHIP because it is the cheapest way to move large quantities. Containers changed the world."Observe
    Australia's Trade (10 min)"Australia's top exports: iron ore, coal, LNG, wheat, beef, wool, gold. Top trade partners: China, Japan, South Korea, USA, ASEAN countries. WA is the biggest exporting state."Learn local
    Trade Flow Diagram (5 min)Students draw a simple flow diagram: WA Iron Ore → Port → Ship → China → Steel → Exports to world.Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Can trace at least 1 trade connection between Australia and another country.

    Lesson 2: Migration and Culture

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Migration Changes Places (10 min)"Migration means moving to live in a new place. Migration changes places — people bring their language, food, religion, celebrations, and ideas. Australia's multicultural identity was built by migration."Listen
    Waves of Migration (15 min)Timeline: 1788 — British colonists / 1850s — Gold rush (Chinese, European) / 1900s — Post-WWII (Italian, Greek, Dutch, German) / 1970s onwards — Vietnam, Lebanon, India, China, Africa, Middle East, and more. "Every wave changed Australian culture — food, festivals, language, sport."Note waves
    Cultural Connections (10 min)"Look at our classroom or school community. Who here has family from another country? You represent Australia's migration story. Your heritage is a geographic connection between places."Reflect
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: How has migration changed Australian places? Name one cultural influence from migration."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 ways migration has changed Australian places.

    Lesson 3: Communication Networks

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    The World Is Connected (10 min)"Today, places are more connected than ever before. We can video-call someone on the other side of the world instantly. News travels in seconds. Trade happens in real time. Communication networks connect all places."Listen
    Communication Infrastructure (15 min)Show: submarine internet cables under oceans (how 95% of global data travels) / satellite networks / mobile phone towers / postal networks. "These invisible networks connect every place on Earth. But not every place has equal access — this is the digital divide."Observe
    Case Study: Remote WA Communication (10 min)"In remote parts of WA — the Kimberley, Pilbara, outback — communication is harder. Some communities rely on satellite internet. Towns like Newman, Port Hedland, Broome have better infrastructure. Mining companies build their own networks."Learn local
    Draw the Network (5 min)"Draw a simple diagram showing how a message sent from Perth travels to London — what does it pass through?"Draw

    Differentiation: EAL students draw communication methods without labels. Gifted students: research one remote community's communication solution.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 ways communication networks connect places and identify that access varies by location.

    WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Place Studies

    Lesson 1: Investigating Our Local Place

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Investigating Local Place (10 min)"Now we apply our geographic skills to investigate a place. We will use the GEGRAPHIC INQUIRY METHOD: Where is it? What is it like? How do people use it? How is it connected to other places? What are the issues?"Learn method
    Our Local Area Investigation (15 min)Teacher leads investigation of local area using local data, photos, maps. Use Google Maps or local council resources. Key questions: What is the terrain? What is built there? What industries exist? What is the population? How is it changing?Investigate
    Data Collection (10 min)Students record data: Land use map of local area, main industries, proximity to coast/river, population trend (growing/staying/shrinking), key features.Collect data
    Write a Place Description (5 min)"In 3–5 sentences, describe our local area using the inquiry questions as a guide."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the local area using geographic inquiry questions.

    Lesson 2: Investigating a Remote Place — The Kimberley

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Introducing the Kimberley (10 min)"The Kimberley is one of the world's last great wilderness areas. It is in the far north of WA — remote, vast, and spectacular. Let's investigate it using our geographic inquiry method."Prepare
    Geographic Inquiry: Kimberley (15 min)WHERE: Far north WA, close to Indonesia and Timor-Leste / WHAT: Vast savanna, ancient sandstone gorges (Bungle Bungle), coastline with massive tides, rivers, waterfalls / CLIMATE: Tropical, wet summers, dry winters / PEOPLE: Small population, many Indigenous communities, pastoralism, mining, tourism / ISSUES: Remote area challenges, Indigenous land rights, environmental protection.Investigate
    Connections to Our Place (10 min)"The Kimberley is connected to Perth (our capital city) but very different. It is closer to Indonesia than to Perth. Its main industries (pastoralism, mining, tourism) are different from ours. It has one of the highest proportions of Indigenous landowners in Australia."Compare
    Write (5 min)"Write 3 sentences using the inquiry questions to describe the Kimberley."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe the Kimberley using the geographic inquiry method with at least 3 of the 5 inquiry questions.

    Lesson 3: Comparing Two Places — Perth vs the Kimberley

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Perth vs Kimberley Comparison (15 min)Create comparison chart:PerthKimberley / Location: SW WA, coastalN WA, remote / Population: ~2.2 million, large~40,000, tiny / Industries: services, mining, educationpastoralism, mining, tourism / Indigenous: Noongar landmany Indigenous communities / Challenges: housing, waterdistance, services, healthCompare
    Venn Diagram (10 min)Students complete a Venn diagram comparing Perth and the Kimberley. Same: both in WA, both have mining, both have Indigenous connections. Different: population, location, economy, climate.Complete Venn
    Write Comparison (10 min)"Write 4–5 sentences comparing Perth and the Kimberley. Include one similarity and one difference."Write

    Differentiation: EAL students use picture-based comparison. Gifted students: also compare to another remote place (e.g., Iceland or rural Canada).

    Success Criteria Check: Completes a Venn diagram with at least 3 items in each section and writes a 4+ sentence comparison.

    WEEK 5 — EXPLAIN: Sustainability

    Lesson 1: What is Sustainability?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Sustainable Development (10 min)"Sustainability means meeting our needs today WITHOUT making it harder for future generations to meet theirs. It is about balance — using the environment but not destroying it. The three pillars are: ENVIRONMENTAL (protect nature) / SOCIAL (fair for people) / ECONOMIC (can afford it). All three must be balanced."Listen
    The Sustainability Test (15 min)"Ask: Will this use the resource faster than it can replenish? Will this pollute or damage the environment? Will this affect people's health or wellbeing? Will future generations be able to use this? If YES to the first two → not sustainable."Learn test
    Sustainable Practice Examples (10 min)Show: Rotational farming (land rests, recovers) / Marine parks (fish populations protected) / Recycling (resources reused) / Solar and wind power (renewable) / Waterwise gardening (less water used).Observe
    Apply the Test (5 min)"Apply the sustainability test to: (1) Logging old-growth forests, (2) Using solar power, (3) Mining iron ore in the Pilbara. Which are sustainable? Why?"Apply

    Success Criteria Check: Can apply the sustainability test to at least 2 examples and justify whether each is sustainable.

    Lesson 2: Sustainable Use — Case Studies

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Sustainable Farming (10 min)"Sustainable farming: crop rotation (different crops each season so soil isn't depleted), minimal tillage (leaves soil healthy), integrated pest management (uses natural predators instead of chemicals), agroforestry (trees on farms for shade and biodiversity)."Listen
    Sustainable Mining (10 min)"Mining is non-renewable — once ore is extracted, it is gone. But modern mining is becoming more sustainable: rehabilitation (returning land to nature after mining), water recycling, solar power for mine sites, Indigenous partnerships in decision-making."Learn
    Marine Stewardship (10 min)"Our oceans are under pressure. Sustainable fishing: catch limits (only take what can replenish), marine parks (protected zones), satellite monitoring of vessels, aquaculture (farming fish responsibly). The Great Barrier Reef Marine Park is an example of large-scale marine protection."Observe
    Write (10 min)"Choose one sustainable practice. Write 3–4 sentences explaining how it works and why it is better for the environment."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 examples of sustainable practices and explain why they are better for the environment.

    Lesson 3: Unsustainable Use — Case Studies

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Unsustainable Practices (10 min)"Some uses of the environment are unsustainable — they damage the environment faster than it can recover. This harms people, animals, and future generations."Listen
    Case Study: The Rabbits in Australia (10 min)"In The Rabbits (by John Marsden), introduced European rabbits destroyed vast areas of Australian landscape — cleared vegetation, outcompeted native animals, caused soil erosion. Rabbits were introduced unsustainably — without understanding the impact. The land suffered enormously."Connect to mentor text
    Case Study: Amazon Deforestation (10 min)"The Amazon rainforest is being cut down for farming (especially cattle and soy). 17% of the Amazon has already been lost. Deforestation causes: loss of biodiversity, displacement of Indigenous peoples, increased carbon in atmosphere, changed rainfall patterns. This is largely unsustainable."Observe
    What Could Have Been Done Differently? (10 min)"For rabbits: better biosecurity at borders. For Amazon: stronger enforcement of protection laws, sustainable farming alternatives, Indigenous land rights recognised. Change is possible when people act."Discuss

    Differentiation: EAL students draw before/after for one unsustainable case. Gifted students: research one contemporary environmental issue.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 1 unsustainable practice and explain what could have been done differently.

    WEEK 6 — ELABORATE: People and Environment

    Lesson 1: Population and Place

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Population Geography (10 min)"Population geography studies where people live and why. Most people in the world live on COASTS because of access to food (fishing), trade (ports), and water. Many live near RIVERS because water is essential for farming and life."Listen
    World Population Map (15 min)Show world population density map. Key observations: East Asia (China, Japan, Korea) very dense / South Asia (India) very dense / Europe dense / North America dense (east coast) / Australia very sparse (coastal cities, very sparse interior) / Africa sparse outside cities / Antarctica no permanent population.Observe
    Why Do People Cluster? (10 min)"People cluster where: there is water, the land is arable (can grow food), there are jobs (cities, ports, mines), the climate is tolerable. People avoid: extreme deserts, very cold polar regions, dense rainforest interiors."Understand
    WA Population (5 min)"WA has 2.7 million people. 80% live in Perth. The rest are in regional towns. Huge areas of WA are almost empty. Why? Because most of WA is arid or semi-arid — not enough water for large populations."Learn local

    Success Criteria Check: Can explain at least 3 reasons why certain places have high or low population density.

    Lesson 2: Urbanisation

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Urbanisation Trend (10 min)"Urbanisation is the process of more people moving to cities. In 1900, 13% of people lived in cities. By 2050, 68% of the world's population is expected to live in cities. This is one of the biggest changes in human history."Listen
    Why Cities Grow (10 min)"Cities grow because: more jobs (varied industries), better services (schools, hospitals, shops), cultural attractions (cinemas, restaurants, events), social opportunities. Rural areas lose people because: fewer jobs, less services, farming automation reduces labour needs."Understand push/pull
    Mega-Cities (10 min)"A megacity has more than 10 million people. Tokyo: 37 million / Delhi: 32 million / Shanghai: 28 million / São Paulo: 22 million / Mexico City: 22 million / Cairo: 21 million. By 2050, there will be 10+ megacities, mostly in Asia and Africa."Observe
    Australian Urbanisation (10 min)"In Australia: 86% of people live in cities. Perth is the 4th largest city. Our cities are growing but not as fast as Asian or African cities because Australia is a large land with a small population."Learn local

    Success Criteria Check: Can explain at least 2 reasons why urbanisation is happening and describe Australia's urbanisation rate.

    Lesson 3: Rural and Remote Challenges

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Rural and Remote Australia (10 min)"Rural and remote areas face unique challenges: fewer services (hospitals, schools may be hundreds of kilometres away), fly-in fly-out (FIFO) work patterns, higher costs (food, fuel, transport more expensive), smaller populations make services hard to sustain."Listen
    FIFO and DIDO (10 min)"Many people in remote WA work FIFO (Fly In Fly Out) or DIDO (Drive In Drive Out). They live in Perth or a regional centre and travel to mine sites for shifts. This keeps families in cities but puts strain on workers and community connections."Learn
    Service Access Gap (10 min)"People in remote areas have lower access to: healthcare (doctors per capita much lower), education (fewer specialist teachers), internet (satellite-dependent in very remote areas), fresh food (food security issues in some communities). This is called the ACCESS DIVIDE."Understand
    Solutions (10 min)"Solutions: telehealth (doctor appointments by video), fly-in specialist doctors, online education, community stores programs, Indigenous ranger programs (jobs and land management). WA government and community organisations are working on these."Learn solutions

    Differentiation: EAL students create a comparison chart between urban and remote challenges. Gifted students: investigate FIFO impacts on families.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 3 challenges faced by rural or remote communities and at least 1 solution being implemented.

    WEEK 7 — ELABORATE + EVALUATE: Environmental Investigation

    Lesson 1: Choose a Place to Investigate

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Choose Your Place (5 min)"Now you become the researcher. Choose one place to investigate deeply for the rest of this week. Options: Great Barrier Reef / Amazon Rainforest / Pilbara Mining Region / Kimberley / Antarctic Peninsula / Greenland / Saharan Africa / Himalayan region. Your job: investigate how and why people use the environment, and whether it is sustainable."Choose
    Set Inquiry Questions (10 min)Students formulate 3 inquiry questions for their place: (1) Where is this place and what is its climate? (2) How do people use the environment there? (3) Is this use sustainable? Why or why not?Write questions
    Gather Initial Data (15 min)Students use provided resources (articles, photos, maps) to begin gathering data for their inquiry questions. Teacher circulates.Research
    Note-Taking (10 min)Students take structured notes: PLACE / ENVIRONMENT / HUMAN USE / SUSTAINABILITY ASSESSMENT.Take notes

    Success Criteria Check: Has chosen a place and formulated 3 geographic inquiry questions about it.

    Lesson 2: Deep Dive Research

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Research Time (20 min)Students continue research using provided texts, images, maps. Focus on: What environment? What resources? How are people using it? What is the impact? Is it sustainable? Teacher and librarian support.Deep research
    Process Information (10 min)"Process means: sort the information, identify key facts, evaluate sustainability, draw conclusions. Your conclusion should answer: How do people use this environment? Is this use sustainable? What should change?"Process
    Prepare Presentation (10 min)Students choose format: written report (6–8 sentences) or visual poster with labels. Must include: location, climate, environment features, human use, sustainability assessment.Prepare

    Success Criteria Check: Has gathered sufficient data on their chosen place and can process it into geographic conclusions.

    Lesson 3: Present Findings

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Presentation Day (30 min)Students present their place investigation to the class. Peers use feedback template: "I learned that..." / "One question I have..." / "One sustainability suggestion..."Present
    Class Discussion (10 min)"What did we learn about how people use environments differently across the world? Which examples showed sustainable use? Which showed unsustainable use? What can we conclude?"Synthesise

    Differentiation: EAL students may present using images with verbal support. NEP students may work in pairs.

    Success Criteria Check: Completes a place investigation with location, environmental description, human use description, and sustainability assessment.

    WEEK 8 — EVALUATE: Hot Task and Reflection

    Lesson 1: Hot Task — Show What You Know

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Summative Assessment

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Set Up (5 min)"Today is your Hot Task. You will investigate how and why people use environments differently in two places, and evaluate which use is more sustainable. You may use your research from Week 7 or choose new places."Listen
    Planning (10 min)Students complete a planner: Place A vs Place B / Climate / Environment features / Human use / Sustainability verdict and reasoning.Plan
    Hot Task (25 min)"Write an investigation: Choose two places. For each: describe the climate, natural features, and how people use the environment. Then evaluate: Which place is using its environment more sustainably? Why? What should change?" Year 3: 8–12 sentences.Independent completion

    Assessment Note: Use rubric to assess. Compare to Cold Task for growth analysis.

    Lesson 2: Cold Task vs Hot Task — Growth Reflection

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Self-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Compare (15 min)Place Cold Task next to Hot Task. "Use green texta to mark 3 things you did better. Use red texta to mark 1 thing to improve."Compare
    Growth Statement (10 min)"I have grown as a geographic investigator because..." Write 4–6 sentences.Write
    Share (10 min)Volunteers share one geographic insight from the unit.Share
    Best Learning (5 min)"What is one thing you know now that you didn't know at the start?"Share

    Success Criteria Check: Completes self-comparison showing growth in geographic investigation skills.

    Lesson 3: Celebration — Sharing Our Learning

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Celebration

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Learning Gallery (15 min)Hot Tasks and investigation posters displayed. Gallery walk with sticky notes.Gallery
    Geo Quiz (10 min)Class quiz: "What is the difference between renewable and non-renewable resources?" / "Name one sustainable practice." / "Why do most people live on coasts?" / "What is urbanisation?"Quiz
    Certificate (5 min)Award "Active Investigator" certificates.Receive
    What's Next? (10 min)"What environmental issue would you like to investigate next? What should we do to protect places we care about?"Share

    DIFFERENTIATION NOTES

    EAL Learners

  • Visual Word Wall: environment, sustainability, renewable, non-renewable, urban, rural, remote, population, climate, trade, migration, inquiry, resources.
  • Sentence Starters: "People in [place] use the environment by..." / "This use is [more/less] sustainable because..." / "One connection between places is..." / "Both places have..." / "The difference is..."
  • Home Language Research: Students may research their family's country of origin as a place study.
  • Partner Support: EAL students paired for research activities.

    • Gifted Learners

  • Research Extension: Investigate a specific environmental issue in depth (e.g., Great Barrier Reef bleaching, Amazon fires, Antarctic ice melt).
  • Policy Proposal: Write a formal proposal to an environmental minister about improving sustainability in one place studied.
  • Comparative Essay: Write a 5-paragraph essay comparing environmental sustainability in 2 places.

    • Students with NEPs

  • Reduced Written Expectation: 4–6 sentences for Year 3 on written tasks.
  • Word Banks: Provided for all written tasks.
  • Oral Option: Students may present verbally.
  • Extra Time: Additional time for all tasks.
  • Scaffolded Templates: Inquiry question template, note-taking template, comparison planner.
