Narrative Writing Unit: The Giver

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Cold Task: Pre-assessment (Week 1, Lesson 1) - Write a narrative about a character in a controlled world who discovers a secret

Hot Task: Post-assessment (Week 8, Lesson 1) - Same as Cold Task

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to write a sophisticated dystopian narrative with a controlled world, a character's discovery, and a transformation — using precise language, thematic depth, and moral complexity.

Success Criteria:

  • I can create a dystopian world with specific rules, controls, and apparent perfection that hides something wrong.
  • I can write a protagonist who goes through transformation — from ignorance/obedience to awareness/rebellion.
  • I can use narrative structure: equilibrium → disruption → discovery → crisis → transformation.
  • I can explore moral complexity — the difference between following rules and doing what is right.
  • I can show, not tell — especially for emotion, memory, and meaning.
  • I can write with thematic depth — exploring questions about freedom, choice, memory, identity.

    WEEK 1 — INTRODUCTION TO THE GIVER

    Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)"Imagine a world where everything seems perfect — no war, no hunger, no pain. But everyone must follow the rules without question. Now write about a character who begins to notice something is wrong."Think
    Task (40 min)Cold Task: Write a narrative about a character living in a controlled, 'perfect' world who begins to discover something is wrong. Show the moment they first sense the truth. Year 6: 400–600 words.Independent writing

    Differentiation: World-building planning sheet available. Theme prompts for students who need guidance.

    Lesson 2: Welcome to the Community (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (20 min)Read *The Giver* — Chapters 1–5 (Jonas's world, the Ceremony, receiving The Giver). Note: Lowry's deceptively simple prose. How does she make sameness feel oppressive?Analyze world-building
    Discuss (15 min)"What do we learn about Jonas's community?" Rules: Everyone is assigned. Family units are determined. Emotional release is policy. Birthmothers exist but children belong to family units. Colour is absent. Snow is manufactured. Climate is controlled. "What is the effect of this control?"Examine rules
    Share (5 min)"What is Lowry SAYING about sameness, control, and choice through this world?"Thematic discussion

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify at least 5 rules/mechanisms of Jonas's community and explain what each controls.

    Lesson 3: A World Without... (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"In The Giver's community, what have they removed? Pain, war, hunger, grief, colour, climate extremes, choice, freedom, memory, love." Each absence is a CONTROL mechanism.List absences
    We Do (20 min)Brainstorm: What would YOU remove from a society to make it 'perfect'? Make a list. Discuss: Removing something always has a cost. What would be lost?Discuss trade-offs
    You Do (10 min)Write in journals: If you could remove one thing from the world to end all pain — but everyone would lose it forever — what would you choose? What would be lost?Personal reflection

    WEEK 2 — WORLD ARCHITECTURE

    Lesson 1: The Rules of the Community (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (10 min)Revisit The Giver rules. Show how each rule creates a specific control. "The rule that there are no choices" controls identity. "The rule that colours are absent" controls memory.Analyze rule systems
    Mini-Lesson (15 min)Dystopian world rules must be: SPECIFIC (not "no crime" but "emotional release of non-compliant citizens") / LOGICAL (in-world reason for every rule) / VISIBLE (how do citizens know the rules? signage, training, consequences)Note rules
    You Do (15 min)Begin building YOUR dystopian world: List 8 rules. For each rule, write WHAT it controls and WHAT happens to rule-breakers.Build world rules

    Lesson 2: Creating Your World (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Show Lowry's precise details: "Invidious" — a made-up word that means something is wrong. The detail of the apple turning red. "Precision" is how the community controls thought.Analyze detail
    We Do (15 min)Class builds a dystopian world together: What is the world called? What has been removed? What are the rules? What does 'perfect' look like? What is the hidden cost?Build collaboratively
    You Do (15 min)Write: Your world has a name, a geography, a history. The world feels normal to those who live there. Write 2–3 paragraphs describing it from the INSIDE — as if someone born there would describe it.Write world

    Lesson 3: Writing the Rules (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Write the RULEBOOK for your world — the official document a citizen would read. 10 rules minimum. Language should feel controlled, calm, official. Underneath, readers should sense the oppression.Write rulebook
    Share (5 min)1–2 students share their most chilling ruleShare

    WEEK 3 — CHARACTER AND IDENTITY

    Lesson 1: Who Are You in a Controlled World? (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (15 min)Read The Giver — Jonas's assignment and early memories. "Who is Jonas BEFORE the transformation?" He is: Obedient, curious, slightly outside (he's nervous about the Ceremony), intelligent, kind. He has not yet had the DISCOVERY that changes him.Analyze pre-change Jonas
    Mini-Lesson (10 min)In dystopian fiction, the protagonist has a TRAJECTORY: Before → After. Before = who they are in the controlled world. After = who they become when they see the truth. The journey between these two states IS the story.Understand trajectory
    You Do (15 min)Write: Who is your protagonist BEFORE the discovery? Describe a normal day in their life. Make the reader see the routine, the rules, the sameness.Write protagonist

    Lesson 2: Character Profiles (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Build Jonas's profile: INSIDE (wants to be good, curious, intelligent, kind, confused by feelings he can't name) + OUTSIDE (ordinary, follows rules, assigned to The Giver by fate) + TRANSFORMATION (becomes aware, makes a choice, takes action). Also note: supporting characters (Father, Mother, Lily, Gabriel).Model profile
    We Do (15 min)Build your protagonist's profile. Include: INSIDE (desires, fears, confusions) / OUTSIDE (how they appear in the world) / TRANSFORMATION (how do they change? What triggers the change?)Build character profile
    You Do (15 min)Add supporting characters: Who is in your protagonist's world? Parent? Mentor? Sibling? Friend? The "Watcher" — the enforcer of the rules?Add supports

    Lesson 3: The Protagonist's Transformation (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Write: The moment your protagonist FIRST NOTICES something wrong. Not the big discovery — the small, first moment. A flicker. A question. A feeling. Make it quiet, specific, and real.Write first noticing
    Share (5 min)Share the first noticing moment — what makes it effective is that it is SMALLPeer response

    WEEK 4 — THE DISCOVERY

    Lesson 1: Breaking the Surface (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (15 min)Read The Giver — the Snowmobile memory and Jonas's first real memory. "What is the first MEMORY Jonas receives? What does it feel like to him?" The snow, the colour, the cold — it is overwhelming. He has never felt any of this.Analyze discovery
    Mini-Lesson (10 min)Discovery scenes work best when the protagonist DOESN'T understand what they are seeing at first. They experience it, then piece it together. Lowry builds the memory slowly — sentence by sentence — because Jonas is learning to feel for the first time.Note technique
    You Do (15 min)Write: Your protagonist receives the first piece of the hidden truth. Write the scene as the protagonist experiences it — confusion, then recognition, then horror.Write discovery

    Lesson 2: The Secret Revealed (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"In The Giver, the secret is not one thing — it is MANY things: colour, pain, love, war, snow, the fact that Elsewhere exists, that release is death, that the community has chosen to erase human experience." The secret is the COST of sameness.Analyze layers
    We Do (20 min)In groups, map the LAYERS of the secret in your world. What is the surface-level wrong? What is the deeper wrong? What is the deepest truth? Draw it as concentric circles. Present to class.Map secrets
    You Do (10 min)Write: Your protagonist understands more than they did before. Write a conversation between your protagonist and a mentor (The Giver figure) where more truth is revealed.Write revelation

    Lesson 3: Writers Workshop (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Peer Feedback

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Write the full DISCOVERY section of your story — the scene where the protagonist pieces together the truth. Conference with 4–5 students.Write and conference
    Share (5 min)One student shares the most effective line of discovery from their draftShare

    WEEK 5 — RISING ACTION AND CRISIS

    Lesson 1: The Decision to Act (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (15 min)Read The Giver — Jonas decides to leave. How does Lowry build to this? Jonas tries to stay, follows the rules, hopes the community can change. Then he realizes: it cannot change. The only option is to leave. This is the CRISIS — the decision to act.Analyze decision
    Mini-Lesson (10 min)Moral choices: The protagonist faces a moment where following the rules means betraying what they now know to be true. The cost of action is high. The cost of inaction is higher. What do they choose?Understand moral choice
    You Do (15 min)Write: Your protagonist makes the decision to act. What is the trigger? What do they risk? What do they stand to lose? Write the decision scene.Write decision

    Lesson 2: Crossing the Threshold (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"Crossing the threshold" — the moment the protagonist leaves the controlled world and enters the unknown. In The Giver, Jonas leaves with Gabe. He crosses the boundary — the river, the boundary markers, the forest. This is the POINT OF NO RETURN.Analyze threshold
    We Do (15 min)Map your story's threshold moment: WHERE is the physical boundary? WHAT does your protagonist cross? WHAT is the moment of no return?Map threshold
    You Do (15 min)Write the THRESHOLD crossing scene — the moment your protagonist leaves everything they know. Make it physical, sensory, and irreversible.Write threshold

    Lesson 3: The Climax (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (5 min)The climax in dystopian fiction is often not a battle — it is a moral reckoning. It is the moment where the world confronts the protagonist with the cost of their choice.Note
    You Do (35 min)Write the CLIMAX of your story — the moment of highest stakes. What is the question your protagonist must answer? "Is the pain worth it?" "Is the memory worth preserving?" "Is freedom worth the cost?"Write climax

    WEEK 6 — RESOLUTION AND MEANING

    Lesson 1: The Aftermath (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (15 min)The ending of The Giver — this is one of literature's most discussed endings. "Does Jonas survive?" Lowry doesn't tell us. He is heading toward Elsewhere — it could be death, it could be freedom, it could be transformation. "Why does Lowry leave it open?"Analyze open ending
    Mini-Lesson (10 min)Multiple endings: TELLING (explain everything) / AMBIGUOUS (leave the ending open, trust the reader) / CIRCLE BACK (ending echoes beginning) / TRANSFORMATION (character has fundamentally changed). Lowry uses AMBIGUOUS + TRANSFORMATION.Study endings
    You Do (15 min)Decide: Which type of ending does YOUR story have? Write the final scene.Write ending

    Lesson 2: Theme — What Is the Story Really About? (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)The Giver is about: The danger of a world without pain / Memory as identity / The cost of control / The importance of choice / Love requires loss / Freedom is worth the risk. These are not lessons — they are questions Lowry asks the reader.Identify themes
    We Do (20 min)Identify themes in YOUR story. Write them as open questions: "Is a world without pain worth living in?" "What do we owe to those who came before us?" "Is there freedom in pain?" Share with class.Identify themes
    You Do (10 min)Write: Does your story answer or question its themes? Is your ending a statement or a question?Reflect

    Lesson 3: Multiple Endings (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Write TWO different endings for your story. 1. The ending you think is most TRUE to the story. 2. An alternative — maybe happier, maybe darker. Share with partner. Which is more honest?Write alternatives
    Share (5 min)Discuss: What makes an ending "true" vs "satisfying"? Are they different?Discuss

    WEEK 7 — POLISHING AND CRAFT

    Lesson 1: Language and Style (I Do → You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Editing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Lowry's style is PRECISE and SPARE. Every word does work. "The lack of colour in the community is not mentioned until Jonas notices it — because in the community, no one has ever seen colour to MISS it." This is SHOWING vs TELLING at the world level.Analyze style
    You Do (30 min)Revise your opening 3 paragraphs. Each sentence: Is this word necessary? Does this image add meaning? Is this the most precise word? Cut what doesn't work. Replace vague words with specific ones.Targeted revision

    Lesson 2: Showing Memory (I Do → You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Writing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)In The Giver, the memories are shown to Jonas through direct, sensory, overwhelming experience. Lowry writes memories as PRESENCE — you feel what Jonas feels. Example: The memory of war is not described — Jonas EXPERIENCES the pain, the loss, the horror.Analyze memory writing
    You Do (30 min)Write a memory scene — a scene where your protagonist receives or experiences a memory from the past. Make it sensory: What do they see? Hear? Feel in their body?Write memory

    Lesson 3: Final Draft (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Publishing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (40 min)Write or type final published narrative. Double line spacing. Add a title and author name. Include a brief author's note explaining the world you built if needed.Prepare final copy

    WEEK 8 — HOT TASK AND REFLECTION

    Lesson 1: Hot Task (Assessment)

    Time: 50 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Hook (5 min)"Think about the controlled world you wrote about in Week 1. Think about what you now know about dystopian writing — the precision, the world architecture, the character transformation, the moral questions. Write something that shows what you have learned."Reflect
    Task (45 min)SAME TASK: Write a narrative about a character living in a controlled, 'perfect' world who discovers the truth. Year 6: 400–600 words. Focus: World-building precision, transformation arc, thematic depth, moral complexity.Independent writing

    Compare Cold Task → Hot Task using rubric. Note growth in world-building craft and thematic depth.

    Lesson 2: Comparing Our Work (We Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Analysis

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (20 min)Compare Cold Task vs Hot Task — what improved? Annotate rubric with specific evidence: world rules, transformation arc, ending qualityCompare
    Share (25 min)Students share specific growth — focus on craft, not just outputCelebrate

    Lesson 3: Celebration and Reflection (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Reflection

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Reflection (25 min)Complete reflection: What does dystopian fiction help us understand about our own world? What did you learn about writing complex narratives? Self-assess against success criteria (1–4).Self-assess
    Celebration (15 min)Read key passages. Discuss: What did this unit help you understand about control, memory, identity, and choice?Community reflection

    ADAPTATIONS

    EAL Students

  • Pre-teach dystopian vocabulary (utopia, dystopia, control, regime)
  • Visual world-building template with prompts
  • Sentence starters for showing transformation
  • Allow home language brainstorming
  • Provide summary of The Giver plot events
  • One-on-one comprehension conferences

    • Gifted Students

  • Compare Lowry's style to other dystopians (Huxley, Orwell, Atwood)
  • Write from an antagonist's perspective
  • Explore the science behind memory/control (neuroscience connection)
  • Write a companion story set in the same world
  • Study banned books and why dystopias are often challenged

    • Students with Additional Needs

  • Reduce word count to 300–400 words
  • Provide world-building template with structured prompts
  • Allow graphic novel format with captions
  • One-on-one conference during writing time
  • Extra time for all tasks
  • Sentence starters for discovery scenes

  • Character profiles and transformation maps
  • Discovery and decision scenes
  • Peer feedback sessions
  • Teacher conferences (Weeks 3, 5, 7)

    • Summative Assessment

  • Cold Task → Hot Task comparison (same rubric, measure growth)
  • Published narrative assessed with rubric
  • Theme identification and author's intent reflection
