Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to write an imaginative narrative about friendship and courage, using literary devices and showing emotional depth through our writing.

Success Criteria:

  • I can plan and write a narrative with a clear structure (beginning, problem, resolution).
  • I can create believable characters with emotional depth and friendship dynamics.
  • I can use the narrative arc (setup, problem, rising action, climax, resolution).
  • I can show emotions through actions, dialogue and thoughts rather than just telling.
  • I can use literary devices: metaphor, simile, symbolism, imagery.
  • I can write with emotional honesty and thematic depth.

    WEEK 1 — INTRODUCTION TO BRIDGE TO TERABITHIA

    Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)"Today you will write a story about friendship and a challenge. Think about someone you know who helped you through a hard time."Think and recall
    Task (40 min)Cold Task: Write a narrative about two characters who are friends and face a challenge together. Show what makes their friendship special. Year 5: 300–450 words.Independent writing

    Differentiation: Character planning sheet available. Word bank for feelings/emotions.

    Lesson 2: Meeting Jess and Leslie (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (20 min)Read *Bridge to Terabithia* — Chapters 1–4 (introducing Jess, Leslie, the move to Terabithia). Note: Paterson's spare, precise language.Listen and note
    Discuss (15 min)"What do we learn about Jess in the first pages? What does he want? What is his life like?" Connect: family dynamics, rural Virginia, feeling like an outsider. Leslie as the new girl who changes everything.Analyse character
    Share (5 min)"What do you notice about how Paterson writes? Short sentences. Real thoughts. No wasted words."Note style

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify Jess's core conflict (wants to be fast/different vs family expectations) and Leslie's role (catalyst).

    Lesson 3: What Makes a Story Meaningful? (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"Bridge to Terabithia is not just a story about kids playing in the forest. It is about grief, courage, friendship, growing up." Themes: Courage isn't the absence of fear / Imagination can be a refuge / Loss changes usExplore themes
    We Do (20 min)Class discussion: "What is the most meaningful story you have ever read? What made it meaningful?" List elements on board: Real emotions / Characters we care about / Something true about lifeDiscuss meaning
    You Do (10 min)Write in journals: What do YOU want your story to be about? What feeling do you want readers to have?Personal goal setting

    WEEK 2 — THE TERRITORY OF THE IMAGINATION

    Lesson 1: Terabithia — A World of Our Own (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (15 min)Read *Bridge to Terabithia* — Terabithia chapters. How does Paterson describe the forest? What makes Terabithia feel real? It's not fantasy magic — it's two children's intense imagination making the ordinary extraordinary.Analyze world-building
    Mini-Lesson (10 min)Terabithia is created by two things: the physical place (creek, forest, abandoned castle) and the children's imagination (they RULES it, they BELIEVE it). "What makes an imaginary place feel real?" Rules + Symbols + History = Believable worldNote world-building
    You Do (15 min)Brainstorm: What would YOUR imaginary kingdom look like? What rules would it have? What would it feel like to go there?Imaginary world planning

    Lesson 2: Creating Your Imaginary World (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Show the Terabithia map Jess and Leslie create. How does having a MAP make the world feel real?Observe
    We Do (15 min)Class collaboratively builds an imaginary world: GEOGRAPHY (physical features) / RULES (how it works) / SYMBOLS (flags, signs, creatures) / HISTORY (how it began)Collaborative world-building
    You Do (15 min)Begin designing YOUR imaginary world: Draw a rough map. Write 5 rules. Give it a name.Design world

    Lesson 3: Building the Fantasy Place (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Develop your imaginary world in detail: Map with labels, creature descriptions, rules written as a manifesto, entry ritual. This world will feature in your narrative.Build world
    Share (5 min)2–3 students share their world's most interesting featureShare

    WEEK 3 — FRIENDSHIP AND CHARACTER

    Lesson 1: Jess and Leslie — An Unlikely Pair (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (15 min)Read *Bridge to Terabithia* — early friendship scenes. How do Jess and Leslie become friends? It is NOT instant — Jess is resistant, Leslie is confident, they meet in the creek.Analyze relationship
    Mini-Lesson (10 min)Friendship in literature: Friends challenge us / Friends see something in us we don't see ourselves / Friends push us beyond comfort. Jess was lonely, Leslie saw his potential, she pulled him into Terabithia.Understand friendship dynamics
    You Do (15 min)Write a paragraph: Think of a time a friend changed you or challenged you. What happened?Personal reflection

    Lesson 2: Character Profiles (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Build Jess's character profile: OUTSIDE (what we see) + INSIDE (what we don't see). Jess: Talented artist, fast runner (wants to be), lonely, feels invisible at home, secretly kind, angry at his situation. Leslie: Creative, fearless, from city, rich parents but emotionally neglected, sees magic in ordinary.Model profile
    We Do (15 min)Build profiles for both characters in your narrative. Character A and Character B. For each: OUTSIDE (look, speech, habits) + INSIDE (fears, wants, secret) + DYNAMIC (how do they change?)Build character profiles
    You Do (15 min)Write full character profiles for your two main charactersIndependent work

    Lesson 3: Writing Your Character (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Write a scene where your two characters meet or interact. Show their friendship forming — what do they say? What do they do? How do they feel? Show, don't tell.Write character scene
    Share (5 min)1–2 students share their scene for feedbackPeer feedback

    WEEK 4 — THE PROBLEM

    Lesson 1: What Presses Against Us (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (15 min)Read *Bridge to Terabithia* — the storm chapter. "What is Jess's real problem?" External: The creek rises. Internal: He feels responsible, he is proud, he doesn't tell anyone his fears.Analyze problem types
    Mini-Lesson (10 min)Two types of problems: External (something happens to your character) and Internal (something your character feels or believes). The BEST stories have both. Jess's external problem = the storm. Jess's internal problem = his pride and fear.Distinguish problems
    You Do (15 min)Decide: What is the EXTERNAL problem in your story? What is the INTERNAL problem (fear, belief, feeling) your character must face?Define problems

    Lesson 2: The Story Problem (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"In Bridge to Terabithia, the problem builds slowly, then something makes it worse. What makes Jess's problem worse?" The race against Janice, the pride of being fast, not telling Leslie he is scared.Analyze problem building
    We Do (15 min)Together, map the story problem on a PROBLEM ARC: SETUP → PRESSURE → COMPLICATION → CRISISBuild problem arc
    You Do (15 min)Apply the problem arc to your own story. What is the setup? What adds pressure? What complicates it? What is the crisis moment?Apply to story

    Lesson 3: Writers Workshop (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Peer Feedback

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (5 min)Today's goal: Write the PROBLEM section of your story.Set goal
    You Do (30 min)Write: The problem is introduced. The pressure builds. Something makes it worse.Write problem section
    Share (5 min)Share problem section with partner — partner identifies: Is the problem clear? Is there internal and external conflict?Peer feedback

    WEEK 5 — RISING ACTION AND CLIMAX

    Lesson 1: Building Tension (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (15 min)Read *Bridge to Terabithia* — the day of the tragedy. How does Paterson build tension? Short sentences. Time slows down. Jess's internal monologue. Everything feels inevitable.Analyze tension
    Mini-Lesson (10 min)Tension techniques: Sentence length variation / Time slowing / Sensory detail / Internal thoughts / Foreshadowing. Use 2–3 techniques in your rising action.Note techniques
    You Do (15 min)Write the RISING ACTION of your story — the section between the problem and the climax. Make the reader feel that something important is at stake.Write rising action

    Lesson 2: The Moment of Crisis (I Do → We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching + Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (15 min)"The CLIMAX is the moment where everything changes — the character faces the worst moment and must respond." In Bridge to Terabithia, the climax is when Jess arrives and Leslie is gone. The tragedy happens OFF PAGE but the aftermath IS the climax. Paterson holds back the worst — we feel it through Jess.Analyze climax
    We Do (15 min)Decide: What is the CLIMAX of your story? The moment your character faces the worst. Write it as a single paragraph.Define climax
    You Do (10 min)Write the CLIMAX scene — the most intense moment of your story.Write climax

    Lesson 3: Writers Workshop (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Continue writing your middle section. Conference with 4–5 students on climactic scenes.Write and conference
    Share (5 min)Read one climactic paragraph to classShare

    WEEK 6 — RESOLUTION AND THEME

    Lesson 1: How Do We End? (I Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explicit Teaching

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Read (15 min)How does Bridge to Terabithia END? Jess builds a bridge to Terabithia. He goes there alone. He lets May Belle in. He plants a tree. "What does the ending mean?" It means Jess has grown. Leslie's gift (the imagination) continues through him. He gives it to May Belle. The grief becomes a gift.Analyze ending
    Mini-Lesson (10 min)Types of endings: Circle back (beginning and end mirror) / Character transformed / Open/ambiguous / Sad but true. Paterson uses ALL THREE: Circle (bridge), Transformation (Jess grows), True (grief remains).Study endings
    You Do (15 min)Decide: What type of ending will YOUR story have? Write the final scene.Write ending

    Lesson 2: Theme — What Is the Story Really About? (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Guided

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)"Theme is what the reader understands about LIFE from your story. Bridge to Terabithia is about: Friendship transforms us / Loss is part of life / We honour the dead by living fully / Courage means feeling fear and going forward anyway."Define theme
    We Do (20 min)Identify themes in Bridge to Terabithia. Write them as statements about life. Then identify the themes in YOUR story. Write: "My story is really about..."Theme identification
    You Do (10 min)Write your story's theme as a sentence. Does your story support this theme? Check your plot.Theme verification

    Lesson 3: Drafting the Ending (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Independent

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (35 min)Complete your story — final draft of the ending. Check: Does it circle back to something from the beginning? Has the character changed? Is there a sense of truth — not just a happy ending but a REAL ending?Complete ending
    Share (5 min)1–2 students share their endingPeer response

    WEEK 7 — POLISHING AND PUBLISHING

    Lesson 1: Revising for Depth (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Revision

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Model: Read a paragraph from Bridge to Terabithia. How does Paterson show emotion without stating it? "She just took my hand and held on." This says everything about friendship without explaining it.Observe showing
    We Do (15 min)Peer swap: Read partner's draft. Mark: Where do you FEEL the emotion? Where does the writer TELL rather than SHOW? Give specific suggestions.Peer revision
    You Do (15 min)Revise 2 key scenes — add showing, remove telling, deepen emotional moments.Targeted revision

    Lesson 2: Language and Literary Devices (I Do → You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Editing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    I Do (10 min)Literary devices to add: SIMILE ("as quiet as a mouse") / METAPHOR ("my heart was a stone") / PERSONIFICATION ("the wind whispered") / SYMBOLISM (the forest as refuge) / IMAGERY (sensory description). Where in your story could you add one of each?Identify devices
    You Do (30 min)Add 2 similes, 1 metaphor and 1 symbol to your story. Underline them. Check they enhance meaning, not just decorate.Add literary devices

    Lesson 3: Final Draft (You Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Publishing

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    You Do (40 min)Write or type final published version of your narrative. Double line spacing. Neat presentation. Add a title and author name.Prepare final copy

    WEEK 8 — HOT TASK AND REFLECTION

    Lesson 1: Hot Task (Assessment)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Assessment

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Hook (5 min)"Think about the story you wrote in Week 1. Think about the stories you have read this unit. You are ready to write something deeper and more meaningful now."Recall journey
    Task (40 min)SAME TASK: Write a narrative about two characters who are friends and face a challenge together. Show what makes their friendship special. Year 5: 300–450 words. Focus: Show emotional depth, use literary devices, create a meaningful ending.Independent writing

    Compare Cold Task → Hot Task using rubric. Note growth in emotional depth and craft.

    Lesson 2: Comparing Our Work (We Do)

    Time: 45 minutes | Type: Analysis

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    We Do (20 min)Compare Cold Task and Hot Task samples. Look for: Emotional depth improvement / Character development / Literary device use / Ending qualityCompare and annotate
    Share (25 min)Students share specific growth moments in their writingCelebrate

    Lesson 3: Reflection and Celebration (We Do)

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Reflection

    PhaseTeacherStudents
    Reflection (25 min)Complete reflection: What did you learn about writing meaningful stories? What will you take forward? Self-assess against success criteria (1–4).Self-assess
    Celebration (15 min)Read favourite excerpts. Acknowledge growth. Display final narratives.Community celebration

    ADAPTATIONS

    EAL Students

  • Pre-teach themes and emotional vocabulary
  • Character profile template with visual supports
  • Allow home language brainstorming for initial ideas
  • Sentence starters for showing emotions
  • Provide Bridge to Terabithia summary in home language
  • One-on-one conference for comprehension support

    • Gifted Students

  • Write from a minor character's perspective (e.g., May Belle)
  • Explore the tragedy in more depth — write a companion piece
  • Analyse Paterson's spare style vs other authors
  • Write an alternate ending
  • Study grief literature — read other Paterson works

    • Students with Additional Needs

  • Reduce word count to 200–300 words
  • Provide story arc graphic organiser
  • Allow graphic novel format with speech bubbles and illustrations
  • Provide character description cards to colour and assemble
  • Extra time for all tasks
  • One-on-one conference during writing time

    • ASSESSMENT NOTES

    Formative Assessment (Ongoing)

  • Journal entries on themes
  • Character profiles
  • Story maps and problem arcs
  • Draft writing with peer feedback
  • Teacher conferences (Weeks 3, 5, 7)

    • Summative Assessment

  • Cold Task → Hot Task comparison (same rubric, measure growth)
  • Published narrative assessed with rubric
  • Theme identification and self-reflection

    • Tracking Progress

    Use the comparison template to document:

  • Cold Task score → Hot Task score
  • Specific skills: emotional depth, literary devices, ending craft
  • Student self-assessment
