Unit Content

My History, Your History — History Unit Plan

Unit Duration: 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons

Year Level: Year 1

Curriculum Link: Australian Curriculum HASS v9 — History (Year 1)

Unit Big Idea: Every person has a history. Our personal histories connect to the histories of our families, our communities, and our country. Understanding that we all have different histories and that history is all around us helps us understand ourselves and each other.

Cold Task: Week 1, Lesson 1 — Draw and write about an object from home that tells your family's story

Hot Task: Week 8, Lesson 1 — Share your family history presentation with the class and compare your history with a partner's

Unit Overview

Learning Intention:

We are learning to explore personal and family histories, understand how the past is different from the present, and develop a sense of how history connects to our daily lives.

Success Criteria:

  • I can describe at least 3 events from my personal past.
  • I can use words like "yesterday," "last year," "long ago," and "a long time ago" correctly.
  • I can explain that objects and photos tell us about the past.
  • I can describe how life in Australia was different for children 50–100 years ago.
  • I can share a story from my family history.
  • I can compare my life today with a child's life in the past.

    WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: History Is All Around

    Lesson 1: Cold Task — Objects Tell Our Stories

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hook (5 min)Show two objects: an old photograph and a new phone. "What is different about these? What story does each one tell? What do you think history means?"Observe and wonder
    Introduction (5 min)"History is the story of what happened BEFORE NOW. History is not just in books — it is all around us. In our families, our homes, our streets. Your task today is to bring history into the room."Listen
    Cold Task (30 min)"Bring an object from home that tells part of your family's story (if you have one, otherwise use the classroom objects provided). Draw the object in the box. Write or tell: What is this object? Whose was it? What story does it tell? Why is it important to your family?" Year 1 expectation: drawing + labels + 2–3 sentences.Independent

    Differentiation: Provide 3 classroom objects from the past (old phone, old toys, old photos) for students without family objects.

    Success Criteria Check: Student produces a drawing of an object and describes at least 1 thing the object tells about the past.

    Lesson 2: What Is History?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Hook)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    History Is Everywhere (10 min)"History is the story of what happened before now. History is all around us — in our homes, our streets, our families, our country. The oldest person in your life knows things that happened when your parents were young. That is history."Listen
    History Sources (15 min)"Historians use SOURCES to find out about the past. Sources are things that help us know what happened. Photos, letters, diaries, objects, buildings, stories passed down by old people. We are going to be HISTORY DETECTIVES this term — using sources to find out about the past."Learn
    Source Sort (10 min)Show image cards: old photograph / letter / teddy bear / building / diary / grandmother's story. "Which of these are sources that could tell us about the past? Which are sources from the past?"Sort
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Draw one source of history (something that tells us about the past)."Draw

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 2 sources that tell us about the past.

    Lesson 3: Stories of Objects

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Engage (Concept Introduction)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Objects Tell Stories (10 min)"Objects can tell us STORIES about people in the past. An old toy tells us children played differently. An old photo tells us what people wore. An old letter tells us what people cared about. Objects are like time machines."Listen
    Mystery Object Activity (15 min)Pass around 2–3 old objects from the past (old telephone, old kettle, old toy, old key). Students guess: What is it? Who used it? What story does it tell?Investigate
    Connect to Cold Task (10 min)"Look at the object you brought or looked at. What question would you like to ask it if it could answer?"Think
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What is one question you could ask an old object to find out about the past?"Write

    Differentiation: EAL students draw their question. Gifted students: bring 2 objects from different time periods to compare.

    Success Criteria Check: Can ask at least 1 question that an old object could answer about the past.

    WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: My Personal History

    Lesson 1: My Life Story

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Everyone Has a Life Story (10 min)"Every person has a life story — what happened from the day you were born until today. Your life story is part of history. You are living history right now!"Listen
    Key Life Events (15 min)Discuss key life events: being born, first steps, first words, starting school, learning to ride a bike, losing a tooth, starting a new school, a special birthday. "Which of these have happened to you? Which are still to come?"Discuss
    My Life Events (10 min)Students draw or write 3 things that have happened in their own life: something that happened when they were a baby / something that happened recently / something they are looking forward to.Write
    Share (5 min)Volunteers share one life event with the class.Share

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 3 events from their own life history.

    Lesson 2: Special Days

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Special Days in Our Lives (10 min)"Some days are more special than others. Birthdays, weddings, religious celebrations, cultural events, public holidays, the day you started school. These are MEMORABLE DAYS — days we remember because they are important."Listen
    Our Special Days (10 min)"What are YOUR special days? Birthday? Cultural celebration? Religious holiday? Family gathering? Write or draw the 3 most special days in your life."Write
    Calendar of Special Days (10 min)"Let's make a class calendar of special days. Who has a birthday in January? February? What cultural celebrations do we have in our class?"Create
    Compare (5 min)"Look at the calendar. How many different special days do we have in our class? What does this tell us about our class?"Compare

    Success Criteria Check: Can name at least 3 special days and explain why they are memorable.

    Lesson 3: My Personal Timeline

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Timelines Tell Stories (10 min)"A TIMELINE is a line that shows events in ORDER — first to last. Historians use timelines to understand when things happened. We use words like: BEFORE (earlier) / AFTER (later) / NEXT / THEN / WHEN."Learn
    Words We Use for Time (15 min)Practice: "I got dressed AFTER I had a shower" / "I ate breakfast BEFORE I came to school" / "First we line up, THEN we go to lunch" / "WHEN I was a baby I couldn't walk."Practice
    My Timeline (15 min)Distribute timeline template. Students draw or write 4 key life events in order: I was born → I started school → I learned to ride a bike → Today. Use arrows and time words.Create
    Share (5 min)3–4 students share their timelines using time words.Share

    Differentiation: EAL students use pictures and arrows only. Gifted students: add 2 more events with dates.

    Success Criteria Check: Creates a personal timeline with at least 4 events in correct sequence using time words.

    WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: My Family History

    Lesson 1: Where Do I Come From?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explore (Investigation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Everyone Has a Beginning (10 min)"Before you were born, your parents were born. Before your grandparents were born, they were children. Everyone comes from a line of people stretching back into the past. Your family history is part of your story."Listen
    Family Tree Introduction (10 min)"A FAMILY TREE is a diagram that shows who is related to whom. At the bottom: YOU. Above: your parents. Above them: your grandparents. We are going to start learning about our family trees."Learn
    My Family Tree (15 min)Distribute simple family tree template. Students write: Me / My mum / My dad / My grandparents (4 boxes). If unknown, leave blank or ask a family member.Draw
    Share (5 min)"You don't have to share private details. But who would you like to ask about your family tree?"Think

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify at least 2 generations in their family (themselves and a parent, grandparent).

    Lesson 2: Family Stories

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Families Have Stories (10 min)"Every family has stories — things that happened that we tell and retell. Some families have stories about how they came to Australia. Some have stories about special places, special people, special events. These stories are part of history."Listen
    Story Types (10 min)"Family story types: HOW WE CAME HERE stories (migration, travel) / SPECIAL PEOPLE stories (someone who did something brave or kind) / SPECIAL PLACES stories (a place that matters to the family) / FUNNY STORIES (something that made everyone laugh)."Learn
    My Family Story (10 min)"Ask someone at home: What is one story our family tells? Who is it about? What happened? Share what you find out tomorrow."Take question home
    If No Story to Share (5 min)"If you don't have a special family story, think about: What story would you like to find out? Who could you ask?"Plan

    Success Criteria Check: Asks at least 1 question about family history to someone at home.

    Lesson 3: Cultural Celebrations

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Families Celebrate (10 min)"Families celebrate things that are important to them. Different families celebrate different things: religious holidays, cultural festivals, national days, family birthdays, community events. These celebrations have histories."Listen
    Celebrations in Our Class (15 min)"What celebrations do we have in our class? Christmas? Eid? Diwali? Hanukkah? Easter? Chinese New Year? NAIDOC Week? These are all part of Australian history because people from all these cultures have come to Australia."Discuss
    Celebration Research (10 min)Students research one celebration from their own family or another culture — what is it called? When does it happen? What do people do? Why is it important?Research
    Share (5 min)"Draw one celebration you know about and label what happens."Draw

    Note: Handle cultural diversity with respect. All celebrations are valid.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 1 cultural celebration and explain when and why it happens.

    WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Then and Now

    Lesson 1: How Was School Different?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Schools Have Changed (10 min)"Schools today look different from schools long ago. Long ago in Australia: some children went to one-room schoolhouses / some children didn't go to school at all / children wrote on slates (soft stone tablets) instead of paper / no computers, no iPads."Listen
    Compare Schools (15 min)Show photos: 1920s Australian classroom / 1950s classroom / today's classroom. "What is the same? What is different? Uniforms, desks, blackboards vs whiteboards, pencils vs computers."Compare
    Interview a Grown-Up (10 min)"Ask a grown-up (parent, grandparent, teacher) about what school was like when they were your age. Compare: What was the same? What was different?"Take question
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: How is school today different from school when your parent or grandparent was your age?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 differences between school today and school in the past.

    Lesson 2: How Was Play Different?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Play Has Changed (10 min)"Children have always played. But HOW they played has changed. Long ago: more outdoor play, less structured time, fewer toys, more home-made games. Today: more indoor play, more organised sport, more electronic games, more structured activities."Listen
    Traditional Games (15 min)Show images: marbles, hopscotch, elastics, stuck in the mud, cricket with improvised bats, skipping. "These games were played by Australian children 50–100 years ago and some are STILL played today! Why do you think they have lasted?"Discuss
    Play a Traditional Game (10 min)Teach and play hopscotch or 4-square. "This game has been played by children around the world for HUNDREDS of years."Play
    Write (5 min)"Draw or write one traditional game and explain how to play it."Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can play at least 1 traditional game and explain why some games last a long time.

    Lesson 3: How Was Home Different?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Homes Have Changed (10 min)"Homes long ago looked and worked differently. 50–100 years ago in Australia: fewer electrical appliances / no air conditioning / no internet / kettles on the stove / no mobile phones / more outdoor living in summer."Listen
    Then and Now: Home Life (15 min)Compare: washing clothes by hand vs washing machine / cooking on wood stove vs electric/gas oven / no air conditioning vs air conditioning / writing letters vs email / no TV vs smart TV.Compare
    Technology Changes Lives (10 min)"Technology has changed how we live at home. Which technology change do you think is the BIGGEST difference between your home life and your grandparents' home life?"Discuss
    Draw (5 min)"Draw one way your home is different from a home 50 years ago."Draw

    Differentiation: EAL students draw and label differences. Gifted students: interview a grandparent about their home life and report.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 differences between home life today and home life 50 years ago.

    WEEK 5 — EXPLAIN: Children in Australia's Past

    Lesson 1: Who Lived Here First?

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples (10 min)"Before 1788, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples lived all over Australia. They had been here for over 65,000 years — the oldest continuous culture on Earth. They had complex societies, languages, and ways of living that suited this land perfectly."Listen
    What Was Life Like? (15 min)"Before colonisation, Aboriginal peoples: lived sustainably on this land / had complex trade networks spanning Australia / had over 250 language groups / had seasonal calendars and deep knowledge of the land / told stories through oral tradition (speaking, not writing). Children learned from Elders, played games, and were part of their community."Learn
    Respectful Learning (10 min)"We need to learn about this history respectfully. One way to do this is to learn the Noongar words for the land we are on. Our school is on Noongar Boodjar (Noongar Country)."Learn
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What do you know about the history of Aboriginal peoples in Australia?"Write

    Note: This lesson should ideally be taught with support from local Aboriginal education resources or an Aboriginal Education Officer.

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 2 things about Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples' life before 1788.

    Lesson 2: Children in the Past

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Children in Australia's Past (10 min)"In the past, Australian children's lives were different depending on WHO THEY WERE. Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children had one life. Children of British colonists had another. Children from different cultural backgrounds had different experiences too."Listen
    Children's Lives in the 1950s (15 min)Show images from the 1950s: children playing in the street / one TV in the house / no computers / children walking to school alone / ABC Kids TV started in the 1950s / pavlova was invented (Aussie food story). "In the 1950s, Australia was very different from today."Observe
    Comparing Children's Lives (10 min)"Compare yourself to an Australian child from the 1950s: Do you go to school? (Yes) / Do you play outside? (Yes) / Do you watch TV? (Yes) / Can you name one big difference?"Compare
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: How is your life similar to or different from an Australian child from the 1950s?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can compare at least 1 similarity and 1 difference between their life and a child's life in the 1950s.

    Lesson 3: Comparing Our Lives

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Explain (Explicit Teaching)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Venn Diagram Introduction (10 min)"A Venn diagram is a way to show what is the SAME and what is DIFFERENT. Two circles that overlap. In the left: things about a child's life in the past. In the right: things about your life. In the middle: what is the SAME."Learn
    Guided Comparison (15 min)As a class, compare your life to a 1950s child: SAME (go to school, have toys, eat dinner with family) / DIFFERENT (computers, mobile phones, more diversity, different toys).Compare
    My Venn Diagram (10 min)Students draw a simple Venn diagram comparing their life to a 1950s child.Draw
    Share (5 min)3–4 students share one similarity and one difference.Share

    Differentiation: EAL students use picture-based Venn diagram. Gifted students: add a third circle for an Aboriginal child's life in the past.

    Success Criteria Check: Completes a Venn diagram with at least 1 similarity and 2 differences between their life and a 1950s child.

    WEEK 6 — ELABORATE: My Community's History

    Lesson 1: Places in Our Community Have History

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Our Community Has a Long Past (10 min)"Our community was here BEFORE US and will be here AFTER US. The buildings, streets, parks, and places we see every day have histories. Someone built them. Someone used them before us. They tell us about the past."Listen
    History in Our Streets (15 min)Show photos of the local area: old street photos from council archives or Trove vs current photos. "Look carefully. What is the same? What has changed? Was there a shop there 50 years ago that is different now?"Observe
    Our Community Map (10 min)Students draw a simple map of their neighbourhood with: my home, my school, 2 shops, 1 park. Label one thing they know about the history of each.Draw
    Exit Ticket (5 min)"Write: What is one question you have about the history of our local area?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can identify at least 2 places in their community that have a history.

    Lesson 2: Community Changes

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    How Do We Know Communities Change? (10 min)"We know communities change because: OLD PHOTOS show us what it used to look like / OLD PEOPLE tell us stories about the past / WRITTEN RECORDS (newspapers, council records) describe the past / ARCHAEOLOGY (digging up the past) reveals history."Learn
    Investigating Community Change (15 min)Show historical photos of the local area. Students identify at least 3 changes: a building that is new / a building that is gone / a change in the street / a change in what was sold.Investigate
    Why Do Communities Change? (10 min)"Communities change because: people build new things / old things break or get replaced / populations grow or shrink / new people move in / technology changes / economic conditions change. Change is normal."Learn
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What is one change that has happened in our community? Why do you think it changed?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can describe at least 1 change in their community and give a reason why it changed.

    Lesson 3: Heritage Places

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Elaborate (Application)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    What Is a Heritage Place? (10 min)"A HERITAGE PLACE is a place that is protected because it is important to our history. Heritage places tell us about the past. They are worth protecting for future generations."Learn
    Heritage Places Around Australia (15 min)Show: Old Melbourne Gaol (Victoria) / Fremantle Prison (WA) / Sydney Opera House (NSW) / The Royal Exhibition Building (Melbourne, UNESCO world heritage) / Aboriginal rock art sites. "These places are protected because they tell us about our history."Observe
    Heritage Places Near Us (10 min)"Are there any heritage places near us? Heritage Council of WA lists significant heritage places. Many towns have a heritage trail or historic buildings."Learn local
    Draw a Heritage Place (5 min)"Draw a heritage place from our local area (or from images shown) and label 1 thing that makes it important."Draw

    Differentiation: EAL students draw a heritage place with teacher support. Gifted students: research one specific heritage place and report.

    Success Criteria Check: Can define what a heritage place is and explain at least 1 reason why it should be protected.

    WEEK 7 — ELABORATE + EVALUATE: History Detectives

    Lesson 1: Being a History Detective

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    History Detectives Use Skills (10 min)"A HISTORY DETECTIVE uses SOURCES to find out about the past. History detective skills: OBSERVE (look carefully) / ASK QUESTIONS (wonder about the past) / INVESTIGATE (look for evidence) / COMPARE (look for what is the same and different) / STORYTELL (retell what you found out)."Learn
    Detective Case Study (15 min)"Let's be history detectives with an old object: an old kettle. OBSERVE: It is metal, has a handle, no electric cord. ASK: Was it used on a stove? By whom? WHEN was it used? INVESTIGATE: Compare to modern kettle. COMPARE: Same (boils water) / Different (no electric plug). STORYTELL: This kettle was used by a family who boiled water on a stove, probably 50+ years ago."Model
    Apply to Your Object (10 min)Students apply history detective skills to the object they brought for the Cold Task. Re-examine it with new eyes. What questions do they now have?Investigate
    Write (5 min)"Write 2 sentences: What is one new question you have about your history object after being a history detective?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Can apply at least 3 history detective skills (observe, ask questions, investigate, compare, storytell) to an object from the past.

    Lesson 2: Investigate an Object

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    History Detective Investigation (20 min)Students investigate their Cold Task object (or a classroom object): OBSERVE it carefully / ASK questions about who used it and when / INVESTIGATE what it is made of, how it works / COMPARE it to a modern equivalent / STORYTELL the object's story in 3–5 sentences. Teacher circulates.Investigate
    Present Your Object (10 min)Students share their object story with a partner. Partner uses feedback: "I learned that..." / "One question I have is..."Share
    Write Story (10 min)"Write your object's story: Who might have used it? When? What was it like? Why was it important?"Write

    Success Criteria Check: Completes a written investigation of an object from the past with at least 3 history detective skills applied.

    Lesson 3: Prepare Hot Task

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Preparation)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Hot Task Preview (10 min)"For tomorrow's Hot Task, you will share your family history story. You may: bring a photo from home / bring an object / draw your family / write about a family story / present the result of your family tree investigation."Preview
    Prepare Your Presentation (15 min)Students prepare their Hot Task. They choose their format: drawing and talking / object display / written story. They should practise: Who am I? Where do I come from? What is one special family story?Prepare
    Practice Share (10 min)3–4 students practise sharing with the class. Class gives feedback: "I learned that..." / "One question I have..."Practice
    Success Criteria Reminder (5 min)"Before tomorrow's Hot Task: Do you have a family story to share? Do you have something to draw or show? Can you use time words?"Reflect

    Differentiation: EAL students may prepare a visual presentation using drawings and photos. NEP students may prepare a verbal presentation.

    Success Criteria Check: Completes a Hot Task preparation plan showing at least 3 elements of their family history.

    WEEK 8 — EVALUATE: Hot Task and Celebration

    Lesson 1: Hot Task — My Family History Presentation

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Summative Assessment

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Set Up (5 min)"Today is your Hot Task. Share YOUR family history story. You may show an object, photo, drawing, or tell us a story. Tell us: Who are the people in your family? Where do you come from? What is one special family story or tradition? What is one way your life is similar to or different from a child's life 50 years ago?"Listen
    Presentations (35 min)Students present their family history. Teacher uses checklist: Family members named? / Family history described? / Use of time words? / Object or story presented?Present

    Assessment Note: Use rubric to assess. Compare to Cold Task for growth analysis.

    Lesson 2: Cold Task vs Hot Task — Growth Reflection

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Evaluate (Self-Assessment)

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    Compare (15 min)Place Cold Task next to Hot Task. "Use a green texta to mark 3 things you did BETTER in your Hot Task. Use a red texta to mark 1 thing you could improve."Compare
    Growth Statement (10 min)"I have grown as a history detective because..." Write 3–5 sentences.Write
    Share (10 min)Volunteers share one thing they know now that they didn't know at the start of the unit.Share
    What Surprised You? (5 min)"What surprised you most about history?"Share

    Success Criteria Check: Completes self-comparison showing growth in historical understanding.

    Lesson 3: Celebration — Our Histories

    Time: 40 minutes | Type: Celebration

    PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
    History Detectives Certificate (10 min)Award "History Detective" certificates.Receive
    Class Book: Our Histories (15 min)Each child contributes one page: "My Family History" — drawing and 2–3 sentences. Bound and placed in the class library.Contribute
    What We Learned (10 min)"What is one thing you learned about history that you will remember?"Share
    What's Next? (5 min)"What period of history would you like to learn more about?"Share

    DIFFERENTIATION NOTES

    EAL Learners

  • Home Language: Students may share family history in home language with translation support.
  • Object-Based: Focus on objects and drawings rather than extended writing.
  • Partner Support: EAL students paired for sharing activities.
  • Cultural Sensitivity: Respect all family structures, migration stories, and cultural practices.

    • Gifted Learners

  • Research Extension: Interview multiple family members about different time periods.
  • Creative Extension: Create a multi-generational family history poster.
  • Heritage Investigation: Research a specific heritage place in the local area.

    • Students with NEPs

  • Reduced Written Expectation: 2–3 sentences for written tasks.
  • Oral Option: All tasks may be presented verbally.
  • Extra Time: Additional time for all activities.
  • Word Banks: Provided for all written tasks.

    • ASSESSMENT NOTES
