Learning Intention

We are learning to investigate how objects and materials can be changed by heat, light, and physical forces, and to describe whether these changes can be reversed or not..

  • I can describe objects and materials using their properties (size, shape, texture, colour, hardness).
  • I can observe and describe changes caused by heat (melting, freezing, cooking).
  • I can observe and describe changes caused by light (shadows, reflections, fading).
  • I can investigate changes caused by physical forces (bending, twisting, stretching, breaking).
  • I can sort changes into REVERSIBLE (can go back) and NOT REVERSIBLE (cannot go back).
  • I can use science words to describe changes and explain my thinking.

Week 1 — ENGAGE: What Changes Around Us?

Lesson 1: Cold Task — Draw a Change

40 min Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Hook (5 min) Show two images: an ice cube and a puddle. "What happened? What changed?" Record: solid → liquid. "Something CHANGED this ice cube. What do you think it was?" Observe and guess
Task (30 min) Cold Task: "Draw something you have seen change. It could be: ice melting, bread turning into toast, a balloon being blown up, playdough being squashed, or something else. Draw WHAT CHANGED. Write or tell us WHAT happened and WHAT made it change." Draw, label, write/tell
Share (5 min) Select 3–4 students to briefly describe their drawings Share
DifferentiationEAL students draw and verbally explain to teacher. NEP students: draw one change with teacher scribing.

Lesson 2: The Tiny Seed — Changes Around Us

40 min Engage (Hook)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Read Aloud (15 min) Read *The Tiny Seed* by Eric Carle. Focus on changes: seed → sprout → plant (irreversible biological change). "The seed CHANGED into something different. Could it change back into a seed?" Listen and observe
Notice Changes (10 min) "What CHANGES did you see in the story?" List: seed planted in soil (hidden), water added, sun shines, seed cracks, root grows down, sprout pushes up, flower blooms. Each step = a change. Identify changes
Connect to Life (10 min) "Have YOU ever seen something change like the seed? A egg becoming a chicken? A caterpillar becoming a butterfly? A cake being baked?" Share personal observations
Record (5 min) Students draw one change from the story in their science books and label it. Draw and label

Lesson 3: Changes We Have Seen

40 min Engage (Prior Knowledge)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Changes Walk (15 min) Take students on a walk around the school. Look for evidence of changes: rust on gate, faded paint, cracked concrete, wet ground after rain, broken branch, peeled bark. Students note or draw 3 changes they see. Walk and record
Changes We Experience (10 min) "What changes happen to YOU every day?" List: hair grows, clothes get dirty/washed, shoes wear out, food is digested, skin gets cut/heals. Contribute to list
Sort Changes (10 min) "Let's sort these changes. Some changes happen because of HEAT. Some because of LIGHT. Some because of MOVEMENT or FORCE. Some because of TIME." Sort changes into categories
Exit Ticket (5 min) "Name one change you saw today. What caused it?" Written or verbal response
DifferentiationEAL students draw and label changes. Gifted students: research one change in detail — what caused it, what type of change is it?

Week 2 — EXPLORE: Heat Changes Things

Lesson 1: Ice Investigation

40 min Explore (Investigation)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Ice Observation (10 min) Each student or pair receives an ice cube on a plate. "Look at the ice. What does it LOOK like? What does it FEEL like? How does it SMELL?" (It shouldn't smell.) Describe in science books. Observe ice cube
Prediction Time (10 min) "What do you think will happen to the ice if we leave it on the plate?" Wait 20 minutes during other activities. "We will come back and check." Make predictions. Make predictions
What Changed? (15 min) Return to ice. "Look at your ice NOW. What changed? Is it the same size? Same shape? Same solid?" (It shrank, changed shape, became water.) Observe and record changes
Why Did It Change? (5 min) "What made the ice change? HEAT! Heat from the room made the ice melt into water. SOLID became LIQUID." Introduce: MELTING. Connect to heat

Lesson 2: Melting Butter

40 min Explore (Investigation)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Set Up (5 min) Show a block of butter. "What is this? What state is it in?" (Solid.) "What do you think will happen if we HEAT it?" Predict
Watch It Melt (20 min) Heat butter in a pan on low heat (teacher demo at front). Students observe and draw what they see at: START (solid butter), 2 MINUTES (soft, starting to melt), 5 MINUTES (liquid butter). Observe and draw stages
Record Observations (10 min) Students complete: "The butter changed from a SOLID to a LIQUID. This is called MELTING. The HEAT from the pan made it melt." Write observations
Connect (5 min) "Ice melts. Butter melts. What else melts? Chocolate? Candles? What do all these things have in common?" Think and share

Lesson 3: Cooking Changes — Seeds to Plants (Then to Food!)

40 min Elaborate (Application)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Seeds to Food (10 min) "The Tiny Seed grew into a sunflower. But what if we COOK the seed? Seeds change when we COOK them." Show images: raw rice vs cooked rice, raw egg vs fried egg, raw potato vs mashed potato. "These changes are NOT reversible. You can't un-cook an egg." Observe images
Before and After (15 min) Students draw: RAW egg → COOKED egg. Label: solid, liquid ( yolk), changed by HEAT. Draw: RAW potato → COOKED mashed potato. Draw before/after
Reversibility Question (10 min) "Can you UNCOOK the egg? Can you un-mash the potato? Can you un-cook the rice?" Vote: YES or NO. "You cannot undo cooking changes. Cooking is an IRREVERSIBLE change." Vote and discuss
Exit Ticket (5 min) "Name one thing that changes when you cook it. Can you undo the change?" Written response
DifferentiationEAL students draw before/after. Gifted students: research what happens at the molecular level when you cook an egg (denaturing proteins).

Week 3 — EXPLORE: Light Changes Things

Lesson 1: Shadows — Where Do They Go?

40 min Explore (Investigation)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Shadow Introduction (10 min) Turn off lights, use a torch to cast a shadow on the wall. "What is that dark shape? What made it?" Move the torch closer/farther, move the object. "The LIGHT made the SHADOW. The shadow is DARK because light couldn't pass through the object." Observe shadows
Shadow Investigation (15 min) Students use torches and objects (toy, hand, block) to create shadows on paper. Change the angle of light — what happens to the shadow? Change the distance — what happens? Record observations. Create and investigate shadows
Shadow Size Challenge (10 min) "Can you make the shadow TALL? Can you make it SHORT? What do you need to do with the light?" Test and record. Experiment with shadow size
Discuss (5 min) "What did you discover about shadows?" Share findings

Lesson 2: Reflections

40 min Explore (Investigation)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Mirror Time (10 min) Each student looks in a small mirror. "What do you see? Is that REALLY you?" (Left-right reversed.) "Your reflection is a REFLECTION — light BOUNCED off your face and into the mirror, then bounced back to your eyes." Look in mirrors
Reflective or Not? (15 min) Test different materials: mirror, white paper, dark paper, shiny foil, wooden spoon, plastic bag. "Does it reflect? Can you see yourself in it?" Sort materials into REFLECTIVE and NOT REFLECTIVE. Test materials
Reflection Drawing (10 min) Draw: an object and its reflection in a mirror. Label: LIGHT, MIRROR, REFLECTION, REFLECTIVE SURFACE. Draw reflection diagram
Exit Ticket (5 min) "What makes a reflection happen?" Written response

Lesson 3: Does Light Change Materials?

40 min Explore (Investigation)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Faded by Light (10 min) Show two images: a faded flag from 50 years ago vs a new one, a sun-bleached fence post. "What happened? The SUN and LIGHT changed the COLOUR over time. This is called FADING." Observe and discuss
Investigation Setup (15 min) Place pieces of coloured paper (red, blue, yellow, white) on the windowsill and cover half of each piece with cardboard. Leave for 1 week (or observe an existing faded object). Students draw predictions: "In one week, the covered part will be [COLOUR] and the exposed part will be [COLOUR]." Set up fading investigation
Discuss What We Know (10 min) "Light can change things over TIME. The longer something is in light, the more it can fade. What things have YOU seen get lighter or darker because of sunlight?" Discuss
Record (5 min) Students write: "LIGHT from the sun can FADE colours over time. This is a slow, irreversible change." Write conclusion
DifferentiationEAL students draw the investigation setup. Gifted students: research how UV light causes fading (photodegradation).

Week 4 — EXPLAIN: Physical Force Changes

Lesson 1: Bending and Twisting

40 min Explain (Explicit Teaching)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
What is Bending? (10 min) Demonstrate: a pipe cleaner bent into a heart shape, a ruler bent slightly. "When you BEND something, you change its SHAPE by applying force. Some things bend EASILY. Some things do NOT bend easily." Watch and observe
Will It Bend? Test (15 min) Students receive: pipe cleaner, paper clip, wooden stick, plastic straw, aluminium foil, rubber band. Test each: BENDS EASILY / DOES NOT BEND. Record in table. Test materials
What is Twisting? (10 min) Demonstrate twisting:毛巾 wrung out, rubber band twisted. "TWISTING means to turn in opposite directions. Some materials twist easily. What did you twist last week?" Watch demonstration
Twist Test (5 min) Students test the same materials for twistability: TWISTS EASILY / DOES NOT TWIST. Update table. Test and record

Lesson 2: Stretching and Elastic

40 min Explain (Explicit Teaching)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Stretch Introduction (10 min) Pull a rubber band slowly. "STRETCH means to pull something to make it LONGER and THINNER. When I let go, it goes back to its original shape! This is called ELASTIC." Watch stretching
Elastic vs Not Elastic (15 min) Test materials: rubber band, balloon, spring, plastic bag, wooden ruler, steel spoon, elastic bandage. STRETCH: goes back to shape (ELASTIC) or DOES NOT go back (NOT ELASTIC). Test and record
What Made It Stretch? (10 min) "We applied FORCE — we PULLED — and the material changed shape. When we let go, some things went back to normal. Some things STAYED stretched." Discuss
Design a Bungee Challenge (5 min) "Imagine you are designing a bungee cord for a jump. Would you use an elastic band or a piece of string? WHY?" Think and answer

Lesson 3: Breaking — Reversible or Not?

40 min Explain + Elaborate
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
What is Breaking? (10 min) "BREAKING means to split something into pieces. When you break a pencil, you cannot UN-break it. The change is NOT reversible." Demonstrate: break a popsicle stick, tear paper. Observe breaking
Reversibility Sort (15 min) Students receive picture cards. Sort into two groups: CAN GO BACK TO HOW IT WAS / CANNOT GO BACK. Items: ice cube (→ water, can freeze again ✓), egg (→ cooked, cannot uncook ✗), rubber band (→ stretched, goes back ✓), playdough (→ shaped, stays ✗), paper (→ torn, stays ✗), chocolate (→ melted, can re-solidify ✓). Sort cards
Write the Rule (10 min) "Some changes can be REVERSED — you can get the original thing back. Some changes CANNOT be reversed — the change STICKS. Once an egg is cooked, it stays cooked. Once paper is torn, it stays torn." Write the rule
Exit Ticket (5 min) "Give one example of a reversible change and one example of an irreversible change." Written response
DifferentiationEAL students sort cards physically and point to their sorted groups. Gifted students: predict which reversible changes require ADDING something (heat to melt, cold to freeze) vs removing something.

Week 5 — ELABORATE: Combining Changes

Lesson 1: Heat + Force Together

40 min Elaborate (Application)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Two Forces at Once (10 min) "Can TWO things change a material at the same time?" Heat + force = bread to toast (heat cooks it, force tears it when you bite). Metal in a forge (heat softens metal, force bends it into tools). Listen and observe
Investigation — Candle Changes (15 min) Light a birthday candle (teacher demo). "What changes do you see? HEAT from the flame melts the wax (reversible). The wick burns (irreversible — turns to ash)." Students draw the candle before and after being lit. Observe candle
Combination Change Sort (10 min) Show: bending a plastic spoon over a flame (heat + force), cooking an egg and then mashing it (irreversible + irreversible), freezing water and then breaking the ice (reversible + irreversible). Can you reverse ALL parts? Sort and discuss
Write (5 min) "Write about one change that uses TWO forces at once." Write description

Lesson 2: Changes in Our Kitchen

40 min Elaborate (Real-World Application)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Kitchen Changes Walk (10 min) Walk to the school kitchen area (or show images). Identify changes: bread toasting (heat), ice in drinks melting (heat), milk being poured (pouring — reversible if poured back), vegetables being cut (force — irreversible), water being boiled (heat — reversible when cooled). Walk and identify
Kitchen Change Cards (15 min) Students receive kitchen change cards: boiling water, cooling soup, slicing bread, whisking egg, freezing fruit, grilling cheese. For each: draw it, name the type of change (heat/force), is it reversible? Complete change cards
Share (10 min) "Which changes in the kitchen are reversible? Which are not?" Discuss: most cooking changes are irreversible. Freezing/thawing is reversible. Cutting is irreversible. Class discussion
Exit Ticket (5 min) "Name one kitchen change that involves heat. Is it reversible?" Written response

Lesson 3: Changes in Our Classroom

40 min Elaborate (Real-World Application)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Classroom Audit (15 min) Students walk the classroom and identify ALL the changes they can see: erasers worn down (rubbing — force, irreversible), paper crumpled (force, irreversible), whiteboard marker dried out (air/heat, irreversible), tape roll getting smaller (force of pulling, irreversible), books getting worn (force/time, irreversible). Walk and list
Reversible Classroom Changes (10 min) "Are there ANY reversible changes in the classroom?" Chair moved (position change — reversible), window opened (force — reversible), water bottle filled (pouring — reversible if poured out). Discuss reversible changes
Class Change Chart (10 min) Create class poster: REVERSIBLE CHANGES vs IRREVERSIBLE CHANGES in our classroom. Contribute to poster
Think (5 min) "Is the world around us mostly reversible or irreversible changes?" Think and share
DifferentiationEAL students draw classroom changes. Gifted students: research one irreversible change in detail — what is the evidence that it cannot be reversed?

Week 6 — ELABORATE: Reversible vs Irreversible

Lesson 1: Can We Go Back?

40 min Elaborate (Sorting and Justifying)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
The Big Sort (15 min) Students receive 12 change scenario cards. Sort into REVERSIBLE or IRREVERSIBLE. Justify each choice to a partner. Sort with justification
Challenge Round (10 min) Tricky cases: "You tear a piece of paper. Can you tape it back? (Yes, but the paper is still torn — the material change is irreversible, the object is fixed.)" "You melt chocolate and then let it set. Is it reversible?" (Yes, it goes back to solid form.) Discuss tricky cases
Reversibility Rules (10 min) "REVERSIBLE: melting/freezing, dissolving/evaporating, stretching/snap back. IRREVERSIBLE: cooking, burning, cutting, tearing, rusting." Write rules in science books. Write rules
Exit Ticket (5 min) "Is baking bread reversible or irreversible? How do you know?" Written response

Lesson 2: The Reversibility Test

40 min Elaborate (Practical Investigation)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Set Up Tests (10 min) "Today we will TEST reversibility. Can we actually get these materials back to their original state?" Test 1: ice cube → water → can we freeze it back? (Yes — if we put it in the freezer.) Test 2: paper → crumpled → can we un-crumple it? (Sort of — it looks different.) Test 3: balloon → blown up → can we get the air out? (Yes — let go of the opening.) Plan tests
Conduct Tests (20 min) Students conduct 3 tests and record: TEST — WHAT I DID — DID IT GO BACK TO EXACTLY THE SAME? Conduct tests
Conclusions (10 min) "Some things can go back to EXACTLY the same state. Some things look similar but are different. Some things cannot go back at all." Draw conclusions

Lesson 3: Changes That Stick

40 min Evaluate (Summary)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Changes We Learned About (15 min) Create a class concept map on the board: in the centre — CHANGES. Branches: HEAT changes (ice→water, butter→liquid, egg→cooked), LIGHT changes (shadows, reflections, fading), FORCE changes (bending, twisting, stretching, breaking). Each branch: REVERSIBLE or IRREVERSIBLE. Contribute to concept map
Apply to the World (10 min) "Think about the world. Why is it important that SOME changes are irreversible? (Rusting can tell us how old something is. Torn paper shows history.) Why is it important that SOME changes ARE reversible?" (We can fix things, recycle, reuse.) Discuss
Draw Your Learning (10 min) Draw a picture showing one reversible and one irreversible change. Label each. Draw and label
Exit Ticket (5 min) "Name one reversible change and one irreversible change you might see this weekend." Written response
DifferentiationEAL students draw and label with teacher support. Gifted students: create a comic strip showing a character who learns about reversible vs irreversible changes.

------- ---------------- ---------

Vocabulary Review (10 min) Matching: MELTING / FREEZING / REFLECTION / FADING / ELASTIC / IRREVERSIBLE. Match word to definition. Vocabulary matching

Written Reflection (15 min) Write: "I used to think... Now I know... The most surprising thing I learned was..." Write reflection

Lesson 3: Hot Task Preparation

40 min Evaluate (Preparation)
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Introduce Hot Task (10 min) Display: "HOT TASK: Investigate a material change. Choose one material (e.g., ice, butter, rubber band, paper). Apply heat, light, or force. Observe what happens. Decide: is this change REVERSIBLE or IRREVERSIBLE? Explain your answer." Model: "If I put chocolate in the sun, it melts. I can put it in the fridge and it solidifies again. This is REVERSIBLE." Understand task
Plan Your Investigation (15 min) Students choose their material and plan: What will I change? How will I change it? What do I think will happen? Do I think it will be reversible? Create investigation plan
Success Criteria Reminder (5 min) "Check: did you name the type of change? Did you describe what happened? Did you explain whether it was reversible or irreversible?" Self-check criteria
Prepare Materials (10 min) Students gather materials and prepare their workspace for Hot Task tomorrow. Prepare
DifferentiationEAL students use sentence starters and word bank. NEP students: use plan template with teacher support.

Week 8 — HOT TASK

Lesson 1: Hot Task — Investigate a Change

40 min Summative Assessment
PhaseTeacher (I Do)Students
------- ---------------- ---------
Set Up (5 min) "Today you investigate a material change independently. Choose your material, follow your plan, observe carefully, and explain what happened. You have 30 minutes." Receive instructions
Investigation (30 min) Students conduct their investigation: choose material, apply change (heat/light/force), observe, record, decide reversibility. Teacher circulates and observes. Independent investigation
Clean Up (5 min) Students clean up materials. Any wet materials placed on drying tray. Clean workspace
DifferentiationEAL students may investigate verbally with teacher scribing. NEP students may use pre-selected materials and templates.
