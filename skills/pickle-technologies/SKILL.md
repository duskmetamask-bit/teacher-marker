# pickle-technologies — AC9 Technologies F-6 Knowledge Base

## Purpose

This skill provides the definitive Technologies teaching knowledge base for Australian F–6 teachers using PickleNickAI. It covers the Australian Curriculum v9.0 (AC9) Technologies strand, practical classroom delivery, assessment strategies, and AITSL alignment.

**Trigger:** When a teacher asks about, plans, or delivers Technologies curriculum content — design and technologies, digital technologies, makerspaces, coding, food tech, assessment, or anything in the AC9 Technologies scope for F–6.

## 1. AC9 Technologies Structure

### Two Strands

| Strand | Code | Focus |
|--------|------|-------|
| **Knowledge and Practices** | AC9T[K]## — AC9T[6]## | Understanding technologies, materials, digital systems, ICT practices |
| **Creating Solutions** | AC9T[C]## | Applying knowledge to design and produce solutions |

The **Creating Solutions** strand is where students do the core work: investigate, design, produce, evaluate, and manage projects.

### Code Format

```
AC9T[year][K|C][number]
```

- `year` = the first year of the band (F=0, 1, 3, 5)
- `K` = Knowledge and Practices strand
- `C` = Creating Solutions strand
- `number` = content descriptor sequence number

**Examples:**
- `AC9TDE2K01` — Design and Technologies, F–2 band, Knowledge and Practices, #01
- `AC9TD45C02` — Design and Technologies, 3–4 band, Creating Solutions, #02
- `AC9T56K03` — Digital Technologies, 5–6 band, Knowledge and Practices, #03

### Band Structure

Years are grouped in bands, not single years:

| Band | Years |
|------|-------|
| Foundation (F) | Kindergarten (age ~5) |
| Band 1 | Years 1–2 |
| Band 2 | Years 3–4 |
| Band 3 | Years 5–6 |

## 2. Two Main Areas

### Design and Technologies

**Core focus:** Designing and making products, systems, and environments to meet needs and solve problems.

**Key concepts:**
- **Materials and technologies specialisations** — working with specific materials (textiles, timbers, metals, polymers, food) and understanding their properties and uses
- **Design processes** — investigating existing solutions, designing to meet needs, producing prototypes, evaluating against design criteria
- **Food and fibre production** — agricultural and food technology, sustainable production, food origins
- **Engineering principles** — mechanical, structural, and civil engineering concepts appropriate to primary level (levers, pulleys, structures, mechanisms)

**Technologies specialisations (taught across F–6):**
- Food and fibre (agriculture, cooking, nutrition)
- Materials (timber, metal, textiles, polymers/plastics)
- Engineering (mechanisms, structures)
- Digital systems (sometimes grouped here)

### Digital Technologies

**Core focus:** Understanding digital systems, computational thinking, data, and responsible technology use.

**Key concepts:**
- **Computational thinking** — breaking problems into steps (decomposition), recognising patterns (pattern recognition), simplifying complexity (abstraction), creating step-by-step instructions (algorithms)
- **Algorithms** — clear sequential instructions. At F–2: recipes, daily routines. At 3–4: flowcharts, basic branching. At 5–6: structured algorithms with variables and iteration
- **Branching** — "if this happens, do that" thinking. Simple at F–2 (choice cards), explicit conditions at 3–6 (if/then/else)
- **Iteration** — repeating steps. F–2: loops in stories/routines. 3–4: simple repeat loops. 5–6: for/while loops
- **Data representation** — binary basics (on/off, 1/0), how computers store text/images numbers, patterns in data
- **Digital systems** — hardware components (input, processing, output, storage), networks, the internet, sensors and actuators
- **ICT core** — digital citizenship, online safety, privacy, responsible use, ethical considerations, creative commons/copyright

## 3. Year Level Bands — What to Teach

### Foundation to Year 2 (F–2)

**Design and Technologies:**
- Explore and sort materials by properties (texture, flexibility, hardness, weight)
- Use simple tools (scissors, hole punches, joining techniques — tape, glue, staplers, string)
- Follow design processes with scaffolded support
- Food from gardens, cooking basics, where food comes from
- Simple mechanisms: rolling, sliding, levers

**Digital Technologies:**
- Play with floor robots (Bee-Bot, Blue-Bot) — direction, sequence
- Create pictures/drawings digitally
- Recognise that computers follow instructions
- Being safe online — stranger danger, privacy, kind online behaviour
- Digital systems basics: mouse/keyboard/screen, input/output的概念

### Years 3–4 (Band 2)

**Design and Technologies:**
- Investigate how existing products are made and why materials are chosen
- Apply simple design process: investigate → design → produce → evaluate
- Select appropriate tools and materials for purpose
- Simple systems thinking: inputs → processes → outputs
- Food and fibre production: sustainable practices, farm-to-table

**Digital Technologies:**
- Unplugged algorithms: create instructions for peers to follow (human robots)
- Branching: flowcharts with decision points ("if the light is red, stop")
- Iteration: repeat loops in simple sequences
- Data in tables: collect, organise, display, interpret simple data
- Digital systems: input/process/output/storage, how devices connect
- Online safety: stronger privacy awareness, cyberbullying, reliable sources

### Years 5–6 (Band 3)

**Design and Technologies:**
- Complex design processes with minimal scaffolding
- Consider user needs, ergonomics, aesthetics, functionality, sustainability
- Specialist tools and techniques (measuring, cutting, joining, finishing)
- Systems thinking: how components work together in a product
- Engineering principles: mechanisms (gears, pulleys, levers), structural integrity
- Food technology: nutrition, food safety, preparation techniques, cultural significance

**Digital Technologies:**
- Computational thinking: decomposition, pattern recognition, abstraction, algorithm design
- Structured algorithms: variables, operators, conditionals, iteration (for/while)
- Data representation: binary number system, character encoding (ASCII basics), image encoding
- Digital systems: networks (LAN basics), internet structure, cloud vs local storage, sensors
- Block-based coding: Scratch, Code.org, Blockly — creating interactive games, stories, simulations
- Digital citizenship: intellectual property, creative commons, ethical online behaviour, digital footprints

## 4. Practical Classroom Approaches

### Design Thinking for Primary

Adapted from the Double Diamond and other frameworks:

**The Process (simplified for primary):**
1. **Empathise** — Who is the user? What do they need?
2. **Define** — What problem are we solving? Write a design brief
3. **Ideate** — Generate many ideas (brainstorm, sketches)
4. **Prototype** — Build a simple version (low-fidelity — cardboard, tape)
5. **Test** — Try it with users, get feedback, improve

**Design Briefs for Students (F–2):**
- Simple one-page briefs with pictures: "We are going to make a… that can…"
- Success criteria as visual icons
- Example: "Design a container that can carry 10 blocks across the room"

**Design Briefs for Students (3–6):**
- Fuller briefs: context, user, constraints, success criteria
- Example: "Design a planter for our kitchen garden that is easy to water, fits on the windowsill, and is made from recycled materials"

### Unplugged Computing (F–2)

**Human Robot Game:**
- One student is the "robot", others give one-step instructions
- Practice sequence: "move 3 steps forward, turn right, pick up the cup"
- Reinforces that computers do exactly what we tell them

**Bee-Bot/Floor Robot Activities:**
- Program sequences of forward/back/left/right
- Map a journey (from X to Y on a grid)
- Debugging: "It went wrong here — what went wrong?"
- Progression: from memorized sequences → planning on paper → actual programming

**Pattern Recognition Games:**
- Pattern ABABAB — what comes next?
- Patterns in numbers: 2, 4, 6, 8, ___
- Patterns in shapes: circle, square, triangle, circle, square, ___
- Connecting patterns to algorithms: "the pattern tells us what to repeat"

### Block-Based Coding (3–6)

**Scratch / ScratchJr:**
- Create interactive stories, games, simulations
- Key skills: sprites, costumes, backdrops, events, loops, conditionals, variables
- Link to cross-curricular content (maths patterns, science simulations, storytelling)
- Share via scratch.mit.edu

**Code.org Courses:**
- Courses A-F for ages 4–14
- Structured lessons with unplugged activities
- Game-based progression keeps students engaged

**Blockly:**
- Google-developed visual programming
- Similar to Scratch but browser-based, good for puzzles

**Typical Progression:**
1. Sequential instructions (move, turn, repeat)
2. Adding blocks (loops, events)
3. Conditionals (if touching, if score > 10)
4. Variables (scoring, counting)
5. Creating original projects from scratch

### Digital Citizenship

**Staying Safe Online (F–2):**
- Never share personal information online
- Stranger danger: people online aren't always who they say they are
- Speak to a trusted adult if something feels wrong
- Keywords: S.A.F.E. — Stop, Ask, Find help, Eject/Exit

**Privacy and Passwords (3–4):**
- Why passwords matter, what makes a strong password
- Don't share passwords with friends
- What is personal information? (name, address, school, phone)
- Cookies and why websites remember us

**Cyberbullying Awareness (5–6):**
- Recognising bullying behaviour online
- Bystander empowerment: how to help
- Reporting mechanisms and school policies
- Digital footprint: everything online is permanent

**Creative Commons and Copyright:**
- Creative Commons licensing (CC BY, CC BY-NC, etc.)
- Why crediting creators matters
- Using licensed images/sounds correctly
- Plagiarism vs inspiration vs theft

## 5. Makerspace on a Budget

Makerspaces don't need to be expensive. The goal is hands-on making, prototyping, and iterative design.

### Low-Cost Materials (the essentials)

| Category | Items |
|----------|-------|
| **Construction** | Cardboard boxes, cardboard tubes, egg cartons, craft sticks (popsicle sticks), straws, pipe cleaners |
| **Fastening** | Sticky tape, masking tape, duct tape, hot glue guns, glue sticks, elastic bands, twist ties, string, wool |
| **Cutting** | Child-safe scissors, paper cutters, craft knives (with supervision) |
| **Measurement** | Rulers, tape measures, kitchen scales |
| **Recycled** | Plastic bottles, tin cans, bottle caps, old CDs, cardboard, newspaper, old fabric |
| **Finishing** | Paints (acrylic, texta), markers, pencils, paper |

### Robotics on a Budget

**Bee-Bot / Blue-Bot (~$90–130 each):**
- Best investment for F–2, good for 3–4
- Visual, physical, no screen needed
- Large range of mats available

**Makey Makey (~$55):**
- Turn any conductive material into a keyboard input
- Connect to Scratch for custom projects
- Great for interdisciplinary projects

**LittleBits (more expensive, ~$100+):**
- Magnetic electronic building blocks
- Good for circuits and engineering concepts
- Worth having if budget allows

**Scratch + micro:bit (~$30 each):**
- Physical computing with visual programming
-教育部 Micro:bit is Australian-Approved for schools

### Makerspace Setup Tips

- **Start with storage** — clear labelling with photos/labels so students self-manage
- **Zones** — separate construction, computing, craft, and discussion areas
- **Borrow from the community** — ask families for recycled materials
- **No 3D printers needed** — cardboard prototypes work fine
- **Digital fabrication** — if you do have a 3D printer or laser cutter, great; if not, hand-cutting is fine
- **Teacher-led sessions initially** — establish norms before releasing to open-ended making

## 6. Food Technology (WA Context)

Food Technology connects to Design and Technologies (food and fibre specialisation) and has strong links to HASS, Health, and numeracy.

### Kitchen Gardens

**Stephanie Alexander Kitchen Garden Program:**
- Schools sign up for the program which provides resources, curriculum links, and professional development
- Focus: growing, harvesting, preparing, and eating fresh food
- Social, environmental, and health outcomes
- Website: kitchen-garden.org.au

**Practical classroom connections:**
- Plant seasonal vegetables (WA climate zones — Mediterranean, temperate, semi-arid)
- Harvest and cook in class or school kitchen
- Track growth: height, leaf count, days to harvest (maths link)
- Record observations in process journals

### Food Safety Basics

**F–2:**
- Wash hands before handling food
- Clean surfaces before preparing food
- Don't taste if hands are dirty
- Simple rules with visuals

**3–4:**
- Cross-contamination prevention (separate raw and cooked, different cutting boards)
- Refrigeration and room temperature safe zones (Danger Zone: 5–60°C)
- Allergens and why they matter (nut allergies, celiac disease)

**5–6:**
- Food safety temperature control ( refrigeration <5°C, cooking >75°C)
- HACCP-inspired principles (hazard analysis) in age-appropriate ways
- Food labelling (use-by vs best-before dates)

### Measuring and Sequencing

**Maths links:**
- Volume and capacity (ml, L) when cooking
- Mass (g, kg) when weighing ingredients
- Fractions (½, ¼, ⅓) — very concrete in cooking
- Time (minutes, reading analog clocks for cooking times)
- Temperature (°C for oven settings)

**Sequencing skills:**
- Following a recipe is an algorithm
- Steps must be in order — introduce debugging: "what if you missed step 3?"
- Students write their own recipes for simple dishes (healthy snacks)

### Cultural Foods

**WA context:**
- Indigenous Australian foods: wattle seed, native thyme, kangaroo meat, Indigenous food practices
- Cultural diversity in Perth/WA: Italian, Greek, Chinese, Vietnamese, Indian, African communities — ask students about their family food cultures
- Celebrations: Christmas puddings, Eid sweets, Diwali sweets, Chinese New Year dumplings
- Food as culture: discuss how food connects to identity and tradition

## 7. Assessment in Technologies

### Process Journals

The most important artifact in Design and Technologies. Students document their entire design process.

**What to include (with examples):**

| Stage | F–2 evidence | 3–6 evidence |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| **Empathise/Investigate** | Photos of inspiration, drawings of things I liked | Research notes, pictures of existing products, what materials are used and why |
| **Define/Design** | My idea drawing with labels, who it's for | Design brief, annotated sketches, CAD drawings, material selection reasoning |
| **Ideate** | Multiple quick drawings (thumbnail sketches) | Multiple solutions considered, pros/cons table |
| **Prototype** | Photos of building process, what I tried that didn't work | Build photos, materials list, construction notes |
| **Test/Evaluate** | Did it work? What would I change next time? | Testing against success criteria, data from testing, revision plan |

**Assessment tip:** Process journals are formative — they're about reflecting and improving, not about getting it "right" first time. Celebrate failed prototypes.

### Product Testing

**Structured testing:**
- Define success criteria before testing
- Test in controlled conditions (same drop height, same weight, same user)
- Record results: what worked, what broke, what surprised you
- Iterate based on test results

**Example (Year 5 bridge challenge):**
- Success criteria: holds 500g weight, spans 30cm, uses under 50g of materials
- Test 1: holds 400g — what failed? Reinforce the load-bearing areas
- Test 2: holds 550g — success! Document what changed

### Peer Evaluation

**Simple peer feedback protocols:**
- Two stars and a wish (two things that worked well, one improvement idea)
- Critique protocols (say something, wonder something, suggest something)
- Gallery walks (students display work, peers rotate and leave sticky note feedback)

### Rubrics for Design and Digital Projects

**Design and Technologies Rubric (generic, adaptable):**

| Criterion | 1 (Beginning) | 2 (Developing) | 3 (Achieving) | 4 (Excelling) |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|---------------|---------------|
| Design process | Needs adult help to follow process | Follows process with scaffolding | Independently follows design process | Fully autonomous design process with iteration |
| Materials selection | Uses random materials | Selects materials based on one property | Justifies material choice based on multiple properties | Evaluates and adapts materials based on testing |
| Technical skills | Struggles with basic tools | Uses tools with some accuracy | Competent tool use with good finish | Expert technique, precise, clean finish |
| Evaluation | Cannot evaluate own work | Evaluates with prompting | Evaluates against criteria | Evaluates, proposes and implements improvements |

**Digital Technologies Rubric (generic, adaptable):**

| Criterion | 1 (Beginning) | 2 (Developing) | 3 (Achieving) | 4 (Excelling) |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|---------------|---------------|
| Algorithm design | Needs step-by-step direction for every action | Creates sequential algorithm with adult support | Designs clear algorithm with appropriate structure | Designs efficient algorithm with optimisation |
| Coding | Code does not run or major logic errors | Code runs with some errors | Code runs correctly, readable | Code runs efficiently, well-documented, extensible |
| Computational thinking | Cannot decompose simple problems | Decomposes with scaffolding | Independently decomposes problems | Deep decomposition with abstraction layers |
| Digital citizenship | Needs reminders about responsible use | Understands principles with prompting | Consistently demonstrates responsible use | Advocates for digital citizenship among peers |

## 8. AITSL Alignment

### Standard 4.2 — Use of Digital Technologies

> "Use of digital technologies is integral to teachers' professional practice and to broaden students' competencies and kreativity in ICT."

**What this looks like in practice:**

| Proficient teacher... | Technologies context |
|----------------------|---------------------|
| Uses digital technologies to design教学 resources | Creates interactive Scratch projects, uses design software |
| Integrates ICT into lesson sequences | Digital technologies as a tool within a unit, not an add-on |
| Broadens students' ICT competencias | Teaching coding, digital systems, computational thinking |
| Uses Technologies curriculum to enhance learning | Students use Technologies to demonstrate understanding across KLAs |

**Demonstrating this standard:**
- Incorporate digital technologies explicitly into unit plans
- Show evidence of students using digital tools to create solutions (not just consume)
- Use digital technologies to collect and analyse student assessment data
- Model responsible and ethical use of technology

### Standard 2.6 — ICT Knowledge

> "Teachers have knowledge of ICT content and pedagogies and how to integrate them into the curriculum."

**What this looks like in practice:**

| Proficient teacher... | Technologies context |
|----------------------|---------------------|
| Knows current ICT tools | Can use Scratch, Code.org, basic CAD, presentation tools |
| Understands how ICT can enhance learning | Can plan authentic technology integration (not tech-for-tech's-sake) |
| Anticipates student misconceptions about technology | Students think computers are "smart" — address what intelligence actually means |
| Uses ICT safely and legally | Models creative commons, correct attribution, privacy practices |

**Demonstrating this standard:**
- Can explain how a computer follows an algorithm (not as magic)
- Understands the difference between application software, system software, and hardware
- Knows how to introduce coding concepts progressively across year levels
- Uses digital systems to differentiate and personalise learning
