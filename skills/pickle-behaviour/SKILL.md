# pickle-behaviour — Classroom Management & Behaviour Support

**Purpose:** Equip F–6 Australian teachers with practical, evidence-aligned behaviour management strategies — from proactive prevention through to restorative follow-up.

**Trigger:** Teacher needs help with classroom management, student behaviour, escalation, de-escalation, or trauma-informed practice.

---

## 1. Core Classroom Management Principles

### The Foundation Mindset
- **Proactive > Reactive.** Set students up to succeed before problems occur. A well-prepared lesson is the best behaviour management tool.
- **Relationship is everything.** Connected students are cooperative students. Invest in the relationship first.
- **Consistency beats intensity.** One calm, predictable response every day beats dramatic interventions sporadically. Everyone in the school applies the same rules the same way.
- **High expectations + high support.** You can demand a lot AND give students what they need to meet it. Don't lower expectations — increase support.
- **Calm = control.** Your mindset shapes the room. If you are calm, confident, and centered, students feel it. If you are anxious or reactive, it spreads.

### Teacher Self-Check
Ask yourself before responding to any behaviour:
1. Is this about the learning (engagement) or the relationship (connection)?
2. Am I reacting from frustration, or responding from strategy?
3. Is my body language projecting calm confidence?

## 2. Prevention Strategies (Proactive)

### Routines — Day One, Every Day
Establish and rehearse routines explicitly, especially in the first weeks:

| Routine | How to teach it |
|---------|----------------|
| Entry to room | Where do they sit? What do they do with their bag? When is the starter task on screen? |
| Transitions | Signal (visual or auditory cue), expectation, rehearsal |
| Exit / pack up | Who packs first? Who dismisses? Materials back where? |
| Material distribution | Don't be the bottleneck — pre-sort, use student helpers, spatial distribution |

Practice the routine, then practice it again. Then praise students for doing it well. It takes repetition — budget for it.

### Visual Schedules & Timetables
- Display the lesson sequence visually (especially for younger students and those who need predictability)
- Use Now/Next boards for individual students who struggle with transitions
- Consistent visual cues reduce anxiety and off-task behaviour

### Engaged Students = Fewer Issues
- High-quality instruction is behaviour management. If the lesson is boring, predictable, or above/below their level — behaviour problems follow.
- Use varied modalities: kinaesthetic, visual, verbal, collaborative
- Build in movement, collaboration, and novelty
- Check for understanding before moving on — don't lose them in the first 5 minutes

### Zones of Proximity
- Move around the room — don't stay at the front or at your desk
- Proximity to off-task students is often enough to redirect without a word
- Position yourself between the student and the distraction, not between the student and their support

### Environmental Design
- Seating: arrange for learning (visibility, collaboration), not isolation as punishment
- Clear sightlines: you should be able to see every student without strain
- Resources pre-prepared: downtime = behaviour problems
- Calm corner: a sensory/regulation space, NOT a punishment corner. Students choose to go there or are invited by the teacher.

### Positive Start to the Lesson
- Hook first: engage before you instruct
- Clear learning intention: "Today we're learning to..." so students know where they're heading
- Enthusiasm is contagious — if you're flat, the room will be flat

## 3. Behaviour Management Toolkit

### Verbal Cues (Use These)

| Instead of... | Say this... | Why |
|--------------|-------------|-----|
| "Stop talking" | "Eyes on me, please" | Tells them what TO do, not what to stop |
| "Be quiet" | "Hands up if you can tell me one thing we learned yesterday" | Positive, builds anticipation |
| "Sit down" | "Here's where your seat is — I need you there now, thanks" | Direct, respectful, no power contest |
| "Why are you doing that?" | "Show me you're ready to learn — what's your first step?" | Redirects to action, not interrogation |
| "That's not okay" | "That choice doesn't match our class agreement — what's the right choice?" | References agreed expectations, not you vs them |

**Countdown:** "Okay, I need eyes on me in 3... 2... 1..." — works well, stay calm and still during the countdown.

### Non-Verbal Strategies

- **Proximity:** Stand near the student who is struggling — often enough
- **Stillness:** Stand still and wait. Don't fill the silence. The gap does the work.
- **Eye contact:** Scan the room. Hold eye contact with off-task students briefly — a private signal.
- **Movement:** Circulate. Students are less likely to drift off when you are moving.
- **Hand signals:** Establish a silent signal for "stop and listen" — practice it explicitly on day one. A raised hand works, or a specific chime/chord on a keyboard.

### Physical Environment

- Seating arrangements support learning — not punitive isolation
- Students who need to move should be near the door/breakout space (not isolated from the group)
- Resources accessible without teacher involvement (labeled, spatial)
- Calm corner: available to any student who needs regulation support. Invited, not sent. "Would you like to go to the calm corner to help your body settle?"

## 4. Responding to Low-Level Disruption

### Ignore + Reinforce
- Low-level behaviour (whispering, slight off-task) that is NOT spreading → ignore it and reinforce the students doing the right thing
- "I can see Mia and Jordan are already on the starter — excellent, well done."
- Move near the off-task student silently — proximity often resets without a word.

**Rule:** If it's not affecting others and it's low-level, don't make it a big deal. Escalating minor behaviour makes it bigger.

### Redirect (Private, Never Public)
When you need to speak to a student:

1. **Go to them** — don't call them to you across the room (public humiliation)
2. **Kneel/sit to their level** — if possible, get on their eye line
3. **Calm, brief:** "I can see something's going on. Can you tell me?" or "What do you need to be ready to learn?"
4. **Offer a way out:** "You need to finish two more questions before you can ask for help — come find me when you're done"

**Never:** lecture, moralize, or reprimand publicly. It puts students on the spot, embarrasses them, and escalates defiance.

### Reset the Class
If the whole class is off-task or escalating:

1. Stop. "Okay, let's stop for a moment."
2. Reset. "We're going to try that again — fresh start."
3. Refocus on learning intention. "Our goal for this section was X — let's give it everything."
4. Give a clear, simple next step. "Open to page 34, I'll be checking who has their book open in the next 30 seconds."

Keep it brief. Don't lecture. Move on.

## 5. Escalation Pathway

Use this progression. Move through steps as needed — don't skip steps unless safety requires it.

| Step | Action | Example Language |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| **1** | Proximity + private redirect | Move near, make eye contact, quiet word |
| **2** | Clear verbal direction | "I need you to put the phone away because it is not allowed at school. Do that now, please." |
| **3** | Warning with specific consequence | "If you continue using your phone, I will need to collect it and hand it to the front office. You will be able to collect it at the end of the day." |
| **4** | Logical consequence applied | Collect phone, complete incident note, contact parent |
| **5** | Remove from situation | "You need some time to settle — come with me to [specific space], we'll sort this out when you're ready." (never send to office alone) |
| **6** | Follow-up repair conversation | **Mandatory** — never skip this. Once the student is regulated: "What happened? What were you feeling? How can we do it differently next time?" |

**Critical:** After any escalation, the repair conversation MUST happen. Even if it's end of day and you have to find them — it must occur before the next time you see them.

## 6. Challenging Behaviour (Complex)

### When Behaviour is Communication
Before you react, ask: **"What is this behaviour telling me?"**

- Unmet need (hungry, tired, overwhelmed, undiagnosed disability)
- Trauma response (amygdala hijack — they literally cannot access the thinking part of their brain in that moment)
- Escape (avoiding work that's too hard or too boring)
- Attention (positive attention vacuum often filled with negative attention)
- Sensory dysregulation

Behaviour that persists rarely responds to punishment alone — it needs understanding and a different response.

### Document Patterns
For students with ongoing challenging behaviour, keep a brief log:
- When (time, context)
- What happened (specific behaviour)
- What you tried (response)
- Outcome (what worked / didn't)

This data is essential if you need to refer to support staff or apply for additional resources.

### Trauma-Informed Responses

Recognize the windows of tolerance:
- **Fight:** Aggression, arguing, defying
- **Flight:** Running, hiding, refusal to engage
- **Freeze:** Shutdown, dissociation, not responding

When a student is dysregulated:
- **Do NOT crowd them.** Give physical space.
- **Lower demands.** "You don't have to do this right now. You can just sit here."
- **Reduce stimulation.** Dim lights, quiet voice, fewer people.
- **Use safe sensory options.** Fidget tools, a weighted item, somewhere to stand instead of sit.
- **Never lecture a dysregulated student.** They cannot process it.

### Restore Connection Before Consequences
- The relationship is the work. After any escalation, repair must come before any punishment or consequence can land properly.
- "We're okay. I still believe in you. But we need to fix what happened."

### When to Involve Support

| Level | Who | When |
|-------|-----|------|
| 1 | Class teacher | First instance, low-level |
| 2 | Head of Student Services (HOS) | Patterns, no improvement, student wellbeing concern |
| 3 | School Psychologist | Complex trauma, mental health, significant disability |
| 4 | Administration | Safety risk, violence, threats, mandatory reporting trigger |

**Document before you refer.** Screenshots, incident notes, dates, what was attempted. "I tried X, Y, Z" is more useful than "they're always like this."

**Mandatory reporting:** If you have reason to believe a child is being abused or neglected — report to the Department of Communities (Child Protection and Family Support). This is a legal obligation, not a behaviour management decision.

## 7. Restorative Practices

Restorative practice shifts the conversation from "What did you do wrong?" to "What happened, and what do we need to do to fix it?"

### The Four Questions

1. **"What happened?"** — Not blame. Get the full picture from all sides. "I want to understand what happened from your point of view."
2. **"Who was affected?"** — Build empathy. "When you did that, how do you think [person] felt?"
3. **"What do you need to do to make it right?"** — Focus on repair, not punishment. "What would make this better?"
4. **"How can I support you next time?"** — Collaborative problem-solving. "What do you need from me to make next time different?"

### Restorative Conversation Script (Post-Incident)
> "Hey, I wanted to catch up about what happened in the room earlier. I'm not here to get you in trouble — I just want to understand and make sure we're good going forward. Can you tell me what was going on for you?"

> "Okay, I hear you. And I want you to understand — when that happened, [person] felt [specific impact]. How do you think we could fix that?"

> "Great. So what's the plan if something like that comes up again and you're feeling [same trigger]?"

### Follow-Up is Mandatory
After any serious incident or escalation, you must have a repair conversation before the next time you see that student. Don't skip it. The relationship depends on it.

## 8. Reward Systems — What Works

### Effective Rewards

- **Verbal praise — specific:** "I noticed the way you helped Sam with that problem — that shows real leadership." Specific > generic.
- **Class rewards — shared experience:** "If the class gets an 85% average on the quiz, we'll have extra recess on Friday." Shared goal, shared celebration.
- **Showcase work:** "That piece is going on our wall of excellence — let's put it there now." Status and recognition.
- **Responsibility privileges:** Class librarian, tech helper, line leader — these motivate more than stickers.
- **Positive contact home:** "I'm going to call your parent to tell them about the effort you put in today." Parents love this. Students love this. Use it.

### Use With Caution or Avoid

- **Individual reward charts with points/stars:** Can create anxiety, dependency, and resentment. If you use them, they must be low-key and private — not public leaderboards.
- **Public comparison:** "Only three people finished — the rest didn't even try." Destroys motivation and relationship.
- **Taking away lunch/play:** Not related to the behaviour, doesn't teach the skill, often escalates. Use logical consequences instead.

## 9. WA Department of Education Policy

Teachers must be familiar with:

### Student Behaviour Policy (WA DoE)
- Mandatory policy — all WA public schools
- Covers expected behaviour, response framework, roles and responsibilities
- Schools must have a documented behaviour policy consistent with this
- Teachers should know their school's specific implementation

### Restrictive Practices
- Physical restraint (physical intervention) is a LAST RESORT only
- Must be in accordance with WA DoE guidelines — unauthorized force is a legal risk
- Chemical restraint, seclusion, and withholding food/medical are prohibited
- Document any physical intervention immediately

### Suspensions and Exclusions
- Principals (or delegated senior staff) can suspend
- Exclusions require principal approval and district office notification
- Parents/carers have review rights
- Ground rules: safety, violence, substance, persistent misbehaviour, non-compliance with behaviour support plan

### Mandatory Reporting
- Obligation to report reasonable belief of child sexual abuse, physical abuse, emotional abuse, neglect
- Report to the Department of Communities — not your principal, not the school
- Teachers are mandatory reporters under the *Children and Community Services Act 2004*
- Protection from liability when reporting in good faith

### Student Code of Conduct
- Every school must have a Student Code of Conduct
- School-wide consistency is required — all staff apply the same expectations
- Know your school's document and follow it

## 10. AITSL Alignment

### Australian Professional Standards for Teachers — Standard 4

**4.1** — Establish supportive, orderly, safe learning environments
- Physical layout, resources, emotional safety

**4.2** — Manage classroom activities (transitions, routines, procedures)
- Explicit teaching of routines, smooth transitions, minimizing downtime

**4.3** — Manage challenging behaviour (calm, respectful, effective strategies)
- De-escalation, escalation pathway, documentation, support referral

**4.4** — Foster positive relationships (mutual respect, trust)
- Relationship-first approach, restorative practice, repair conversations

**4.5** — Implement engagement strategies for students with challenging behaviour
- Differentiation, sensory supports, trauma-informed adjustments, individual behaviour support plans

### Evidence of Practice
When demonstrating Standard 4 infolios or observations, collect:
- Incident notes with reflection on response
- Behaviour support plans (co-designed with HOS/specialist)
- Documentation of restorative conversations
- Evidence of proactive strategies (visual schedules, routine instruction, environmental design)

## Quick Reference Card

**When in doubt, ask:**
1. Is this proactive? Can I prevent it next time?
2. Is this about the relationship? Have I connected before correcting?
3. Is my response calm and consistent?
4. Have I repaired the relationship after escalation?
5. Is this aligned with WA DoE policy and AITSL Standard 4?

**The Golden Rule:** A calm teacher in control of themselves will always manage behaviour better than an energetic, loud, reactive teacher. Calm is the move.
