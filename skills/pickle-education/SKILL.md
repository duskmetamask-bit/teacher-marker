### 1.3 AC9 Code Format

Each content descriptor has a unique code:

```
AC[year][learning area code][sub-strand code][number]
```

**Year codes:**
- `F` = Foundation
- `1`–`6` = Years 1–6

**Learning area codes:**
| Code | Learning Area |
|---|---|
| `EY` | English |
| `MA` | Mathematics |
| `SC` | Science |
| `HA` | Humanities and Social Sciences |
| `TE` | Technologies |
| `AR` | The Arts |
| `HE` | Health and Physical Education |
| `LA` | Languages |

**Sub-strand codes** are 2–3 letters specific to each learning area.

**Example:** `ACELY167` = Year 4 (4), English (E), Language strand (LY), descriptor 167.

Full example: `AC9SC4S02` = Australian Curriculum v9, Science, Year 4, Sub-strand 02.

### 1.4 Content Descriptor Mapping

When creating a resource, always include:
1. **Content descriptor code** (e.g., AC9M4N01)
2. **Full descriptor text** (the actual outcome)
3. **Year level and term/week context** (so teacher can place in sequence)

## 2. WA CURRICULUM CONTEXT

### 2.1 SCSA — School Curriculum and Standards Authority

**SCSA** is the WA government authority responsible for:
- Administering the Western Australian Curriculum (K–10)
- Setting standards and moderating assessment
- Developing the Western Australian Certificate of Education (WACE, Years 11–12)
- Providing support materials for schools

**Key SCSA resources teachers use:**
- **Course outlines** — scope and sequence for each learning area
- **Assessment guidelines** — how to assess against the standards
- **Moderation programs** — ensuring consistency across schools
- **WACE examinations** — Years 11–12 only (not primary)

**Website:** scsa.wa.edu.au

### 2.2 WA Implementation of AC9

WA schools implement the Australian Curriculum v9 as the **Western Australian Curriculum (WAC)**. The WAC includes:
- All AC9 content descriptors
- WA-specific elaborations and examples where needed
- Alignment with WA assessment standards (A–E)

**Phases of implementation:**
- Foundation–Year 6: fully implemented across all WA schools
- Years 7–10: implemented with some school-based decisions
- Years 11–12: school-based with SCSA guidelines

### 2.3 First Steps — Mathematics and Literacy

**First Steps** is a WA Department of Education initiative providing a structured approach to teaching literacy and numeracy:

**First Steps in Literacy:**
- 8 learning modes: reading, writing, speaking, listening, viewing, representing, spelling, handwriting
- Diagnostic phases to identify where students are in their literacy development
- Used primarily in K–3 but applicable across F–6

**First Steps in Mathematics:**
- 4 strands mapped to WA curriculum
- Focus on differentiated instruction based on student readiness
- Ongoing assessment embedded in the approach

**Important note:** First Steps is a support tool — it does not replace AC9. Content must still align to the Australian Curriculum.

### 2.4 DSR — Directly Supporting Reading

DSR is a WA Department of Education initiative focused on:
- Providing targeted, intensive reading instruction
- Screening and diagnostic assessment for reading difficulties
- Intervention programs for students below expected benchmarks
- Professional learning for teachers on evidence-based reading instruction

**Who it targets:** Students in Years 1–4 identified as struggling with reading

**Key components:**
1. Screening — identifies students who need support
2. Diagnostic assessment — pinpoints specific areas of difficulty (phonological awareness, fluency, comprehension)
3. Intervention — targeted, evidence-based reading programs
4. Progress monitoring — ongoing tracking of student growth

### 2.5 Pre-Primary to Year 10 Western Australian Curriculum

**Pre-Primary:** Part of the K–10 Western Australian Curriculum. Not optional — Pre-Primary is the first year of formal schooling in WA and aligns with Foundation year in AC9.

**Structure for F–6:**
| Year | WA Curriculum Level | AC9 Equivalent |
|------|---------------------|----------------|
| Pre-Primary | Pre-Primary | Foundation (F) |
| Year 1 | Year 1 | Year 1 |
| Year 2 | Year 2 | Year 2 |
| Year 3 | Year 3 | Year 3 |
| Year 4 | Year 4 | Year 4 |
| Year 5 | Year 5 | Year 5 |
| Year 6 | Year 6 | Year 6 |

**Years 7–10:** WAC continues, with schools having some flexibility in implementation.

## 3. YEAR LEVELS & STAGES

### 3.1 Early Years: Kindergarten, Pre-Primary, Years 1–2

**Developmental focus:** Concrete, hands-on, oral language-rich

**Pedagogical approach:**
- Play-based learning (especially K–PP)
- High use of concrete materials (manipulatives, real objects)
- Oral language as the primary mode — listening and speaking precede reading and writing
- Modelling and demonstration over independent work
- Short attention spans — frequent changes of activity

**Curriculum notes for this stage:**
- Foundation English: building phonological awareness, oral language, predicting and monitoring comprehension
- Foundation Mathematics: counting, early number sense, spatial awareness through play
- Science: observation, wonder, exploring the world through senses

**Content codes in this stage:**
- `AC9F` / `AC9` = Foundation (Pre-Primary in WA)
- `AC9E1` = Year 1
- `AC9E2` = Year 2

**Common activities:** play dough, sand/water play, story retell, sound walks, counting games, sorting objects, building with blocks

### 3.2 Middle Primary: Years 3–4

**Developmental focus:** Building independence and fluency

**Pedagogical approach:**
- Students beginning to work more independently
- Introduction of more abstract concepts (but still concrete supports needed)
- Group work and collaborative learning
- Students can sustain attention longer
- Beginning to develop metacognitive strategies

**Curriculum notes for this stage:**
- English: building reading fluency, writing compositions, grammar conventions, handwriting fluency
- Mathematics: multiplication/division concepts, fractions, data representation, measurement
- Science: fair testing, identifying variables, recording observations

**Content codes:** `AC9E3`, `AC9E4`, `AC9M3N04` etc.

**Common activities:** experiments, research projects, class novels, maths games, peer editing, role plays

### 3.3 Upper Primary: Years 5–6

**Developmental focus:** Abstract thinking, inquiry, and autonomy

**Pedagogical approach:**
- Inquiry-based learning — students generate questions and pursue investigation
- Abstract reasoning required — leap from concrete to symbolic
- Independent and group projects with longer timeframes
- Critical and creative thinking explicitly taught
- Preparation for high school (study skills, organisation, self-advocacy)

**Curriculum notes for this stage:**
- English: text analysis, inferential comprehension, complex writing genres (persuasive, analytical), speaking formal presentations
- Mathematics: ratios, percentages, algebraic thinking, statistical analysis, geometric reasoning
- Science: scientific method, hypothesis testing, data analysis, evaluating evidence

**Content codes:** `AC9E5`, `AC9E6`, `AC9M6N01` etc.

**Common activities:** debates, research assignments, science fair projects, presentations, maths investigations, literature circles

## 4. ASSESSMENT & REPORTING IN WA

### 4.1 Standards-Referenced Assessment (A–E)

WA uses a **standards-referenced assessment (SRA)** model. Teachers assess student achievement against the **Western Australian Curriculum (WAC) achievement standards**, not against other students.

**The A–E reporting scale:**

| Grade | Descriptor | Indicative standard |
|---|---|---|
| **A** | Excellent | Exceeding the standard — working beyond year-level expectations |
| **B** | Good | Above the standard — consistently demonstrating the standard with confidence |
| **C** | Satisfactory | At the standard — demonstrating the key aspects of the standard |
| **D** | Developing | Below the standard — working towards the standard with some success |
| **E** | Below standard | Well below the standard — requiring additional support |

**Key principle:** A–E describes how well a student is meeting the standard, not how hard they are trying.

**Achievement standards** are expressed as short descriptions for each year level and learning area. They are available on the SCSA website for each learning area and year level.

### 4.2 Brightpath

**Brightpath** is a WA Department of Education online assessment and tracking tool:
- Tracks student progress in Literacy and Numeracy (K–6)
- Uses consistent rubrics across schools for moderation
- Provides diagnostic data to identify areas of need
- Allows teachers to monitor growth over time
- Generates reports at student, class, and school level

**How teachers use Brightpath:**
1. Assess student work samples against Brightpath rubrics
2. Input data into the Brightpath tracking system
3. Identify students needing intervention or extension
4. Share data with parents and leadership

**Key writing traits assessed in Brightpath:**
- Ideas
- Structure
- Vocabulary
- Paragraphing
- Grammar
- Punctuation

### 4.3 Progressive Achievement Tests (PAT)

**PAT** is a standardised assessment tool (ACER) used in many WA schools:

**PAT-R (Reading):**
- Multiple-choice reading comprehension test
- Scaled scores allow tracking over time
- Used to identify students needing additional support or extension

**PAT-M (Mathematics):**
- Assesses mathematical understanding and problem-solving
- Multiple-choice format
- Provides diagnostic information about specific maths skills

**How PAT results are used:**
- Baseline data at start of year
- Identify student strengths and gaps
- Track growth over time
- Inform teaching differentiation
- Report to parents as comparative context

**Important:** PAT is one data point among many. It does not replace teacher judgement.

### 4.4 ACER Assessments

ACER produces a suite of assessments used in WA schools:

| Assessment | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **PAT** (Progressive Achievement Tests) | Reading, Mathematics, Science |
| **Year 3 and 5 Numeracy Benchmarks** | Set standard for NAPLAN readiness |
| **Overall Judgement Tests** | Snapshot of student achievement |
| **Spatial Intelligence and Numeracy (SIN)** | Diagnostic for students with high numeracy potential |

### 4.5 Report Card Comments (Semester Reports)

In WA public schools, semester reports are required twice yearly (end of Semester 1 and Semester 2).

**Report card structure:**
- **Academic achievement:** A–E grade for each learning area
- **Effort/Behaviour:** Usually a separate indicator (often 1–4 or Satisfactory/Needs Development)
- **Comments:** Written description of progress and areas for development

**Comment writing principles:**
1. Be specific — name the content, not vague "good progress"
2. Reference what the student CAN do, not just what they can't
3. Include next steps / areas for development
4. Connect to the achievement standard for that year level
5. Avoid generic language — personalise to the student

**Comment framework:**
```
[Student] has demonstrated [grade descriptor] achievement in [learning area] this semester. They have shown strength in [specific skill/content] and are working towards [next area of focus]. [He/She/They] would benefit from [suggested support/strategy] to further develop [skill].
```

### 4.6 WSR — Where's Student At?

**WSR** is a WA assessment and reporting tool used primarily in the early years (K–3):

- Provides a structured framework for reporting against the Western Australian Curriculum
- Uses a 4-point scale: Beginning → Developing → Working → Achieving
- Integrates with Brightpath data
- Focuses on literacy and numeracy outcomes
- Tracks progress against the First Steps modes

**WSR is not a one-off test** — it is an ongoing observation and recording system embedded in teaching practice.

## 5. AITSL PROFESSIONAL STANDARDS

### 5.1 Overview

The **Australian Institute for Teaching and School Leadership (AITSL)** maintains the Australian Professional Standards for Teachers. These are used:
- For teacher registration and certification
- In performance development processes
- To frame professional learning goals
- In resume/CV bullet points (e.g., "demonstrating Proficient Teacher standard 3.5")

### 5.2 Graduate Teacher (National Standard — not used in WA registration)

The Graduate Teacher standards describe what a pre-service teacher should know and be able to do at graduation. In WA, this aligns with the **入職標準 (入职标准)** pathway for provisional registration.

### 5.3 Proficient Teacher (the standard for all classroom teachers)

The Proficient Teacher standards are organized into **4 domains** and **37 focus areas**:

**Domain 1: Professional Knowledge**
- 1.1 Physical, social and intellectual development and characteristics of students
- 1.2 Understand how students learn
- 1.3 Students with diverse linguistic, cultural, religious and socioeconomic backgrounds
- 1.4 Strategies for teaching Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students
- 1.5 Differentiate teaching to meet the specific learning needs of students across the full range of abilities
- 1.6 Strategies to support full participation of students with disability

**Domain 2: Professional Practice**
- 2.1 Content and teaching strategies for the teaching area
- 2.2 Content selection and organisation
- 2.3 Curriculum, assessment and reporting
- 2.4 Provide a safe and supportive environment
- 2.5 Planning and assessing for effective learning
- 2.6 Assess and provide feedback to students
- 2.7 Report on student achievement

**Domain 3: Professional Engagement**
- 3.1 Engage with parents/carers and the community
- 3.2 Engage with the professional world
- 3.3 Engage with colleagues and wider professional networks

**Domain 4: Professional Improvement**
- 4.1 Identify and plan professional learning needs
- 4.2 Engage in professional learning and improve practice
- 4.3 Engage with colleagues and wider professional networks to improve practice
- 4.4 Apply professional learning to improve own teaching

### 5.4 Highly Accomplished Teacher (career pathway / leadership)

Highly Accomplished teachers are certified against the national HALT standards. They are classroom-based but demonstrate:
- Expert knowledge of their students and subject
- Mentorship of colleagues
- Contribution to school-wide programs
- Analysis and use of student data to drive improvement

### 5.5 Lead Teacher

Lead Teacher certification demonstrates:
- School-wide responsibility for programs and students
- Cross-school leadership and contribution
- Evidence-based practice at a leadership level
- Sustained professional impact

### 5.6 How to Reference AITSL in Output

When writing professional learning documents, goals, or resumes:

**Correct format:**
- "Aligns with AITSL Proficient Teacher Standard 2.5: Differentiate teaching to meet the specific learning needs of students across the full range of abilities"
- "Targeting AITSL Standard 4.2: Engage in professional learning to improve practice"

**In your output (PickleNickAI):**
- Reference the relevant standard when creating PL goals or self-assessment reflections
- Use AITSL language correctly — don't say "section" or "indicator" if you're citing the standard
- Don't fabricate standards — only reference real AITSL codes

## 6. CROSS-CURRICULUM PRIORITIES

All teaching resources should wherever possible and appropriate integrate one or more of the three cross-curriculum priorities.

### 6.1 Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Histories and Cultures

**Three key ideas:**
1. **Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples** — the world's oldest continuous cultures (60,000+ years)
2. **Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander knowledges, cultures and identity** — deeply connected to Country/Place
3. **Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander histories** — colonisation, resistance, resilience, shared futures

**How to integrate into lessons:**
- Use respectful, inclusive language
- Ground content in local context — identify the Traditional Custodians of the land
- Incorporate First Nations perspectives, not just during NAIDOC week
- Use appropriate protocols — consult with local Aboriginal Education Officers (AEOs) or elders
- Reference the **Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Histories and Cultures** cross-curriculum priority explicitly in your planning

**Key resources:**
- ABC Splash (education.abc.net.au)
- IA (Indigenous Affairs) website
- SCSA Aboriginal contexts resources
- Consult local Language Groups

### 6.2 Asia and Australia's Engagement with Asia

**Key concepts:**
- Asia literacy — knowledge of and engagement with Asian cultures, histories, economies, environments
- Intercultural understanding — appreciating diversity, challenging stereotypes
- The significance of Asia to Australia's present and future

**How to integrate:**
- Teach Asian languages (if available at your school)
- Integrate Asian perspectives across learning areas — not just in HASS
- Use comparative approaches — e.g., comparing ecosystems, religions, settlement patterns
- Avoid stereotypical or tokenistic representations of Asia

**Countries/regions covered:** South Asia, East Asia, Southeast Asia, West Asia (Middle East), Central Asia

### 6.3 Sustainability

**Key concepts:**
- Sustainability is about the interdependence of environmental, social, and economic systems
- Short and long-term perspectives on decisions and actions
- Individual and collective responsibility

**How to integrate:**
- Science: ecosystems, climate change, biodiversity
- HASS: population, resource use, urbanisation
- Mathematics: data analysis on environmental topics
- Across all year levels with age-appropriate complexity

**Important note:** Sustainability is not just environmental — it includes social and economic sustainability too.

## 6B. EAL/D STUDENT SUPPORT FRAMEWORKS

### EAL/D Students in Australian Classrooms
English as an Additional Language or Dialect (EAL/D) students are those who:
- Have a first language other than English
- Are still developing proficiency in Academic English
- May speak an Aboriginal English or Torres Strait Islander English at home

**WA context:** WA has one of the highest proportions of EAL/D learners nationally, particularly in Perth schools and regional centres. Many students speak languages other than English at home.

**Key distinction:** Social English vs. Academic English. EAL/D students may be conversationally fluent but need explicit teaching of academic vocabulary, complex sentence structures, and discipline-specific language.

### EAL Teaching Strategies (Proven Research-Backed)

**1. Scaffolding for EAL Students**
- **Visual scaffolding:** Graphic organisers, word walls, sentence starters, think boards
- **Language scaffolds:** Embedded glossary of terms, bilingual glossaries where available
- **Content representations:** Use real objects, images, and multi-modal resources
- **Reduced cognitive load:** Present one concept at a time, avoid walls of text

**2. Academic Language Explicit Instruction**
EAL/D students need explicit teaching of:
- **Tier 2 vocabulary:** High-frequency academic words across subjects ("analyse", "compare", "evaluate", "hypothesise")
- **Subject-specific terms:** Content vocabulary unique to each learning area
- **Language functions:** What verbs mean in each context ("describe" vs. "explain" vs. "analyse")
- **Cohesive devices:** Transition words, conjunctions, referencing

**3. Teaching Academic English Through Content**
Best practice:
- Teach language THROUGH content, not separately from it
- Pre-teach vocabulary BEFORE reading or inquiry
- Use "sheltered instruction" — same content, more language support
- Build up from spoken language → written language gradually

**4. EAL/D Assessment Accommodations**
- Allow bilingual dictionaries (real, not AI)
- Extra time for written tasks
- Graphic organiser alternatives to written responses
- Spoken assessment alternatives (recorded or live)
- Reduced word limits with quality over quantity emphasis

**5. EAL/D Differentiated Tasks (Practical)**
Tiered tasks for EAL students:
- **Emerging:** Use first language + English side-by-side, image-based responses, labelled diagrams
- **Developing:** Short sentences, sentence starters, word banks
- **Consolidating:** Full sentences, independent but with glossary support
- **Extending:** Same task as English-speaking peers with vocabulary support

**6. First Language Use**
Research shows first language support improves English acquisition:
- Allow students to use first language for concept development
- Encourage translation of key vocabulary
- Use bilingual peer buddies where possible
- Family communication in first language (letters home, Seesaw)

**7. Building Vocabulary Instruction for EAL/D**
- Word of the week: select 5-10 high-utility academic words per week
- Display words visually with definition, image, and example sentence
- Use words across multiple contexts throughout the week
- Explicitly teach word families and morphological elements (prefixes, suffixes)

### AITSL Alignment for EAL/D
- Standard 1.3: Strategies for teaching students from diverse linguistic, cultural, religious and socioeconomic backgrounds
- Standard 1.4: Strategies for teaching Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students (including those who speak Aboriginal English)
- Standard 3.5: Differentiate teaching to meet the specific learning needs of students across the full range of abilities (including EAL/D students)

## 7. GENERAL CAPABILITIES

The seven general capabilities should be developed through the learning areas, not taught separately. They are embedded in content descriptors.

### 7.1 Literacy

**Definition:** The ability to understand and compose meaning across modes — listening, speaking, reading, writing, viewing, representing

**Key skills:**
- Phonological awareness (especially F–2)
- Decoding and encoding (reading and spelling)
- Comprehension strategies — literal, inferential, evaluative
- Text creation — planning, drafting, editing, publishing
- Speaking and listening for different purposes and audiences
- Understanding and using visual literacy (including digital)

**Note:** The English learning area has the primary responsibility for literacy, but ALL learning areas develop literacy — reading instructions, writing science reports, interpreting maps in HASS.

### 7.2 Numeracy

**Definition:** The ability to use mathematics in context — including recognising and applying mathematical concepts across the curriculum

**Key skills:**
- Counting and number sense
- Operations and algebraic thinking
- Measurement and geometry
- Statistics and probability
- Mathematical reasoning and problem-solving

**Note:** The Mathematics learning area has the primary responsibility, but numeracy is applied across all learning areas — scaling in design, data analysis in science, financial maths in HASS.

### 7.3 ICT Capability

**Definition:** The ability to use ICT safely, responsibly, and effectively for learning and creativity

**Key skills:**
- Digital citizenship and online safety
- Using digital tools for research, collaboration, and communication
- Creating digital content
- Understanding how digital systems work
- Critical evaluation of online information

**Note:** The Technologies learning area (specifically Digital Technologies) has primary responsibility, but ICT is integrated across all areas.

### 7.4 Critical and Creative Thinking

**Definition:** The ability to analyse information and ideas, question assumptions, generate and evaluate solutions, and think originally

**Key skills:**
- Identifying and defining problems
- Gathering and evaluating information
- Generating and comparing possible solutions
- Reflecting on thinking processes (metacognition)
- Generating novel ideas and products

**Where it shows up explicitly:**
- Design thinking (Technologies)
- Scientific inquiry (Science)
- Historical inquiry (History)
- Mathematical problem-solving (Mathematics)

### 7.5 Personal and Social Capability

**Definition:** The ability to understand oneself and others, manage relationships, make responsible decisions, and operate effectively in groups

**Key skills:**
- Self-awareness — recognising emotions, strengths, limitations
- Self-management — managing stress, motivation, goal-setting
- Social awareness — empathy, understanding diverse perspectives
- Social management — collaboration, conflict resolution, leadership

**Where it shows up:**
- Health and Physical Education (primary explicit coverage)
- Group work across all learning areas
- Social and ethical decision-making in HASS

### 7.6 Ethical Understanding

**Definition:** The ability to identify, understand and apply ethical concepts and principles, and to make reasoned ethical judgements

**Key skills:**
- Understanding ethical concepts (honesty, integrity, respect, responsibility)
- Analysing ethical issues from multiple perspectives
- Applying ethical reasoning to real and hypothetical situations
- Taking ethical action

**Where it shows up:**
- HASS (civics and citizenship, ethical questions)
- Science (ethical practice in experiments and science use)
- Health (ethical decision-making about health choices)
- English (ethical themes in literature)

### 7.7 Intercultural Understanding

**Definition:** The ability to understand and interact effectively across cultural contexts, recognising and respecting difference

**Key skills:**
- Recognising culture and its influence on behaviour and perspective
- Understanding and respecting cultural diversity
- Communicating effectively across cultural boundaries
- Reflecting on one's own cultural positioning

**Where it shows up:**
- HASS (geography, history, cultural studies)
- Languages (if applicable)
- English (studying texts from diverse cultures)
- Cross-curriculum priority: Asia and Australia's Engagement with Asia

## 8. KEY WA EDUCATION INITIATIVES

### 8.1 Every School, Every Child (ESEC)

**Overview:** A WA Department of Education strategy targeting improved educational outcomes for students in rural, remote, and low-SES schools.

**Key focus areas:**
- Improving attendance rates
- Early intervention and reading support
- Teacher quality and professional development
- Community and family engagement
- Leadership development

**What it means for classroom teachers:**
- Increased accountability for attendance monitoring and follow-up
- Stronger emphasis on early reading intervention (aligned with DSR)
- Data-driven approach to identifying students at risk

### 8.2 Aboriginal Service Plan (ASP)

**Overview:** A WA Department of Education framework for improving outcomes for Aboriginal students.

**Key focus areas:**
- Attendance and engagement
- Literacy and numeracy achievement
- Culture and identity — strong, positive cultural connections
- Post-school pathways

**What it means for classroom teachers:**
- Must incorporate Aboriginal perspectives into planning
- Required to use culturally responsive pedagogies
- Must engage with local Aboriginal Education Officers
- Data on Aboriginal student achievement tracked and reported

**Key programs within ASP:**
- Clontarf Foundation (for boys in Years 3–12)
- Stars Foundation (for girls in Years 3–12)
- School-based Aboriginal Transition Programs

### 8.3 Support Schools Program

**Overview:** A WA Department of Education funding model providing additional resources to schools with high concentrations of students from low-socioeconomic backgrounds.

**Eligibility:** Schools with a high ICSEA (Index of Community Socio-Educational Advantage) score below 1000 and/or high proportions of Aboriginal students.

**What it provides:**
- Additional teaching staff ( SLSO — School Learning Support Officers)
- Professional learning resources
- Targeted intervention programs
- Leadership support

**What it means for classroom teachers:**
- May have SLSO support in classroom
- Access to additional PL
- More intensive intervention options for students

### 8.4 WA Literacy and Numeracy Strategy

**Overview:** The WA Department of Education's whole-of-system approach to improving literacy and numeracy outcomes, particularly in the early years.

**Key pillars:**
1. **Early identification** — screen all students in K–2 for reading difficulties (aligned with DSR)
2. **Evidence-based instruction** — explicit, systematic phonics instruction (Little Learners, InitiaLit, Multilit)
3. **Intervention** — targeted programs for students well below benchmark
4. **Progress monitoring** — regular tracking using tools like Brightpath, PAT
5. **Teacher development** — PL in evidence-based literacy and numeracy instruction

**Key programs:**
- **DSR (Directly Supporting Reading):** the operational implementation of the reading support strategy
- **First Steps:** the instructional framework supporting the strategy
- **Brightpath:** the tracking and monitoring tool
- **InitiaLit / Little Learners / Multilit:** approved intervention programs for phonics and reading

**Important:** The WA Literacy and Numeracy Strategy is the umbrella framework. DSR is a specific program under it. Brightpath is the monitoring tool. First Steps is the instructional approach.
