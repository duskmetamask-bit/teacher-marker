# pickle-assessment

## Purpose
Assessment literacy for Australian primary school teachers. This skill covers how to interpret assessment data (PAT, Brightpath, WSR, NCCD), run moderation, design valid tasks, and use data to drive instruction.

**This skill is for PickleNickAI when generating assessments, rubrics, or data-driven differentiation recommendations.**

---

## ASSESSMENT FRAMEWORKS IN WA

### Standards-Referenced Assessment (SRA)

WA uses a **standards-referenced assessment (SRA)** model. Teachers assess student achievement against the **Western Australian Curriculum (WAC) achievement standards**, not against other students.

**The A–E reporting scale:**

| Grade | Descriptor | Meaning |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **A** | Excellent | Exceeding the standard — working beyond year-level expectations |
| **B** | Good | Above the standard — consistently demonstrating the standard with confidence |
| **C** | Satisfactory | At the standard — demonstrating the key aspects of the standard |
| **D** | Developing | Below the standard — working towards the standard with some success |
| **E** | Below standard | Well below the standard — requiring additional support |

**Key principle:** A–E describes how well a student is meeting the standard, not how hard they are trying or where they rank.

## PAT — PROGRESSIVE ACHIEVEMENT TESTS (ACER)

### What PAT Measures

PAT tests by ACER assess student achievement in:
- **PAT-R** — Reading comprehension
- **PAT-M** — Mathematics understanding and problem-solving
- **PAT-Listening** — Listening comprehension (some year levels)
- **PAT-Science** — Science understanding (optional)

### Understanding Scaled Scores

PAT uses **scaled scores** — a continuous scale that allows comparison across year levels and over time. A scaled score is NOT a percentage.

**Key principle:** The same scaled score = same level of achievement, regardless of which year level or test form was used.

### PAT-R (Reading) — Score Interpretation

| Scaled Score | Interpretation | Instructional Response |
|-------------|----------------|----------------------|
| **Below 100** | Beginning reading | Intensive intervention, decodable texts, phonological awareness focus |
| **100–110** | Developing | Small-group targeted support, explicit comprehension strategy instruction |
| **110–130** | At standard | Quality core instruction, guided reading, comprehension strategy focus |
| **130–140** | Strong | Extension tasks, richer texts, independent analysis, literature circles |
| **Above 140** | Very High | Acceleration options, independent inquiry, compact curriculum |

### PAT-M (Mathematics) — Score Interpretation

| Scaled Score | Interpretation | Instructional Response |
|-------------|----------------|----------------------|
| **Below 100** | Beginning | Concrete manipulatives, re-teach foundations, diagnostic assessment |
| **100–110** | Developing | Explicit instruction with scaffolding, procedural fluency focus |
| **110–130** | At standard | Core instruction, problem-solving emphasis, varied practice |
| **130–140** | Strong | Problem-solving challenges, open-ended investigations, peer tutoring |
| **Above 140** | Very High | Extension mathematics, competition problems, inquiry projects |

### Using PAT Data for Differentiation

**Step 1: Identify the student's zone**
- Beginning / Developing / At Standard / Strong / Very High

**Step 2: Plan backwards from the standard**
- Beginning → What foundation skills are missing? Build those first.
- Developing → Target the gap between current and year-level standard.
- At Standard → Deepen understanding with complex applications.
- Strong/Very High → Extend beyond the year-level standard.

**Step 3: Set a specific, measurable goal**
- One goal per term
- Example: "PAT-R from 105 to 115 — improve inferential comprehension"
- Share goal with student (age-appropriate)

**Step 4: Monitor progress**
- PAT is ONE data point — use alongside teacher judgement + formative assessment
- Re-assess mid-term if available or use school-based assessments

### Important Caveats About PAT

- **One test, one day** — PAT scores are snapshots, not definitions of a student
- **Test-taking factors** — anxiety, fatigue, unfamiliarity with format can suppress scores
- **Alignment** — PAT items may not perfectly align with AC9 content descriptors
- **Use in combination** — PAT + teacher judgement + Brightpath + formative = complete picture

### NAPLAN Alignment with PAT

| NAPLAN Band | PAT-R Approximate Range | PAT-M Approximate Range |
|-------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Band 1 (Year 3 early) | 90–105 | 90–105 |
| Band 2 | 105–115 | 105–120 |
| Band 3 | 115–130 | 120–135 |
| Band 4 | 130–145 | 135–150 |
| Band 5+ | 145+ | 150+ |

*Note: These are approximate. NAPLAN and PAT use different scales.*

## BRIGHTPATH

### What Brightpath Is

Brightpath is a WA Department of Education online assessment and tracking tool:
- Tracks student progress in Literacy and Numeracy (K–6)
- Uses consistent rubrics across schools for moderation
- Provides diagnostic data to identify areas of need
- Allows teachers to monitor growth over time
- Generates reports at student, class, and school level

### How Brightpath Rubrics Work

Brightpath assesses student work samples against **analytic rubrics** — separate scores for different aspects of a task.

**Key writing traits assessed:**
1. **Ideas** — Depth, originality, clarity of thinking
2. **Structure** — Organization, coherence, paragraphing
3. **Vocabulary** — Word choice, precision, range
4. **Paragraphing** — Logical flow, transitions
5. **Grammar** — Sentence structure, agreement
6. **Punctuation** — Accuracy, variety

**Important:** Each trait is marked on a 1–6 scale:
| Score | Descriptor |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | Beginning — minimal evidence of the trait |
| 2 | Emerging — some evidence, significant gaps |
| 3 | Developing — basic evidence, meets some criteria |
| 4 | Adequate — solid evidence of the trait |
| 5 | Strong — confident, consistent evidence |
| 6 | Excellent — sophisticated, refined evidence |

### Using Brightpath Data for Instruction

**From Brightpath scores to teaching:**
- If Ideas = 2 → Focus on generating and developing ideas in writing
- If Structure = 2 → Explicitly teach text structure (hook, body, conclusion)
- If Vocabulary = 2 → Rich vocabulary instruction, word-of-the-week

**Do NOT:** Use Brightpath scores as the only measure of student writing quality. Brightpath is a tool, not a verdict.

## WSR — WHERE'S STUDENT AT?

### What WSR Is

WSR is a WA assessment and reporting tool used primarily in the early years (K–3):
- Structured framework for reporting against the Western Australian Curriculum
- 4-point scale: Beginning → Developing → Working → Achieving
- Integrates with Brightpath data
- Focuses on literacy and numeracy outcomes
- Ongoing observation and recording system embedded in teaching practice

### WSR is NOT a one-off test — it is:
- Continuous observation
- Embedded in daily teaching
- Collaborative (teachers discuss students together)
- Used to inform planning and differentiation

### WSR Scale Descriptors

| Level | Meaning | Teaching Implication |
|-------|---------|---------------------|
| **Beginning** | Student is working towards early aspects of the standard | Needs significant support, focus on foundational skills |
| **Developing** | Student is working towards the standard | Targeted instruction, scaffolded tasks |
| **Working** | Student is working at the standard | Consolidation, some extension |
| **Achieving** | Student is achieving the standard consistently | Extension, deeper application |

## NCCD — NATIONAL CONSISTENT COLLECTION OF DATA

### What NCCD Is

The NCCD is a federally mandated annual collection of data about students with disability. Schools must provide evidence of the level of adjustment and support provided.

**Who is included:**
- Students with a disability who have an current personalised learning plan (PLP)
- Students with a disability who receive reasonable adjustments in classroom
- Students who access specialised support programs due to disability

**Four levels of adjustment:**

| Level | Descriptor | What It Means |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| **Quality Differentiated Teaching Practice** | Adjustments are integral to quality teaching for all students | No additional funding loaded |
| **Supplementary** | Some additional support above and beyond quality differentiated teaching | Some funding loaded |
| **Substantial** | Significant, ongoing adjustments in place | Higher funding loaded |
| **Extensive** | Full-time support required, extensive adjustments | Highest funding loaded |

### What Teachers Need for NCCD Documentation

**Minimum evidence requirements:**
1. **Personalised Learning Plan (PLP)** — Current, signed, with specific goals
2. **Assessment data** — Showing the student's performance and progress
3. **Adjustments log** — What was provided, when, how often
4. **Teacher observations** — Notes on engagement, participation, achievement

**For PickleNickAI:** When generating PLPs or adjustment recommendations, include:
- Specific, measurable goals (SMART goals)
- Description of the adjustment
- How progress will be measured
- Review date

### NCCD Goals — SMART Goal Framework

**S.M.A.R.T. Goals:**
- **Specific** — What exactly will the student achieve?
- **Measurable** — How will we know when they've achieved it?
- **Achievable** — Is this realistic given the student's starting point?
- **Relevant** — Does this link to their learning needs?
- **Time-bound** — When will we review progress?

**Example NCCD Goal (Year 3 Reading):**
> "By the end of Term 2, [Student] will read Level 18 texts at 90% accuracy, as measured by running records, with continued work on Level 20 texts in class."

## MODERATION

### What Moderation Is

Moderation is the process of teachers collectively agreeing on standards by comparing student work samples. It ensures consistency of judgement across teachers, year levels, and schools.

**WA Context:**
- Brightpath rubrics include moderated work samples for each score point
- Teachers use these to calibrate their own marking
- School-wide moderation meetings are required annually for core subjects

### Moderation Process

**Step 1: Establish the standard**
- Read the AC9 achievement standard for the year level and learning area
- Identify what "at standard" looks like

**Step 2: Review Brightpath anchor papers**
- Brightpath provides examples of work at each score point
- Teachers collectively agree on why each sample scored as it did

**Step 3: Apply to your students' work**
- Mark student work against the agreed standard
- Discuss any borderline cases as a group
- Come to consensus on the grade

**Step 4: Record and report**
- Apply the agreed grade to student records
- Use the A–E scale aligned to the WAC achievement standard

### Running a Moderation Session

**Practical structure (60 minutes):**
1. **10 min** — Administrator or lead teacher frames the standard for today
2. **20 min** — Small groups mark 3 anonymised work samples, record reasoning
3. **20 min** — Whole group compares marks, discusses discrepancies, agrees on standards
4. **10 min** — Apply agreed standards to actual student work

**Key question to ask in moderation:**
- "What does at-standard work look like?"
- "What would a strong B look like vs a weak B?"
- "What's the difference between C and D here?"

## TASK DESIGN — PRINCIPLES FOR VALID ASSESSMENT

### What Makes an Assessment Task Valid?

1. **Aligned to the AC9 content descriptor** — The task actually measures what the descriptor asks
2. **Scaffolded appropriately** — Students can show what they know without being blocked by irrelevant difficulty
3. **Clear success criteria** — Students know what they're aiming for before they start
4. **Consistent conditions** — Same task, same conditions for all students (unless differentiation is intentional)
5. **Scored against a rubric** — Criteria are known, shared, and applied consistently

### Alignment Check — Does Your Task Match the Descriptor?

**Descriptor:** AC9E4LY02 — "Understand how texts can be composed to influence audiences"

**Weak task:** "Write a persuasive paragraph about why school uniforms should be banned."

**Strong task:** "Write TWO different versions of a letter to your principal about school uniforms — one that tries to PERSUADE the principal to remove uniforms, and one that tries to PERSUADE the principal to keep them. Explain in a reflection note why each version uses different techniques to influence the audience."

**Why the strong task is better:**
- Actually measures "how texts can be composed to influence audiences" — not just writing a persuasive paragraph
- Requires comparison and analysis (higher-order thinking)
- Students demonstrate understanding of author's intent and technique

## RUBRIC DESIGN

### Analytic vs Holistic Rubrics

| | Analytic | Holistic |
|--|--|--|
| **What it is** | Separate grades per criterion | One overall grade, impression-marked |
| **Best for** | Summative assessment, complex tasks, reporting | Quick screening, low-stakes tasks, revision drafts |
| **Pros** | Precise feedback, diagnostic | Fast, holistic sense of quality |
| **Cons** | Time-consuming, can fragment the task | Less feedback, can feel unfair to students |
| **For reporting** | Mark against criteria, convert to single A–E for report | — |

### Rubric Descriptor Rules

Descriptors must be:
- **Specific** — "uses vivid verbs to create atmosphere" not "good writing"
- **Observable** — written behaviours a teacher can actually see
- **Distinct** — each grade is clearly different from the next
- **Non-ambiguous** — two teachers marking the same work should land on the same grade

**Rule:** If you're marking more than 5 criteria, you're marking compliance, not learning.

### A–E Descriptor Template for Rubrics

```
A — Excellent: [What an A student does that a B student doesn't]
B — Good: [What a B student does that a C student doesn't]
C — Satisfactory: [What a C student does that a D student doesn't]
D — Developing: [What a D student does that an E student doesn't]
E — Below Standard: [What an E student is missing]
```

## FEEDBACK — RESEARCH-BACKED PRACTICE

### The Hattie & Timperley (2007) Model

| Level | Question | Example |
|-------|---------|---------|
| **Task level** | "How well am I doing?" | "Your introduction clearly states your argument." |
| **Process level** | "How am I doing it?" | "Your paragraph transitions show you understand structure." |
| **Self-regulation** | "How can I improve?" | "Next time, check your rubric before you submit." |
| **Self** | (Avoid) | "Great work!" — no actionable info |

**Most effective:** Moves from task level → process level → self-regulation.

### Feed Up / Feed Back / Feed Forward

| Type | Question | When to Use |
|------|----------|------------|
| **Feed UP** | Where are we going? | Unit launch, before task introduction |
| **Feed BACK** | How did we do? | Daily marking, after task completion |
| **Feed FORWARD** | Where to next? | Priority for daily marking and report comments |

### PickleNick Feedback Rules

**1. Be specific — cite evidence:**
❌ "Great job!"
✅ "Your opening paragraph hooks the reader — 'The moment she walked in, everything changed.' Keep that energy into paragraph 2."

**2. Name the specific error with correction:**
❌ "You need to improve your punctuation."
✅ "Your commas inside lists are missing — e.g., 'She bought apples, oranges, bananas' needs commas before each item."

**3. Give one actionable next step:**
❌ "Try harder next time."
✅ "Your next step is to add one piece of evidence per paragraph. Look at your topic sentence first — does every sentence after it support it?"
