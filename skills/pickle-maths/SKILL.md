# PART 2 — WA CONTEXT

## WA Department of Education — Mathematics Approach

The WA Department of Education supports a **differentiated, inquiry-based** approach to maths. Key documents:
- **First Steps in Mathematics** — the core pedagogical resource (see below)
- **Curriculum and Assessment Advice** — operationalising AC9
- Emphasis on **personalised learning pathways**, targeting point-of-need
- DSR (Differentiated Skills Resource) used alongside First Steps for intervention
- **Brightpath** used for moderated, consistent assessment

## First Steps in Mathematics

A WA-developed pedagogical resource organised around **big ideas** in mathematics. Replaced the old "maths strands" with 6 organising strands:

1. **Number — Sense and place** — flexible number thinking, not just procedures
2. **Number — Operations** — additive and multiplicative thinking
3. **Measurement — Sensing measures** — length, area, volume, time, mass
4. **Space — Mapping** — location, direction, spatial reasoning
5. **Statistics — Processing data** — collecting, displaying, interpreting
6. **Chance — Processing uncertainty** — probability reasoning

**Key phases within First Steps:**
- **Phase 1: Emergent** — students cannot yet count reliably; need concrete experiences
- **Phase 2: Perceptual** — students count using physical materials; can manipulate objects
- **Phase 3: Figurative** — students mentally image quantities; beginning to reason abstractly
- **Phase 4: Properties** — students use properties of numbers/operations to reason
- **Phase 5: Formal** — students apply formal algorithms and symbolic notation

**Use this to differentiate:** identify which phase a student is in, then plan for the next phase. Don't assume higher year level = higher phase.

## DSR — Differentiated Skills Resource

Used in WA government schools for intervention. Aligned to WA curriculum.
- Organised by **domains**: Number, Measurement, Space, Statistics, Probability
- **Three levels** per domain:
  - **Level 1** — F–2 range
  - **Level 2** — 3–4 range
  - **Level 3** — 5–6 range
- Used to identify specific gaps; targets specific skills rather than full units
- **Best for:** students significantly below year-level expectations who need targeted, sequential intervention
- **Note:** DSR is a gap-filling tool, not a full curriculum. Use alongside normal instruction.

## Brightpath Numeracy

Brightpath is a **WA assessment and moderation platform** used in government schools.
- **Purpose:** Consistent, moderated assessment across schools
- **Numeracy scales** — teachers assign a category (e.g., Numeracy 4, Numeracy 7)
- **How it works:** Samples of student work are moderated against rubrics; rubric categories map to a numeric scale
- **Use for:** Tracking growth over time, identifying students for DSR intervention, reporting against WA standards
- **Limitation:** Not diagnostic — it tells you WHERE a student is, not exactly WHY

## PAT-M — Progressive Achievement Test — Mathematics

Standardised, multiple-choice assessment used Australia-wide (ACER).
- **Scaled scores:** Not raw marks — converted to a scale that allows comparison across year levels and time
  - **Stanine (ST)** — 1–9 scale; 4–6 is "average"
  - **Scale score** — continuous scale (e.g., 100–200+); more granular than stanine
- **What it measures:**
  - **PAT-M Number** — place value, operations, mental computation
  - **PAT-M Reading** — comprehension (separate test)
  - **PAT-M Vocabulary** — word meaning
- **Interpreting scores:**
  - Scaled score ~140–160 for a Year 4 student = at expected level
  - Scale scores increase roughly 8–12 points per year (on the ACER scale)
  - Below 130 in Year 4 = significant gap; refer to DSR
  - Above 170 in Year 4 = extension needed
- **Best use:** Baseline at start of year, track growth at end of year. Don't use as diagnostic — use to identify WHO needs further assessment.

# PART 3 — COMMON DIFFICULTIES AND TEACHING STRATEGIES

## Number Sense

**What it is:**
Number sense is the ability to understand numbers flexibly, make mental calculations, recognise relationships between numbers, and work with quantities intuitively. It's not about speed — it's about depth of understanding.

**Why it matters:**
Students with strong number sense can manipulate numbers, check their own work, and adapt strategies. Students without it rely on memorised procedures and collapse when problems deviate from standard formats.

**Signs a student lacks number sense:**
- Counts on fingers past 10
- Cannot estimate if an answer is reasonable
- Does not see connections (e.g., 7 + 8 is close to 7 + 7 + 2)
- Cannot mental compute simple additions
- Treats all maths as arbitrary rules

**How to teach it:**
1. **Subitising** — show dot patterns, flash cards for instant recognition (F–2)
2. **Make-a-ten / bridging ten** — 8 + 5 = 8 + 2 + 3 = 10 + 3 = 13
3. **Number lines** — physical and visual; build mental number lines
4. **Open number talks** — daily 5–10 minute discussions: "How did you see 7 + 6?"
5. **Estimation games** — "I have 47 lollies, estimate how many boxes of 10"
6. **Part-part-whole** — model relationships between numbers (e.g., 4 and 6 make 10)

## Multiplication and Division

### Core Strategies (in order of teaching):

**1. Repeated addition (Year 1–2)**
3 × 4 = 3 groups of 4 = 4 + 4 + 4 = 12

**2. Array / area model (Year 2–3)**
Draw rows and columns. 3 rows of 4 = 3 × 4 = 12. Connect to grid paper.

**3. Doubling and halving (Year 3–4)**
4 × 6 = (2 × 6) + (2 × 6) = 12 + 12 = 24

**4. Break apart / distributive property (Year 3–4)**
7 × 8 = (7 × 5) + (7 × 3) = 35 + 21 = 56

**5. Lattice multiplication (Year 4–5)**
Draw diagonal grid. Multiply each digit. Fill in diagonals. Sum diagonals from right.
Best for: students struggling with traditional algorithm. Builds conceptual understanding before formal algorithm.

**6. Traditional algorithm (Year 4–6)**
Vertical multiplication with carrying. Teach AFTER students understand WHY it works.

### Division Strategies:

**1. Sharing (Year 1–2)**
"I have 12 apples, share them equally among 3 friends. How many each?" (12 ÷ 3)

**2. Grouping / repeated subtraction (Year 2–3)**
"How many groups of 4 are in 12?" — keep subtracting until you reach 0

**3. Chunking (Year 3–4)**
20 ÷ 4: chunk groups of 4 → 4, 8, 12, 16, 20 = 5 groups. 20 ÷ 4 = 5.

**4. Inverse multiplication (Year 3+)**
"32 ÷ 8 = ? → what × 8 = 32?" → 4 × 8 = 32, therefore 32 ÷ 8 = 4.

### Facts Practice:

- **Maths Millionaire / games** — timed but low-stakes
- **Skip counting grids** — fill in missing numbers
- **Flash cards with trick questions** — "What's 7 × 8? What's 8 × 7? Are they the same?"
- **X-table songs** — 3s, 4s, 6s, 7s, 8s — use when commuting

## Fractions

### Visual Models — Teach in this order:

**1. Area models (Year 2–3)**
Halves, quarters, thirds — colour in parts of a shape. "Shade 3/4 of this rectangle."

**2. Set models (Year 3–4)**
"3 of these 8 apples are green. What fraction are green?" — fractions of a collection.

**3. Number lines (Year 4–5)**
Place 1/2, 1/4, 3/4 on a number line between 0 and 1. Build to comparing fractions.

**4. Linear models (Year 4–5)**
Fraction strips, Cuisenaire rods — "How many 1/4 strips does it take to equal one whole?"

### Key Misconceptions to Address:

| Misconception | Why it happens | How to fix |
|--------------|---------------|-----------|
| "Bigger denominator = bigger number" | Students compare numerators | Number line; place fractions visually |
| "3/5 + 2/5 = 5/10" | Forgetting to keep denominator | Always model conceptually first |
| "1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6 because they look the same size" | Actually correct — use to teach equivalence | Use area models to show equivalence |
| "Fractions must be less than 1" | Only exposed to proper fractions | Introduce improper fractions (Year 5+): 5/3, 7/4 |

### Equivalence (Year 4–5):

Use a fraction wall. Show that 1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6 = 4/8. Build from visual → abstract generalisation.

### Operations with Fractions:

- **Add/subtract (same denominator):** 2/7 + 3/7 = 5/7 — keep denominator, add numerators
- **Add/subtract (different denominator):** find equivalent fractions with common denominator first
- **Multiply:** a/b × c/d = (a×c)/(b×d). "Half of 3/4" → 1/2 × 3/4 = 3/8
- **Divide:** a/b ÷ c/d = a/b × d/c (invert and multiply)

## Decimals

### Place Value Foundation (critical before decimals):

| Position | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Hundreds | 100 |
| Tens | 10 |
| Ones | 1 |
| Tenths | 0.1 (1/10) |
| Hundredths | 0.01 (1/100) |
| Thousandths | 0.001 (1/1000) |

### Connecting Decimals to Fractions:

0.3 = 3/10. 0.25 = 25/100 = 1/4. Use Base-10 blocks (MAB) to show that 1 flat = 1 whole, 1 rod = 0.1, 1 cube = 0.01.

### Operations with Decimals:

- **Addition/subtraction:** align decimal points. 3.7 + 2.45 = 3.70 + 2.45 = 6.15
- **Multiplication:** count decimal places in BOTH numbers, total them in result. 1.2 × 0.3 → 12 × 3 = 36 → move 2+1 = 3 decimal places → 0.36
- **Division:** make divisor a whole number by moving decimal; move same amount in dividend. 4.5 ÷ 0.5 → 45 ÷ 5 = 9

### Common Errors:

| Error | Why | Fix |
|-------|-----|-----|
| 3.7 + 2.45 = 6.15 (right) but student writes 6.2 | Doesn't align decimal places | Grid paper; always write 3.70 first |
| 0.4 + 0.3 = 0.7 (right) student writes 0.7 | Usually fine, but some think decimal = extra digit | Connect to fractions: 4/10 + 3/10 = 7/10 |

### Real-world connections:
- Money: $3.45 = 345 cents = 3 dollars + 45 cents
- Measurement: 1.75 m, 2.3 kg, 0.5 L
- Sport scores, Olympics

## Algebra (Year 5–6)

### Patterns (Year F–4 foundation):

- Colour patterns: red, blue, red, blue...
- Shape patterns: triangle, square, triangle, square...
- Number patterns: 2, 4, 6, 8... (skip counting as pattern)
- Pattern rule: "Start at 3, add 5 each time"

### Variables (Year 5–6):

A variable is a **symbol that represents a number** — not a letter that moves.

**Common introduction (Year 5):**
"If I have some apples and I add 3 more, I have 8. How many did I start with?"
n + 3 = 8 → n = 5

**Teaching approaches:**
1. **Weighing scale model** — a balance scale with shapes; "If both sides weigh the same, what does the shape weigh?"
2. **Puzzle framing** — "I'm thinking of a number. I double it and add 5. I get 17. What's my number?"
3. **T-charts** — make input/output tables:

| In (x) | Out (y) |
|--------|---------|
| 1 | 3 |
| 2 | 6 |
| 3 | 9 |
| x | ? |

Pattern: y = x × 3

### Equations (Year 6):

- Solve one-step: x + 5 = 12 → x = 7
- Solve two-step: 2x + 3 = 11 → 2x = 8 → x = 4
- Always check: substitute answer back into original equation

### Order of Operations — BEDMAS (Year 6):

**B**rackets first → **E**xponents → **D**ivision and **M**ultiplication (left to right) → **A**ddition and **S**ubtraction (left to right)

Example: 3 + 2 × (4 − 1)²
= 3 + 2 × 3²
= 3 + 2 × 9
= 3 + 18
= 21

**Common mistake:** 5 − 2 + 1 = 5 − 3 = 2 (wrong — addition/subtraction go left to right, so 5 − 2 + 1 = 4)

## Problem-Solving

### CUBES (Problem-Solving Steps):

**C** — Circle key numbers
**U** — Underline the question
**B** — Box the problem type (join, separate, part-part-whole, compare)
**E** — Evaluate — check your work
**S** — Solve and check

### RUCSAC:

**R**ead the problem
**U**nderstand — what do I know? What do I need?
**C**hoose a strategy
**S**olve
**A**nswer — does it make sense?
**C**heck — does the answer fit the problem?

### Heuristic Strategies (teach explicitly):

| Strategy | When to use |
|----------|-------------|
| Draw a picture/diagram | Geometry, spatial problems |
| Make a table/chart | Organising data, patterns |
| Act it out | Division (sharing), chance experiments |
| Guess and check | Multi-step, trial and improvement |
| Work backwards | Inverse operations, algebra |
| Find a pattern | Sequences, generalisation |
| Break into parts | Multi-step word problems |
| Simplify the problem | Large numbers — use easier numbers first |

### Example Problem (Year 4):
"Mia has 156 stamps. She gives 38 to her brother and buys another 45. How many does she have now?"

Strategy: picture/numbers → 156 − 38 = 118 → 118 + 45 = 163

### Example Problem (Year 6):
"A rectangle has perimeter 24 cm. Its length is twice its width. What are the dimensions?"

Strategy: algebraic — 2L + 2W = 24, L = 2W → 2(2W) + 2W = 24 → 6W = 24 → W = 4, L = 8

# PART 5 — PEDAGOGICAL FRAMEWORKS

## CPA — Concrete-Pictorial-Abstract

**What it is:**
A three-stage approach to mathematical learning:
1. **Concrete** — physical manipulatives students physically handle
2. **Pictorial** — visual representations students draw or see
3. **Abstract** — symbolic notation (numbers, symbols)

**Why it works:**
Students build understanding from physical experience → visual representation → abstract symbol. Skipping stages creates gaps.

**CPA sequence example — introducing multiplication (Year 2):**
- **Concrete:** Students make 3 groups of 4 using counters. Count: 4, 8, 12.
- **Pictorial:** Students draw 3 circles with 4 dots inside each. Show array.
- **Abstract:** Students write 3 × 4 = 12. Connect dots to numbers.

**CPA sequence example — fractions (Year 3):**
- **Concrete:** Fold paper to show halves, quarters. Share 12 counters equally.
- **Pictorial:** Draw area model of fractions. Show fraction bar.
- **Abstract:** Write 3/4 = 0.75 = 75%.

**CPA sequence example — decimals (Year 5):**
- **Concrete:** Base-10 blocks — 1 flat = 1 whole, 1 rod = 0.1, 1 cube = 0.01
- **Pictorial:** Draw the flat/rod/cube representation
- **Abstract:** 3.27 = 3 + 0.2 + 0.07

## Number Talks

**What it is:**
Short, daily 5–10 minute classroom discussions where students mentally solve problems and explain their thinking. The teacher presents a problem; students solve in their heads, then share strategies.

**Why it works:**
- Builds mental computation fluency
- Exposes students to multiple strategies
- Develops mathematical vocabulary
- Reveals student thinking (teacher formative assessment)

**How to run a Number Talk:**
1. Teacher writes a problem on the board (no numbers in students' heads)
2. Students solve mentally (wait 30–60 seconds — no hands up yet)
3. Teacher asks for strategies: "Who can share how they saw it?"
4. Students explain; teacher records on board (capture student language)
5. Other students can add or question
6. Make connections: "Who saw it differently?"

**Number Talk examples by year level:**

| Year | Problem | Purpose |
|------|---------|---------|
| F–1 | 8 + 3 = ? | Count on / bridging ten |
| 1–2 | 17 + 8 = ? | Partitioning / near ten |
| 2–3 | 4 × 6 = ? | Multiplication strategies |
| 3–4 | 99 + 47 = ? | Compensation |
| 4–5 | 1/2 + 1/4 = ? | Fraction addition |
| 5–6 | 25% of 80 = ? | Proportional thinking |

**Rule:** Never say a strategy is wrong. Say "I like that thinking" and redirect. The goal is multiple valid approaches.

## Maths Workshop

**What it is:**
A structured, student-led approach to inquiry-based maths. Students rotate through stations while teacher works with small groups.

**Typical structure (60-minute lesson):**
- **Mini-lesson** (10 min): Teacher introduces concept, models, poses problem
- **Workshop** (40 min): Students rotate through stations
  - Station 1: Teacher-led small group (targeted instruction)
  - Station 2: Independent practice (worksheet, digital)
  - Station 3: Hands-on / manipulative activity
  - Station 4: Reflection / journal
- **Maths Journal / Share** (10 min): Students share what they learned

**Best for:**
- Mixed-ability classrooms
- Inquiry units (e.g., "How do we measure without rulers?")
- Students who need concrete experiences

**Teacher-led station — what to teach:**
Use this for targeted, point-of-need instruction. Groups based on pre-assessment, not year level.

## Formative Assessment in Maths

**Exit Tickets (quick, 5 questions):**
Written at end of lesson. Example (Year 3 fractions):
1. Draw a shape and shade 3/4 of it.
2. What is 1/2 + 1/4? Show your answer.
3. True or false: 1/2 > 3/4. Explain.
4. Share 12 counters equally among 4 friends. How many each?
5. Write a word problem for 3/4.

**Diagnostic Tasks:**
Open-ended tasks that reveal thinking. Example (Year 4 multiplication):
"Find all the ways to arrange 24 squares. Write the dimensions of each arrangement. What patterns do you notice?"

**Observation checklists:**
Use during group work. Note who is:
- Using efficient strategies
- Persevering vs giving up
- Making connections to prior learning
- Misapplying strategies

**Self-assessment — "Traffic Light"**
Students hold up green (I can do this), yellow (I have some questions), red (I need help). Quick gauge for next lesson planning.

**Brightpath Moderation (WA):**
After collecting work samples, moderate against Brightpath rubrics to ensure consistent teacher judgement against WA standards. Essential for reporting.
