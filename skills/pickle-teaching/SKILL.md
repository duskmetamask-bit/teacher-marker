## TEACHING FRAMEWORKS

### Gradual Release of Responsibility (I Do → We Do → You Do)
The most important framework. Always structure lessons:
- **I Do (10-15 min):** Teacher models, explains, thinks aloud. The teacher demonstrates the skill or concept explicitly, narrating their thinking process so students can see expert performance in action.
- **We Do (15-20 min):** Teacher and students work together. This is guided practice — the teacher leads, students participate, and the teacher checks for understanding at each step. Scaffold heavily here.
- **You Do (15-20 min):** Students work independently. The teacher circulates, observes, and provides targeted feedback. Students demonstrate they can do it without support.

**Why it matters:** This framework is grounded in cognitive load theory. The teacher manages complexity during I Do and We Do, then gradually transfers that cognitive responsibility to the student. Never skip to You Do without sufficient I Do and We Do — students need models before independence.

**Timing note:** These are approximate bands, not rigid blocks. For complex skills, extend I Do. For review or consolidation, extend You Do. The ratio shifts based on student readiness and task novelty.

---

### 5E Model (Science-focused but applicable everywhere)
**Engage (5 min):** Hook student interest and activate prior knowledge. Use a question, prop, provocation, or brief activity that connects to students' experiences. The goal is curiosity and readiness to learn.

**Explore (15 min):** Students investigate the concept hands-on, often in pairs or small groups. They manipulate materials, test ideas, and develop questions. The teacher observes and asks probing questions without explaining yet.

**Explain (10 min):** The teacher consolidates what emerged from exploration. Students share findings, the teacher introduces academic vocabulary, and direct instruction fills gaps. This is where concepts are named and clarified.

**Elaborate (15 min):** Students apply the concept to a new context or problem. They extend their understanding and make connections to other areas. This deepens transfer.

**Evaluate (5 min):** Formative or summative assessment. Students demonstrate understanding in some form — verbal explanation, written response, practical demonstration, or exit ticket.

**Adaptations:** Humanities and English teachers can replace "Explore" with close reading or source analysis. Maths teachers can use it for investigation-based lessons. The 5Es remain the spine.

### WIEP Framework
A planning and reflection tool for every lesson:

- **W — What is the learning intention?**
  State it in student language: "Today we are learning to [verb + content]." Avoid vague statements like "understand fractions." Be specific: "Today we are learning to add fractions with unlike denominators." Display it visibly and refer back to it.

- **I — Implementation — how will you teach it?**
  Detail the learning sequence, strategies, resources, and differentiation. What will you do, what will students do, how long will each phase take? This is your lesson map.

- **E — Evaluation — how will you know they've learned?**
  Describe how you will gather evidence of learning during and after the lesson. What will students say, write, or do that shows they've met the intention? This keeps assessment purposeful.

- **P — Planning — activities and resources**
  List specific activities, materials, worksheets, digital tools, and any prep needed. Include timing for each activity and any grouping structures.

**Use it for:** planning, self-reflection, peer observation feedback, and mentor discussions. It's especially useful for pre-lesson planning and post-lesson reflection.

### Direct Instruction
**Best for:** new concepts, literacy/numeracy foundations, Years 1-3, skill acquisition where there is a known correct procedure.

**Structure:**
1. **Warm-up (3-5 min):** Quick retrieval practice — review prior knowledge that connects to today's content. Activates memory and sets expectations.
2. **I Do — Model (10-15 min):** Explicit demonstration of the skill. Think aloud extensively — show all the decisions, not just the steps. Use a gradual release within this phase: teacher does, then teacher + students together, then students try with support.
3. **We Do — Guided Practice (15-20 min):** Shared work. Students try with teacher guidance. Frequent checking for understanding. Error-correct immediately.
4. **You Do — Independent Practice (10-15 min):** Students work alone. Teacher circulates. Collect evidence of learning.
5. **Closing (3-5 min):** Review what was learned, connect to next lesson, celebrate progress.

**Key features:** High teacher involvement, clear explanations, minimal ambiguity, immediate feedback. Do NOT confuse direct instruction with boring lectures — good direct instruction is interactive, responsive, and engaging.

---

### Inquiry-Based Learning
**Best for:** open-ended projects, Years 4-6, HASS, Science, Design Technologies, any unit where students drive the investigation.

**Structure:**
1. **Wonder:** Students generate questions about a topic or phenomenon. The teacher curates and organises these into investigation paths. This builds ownership and intrinsic motivation.
2. **Investigate:** Students research, experiment, observe, survey, or analyse using multiple sources and methods. The teacher facilitates and guides, providing resources and questioning.
3. **Construct:** Students build their understanding or product — a written response, model, presentation, solution, or creative output. They synthesise what they've learned.
4. **Reflect:** Students examine their learning process — what worked, what didn't, what they'd do differently. Metacognition is central here.
5. **Present:** Students share their findings or products with an audience — peers, another class, parents, or a digital platform. Authentic audience elevates quality.

**Teacher role in inquiry:** Facilitator, curator of resources, questioner, connector — not the primary source of information. Resist the urge to lecture during investigate phases.

**Scaffolding inquiry:** Years 1-3 need more structure — provide graphic organisers, sentence stems, and more teacher direction. Years 4-6 can handle more open inquiry with longer timeframes.

## CLASSROOM INSTRUCTION PRINCIPLES

### Explicit Teaching
The evidence base for explicit teaching is strong. It works particularly well for novices, students with learning difficulties, and when teaching complex or unfamiliar content.

**Core practices:**
- **State the learning intention clearly:** "Today we are learning to..." Write it on the board. Refer back to it at the end. Students need to know the destination.
- **Break skills into small, sequential steps:** Don't present a complex skill as one monolithic thing. Sequence the sub-skills. Teach each one, then chain them.
- **Model each step with think-alouds:** Show your expert thinking. "Now I'm going to... because..." Verbalise the decision-making process, not just the procedure.
- **Check for understanding every 5-7 minutes:** Don't wait until the end of a 60-minute lesson to discover students are lost. Use questioning, observation, mini-whiteboards, thumbs, exit tickets.
- **Provide immediate, specific feedback:** Tell students exactly what they did correctly or incorrectly and what to do next. "You've swapped the numerator and denominator — remember, the denominator tells us the total number of parts."

**When explicit teaching is NOT enough:**
Explicit teaching efficiently transmits knowledge, but it doesn't develop deep understanding on its own. Combine it with guided and independent practice, and follow up with application in new contexts. Students need to do the thinking, not just listen to the teacher think.

---

### Checking for Understanding (CFU)
CFU should be woven throughout every lesson, not reserved for the end. Use a mix of the following:

- **Thumbs up/down/sideways:** Quick, whole-class check. Ask a yes/no or agree/disagree question. Thumbs sideways means uncertainty — use it.
- **Mini whiteboards:** Students write responses on individual whiteboards and hold them up simultaneously. Allows you to see every student's response. Excellent for maths, vocab, sentence construction.
- **Turn and talk (Pair Share):** "Turn to your partner and explain..." Use for processing complex ideas, justifying reasoning, or discussing a text. Monitor pairs while they talk.
- **Exit tickets:** 1-3 questions answered on a card or sticky note as students leave. Review before the next lesson. "What did you learn today? What are you still unsure about?"
- **Cold calling (with care):** Select a student randomly — not volunteers. Create a safe environment where wrong answers are normal and part of learning. Use cold call to maintain engagement, not to catch people out.
- **No hands up (random selection):** Prevents the same students dominating. Use raffle sticks, random name generators, or playing cards. All students must be ready because any could be called.
- **Traffic light cards / fingers:** Students hold up coloured cards or show fingers (1-5 confidence level) to indicate understanding without public exposure.
- **Observation:** Watch students' facial expressions, body language, and work during independent practice. Some of your best CFU is observational.

**Design CFU to reveal misconceptions**, not just recall. Ask "Why?" and "How do you know?" to get at reasoning, not just answers.

---

### Feedback Principles
**Timely:** Give feedback during the lesson (verbal, written on work samples) or same-day (marking returned promptly). Delayed feedback loses its power, especially for younger students.

**Specific:** "Your introduction clearly states your three main points and the historical context you provided helps the reader understand why the event mattered." Tell them WHAT was good and WHY.

**Forward-looking (feed forward):** "Now that you've established your argument clearly, your next step is to strengthen your evidence. Consider adding a primary source that directly contradicts your claim — that will show deeper analysis."

**Feed up:** Acknowledge achievement — name what was accomplished. "You've mastered the structure of an argument essay. Your paragraphs are now consistently well-structured."

**Feed forward:** The next step for improvement. "To move from proficient to excellent, you need to work on varying your sentence length and integrating quotes more smoothly."

**Written feedback best practices:**
- Mark one or two things well and one or two things to improve per piece
- Use symbols and codes for consistency (spelling, punctuation, structure)
- Students should have an opportunity to respond to feedback and act on it
- Follow up — check that feedback was acted upon

### Classroom Culture
Culture is the invisible architecture of your classroom. It determines whether your instructional practices land.

**High expectations:** Every student can learn. Every student deserves rigorous, engaging work. Avoid lowering expectations for students based on prior achievement or background. Communicate belief in every interaction.

**Growth mindset language:**
- Replace "I'm not good at this" with "I'm not good at this yet."
- Use "Not yet" instead of "No" for incomplete work.
- "Developing" — acknowledge current state without defining potential.
- Praise effort, strategy, and progress — not just ability.
- Normalise mistakes as part of learning: "Mistakes are how our brains grow."

**Mistake-friendly classrooms:** When a student makes an error, treat it as a learning opportunity — yours and the class's. "That's interesting — let's look at why that happened." This reduces anxiety and increases risk-taking.

**Student voice and agency:**
- Give students choices where possible (topic, method, grouping, timing).
- Invite input on classroom norms and consequences.
- Let students lead discussions, presentations, and peer feedback.
- Respond to student ideas and build on them.

**Consistency and predictability:** Students feel safe when they know the routine. The first five minutes set the tone. The last five minutes close cleanly. Routines run the lesson — not the teacher's voice.

## BEHAVIOUR MANAGEMENT

### The First 5 Minutes
The beginning of every lesson is the most important window for behaviour management. What happens in the first five minutes predicts the rest.

**Greet students at the door:** Make eye contact, smile, say their name. This personal connection immediately reduces low-level behaviour issues. It communicates: "I see you. You belong here."

**Settling routine (consistency is key):**
Choose a signal that always means "focus time" and practise it until it's automatic. Examples:
- "Hands on heads, eyes on me" — everyone stops, looks.
- "1-2-3, eyes on me" — teacher counts down.
- Silent signal — hand up, wait for silence.
- Bell, chime, or visual timer cue.

**Do NOT begin instruction until you have silence.** Rewarding incomplete transitions with instruction teaches students that they don't need to settle. Wait. It's worth it.

**Daily schedule/review of expectations:**
- Display the lesson agenda or WALT (We Are Learning To) visibly.
- Briefly state the sequence: "Today we'll do X, then Y, then Z."
- Remind of any specific behavioural expectations for the lesson's activities.

**Warm-up activity ready from the moment they sit down:**
- Students should start working before you speak. A task on the board, a worksheet on the desk, a digital activity ready to go.
- This eliminates downtime, manages behaviour, and optimises learning time.
- Warm-up should review prior knowledge and activate schema for today's content — not busywork.

---

### Proactive Management
Preventing behaviour issues is far more effective than managing them reactively. These strategies work before problems emerge.

**Routine and ritual:**
Every recurring event in your classroom should have a routine:
- How to enter the room
- How to submit work
- How to ask for help
- How to transition between activities
- How to pack up at the end of lesson

Teach these explicitly, model them, and reinforce them consistently until they are automatic. Rituals reduce cognitive load for students and free up your mental energy.

**Positive narration:**
- Narrate the behaviour you WANT to see, not the behaviour you don't want.
- "I love how Saraya has her book open and is already on question 2." — this prompts others to do the same without calling anyone out.
- "I can see three groups are ready to share — I can't wait to hear their ideas."
- Publicly notice and appreciate effort, preparation, and cooperation.

**Proximity:**
- Move around the room. Don't stay at the front.
- Stand near students who need extra support during independent work.
- Physical presence prevents most low-level disruption.
- Use movement strategically — not randomly.

**Distinct transitions:**
- Warn before transitions: "In two minutes, we'll be moving to the sharing phase."
- Give clear, single-step instructions for what happens next — not a list.
- Use a transition signal (music, chime, countdown).
- Have materials ready for the next activity before students need them.
- Count students to confirm full attention before dismissing.

---

### Responsive Management
When proactive strategies aren't enough, respond calmly, consistently, and privately.

**Private, not public — redirect quietly first:**
- Walk to the student. Speak quietly. Give them a chance to correct without public shame.
- "I noticed you're finding it hard to focus — what's going on?"
- Only escalate to public consequences if private redirect fails.

**Offer choice (internalisation over compliance):**
- "Would you like to continue working at your desk or would you prefer to move to the quiet study area?"
- "You can have your phone in your bag now, or I can hold it until the end of the lesson — your choice."
- Choice gives students agency and reduces resistance. It also reveals whether the student can self-regulate.

**Natural consequences:**
- "If you haven't finished your draft in class time, it will need to be completed for homework."
- "Because the equipment wasn't handled safely, we'll pause the experiment until everyone is ready to follow the protocol."
- Consequences should be related, respectful, and reasonable — not punitive.

**Escalate only when necessary:**
1. Private redirect → 2. Name the behaviour and its impact → 3. Offer choice + consequence → 4. Move to follow-through (implement the consequence calmly) → 5. Document and follow up → 6. Involve support staff/HOD/parents if required.

**De-escalation (for heightened behaviour):**
- Lower your voice, not your authority.
- Give space and time.
- Avoid physical confrontation.
- Focus on safety, not winning.
- Remove audience where possible.
- Agree on a path forward before moving on.

**Document everything.** Keep a log of repeated behaviours, strategies tried, and outcomes. This protects you and informs support requests.

## DIFFERENTIATION (Overview — full skill is separate)

Differentiation is not about creating separate lessons for every student. It's about providing the right level of challenge and support so that every student makes progress.

### Four dimensions of differentiation:

**1. Content — What students learn**
Same concept, different depth or complexity:
- Some students work on foundational skills while others extend
- Tiered tasks (challenging, standard, supportive)
- Compact or extend based on prior knowledge

Example in maths: Adding fractions:
- Support group: uses visual manipulatives, same denominators
- Standard group: unlike denominators, word problems
- Extension group: mixed numbers, multi-step problems

**2. Process — How they learn it**
Scaffolding and support adjusted to student needs:
- Graphic organisers
- Sentence stems and vocabulary support
- Small group instruction
- Peer tutoring or collaborative structures
- Pre-teaching key vocabulary

**3. Product — How they show what they know**
Varied output options allow students to demonstrate understanding in different ways:
- Written report vs. oral presentation vs. visual poster vs. video
- Word limits adjusted
- Scaffolded templates vs. open-ended format
- Allow use of technology for students with writing difficulties

**4. Environment — Where and how they learn**
- Flexible seating arrangements
- Quiet study zones vs. collaborative spaces
- Small group instruction area
- Independent workstations
- Choice of working alone or with peers

**Differentiation in practice:**
- Use data (formative assessment, prior results) to group flexibly — not fixed ability groups always
- Differentiation is most effective in the We Do / guided practice phase
- Independent practice can be differentiated by offering choice boards or tiered tasks
- Regularly review grouping and support based on progress
- Not every lesson needs full differentiation across all four dimensions

- **5.2:** Use assessment data to provide feedback that is timely, explicit, and forward-looking
- **5.3:** Understand and apply the school's reporting policy and guidelines
- **5.4:** Understand the purpose of and provide meaningful feedback that guides students' learning
- **5.5:** Understand and apply moderation of student assessment to ensure consistent judgement

**Practical alignment:**
- Build in CFU throughout every lesson (Standard 5.1)
- Provide specific, timely feedback that tells students what to do next (Standard 5.2, 5.4)
- Use exit tickets to gather evidence for reporting (Standard 5.1, 5.3)
- Align assessment tasks with the learning intention (Standard 5.5)

**Year levels:**
- Kindergarten (K) — optional, typically 3-4 year olds
- Pre-Primary (PP) — compulsory before Year 1

**School terms (approximate — check current DOE calendar):**

- Term 2: Mid-April to late June

**Note:** WA's school calendar often differs from eastern states by 1-2 weeks. Always check the current year's schedule.

### WA Quality Teaching Model (QTM)
Developed in WA and used across Australian schools. The QTM identifies three dimensions of quality teaching:

**Dimension 1 — Environment**
- Supportive environment (students feel safe, known, valued)
- Explicit quality criteria (students know what quality looks like)
- Student self-regulation (students manage their own behaviour and learning)

**Dimension 2 — Teaching**
- Background knowledge (teacher knows the content deeply)
- Learning environment (physically and psychologically safe)
- Narrative (lessons are coherent and meaningful, connected to students' lives)
- Deep knowledge (content is explored in depth, not superficially)
- Higher-order thinking (students apply, analyse, evaluate, create)

**Dimension 3 — Rigour**
- Schema formation (students build mental frameworks for understanding)
- Problematic knowledge (students engage with complexity, uncertainty, different perspectives)
- Metalanguage (students use academic language to discuss learning)
- Low-risk environment (students feel safe to take risks and make mistakes)

**How QTM informs lesson planning:** When planning, ask yourself:
- Is the environment safe and supportive?
- Are students clear on what quality looks like?
- Is there genuine higher-order thinking, not just recall?
- Are students being asked to engage with complex, possibly contested ideas?
- Is academic language being explicitly taught and used?

### Western Australian Certificate of Education (WACE)
Students in Years 11 and 12 work toward the WACE. Key features:

**WACE requirements (current — check SCSA for updates):**
- Complete at least 20 units (typically 4 Year 11 courses = 8 units; 4 Year 12 courses = 8 units, with 4 units remaining)
- Complete at least 4 Year 12 units
- Meet literacy and numeracy standards (typically through OLNA — Online Literacy and Numeracy Assessment)
- Complete at least one course from List A (English/Humanities/Arts/Languages) and one from List B (Maths/Science/Technologies)

**ATAR vs. General vs. Foundation courses:**
- **ATAR courses:** Exam-based, scaled for university entrance. Most competitive entry courses.
- **General courses:** Portfolio/school-based assessment only. Recognised for WACE but not ATAR.
- **Foundation courses:** For students working below WACE standard. WACE-recognised but not ATAR.
- **VET industry-specific courses:** Vocational education and training, can count toward WACE

**WACE breadth and depth:** Students are encouraged to study across different learning areas, not specialise too early.

### First Steps (Numeracy and Literacy)
First Steps is a resource used widely in WA (and nationally) to map the development of literacy and numeracy skills. It identifies phases of development:

**First Steps in Literacy:**
- Reading: Decoding → Orthographic → Syntactic → Semantic → Pragmatic knowledge
- Writing: Foundational → Recording → Consolidating → Extending
- Spelling: Phases from emergent to proficient
- Oral Language: Listening → Speaking

**First Steps in Numeracy:**
- Mathematical development phases: Emergent → Perceptual → Figurative → Counting → Facile
- Number sense, measurement, space, statistics, probability, algebra

**How to use First Steps:**
- Use the developmental frameworks to identify where individual students are at
- Plan instruction that targets the next phase
- Avoid teaching concepts above a student's current phase without building foundations first
- Use First Steps as a diagnostic tool, not a rigid curriculum

### DSR — Directly Support Reading
DSR is a WA-specific reading intervention program used in many WA primary schools. It is designed for students in Years 3-6 who are struggling with reading.

**DSR approach:**
- Explicit, systematic phonics instruction
- Multi-sensory techniques
- Small group or individual intervention
- Daily sessions (typically 30-45 minutes)
- Progress monitoring every 5 sessions
- Structured scope and sequence

**Who DSR is for:**
Students who have completed initial literacy instruction (Prep-Year 2 phonics programs) but still struggle to decode fluently. DSR is not a replacement for core literacy instruction — it supplements it.

**Classroom teacher's role:**
- Identify students who may need DSR through reading data
- Communicate with DSR specialist/support staff
- Integrate DSR strategies into classroom reading instruction
- Monitor progress and communicate with parents

### First 100 Days — WA Induction Framework
The First 100 Days framework supports new WA teachers (particularly those in their first year or new to WA government schools). It provides a structured progression:

**Days 1-20 — Orient and Survive:**
- Learn school systems, policies, and culture
- Meet key staff and support networks
- Learn class profiles and individual student needs
- Begin building relationships with students
- Focus on basic lesson delivery

**Days 21-50 — Settle and Learn:**
- Develop and refine classroom management
- Establish routines and procedures
- Begin collecting assessment data
- Engage with the school community (parents, carers)
- Start differentiating for individual students

**Days 51-100 — Grow and Reflect:**
- Deepen pedagogical content knowledge
- Use data to inform teaching
- Engage in professional learning communities
- Seek and act on feedback
- Develop a reflective practice habit

**Key message for PickleNickAI:** When generating resources or advice for early-career teachers, calibrate to their development phase. Someone in Days 1-20 needs basic, actionable guidance. Someone in Days 51-100 can engage with more nuanced pedagogy.

### AI HALLUCINATION SAFEGUARDS (Research-Backed)

PickleNickAI generates curriculum content for Australian teachers. Hallucinations in educational content can cause teachers to waste time, misinform students, or breach curriculum requirements. These safeguards are non-negotiable.

**MIT Research (2024) on reducing hallucinations:**

**1. Chain-of-Thought Reasoning**
Before any output, the agent must:
- Identify the AC9 content descriptor code explicitly
- State what year level and learning area
- Confirm alignment before generating
- If uncertain, say "I'm less confident about..." — do not fabricate

**2. RAG-Anchored Generation (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)**
- Always anchor to the AC9 curriculum codes provided in the prompt
- Do NOT generate content from general knowledge when curriculum codes are given
- If a teacher asks about something outside the loaded curriculum knowledge, flag it

**3. Temperature Control**
- For factual content (rubrics, assessment tasks, unit plans): use precise, constrained outputs
- Avoid creative or speculative interpretations of curriculum requirements
- Default to factual precision, not expressive variation

**4. Structured Prompts (Research: 40% quality improvement)**
When generating content, always apply the AI Prompt Formula:
- **Role:** "You are an Australian primary school teacher..."
- **Task:** "Generate a unit plan for..."
- **Context:** "Year 4, AC9E4LA, 6-week narrative writing unit..."
- **Format:** "Include lesson outlines, timing, AC9 codes, rubric..."

**5. Curriculum Citation Requirement**
Every factual claim in output must include the AC9 code reference. Example:
> "Students will identify the main idea and supporting details (AC9E4LA — Language for interaction)"
Do NOT generate content without citing the relevant AC9 code.

**6. Confidence Flagging Protocol**
If the agent is uncertain about:
- An AC9 code that doesn't exist
- A year-level expectation
- A teaching strategy
It must say: "I'm less confident about [specific item] — verify against official AC9 curriculum." Never fabricate to fill gaps.

**7. Output Disclaimer (Mandatory)**
Every generated unit plan, lesson, rubric, or assessment task must include:
> "Generated by PickleNickAI. Verify alignment against official AC9 curriculum before use."

## TRAUMA-INFORMED PRACTICE

### Why Trauma-Informed Matters in Australian Classrooms
Research from the Australian Child Trauma Study and beyond shows:\n- 1 in 4 Australian children has experienced a potentially traumatic event\n- Trauma affects brain development, memory, attention, and emotional regulation\n- Trauma responses can look like "behaviour problems" — they are not\n- A trauma-informed classroom is a better classroom for ALL students\n
### The Three R's of Trauma-Informed Practice\n1. **Realise** — Recognise how trauma affects students' learning and behaviour\n2. **Recognise** — Identify signs of trauma in students' responses and reactions\n3. **Respond** — Use trauma-informed strategies in teaching and interaction\n
### Key Classroom Strategies\n
**1. Safety and Predictability**\n- Establish consistent daily routines and rituals\n- Give warnings before transitions ("In 2 minutes, we'll pack away...")\n- Use visual schedules, especially for students who need predictability\n- Make your classroom a physical and emotional safe space\n- Follow through on promises and commitments\n
**2. Connection Before Content**\n- Relationship-building before academic demands\n- Greet students by name at the door — eye contact, warmth\n- Take time to "check in" — mood meters, thumbs, how are you feeling?\n- One positive interaction per student per day minimum\n- Students who feel connected are more ready to learn\n
**3. Regulation Before Rigour**\n- Teach emotional regulation skills explicitly ("When you're frustrated, try...")\n- Provide calm-down spaces or zones (not punishment corners)\n- Use sensory breaks — movement, tactile tools, quiet spaces\n- Co-regulation: model calm breathing, regulate together before demanding academic work\n- Recognise when a student is in "fight-flight-freeze" — they cannot access learning in that state\n
**4. Language and Communication**\n- Use calm, neutral tone — avoid commands when possible\n- Offer choices rather than demands ("Would you like to...")\n- Avoid public corrections or calling out — private redirect only\n- Acknowledge effort and progress, not just outcomes\n- Use "I notice..." language ("I notice you're finding this hard — here's what we can do...")\n
**5. Flexibility in Expectations**\n- Allow movement breaks without stigma\n- Accept different forms of output (verbal, written, drawn)\n- Build in sensory regulation time\n- Reduce cognitive load when a student is dysregulated\n- Trauma responses are not choices — they are survival responses\n
**6. Behaviour as Communication**\n- "What's the behaviour telling me?" — not "How do I punish it?"\n- Escalated behaviour often means a need isn't being met\n- Trauma-informed behaviour support = understanding + meeting the need\n- Record behaviour patterns — what triggers it? What helps?\n- Work with wellbeing team, psychologist, support staff\n
### WA-Specific Trauma Resources\n- **Student Support Services (SSS):** WA Department of Education psychologists and student services\n- **Handle With Care:** WA Police and Education partnership for children exposed to domestic violence\n- **Kids' Helpline:** 1800 55 1800 (24/7)\n- **School chaplain or wellbeing coordinator**\n- **Aboriginal Education Officers** — for supporting Aboriginal students with trauma\n

## UNIVERSAL DESIGN FOR LEARNING (UDL)\n
### What is UDL?\nUniversal Design for Learning (CAST, 2018) is a framework for designing flexible learning experiences that work for ALL students — not as an afterthought, but from the start.\n
**Three core principles:**\n\n**1. Multiple Means of ENGAGEMENT (The WHY)**\n- Tap into students' interests and motivations\n- Offer choices to increase ownership\n- Vary levels of challenge and support\n- Foster collaboration and community\n- Use rewards that are meaningful, not generic\n\n**2. Multiple Means of REPRESENTATION (The WHAT)**\n- Present information in multiple formats (text, audio, video, images)\n- Define vocabulary and symbols explicitly\n- Break down complex concepts into smaller parts\n- Highlight patterns and critical features\n- Guide information processing with graphic organisers\n\n**3. Multiple Means of ACTION & EXPRESSION (The HOW)**\n- Allow students to demonstrate learning in different ways\n- Provide digital tools for writing and creating\n- Allow verbal responses vs. written vs. visual\n- Scaffold executive function (planning, organisation, time management)\n- Use rubrics that clarify expectations for different forms of output\n\n### UDL vs. Differentiation — What's the Difference?\n\n| Aspect | Differentiation | UDL |\n|--------|----------------|-----|\n| **Approach** | Reactive — change something after it's not working | Proactive — design flexibly from the start |\n| **Target** | Students with identified needs | ALL students |\n| **Timing** | Teacher adjusts per student/group | Built into every lesson design |\n| **Effort** | More effort per student | Less effort once designed |\n\n**Best practice:** Use BOTH. UDL as the foundation (design), differentiation as targeted support for specific students.\n\n### UDL Strategies for Australian Primary Classrooms\n\n**Representation (Input):**\n- Display content in text AND video AND audio\n- Use visual timelines, graphic organisers, concept maps\n- Pre-teach vocabulary with images and examples\n- Highlight key words in texts (bold, colour)\n- Provide anchor charts that remain visible\n\n**Engagement (Motivation):**\n- Offer student choice in topics, products, groupings\n- Gamify where appropriate (points, challenges, competition)\n- Connect content to students' lives and interests\n- Build in collaboration — peer learning structures\n- Vary the level of challenge (build in support AND extension)\n\n**Action & Expression (Output):**\n- Allow verbal explanations vs. written vs. visual diagrams\n- Use digital tools (Padlet, Seesaw, Canva)\n- Provide sentence starters and writing frames\n- Allow extra time for some, not all\n- Use rubric checklists so students know the path to success\n\n### UDL and AC9 Alignment\nUDL supports the AC9 general capabilities:\n- Literacy (multiple modes of reading and composing)\n- Numeracy (multiple representations of mathematical ideas)\n- ICT Capability (digital tools for learning)\n- Critical and Creative Thinking (multiple pathways to demonstrate thinking)\n---
