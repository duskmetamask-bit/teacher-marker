## 1. RUBRIC DESIGN

### What to Assess — The Criteria Principle

**Never assess everything.** Choose 3–5 criteria maximum per task. Spread across:
- **Knowledge** — does the student know the content?
- **Application** — can they use what they know?
- **Skill/Process** — can they perform the target skill?
- **Communication** — can they express their thinking?

> **Rule:** If you're marking more than 5 criteria, you're marking compliance — not learning.

---

### Descriptors — What Makes Them Work

Descriptors must be:
- **Specific** — not "good writing" but "uses vivid verbs to create atmosphere"
- **Observable** — written behaviours a teacher can actually see in the work
- **Distinct** — each grade level is clearly different from the next
- **Non-ambiguous** — two teachers marking the same work should land on the same grade

**Bad descriptor:** "Student shows good understanding"
**Good descriptor:** "Explains the main idea using student's own words and provides at least one supporting detail from the text"

---

### Marking Scales

| Scale | Used For | Grades |
|-------|----------|--------|
| **A–E (WA Standard)** | Most tasks, all year levels | A, B, C, D, E |
| **1–4 (US Standard)** | Some US curricula, standards-based grading | 4=Exceeds, 3=Proficient, 2=Developing, 1=Beginning |
| **0–10 (Numerical)** | High-stakes exams, rubrics with precise calibration | 0–10 per criterion or overall |

> **PickleNick Default: A–E** — This is the WA standard and your primary scale.

---

### WA A–E Standard Descriptors by Year Level

These are the **system-level descriptors** SCSA uses as the anchor. Your task rubrics should align with these.

| Grade | Descriptor (all year levels, adjusted for task) |
|-------|------------------------------------------------|
| **A** | *Demonstrates comprehensive understanding of the concept/skill. Applies knowledge in new and unfamiliar contexts with high accuracy and sophistication. Shows a well-developed, consistent and refined skill level across the task. Meets all requirements of the task at a level that significantly exceeds the standard expected.* |
| **B** | *Demonstrates clear understanding of the concept/skill. Applies knowledge appropriately in familiar and some unfamiliar contexts with accuracy. Shows solid skill development with consistent technique. Meets requirements of the task with only minor gaps or omissions.* |
| **C** | *Demonstrates satisfactory understanding of the concept/skill. Applies knowledge in familiar contexts with adequate accuracy. Shows adequate skill development that meets the core requirements of the task. Some errors or omissions present but does not impact overall purpose.* |
| **D** | *Demonstrates partial understanding of the concept/skill. Shows developing skill with some accurate application. Meets some requirements of the task. Significant errors or omissions present that impact the overall quality or completion of the task.* |
| **E** | *Demonstrates limited understanding of the concept/skill. Skills require significant teacher support to demonstrate. Few requirements of the task are met. Significant inaccuracies and omissions across the response.* |

> **Adjust the language** for year level — a Year 3 "comprehensive understanding" looks different from a Year 6 one. Use simpler vocabulary and shorter expectations for lower primary.

---

### Holistic vs Analytic Rubrics

| | **Holistic** | **Analytic** |
|--|--|--|
| **What it is** | One overall grade, impression-marked | Separate grades per criterion |
| **Best for** | Quick screening, low-stakes tasks, revision drafts | Summative assessment, complex tasks, reporting |
| **Pros** | Fast, holistic sense of quality | Precise feedback, diagnostic |
| **Cons** | Less feedback, can feel unfair to students | Time-consuming, can fragment the task |
| **Example** | "This is a strong B — clear ideas but needs more evidence" | "Structure B, Argument C+, Vocabulary A-" |

> **PickleNick tip:** Use **analytic rubrics to mark, holistic to report.** Mark against criteria so you can give specific feedback, but convert to a single A–E for the report card.

## 2. A–E GRADE DESCRIPTORS (Detailed)

### What Each Grade Means in Practice

#### A — Excellent

**Defining traits:**
- Demonstrates comprehensive and sophisticated understanding of the concept
- Applies knowledge in new, unfamiliar, or complex contexts — not just formulaic repetition
- Skill is well-developed, refined, and consistently applied across the whole task
- Meets ALL requirements of the task, often at a level beyond what was taught
- Work stands on its own — no significant errors, omissions, or gaps
- Shows initiative, insight, or original thinking

**In the work, you'll see:**
- Accurate, confident use of terminology
- Multi-step application without prompting
- Connections to real-world or unseen contexts
- Evidence of pride and attention to detail

**Watch out for:** Don't grade up because the work is neat or the student is "high achieving." An A must earn it on the task rubric.

---

#### B — Strong

**Defining traits:**
- Demonstrates clear and solid understanding — not just surface recall
- Applies knowledge appropriately in familiar contexts, with some extension to new ones
- Skill level is consistent and solid throughout
- Meets almost all requirements — minor gaps or omissions only
- Shows strong technique with negligible errors that don't affect meaning or outcomes

**In the work, you'll see:**
- Accurate use of key concepts and terminology
- Consistent application of the skill or process
- Only small, inconsequential errors
- Work that clearly meets the standard

**Watch out for:** Don't use B as a "almost an A" grade. A B stands on its own — it's solid, quality work with minor room to grow.

---

#### C — Satisfactory

**Defining traits:**
- Demonstrates satisfactory understanding — the core is there but not deeply developed
- Applies knowledge in familiar contexts adequately
- Shows adequate skill development — meets the basic requirements
- Some errors or omissions present but they don't destroy the purpose of the task
- Work is completed and achieves its basic purpose

**In the work, you'll see:**
- Core concepts present but not always precisely explained
- Basic technique — gets the job done but not refined
- Some errors that occasionally affect meaning or outcomes
- Task requirements met at the minimum standard

**Watch out for:** C is the **standard** — not a failure. This is the expected year-level standard. Don't mark too hard and push satisfactory work to a D.

---

#### D — Developing

**Defining traits:**
- Demonstrates partial understanding — significant gaps in comprehension
- Shows developing skill — can do some parts but not the whole task
- Meets only some requirements
- Significant errors or omissions that impact the overall quality or completeness
- Work is incomplete or inaccurate in notable ways

**In the work, you'll see:**
- Some correct elements but work is inconsistent
- Missing key components of the task
- Errors that frequently affect meaning or outcomes
- Work that looks rushed or shows conceptual confusion

**Watch out for:** Don't use D as a "gentle fail." If the work genuinely only partially meets requirements, D is correct. But don't D-mark work that's actually satisfactory.

---

#### E — Beginning

**Defining traits:**
- Demonstrates limited understanding — little evidence of the concept or skill
- Skills require significant teacher support to demonstrate
- Few requirements of the task are met
- Significant inaccuracies and omissions throughout
- Work is substantially incomplete or fundamentally misunderstands the task

**In the work, you'll see:**
- Minimal attempt at the task
- Major misconceptions or off-topic responses
- Fundamental inaccuracies
- Work that requires re-teaching of the concept

**Watch out for:** E should be rare and meaningful. It signals the student needs substantial intervention, not just "more effort."

## 3. FEEDBACK WRITING

### The Feedback Framework: Feed Up / Feed Forward / Feed Back

| Type | Question | Purpose |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Feed UP** | Where are we going? | Clarify the goal — what does success look like? |
| **Feed BACK** | How did we do? | Identify what was done well and what needs work |
| **Feed FORWARD** | Where to next? | Give one specific, actionable next step |

> **Best feedback contains all three** — but you can prioritise Feed Forward for daily marking and Feed Up for unit launch.

---

### The PickleNick Feedback Rules

**1. Be Specific — Cite Evidence**
> ❌ "Great job!"
> ✅ "Your opening paragraph hooks the reader straight away — 'The moment she walked in, everything changed.' That's strong. I'd keep that energy into paragraph 2."

> ❌ "You need to improve your punctuation."
> ✅ "Your full stops are mostly correct, but your commas inside lists are missing — e.g., 'She bought apples, oranges, bananas' needs commas before each item."

**Rule:** Name the exact part of the work you're giving feedback on. Students can't act on vague praise or generic criticism.

---

**2. Be Balanced — One Strength + One Next Step**

Keep it simple:
> "You've clearly understood the main character. She's described as 'sneaky and determined' with two strong examples. **Next step:** in your next paragraph, show us HOW she demonstrates these traits through her actions — not just tell us what she's like."

**The 2:1 ratio rule:** Two pieces of positive feedback for every one constructive next step. Students shut down with too much criticism.

---

**3. Be Growth-Oriented — "Next Time Try..."**

> ❌ "Your paragraph is disorganised."
> ✅ "Next time, try putting your topic sentence first, then your evidence, then your explanation. That structure helps readers follow your thinking."

Frame the next step as an opportunity, not a failure.

---

**4. Be Actionable — Student Must Know Exactly What To Do**

> ❌ "Revise your conclusion."
> ✅ "Add one more sentence to your conclusion that answers the question: 'So what? Why does this matter?' It will lift your grade from C to B."

If the student can't do the next step after reading your feedback, your feedback has failed them.

---

### Feedback vs Marking — Which Are You Doing?

| **Marking** | **Feedback** |
|-------------|--------------|
| Evaluates performance | Moves learning forward |
| Assigns a grade | Describes quality |
| Looks backward | Points forward |
| Teacher-focused | Student-focused |
| "You got 12/20" | "Your data analysis was strong — next, challenge yourself to evaluate the reliability of your sources" |
| Done after the fact | Most powerful during the process |

> **Marking without feedback is compliance.** Feedback without marking is coaching. The best teachers do both — mark the work, then write the feedback to move that student forward.

## 4. FORMATIVE vs SUMMATIVE ASSESSMENT

### Formative Assessment — "Checking for Understanding"

**When:** During the learning unit — while students are still developing skills.

**What it looks like:**
- Exit tickets (e.g., "Write one thing you learned today and one question you still have")
- Thumbs up / thumbs down polling
- Draft work — first attempts, mind maps, plans
- Cold calling / targeted questioning during instruction
- Mini-whiteboard responses
- Traffic light cards (green/yellow/red)
- Quick verbal checks ("Turn to your partner and explain...")

**How to mark it:**
- Light. Fast. Usually **no grades**.
- Use a checkmark, a plus/delta, or a brief note
- Rubric not needed — you're checking for understanding, not ranking
- Circle key errors to address in next lesson

**Your role:** Diagnostic. Adjust your teaching based on what you find. If 70% of the class misses the same concept, re-teach it.

---

### Summative Assessment — "Evaluating Learning"

**When:** End of a unit, end of term, end of year — when learning is complete.

**What it looks like:**
- End-of-unit tests
- Extended writing tasks / projects
- Performance tasks
- Portfolios
- Presentations with rubrics
- Comparison tasks (cold vs hot task)

**How to mark it:**
- Full rubric marking — all criteria, all descriptors
- Grades recorded and reported
- Precise, specific feedback
- A–E grade assigned

**Your role:** Evaluative. This is the checkpoint — has the student met the standard?

---

### The Marking Gradient

| Purpose | Speed | Depth | Grades? |
|---------|-------|-------|---------|
| Formative check | Seconds | Glance | No |
| Draft feedback | 2–5 min | Criteria check | Optional |
| Summative mark | 10–20 min | Full rubric | Yes |
| External/moderated | 15–30 min | Deep + cross-reference | Yes |

## 5. COLD TASK vs HOT TASK

### What Is a Cold Task / Hot Task?

**Cold Task:** A pre-assessment task administered BEFORE the unit of instruction. Same task as the hot task. Establishes a baseline — what the student can do before teaching.

**Hot Task:** A post-assessment task administered AFTER the unit. Same task as the cold task. Measures growth — what the student can do after teaching.

**Growth** = The difference between cold and hot task performance.

---

### Why Use Both?

- It makes **student growth visible and measurable**
- It prevents teaching to the test — you're measuring progress, not performance at a single point
- It empowers students to see their own improvement over time
- It gives you data on teaching effectiveness

---

### The Process

```
UNIT BEGINS
    │
    ▼
COLD TASK (same task as hot)
Students attempt without prior instruction
Teacher marks → establishes baseline
    │
    ▼
INSTRUCTION (differentiated to student needs from cold data)
    │
    ▼
HOT TASK (same task as cold)
Students attempt again after instruction
Teacher marks → measures growth
    │
    ▼
REPORT — Cold → Hot growth is documented
    │
    ▼
FEEDBACK — student sees their growth
```

---

### Rules for Cold/Hot Tasks

1. **Same rubric for both** — you must measure against identical criteria to show growth
2. **Don't teach to the cold task** — use the data diagnostically, not to drill the exact task
3. **Mark both fairly** — if a student bombs the cold task but nails the hot, that growth deserves recognition
4. **Show students the comparison** — when students see their cold → hot improvement, motivation skyrockets

## 6. MARKING EFFICIENCY

### Mark Smarter — The PickleNick Efficiency Stack

#### Tier 1: Whole-Class Marking (Fastest)

**Method:** Mark ONE student's work against the rubric. Note what you marked. Apply those annotations to the rest.

**Best for:** When the whole class did the same task and the errors are systemic.

**How:**
1. Mark Student A fully — make notes against each criterion
2. Student B → "Same as A for Structure + Language. Different on Conventions — missing full stops on 3 sentences."
3. Student C → "Same as A and B. Conventions improved — only 1 error."

**Speed gain:** 60–80% faster than marking each individually.

---

#### Tier 2: Rubric-Only Marking

**Method:** Mark ONLY against the rubric criteria. Do NOT correct every error.

**Rule:** Your rubric criteria are what you're assessing. Everything else — minor spelling, handwriting, punctuation in a maths problem-solving task — let it go unless it's your assessed criterion.

**Example — Narrative Writing Rubric:**
- Criterion: Character development → mark character
- Criterion: Language → mark language
- Other errors → note but don't over-mark

If it's not on the rubric, it's not your job to mark it this time.

---

#### Tier 3: Audio Feedback

**Method:** Record a 30–60 second voice note per student using your device's voice memos. Describe what was strong, what needs work, what's the next step.

**Benefits:**
- 3x faster than writing
- More personal — students hear your voice and tone
- Conveys nuance writing can't (enthusiasm, concern, encouragement)
- Great for EAL students who struggle to read written feedback

**PickleNick tip:** Keep a folder of reusable audio feedback phrases you can paste:
- "Great effort today — I can see you're working on..."
- "You've made really strong progress here. Next step is..."
- "I noticed you struggled with [concept]. Let's fix that by..."

---

#### Tier 4: Live Marking During Class

**Method:** Mark in real time while students are still working. Walk the room with the rubric. Mark as you go.

**Best for:** Drafts, practice tasks, early-stage work.

**How it works:**
1. Students start the task
2. You circulate — mark against rubric while they work
3. Give brief verbal feedback on the spot ("Sarah — your structure is A standard here, well done.")
4. For students who need more — pull them aside or note it for later

**Benefits:**
- Students get immediate feedback
- You catch misconceptions before they're baked in
- Reduces post-lesson marking load to near zero

---

#### Tier 5: Don't Mark Everything — Prioritise

**The 80/20 rule for marking:**

| Priority | What to Mark | Why |
|----------|--------------|-----|
| **Always** | Summative assessments | Reporting requirement |
| **Always** | Cold task → Hot task comparisons | Shows growth |
| **Sometimes** | Drafts before final submission | Teaches revision |
| **Rarely** | Every worksheet | Compliance ≠ learning |
| **Never** | Practice sheets, bell activities | Low stakes — student self-assess |

> **Ask yourself:** "If I don't mark this, does anyone learn less?" If no — don't mark it.

## 7. COMMON PITFALLS

### Pitfall 1: Over-Marking

**What it looks like:**
- Correcting every single error on every single page
- Using three colours (red/green/blue) to annotate every mistake
- Writing longer feedback than the student's actual work
- Marking things not on the rubric

**Why it's a problem:**
- Students receive overwhelming feedback and process none of it
- You spend 3 hours marking a stack that took students 20 minutes
- It creates compliance marking — "red pen is teacher happy" — not learning

**Fix:**
> Limit yourself to 2 strengths and 1 next step per piece of work. Stick to rubric criteria. If it's not on the rubric, note it but don't mark it as a failure.

---

### Pitfall 2: Catching Students Making Mistakes

**What it looks like:**
- Interrupting students mid-task to correct every error you see
- Circulating just to find mistakes
- Marking so fast you catch every error as it happens

**Why it's a problem:**
- You interrupt the learning process with a mistake that hasn't had time to consolidate
- Students become risk-averse — "Why bother try if the teacher will just correct me?"
- It makes feedback feel punitive rather than growth-oriented

**Fix:**
> Let students make mistakes during practice. Note them. Address in your feedback or in the next lesson's direct instruction. **Save corrections for after the draft is complete.**

---

### Pitfall 3: Marking for Compliance

**What it looks like:**
- Ticking boxes on a checklist without genuinely assessing quality
- "Great!" on every page regardless of actual quality
- Marking to show you've been there, not to move learning forward
- All students getting the same comments because you're templating

**Why it's a problem:**
- Students learn to game the system — they see the tick and stop reading feedback
- Fake marking is worse than no marking — it creates a false sense of progress
- It wastes your time for zero learning gain

**Fix:**
> Every tick should mean something. If a student sees a tick, they should know exactly why it's there. If you can't explain why it earned that tick — take the tick away.

---

### Pitfall 4: Too Much Feedback

**What it looks like:**
- Page after page of written feedback
- 10 different things to fix
- Feedback that requires a degree to understand
- Students who read your feedback and feel worse than before

**Why it's a problem:**
- Cognitive overload — students can't process 10 things at once
- They default to ignoring all of it
- The feedback becomes noise, not signal

**Fix:**
> **One priority next step.** That's it. If a student can walk away remembering just ONE thing from your feedback, it was a success. Choose the one thing that will make the biggest difference.

## 8. SAMPLE RUBRIC FORMATS

### Rubric A: Narrative Writing (Years 3–4)

**Task:** Write a narrative story with at least 3 paragraphs. Your story should have a beginning, middle, and end. Include at least one character who faces a problem.

| Criterion | A (Excellent) | B (Strong) | C (Satisfactory) | D (Developing) | E (Beginning) |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|-----------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Structure** | Story has a clear, logical sequence with a well-developed beginning, middle, and end. Paragraphs are clearly organised with smooth transitions. | Story has clear beginning, middle, and end. Paragraphs are organised and transitions are mostly effective. | Story has recognisable beginning, middle, and end. Basic paragraph structure present with some organisational issues. | Structure is unclear or incomplete. Beginning, middle, or end missing. Paragraphs lack organisation. | No clear structure. Story is disorganised or substantially incomplete. |
| **Character** | At least one well-developed character with clear traits, motivations, and actions. Reader understands the character's perspective. | Character is clearly described with at least 2–3 traits. Character's actions are connected to their traits. | Character is present with at least one identifiable trait. Character acts in ways that are mostly consistent. | Character is mentioned but traits are unclear or contradictory. Character's actions don't reflect their described traits. | Character is absent or so minimally developed the story lacks a narrative focus. |

Language creates interest. | Adequate language with mostly appropriate word choices. Simple descriptions used to convey meaning. Some repetitive or basic language. | Language is basic or repetitive. ## 9. RESEARCH-BACKED FEEDBACK FRAMEWORKS

### Hattie & Timperley (2007) — The Model That Works
The most cited feedback research in education. Four levels of feedback:

| Level | Question | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Task level** | "How well am I doing?" | "Your introduction clearly states your argument." |
| **Process level** | "How am I doing it?" | "Your paragraph transitions show you understand the structure." |
| **Self-regulation** | "How can I improve?" | "Next time, try checking your rubric before you submit." |
| **Self** | (Avoid) | "Great work!" — too vague, no actionable info |

**Most effective feedback:** Moves from task level → process level → self-regulation. Avoid self-level feedback — it doesn't tell students what to do differently.

### Visible Thinking (Harvard Project Zero)
Feedback should make thinking visible:
- **Describe the thinking:** "I can see you analyzed the character's motivations here..."
- **Compare to a standard:** "That's a B-level paragraph. To reach A, you'd need..."
- **Ask a question:** "What would happen if you moved this evidence to paragraph 2?"

### Rosenshine's Principles of Instruction
Feedback aligns with Principle 9 (instruction) and Principle 10 (regular reviews):
- Give timely, specific feedback on correct and incorrect reasoning
- Build in opportunities for students to revise their work based on feedback
- Use cold/hot task comparison to show growth (directly visible feedback)

## 10. PAT SCALED SCORES — INTERPRETATION GUIDE

### What Are PAT Scaled Scores?
PAT (Progressive Achievement Tests) by ACER use **scaled scores** that allow comparison across year levels and over time. Unlike raw scores, scaled scores account for the difficulty of the test and student growth.

**Key principle:** A scaled score is NOT a percentage. It is a continuous scale where:
- The same scaled score represents the same level of achievement regardless of year level
- Growth can be tracked across years using the same scale
- Schools can compare cohort performance against national norms

### PAT-R (Reading) — Score Interpretation

| Scaled Score Range | Interpretation | Instructional Implication |
|--------------------|----------------|-------------------------|
| **Below 100** | Beginning reading — below typical for year level | Immediate intervention, decodable texts, phonological awareness focus |
| **100–110** | Developing — working towards typical standard | Targeted small-group support, explicit comprehension strategy instruction |
| **110–130** | Typical/Average — at expected standard for year level | Quality core instruction, guided reading, comprehension strategy focus |
| **130–140** | Strong — above typical, working towards upper bands | Extension tasks, richer texts, independent analysis |
| **Above 140** | Very High — significantly above year level standard | Acceleration options, literature circles with complex texts, independent inquiry |

**WA Context:** NAPLAN Year 3 and 5 have similar bands. PAT-R scores can predict NAPLAN band placement roughly.

### PAT-M (Mathematics) — Score Interpretation

| Scaled Score Range | Interpretation | Instructional Implication |
|--------------------|----------------|--------------------------|
| **Below 100** | Beginning — needs intensive support | Concrete manipulatives, re-teach foundations, diagnostic assessment |
| **100–110** | Developing — working towards standard | Explicit instruction with scaffolding, procedural fluency focus |
| **110–130** | Typical — at expected standard | Core instruction, problem-solving emphasis, varied practice |
| **130–140** | Strong — above typical | Problem-solving challenges, open-ended investigations, peer tutoring |
| **Above 140** | Very High — significantly above standard | Extension mathematics, competition problems, inquiry projects |

### Using PAT Data for Differentiation

**Step 1: Identify the student's zone** (Beginning / Developing / Typical / Strong / Very High)
**Step 2: Plan backwards from the standard**
- Beginning: What foundation skills are missing? Build those first.
- Developing: Target the gap between current and year-level standard.
- Typical: Deepen understanding with complex applications.
- Strong/Very High: Extend beyond the year-level standard.

**Step 3: Use the data to set goals**
- One specific, measurable goal per term
- E.g., "PAT-R from 105 to 115 — improve inferential comprehension"
- Share goal with student (age-appropriate)

**Step 4: Monitor progress**
- PAT is one data point — use alongside teacher judgement and formative assessment
- Re-assess mid-year if available or use school-based assessments

### Important Caveats About PAT
- **One test, one day** — PAT scores are snapshots, not definitions of a student
- **Test-taking factors** — anxiety, fatigue, unfamiliarity with test format can suppress scores
- **Alignment:** PAT items may not perfectly align with AC9 content
- **Use in combination:** PAT + teacher judgement + Brightpath + formative assessment = complete picture
