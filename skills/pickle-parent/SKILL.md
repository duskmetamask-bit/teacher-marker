## PURPOSE

This skill equips teachers to communicate effectively with parents and guardians — from everyday updates to challenging conversations. It covers proactive relationship-building, structured interviews, written communication, difficult conversations, diverse family contexts, privacy obligations, and WA-specific resources.

Use this skill whenever you need to:
- Prepare for parent-teacher interviews
- Write reports (A–E, WA system)
- Respond to an upset or difficult parent
- Communicate with culturally or linguistically diverse families
- Navigate privacy, confidentiality, or mandatory reporting
- Build ongoing parent engagement

## SECTION 1 — PARENT COMMUNICATION PRINCIPLES

### Build Trust Before You Need It

Parents are more receptive to difficult news if they already trust you. Trust is built through:
- **Regular, positive contact** — not just when something goes wrong
- **Visible accessibility** — brief conversations at pick-up, open-door policy notes in newsletters
- **Consistent follow-through** — if you say you'll do something, do it and let them know

> **Example:** At the start of the year, send a welcome message: *"I'm looking forward to working with [child] this year. My door is always open — feel free to message me on Seesaw or email me at [address] with any questions."*

### Be Proactive With Good News

A common mistake is only contacting parents when there's a problem. Flip this:
- Send a positive Seesaw post about something their child did well this week
- Flag a moment of growth: *" [Child] really nailed that concept today — proud of them!"*
- Announce upcoming topics so parents can chat with their child about it at home

Parents who hear from you regularly are far more receptive when you do need to raise a concern.

### Cultural Sensitivity

- **Don't assume family structures** — not all homes have two parents; not all parents have the same language; not all families operate the same way
- **Ask about communication preferences** — some parents prefer email, others Seesaw or paper notes
- **Be aware of cultural norms around authority, questioning teachers, and privacy**
- **Respect cultural obligations** — sorry business, NAIDOC week, religious observances

### Document Everything

Keep records of:
- Date and method of contact (email, phone, Seesaw, in-person)
- What was discussed
- Any agreed actions and who is responsible
- Follow-up completed

> This protects you, ensures continuity if another teacher is involved, and supports school reporting requirements.

### Use School-Approved Channels Only

- Never use personal social media or messaging apps to communicate with parents
- Use Seesaw, school email, school diary, or approved platforms only
- If a parent messages you on a non-approved channel, redirect politely: *"I prefer to keep all school communication through Seesaw or email — it's better for our records!"*

## SECTION 2 — PARENT-TEACHER INTERVIEWS

### Before the Interview

- Review the student's file: academic progress, behaviour notes, any previous parent concerns
- Prepare 2–3 work samples that show both strengths and areas for growth
- Note specific, observable behaviours — avoid vague generalisations
- Have a suggested goal or focus area ready, but be open to parent input
- Confirm booking and remind parents of time (5–10 minutes is standard)

### Structuring the Interview

| Phase | Time | What to Do |
|-------|------|------------|
| Open | 1–2 min | Welcome, thank them for coming, ask an open question |
| Explore | 2–3 min | Listen — let the parent share their perspective |
| Share | 2–3 min | Present work samples, observations, progress |
| Collaborate | 2 min | Set a shared goal or next steps |
| Close | 1 min | Summarise, confirm follow-up actions, invite questions |

### Opening Questions

Start with something open — never begin with a problem:
- *"How does [child] feel about school this year?"*
- *"What's been working well at home with learning?"*
- *"What are you most proud of about [child]'s progress?"*
- *"What would you like to see more of or less of?"*

### Sharing Work Samples

**Show, don't just tell.** Bring 2–3 pieces of work:
- One that shows a strength
- One that shows an area of growth
- One that shows something the child is proud of

Walk through each one: *"This is what I see in this piece..."*

### Balancing Strengths and Growth

The sandwich approach works well but don't force it:
- **Top slice:** Start with something genuine and specific — a strength, a moment of growth
- **Middle:** Share what you're working on and why — framed as "here's what we're doing"
- **Bottom slice:** End on a forward-looking note — a specific, achievable goal

> **Avoid:** *"She's great at reading but struggles with maths."* Instead: *"She's showing real confidence in reading — we're building on that by... In maths, here's what we're focusing on..."*

### Setting Collaborative Goals

Use the **"What, how, who"** framework:
- *"What do we want [child] to achieve?"*
- *"How will we know it's working?"*
- *"Who will do what?"*

Write it down and send a follow-up summary.

### When a Parent is Upset or Angry

1. **Stay calm** — don't match their energy
2. **Listen without interrupting** — let them finish
3. **Acknowledge the emotion** — *"I can hear this is really important to you"*
4. **State facts only** — what you observed, what happened, what you did
5. **Collaborate on next steps** — *"What would be helpful going forward?"*
6. **Follow up in writing** — always

> **Do not:** Promise things you can't deliver, argue, dismiss their concern, or make it about the child's character.

## SECTION 3 — WRITTEN COMMUNICATION

### Report Writing (WA A–E Context)

**A–E Reporting (WA Department of Education):**
- **A** — Outstanding achievement relative to the expected standard
- **B** — High achievement relative to the expected standard
- **C** — Satisfactory achievement relative to the expected standard
- **D** — Partial achievement relative to the expected standard
- **E** — Minimal achievement relative to the expected standard

**Writing effective comments:**
- Use the student's name
- Reference specific work or assessment tasks
- Describe what the student can do (not just a grade)
- Identify one or two specific areas for growth
- Avoid jargon — parents are not educators

> **Example:** *"Mia consistently applies her phonics knowledge to decode unfamiliar words in guided reading sessions. She is developing her comprehension skills by making inferences from text. Next focus: using text evidence to support her answers when discussing characters' motivations."*

**Narrative vs checklist:** Many WA schools use a mix. Narratives are preferred for giving genuine insight. Keep paragraphs tight (2–4 sentences per subject). Do not repeat the same comment across subjects.

**Avoid in reports:**
- Vague statements (*"Charlie is working at grade level"*)
- Future tense promises (*"Will improve..."*)
- Comparing to other students
- Emotional language (*"Poor Noah was frustrated..."*)

### Seesaw / Blog Posts

**The 3:1 positive-to-informative ratio:** For every post that flags something to work on, post three celebrating wins.

**Effective Seesaw posts:**
- Photo + caption: show the learning, not just the activity
- Use the child's voice where possible: *"I chose this because..."*
- Tag specific learning goals (e.g., "Uses phonics to decode words")
- Keep it brief — 2–3 sentences max
- Invite family participation: *"Ask your child about..."*

> **Example:** *"Today we explored how authors use descriptive language to build tension. [Child] wrote an incredibly vivid sentence about the storm — ask them what their favourite word choice was!"*

### Newsletters

- Keep it brief — one page max
- Use clear headings, bullet points
- Include visuals where possible
- Lead with what's happening THIS WEEK
- Include a "What we're learning" section in plain language
- Flag upcoming dates with clear actions ("Permission slips due Friday")
- Positive student shout-outs

### Formal Letters

**Structure:**
1. School letterhead / signature block
2. Date
3. Dear [Parent/Guardian] or [Family name]
4. Clear subject line
5. Body — purpose, key information, any action required
6. Sign with name and position

**Tone:** Professional, warm, factual. Avoid threatening language even when following up on non-payment or non-attendance.

> **Example:** *"We are writing to inform you that [child] has been absent for 8 of the last 20 school days. Regular attendance is strongly linked to academic progress. We would like to arrange a meeting to discuss any barriers to attendance and explore how we can support you and [child]. Please contact us on [number] to arrange a convenient time."*

### Email Etiquette

- **Subject line:** Clear, specific — *"Year 3 — [Child's name] — Reading Support Plan"*
- **Tone:** Professional, warm, concise
- **Format:** Short paragraphs, bullet points for lists
- **Timing:** Respond within 2 business days — if you need more time, acknowledge and give a timeline
- **Sensitive content:** Do not send behaviour incidents or sensitive information via email — offer a phone call or meeting instead

### SMS

**Appropriate uses:**
- Reminders ( incurs, events, permission slips due)
- Brief confirmations (appointment times)
- Positive check-ins (with prior consent)

**Not appropriate:**
- Sensitive information (behaviour, welfare concerns)
- Detailed explanations
- Anything you'd be uncomfortable seeing on a screen in 5 years

> **Privacy note:** Never include student names in SMS if sending group messages — use BCC or a messaging platform that maintains privacy.

## SECTION 4 — MANAGING DIFFICULT CONVERSATIONS

### When a Parent is Upset About Their Child

Use this script as a starting framework:

1. **Listen fully** — *"I want to make sure I understand what's been happening. Can you tell me more?"*
2. **Acknowledge emotion** — *"That sounds really frustrating / worrying. I understand."*
3. **State facts** — *"Here's what I've observed..."* (specific, behaviour-based, not interpretative)
4. **Explain actions** — *"What I've done is..."* / *"What we're doing at school is..."*
5. **Collaborate** — *"What do you think would help?"* / *"Let's agree on..."*
6. **Follow up** — Confirm in writing within 48 hours

**Key rule:** Focus on observations and actions, not character judgments. Say *"In maths, [child] has been finding it hard to focus for more than 10 minutes"* — NOT *"Your child can't concentrate."*

### When a Parent Complains About Another Teacher

- **Listen without engaging** — don't agree, don't defend, don't dismiss
- **Acknowledge their concern** — *"Thank you for telling me. I take that seriously."*
- **Do not promise to fix it** — *"I'm not the right person to speak to about that, but here's what I can do..."*
- **Document** — date, time, what was said, what you did
- **Refer appropriately:**
  - Teaching colleague concern → Head of Learning Area / Line manager
  - Serious welfare concern → Principal / Deputy
  - Mandatory reporting threshold → Follow your legal obligations immediately

> **Never promise confidentiality** if what they tell you triggers a mandatory reporting obligation.

### When a Child's Behaviour Is Concerning

**Use the observation-first approach:**
- Describe what you saw: *"In today's maths lesson, [child] was out of his seat four times and called out twice without putting his hand up"*
- NOT: *"He's disruptive and doesn't listen"*

**Frame around collaboration:**
- *"Here's what we're doing at school..."* (not *"Here's what's wrong with your child"*)
- *"What do you notice at home?"*
- *"Let's try [strategy] for two weeks and see how it goes"*

**Suggest professional support when appropriate:**
- *"Have you considered speaking to our school psychologist? I can make a referral if that would be helpful"*
- *"Sometimes families find it useful to..."*
- Be careful not to diagnose or suggest you know more than you do — **refer, don't diagnose**

### Following Up After Difficult Conversations

Always follow up in writing — email or Seesaw message — even if the conversation went well. Include:
- Date and method of conversation
- Key points discussed
- Agreed actions (who does what, by when)
- Invitation to contact you if anything changes

## SECTION 5 — PARENT ENGAGEMENT STRATEGIES

### Volunteer Programs

- Establish clear guidelines upfront — what volunteers can and cannot do
- Provide orientation/training
- Set boundaries clearly and kindly (*"We love having help — but the best way to support is by [specific action]"*)
- Always have a teacher present — volunteers are not in charge of a class

### Classroom Helpers

- Start with a trial period
- Give specific, structured tasks
- Provide feedback — thank them and share what worked
- Be clear about confidentiality (*"Please don't discuss what you see in the classroom with others"*)
- Respect their time — don't make them feel obligated

### Learning at Home Activities

**Avoid vague "homework" instructions.** Instead:
- Give specific, actionable tasks: *"Read the picture book together and ask your child what the character's problem was"*
- Connect to what you're doing in class
- Make it doable in 10–15 minutes
- Offer choices: *"Option A: practice counting by 2s. Option B: play a board game and count points together"*

### Parent Information Sessions

- Keep them short (30–45 minutes max)
- Make them practical — not just theory
- Use visuals, handouts, examples of student work
- Offer multiple times or modes (in-person + online)
- **Top topics:** How we teach reading, How we teach maths, Supporting your child at home, Social-emotional learning

### Cultural Events That Involve Families

- Invite elders or community members to share (with proper acknowledgement and consent)
- Celebrate NAIDOC Week, Reconciliation Week, cultural diversity days
- Co-design with families — ask what they'd like to see
- Be mindful of financial barriers — no-cost or low-cost options

## SECTION 6 — DIVERSE FAMILY CONTEXTS (AUSTRALIAN)

### Single-Parent Families

- Don't assume two-parent households — use "family" language broadly
- In forms and communication, use "parent/guardian" not "mum and dad"
- Be aware that single-parent families may have less bandwidth for school involvement — don't pressure, but do welcome

### Blended Families and Custody Arrangements

- Do **not** share information with a non-custodial parent without checking your school's policy and any court orders
- If unsure, check with your principal or admin before releasing any information
- Use language like "parent/guardian" in communication
- Don't take sides or make informal arrangements outside school policy

### Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Families

**Relationship before rigour.** In many communities, relationship is the prerequisite to all other engagement.

- Learn about your local community and language group
- Acknowledge Country in your classroom and communications
- Engage with当地 elders and community representatives where appropriate
- Use local Aboriginal education support officers as a bridge
- Be aware that some families may have had negative experiences with schooling — build trust through consistent, respectful contact
- **Avoid:** Stereotyping, assuming all Indigenous families have the same experience, performative gestures without substance

### EAL/D Families (English as an Additional Language/Dialect)

- Use simple, plain English — not simplified vocabulary, just clearer structure
- Offer translated resources where the school has them
- Use visual communication — Seesaw is excellent for this
- Consider interpreter services for important meetings (arrange in advance through the school)
- Don't assume a child's English proficiency reflects the family's — parents may be highly educated in their own language

### LGBTQ+ Inclusive Language

- Don't assume family structures — use "family" language, "parent/carer"
- When a child has two mums or two dads, affirm this naturally: *"Tell [child's name] I said hi — give your parents my best"*
- If a parent raises a concern about LGBTQ+ content, listen respectfully, explain your school's approach, and refer to policy — do not debate

### Financial Hardship

- Never discuss a family's financial situation with other parents
- When communicating about unpaid canteen orders, camps, or excursions, use neutral language and offer support quietly: *"If you need support with this, please come and speak with me — we have some options"*
- Don't require children to announce when they don't have supplies or money
- Be aware that some families may not have internet access at home — offer alternative communication methods

## SECTION 7 — PRIVACY AND CONFIDENTIALITY

### What Teachers Can and Cannot Share

**Can share (within school context):**
- General academic progress and assessment data
- Classroom observations relevant to learning and wellbeing
- Information needed for educational adjustments
- Information shared with appropriate colleagues and leadership

**Cannot share without consent:**
- Detailed welfare or health information beyond educational needs
- Family situation details (custody, financial, home situation)
- Information a student has shared in confidence (unless mandatory reporting threshold is met)
- Another parent's information with a third party

**Rule of thumb:** If in doubt, ask your principal. Document your decision.

### Mandatory Reporting (Western Australia)

In WA, **mandatory reporting** applies to teachers and school staff under the *Children and Community Services Act 2004*.

**You must report reasonable belief of:**
- Physical abuse
- Sexual abuse
- Emotional harm / psychological abuse
- Neglect

**Practical steps:**
1. Do not investigate yourself — gather only what is needed to form a reasonable belief
2. Report to the Department of Communities — Child Protection and Family Support (13 27 43, available 24/7)
3. Inform your principal
4. Document what you observed, what you were told, what you did

> **You do not need certainty — you need reasonable belief.** If in doubt, consult with your principal or the Department of Communities.

### Consent for Photos and Publications

- WA schools generally have a media consent form completed at enrolment
- Know who has and hasn't consented — keep a register
- Even with consent, use good judgment — avoid photos that could identify vulnerable students
- Never post photos of students on personal social media
- When in doubt, ask the parent directly before posting

### Student Voice and Agency

- As children get older, involve them in what is shared with parents — especially for Seesaw posts, report comments, and interview goals
- Teach children that they can have a say in their learning conversations
- For sensitive issues, balance the child's right to privacy with the parent's right to information — check with your principal if these conflict

## SECTION 8 — WA-SPECIFIC PARENT COMMUNICATION

### WA Department of Education Guidelines

The WA Department of Education's parent communication expectations are embedded in the school's Communication Policy. Key points:
- Use approved channels only (Seesaw, email, school diary, Compass)
- Document significant parent contact
- Follow up verbal concerns in writing
- Principal approval required for external media or public communications referencing the school

### Student Engagement Services (SES)

For students with attendance or engagement issues:
- SES can support schools and families to re-engage students
- A teacher can request an SES referral through their principal
- SES involvement is collaborative — not punitive

### School Psychologists and Welfare Coordination

- School psychologists provide assessment, counselling, and intervention support
- For complex welfare concerns, coordinate through your school's welfare referral process
- Always obtain parent consent before referring a student to external support services (unless a mandatory reporting obligation overrides this)

### CESE Parent Resources

The Centre for Education Statistics and Evaluation (NSW, but useful frameworks) and WA-based resources:
- Report writing guides from the WA Department of Education
- Parent communication frameworks
- Engagement tools for diverse families

> **Note:** WA uses SCASA (School Curriculum and Standards Authority) for moderation of standards — familiarise yourself with the standard elaborations for your year level and learning area.

## SECTION 9 — AITSL ALIGNMENT

### Standard 3.1 — Engage parents/carers in the learning process

**What this means in practice:**
- Use parent communication as a two-way street — not just reporting, but inviting input
- Communicate learning goals and strategies so parents can support at home
- Use parent knowledge of the child to inform your teaching
- Create opportunities for parents to be involved in learning, not just events

**Evidence for accreditation:**
- Seesaw posts showing learning with family follow-up invitations
- Parent information session notes and materials
- Interview records showing parent input into goal-setting
- Newsletters explaining the "why" behind what's being taught

### Standard 4.4 — Foster positive relationships with parents and community

**What this means in practice:**
- Respond to parents professionally and promptly
- Handle difficult conversations with care and follow-through
- Acknowledge and respect diverse family contexts
- Build relationships before you need them

**Evidence for accreditation:**
- Parent feedback (formal or informal)
- Documentation of parent communication
- Engagement with community cultural events and elders
- Referral records showing coordination with support services

### Standard 7.4 — Engage with parents/carers about student learning

**What this means in practice:**
- Reports, interviews, and Seesaw posts that share specific, meaningful information about learning
- Collaborative goal-setting with families
- Follow-up on previously discussed concerns or goals

**Evidence for accreditation:**
- Report samples with descriptive, specific comments
- Parent-teacher interview records
- Communication logs showing follow-through
- Records of goals set with families and progress against them
