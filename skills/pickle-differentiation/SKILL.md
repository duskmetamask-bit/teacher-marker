# pickle-differentiation

## Purpose
This skill is the definitive differentiation guide for PickleNickAI. It covers EAL/D students, gifted learners, students with SEN/disability, trauma-informed practice, Universal Design for Learning (UDL), and specific tiered differentiation strategies for Australian F–6 classrooms.

**Every PickleNickAI output should draw from this skill when generating differentiated activities, accommodations, or modifications.**

---

## DIFFERENTIATION — THE CORE PRINCIPLE

Differentiation is NOT about creating separate lessons for every student. It is about providing the right level of challenge and support so that EVERY student makes progress.

**The four dimensions of differentiation:**

| Dimension | What It Means | Example |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| **Content** | What students learn | Same concept, different depth |
| **Process** | How they learn it | Scaffolding, grouping, support |
| **Product** | How they show what they know | Verbal vs written vs visual output |
| **Environment** | Where and how they learn | Flexible seating, quiet zones |

**Rule:** Not every lesson needs differentiation across all four dimensions. Prioritise based on student need.

## EAL/D STUDENTS — ENGLISH AS AN ADDITIONAL LANGUAGE OR DIALECT

### Who Are EAL/D Students?

EAL/D students are those who:
- Have a first language other than English
- Are still developing proficiency in Academic English
- May speak Aboriginal English or Torres Strait Islander English at home

**WA Context:** WA has one of the highest proportions of EAL/D learners nationally, particularly in Perth schools and regional centres.

**Critical distinction:** Social English vs. Academic English. EAL/D students may be conversationally fluent but need explicit teaching of academic vocabulary, complex sentence structures, and discipline-specific language.

### The EAL/D Learning Progression

| Stage | Characteristics | Teaching Priority |
|-------|----------------|------------------|
| **Beginning** | Limited English, may be literate in L1 | Build vocabulary, use L1 support, visual scaffolding |
| **Emerging** | Some English, basic social communication | Academic vocabulary, sentence structures, guided practice |
| **Developing** | Growing confidence, can communicate in structured contexts | Complex sentences, genre writing, extended talk |
| **Consolidating** | Can engage with most classroom content with support | Independence, abstract concepts, nuance |
| **Fluent** | Can access curriculum at year level with light support | Extension, critical thinking, native-level complexity |

### EAL/D Teaching Strategies — Research-Backed

**1. Scaffolding for EAL Students**
- **Visual scaffolding:** Graphic organisers, word walls, sentence starters, think boards
- **Language scaffolds:** Embedded glossary of terms, bilingual glossaries where available
- **Content representations:** Real objects, images, multi-modal resources
- **Reduced cognitive load:** One concept at a time, avoid walls of text

**2. Academic Language Explicit Instruction**
EAL/D students need explicit teaching of:
- **Tier 2 vocabulary:** High-frequency academic words across subjects ("analyse", "compare", "evaluate", "hypothesise")
- **Subject-specific terms:** Content vocabulary unique to each learning area
- **Language functions:** What verbs mean in each context ("describe" ≠ "explain" ≠ "analyse")
- **Cohesive devices:** Transition words, conjunctions, referencing

**3. Teaching Language Through Content**
Best practice:
- Teach language THROUGH content, not separately from it
- Pre-teach vocabulary BEFORE reading or inquiry
- Use "sheltered instruction" — same content, more language support
- Build from spoken language → written language gradually

**4. EAL/D Assessment Accommodations**
- Allow bilingual dictionaries (real, not AI)
- Extra time for written tasks
- Graphic organiser alternatives to written responses
- Spoken assessment alternatives (recorded or live)
- Reduced word limits with quality-over-quantity emphasis

**5. EAL/D Differentiated Tasks — Practical**

| Stage | Task Adaptation |
|-------|----------------|
| **Beginning** | Use L1 + English side-by-side, image-based responses, labelled diagrams |
| **Emerging** | Short sentences, sentence starters, word banks |
| **Developing** | Full sentences, independent but with glossary support |
| **Consolidating** | Same task as English-speaking peers with vocabulary support |
| **Fluent** | Same task with extension options |

**6. First Language (L1) Use**
Research shows L1 support improves English acquisition:
- Allow students to use L1 for concept development
- Encourage translation of key vocabulary
- Use bilingual peer buddies where possible
- Family communication in L1 (letters home, Seesaw)

**7. Building Vocabulary for EAL/D**
- Word of the week: 5–10 high-utility academic words per week
- Display words visually: definition + image + example sentence
- Use words across multiple contexts throughout the week
- Explicitly teach word families and morphological elements (prefixes, suffixes)

### AC9 EAL Content Descriptors

AC9 includes specific EAL content descriptors for students who need additional English support. Reference:
- `AC9E[year]LE` range for EAL-specific outcomes
- These build progressively from Foundation to Year 6
- Designed for students who speak a language other than English at home

### AITSL Alignment for EAL/D

- **Standard 1.3:** Strategies for teaching students from diverse linguistic, cultural, religious, and socioeconomic backgrounds
- **Standard 1.4:** Strategies for teaching Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students (including Aboriginal English speakers)
- **Standard 3.5:** Differentiate teaching to meet the specific learning needs of students across the full range of abilities (including EAL/D students)

## GIFTED AND TALENTED STUDENTS

### Identifying Gifted Learners

Gifted students may demonstrate:
- Rapid acquisition and retention of new information
- Exceptional ability to think abstractly, analytically, or creatively
- Intense focus and persistence on areas of interest
- Advanced vocabulary and complex sentence structures
- Leadership and ability to mentor other students

**Note:** Giftedness is NOT always visible in academic performance. Some gifted students underperform due to boredom, lack of challenge, or social-emotional factors.

### Differentiation for Gifted Learners

**Content — Compact and Extend:**
- Pre-assess to find what they already know → don't re-teach
- Compact the curriculum so they cover core content faster
- Then extend into deeper, more complex, or broader applications
- Example: If a Year 4 student already understands multiplication facts, move to multiplicative thinking and patterns rather than more drill

**Process — Inquiry and Critical Thinking:**
- Pose open-ended questions with multiple solution paths
- Encourage inquiry-based learning where they generate their own questions
- Require justification: "How do you know?" "Can you prove it?"
- Challenge assumptions and explore edge cases

**Product — Real Audience and Authentic Purpose:**
- Avoid "more of the same" (10 questions instead of 5)
- Give genuine choices in how to demonstrate learning
- Connect to real-world problems or audiences
- Require higher-order thinking products: analysis, synthesis, creation

**Environment — Like-Minded Peers and Autonomy:**
- Sometimes grouping with similar-ability students is appropriate
- Allow independent study or research projects
- Provide mentorship opportunities (teaching others deepens learning)
- Flexible pacing — don't keep them waiting for the rest of the class

### Gifted Students and Underachievement

**Watch for:**
- Perfectionism → refusal to try if they can't guarantee success
- Boredom → disengagement, disruptive behaviour
- Twice-exceptional (2E) students — gifted + learning difficulty or ADHD/ASD
- Social isolation if surrounded by same-age peers with different interests

**What helps:**
- Challenge level matched to readiness (not age-level)
- Growth mindset language: "This is hard because you're being stretched"
- Choice and autonomy
- Social-emotional support alongside academic extension

## STUDENTS WITH DISABILITY / SEN

### NCCD Adjustments in Australian Schools

Under the NCCD, teachers must document adjustments for students with disability. Four levels:

| Level | What It Means | Example Adjustments |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Quality Differentiated Teaching Practice** | Adjustments integral to quality teaching for all | Universal Design for Learning, flexible grouping |
| **Supplementary** | Some additional support above quality teaching | In-class SLSO support, targeted small group |
| **Substantial** | Significant, ongoing adjustments | Regular specialist support, modified curriculum |
| **Extensive** | Full-time support required | One-on-one aide, full modified program |

### Specific Disability Categories and Strategies

**1. Specific Learning Disorder (Dyslexia, Dyscalculia)**
- **Literacy difficulties (Dyslexia):** Explicit phonics instruction, multi-sensory approaches, assistive technology (text-to-speech, dyslexia-friendly fonts)
- **Numeracy difficulties (Dyscalculia):** Concrete manipulatives, number sense building, reduced cognitive load, real-world contexts
- **Key principle:** Difficulty ≠ low ability. Adjust the HOW, not the expectation.

**2. Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)**
- **Strategy:** Reduce seat time on passive tasks, increase movement breaks, break long tasks into shorter chunks
- **Strategy:** Visual schedules, timers, immediate feedback loops
- **Key principle:** Engagement and interest reduce ADHD symptoms. Bore = behaviour problems.

**3. Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)**
- **Strategy:** Predictability — routines, advance notice of changes, visual schedules
- **Strategy:** Explicit teaching of social rules and unwritten expectations
- **Strategy:** Sensory accommodations — noise-reducing headphones, fidget tools, movement breaks
- **Key principle:** What looks like non-compliance may be anxiety, sensory overload, or inability to read social cues.

**4. Intellectual Disability**
- **Strategy:** Simplify content (not diluted — simplified core concepts)
- **Strategy:** Focus on functional skills alongside academic content
- **Strategy:** Use real materials, real contexts, repeated practice
- **Key principle:** Every student can learn. Adjust the pathway, not the destination.

**5. Physical Disability / Mobility Impairment**
- **Strategy:** Environmental access first — can they physically access the learning space?
- **Strategy:** Assistive technology — voice-to-text, switch access, modified equipment
- **Strategy:** Participation in physical education adapted to their ability
- **Key principle:** Physical limitation ≠ cognitive limitation.

**6. Social-Emotional / Mental Health**
- **Strategy:** Check-in systems — mood meters, traffic light cards, daily check-in
- **Strategy:** Reduce stress triggers, provide safe spaces
- **Strategy:** Build in regulation activities, calm-down strategies
- **Key principle:** Students can't learn if they're in crisis. Regulation before academics.

### NCCD SMART Goals for Students with Disability

When generating PLPs or adjustment goals for PickleNickAI, use SMART goals:

> **Example (Year 4 Reading):**
> "By the end of Term 2, [Student] will read Level 18 texts at 90% accuracy on three consecutive running records, as measured by teacher running record assessment, with continued guided reading support at Level 20 in class."

## TRAUMA-INFORMED PRACTICE

### Why Trauma-Informed Matters

Research from the Australian Child Trauma Study shows:
- 1 in 4 Australian children has experienced a potentially traumatic event
- Trauma affects brain development, memory, attention, and emotional regulation
- Trauma responses can look like "behaviour problems" — they are not
- A trauma-informed classroom is a better classroom for ALL students

### The Three R's of Trauma-Informed Practice

1. **Realise** — Recognise how trauma affects students' learning and behaviour
2. **Recognise** — Identify signs of trauma in students' responses
3. **Respond** — Use trauma-informed strategies in teaching and interaction

### Key Classroom Strategies

**1. Safety and Predictability**
- Establish consistent daily routines
- Give warnings before transitions ("In 2 minutes, we'll pack away...")
- Visual schedules for students who need predictability
- Make the classroom a physical and emotional safe space
- Follow through on promises and commitments

**2. Connection Before Content**
- Relationship-building before academic demands
- Greet students by name at the door — eye contact, warmth
- Take time to "check in" — mood meters, thumbs, "how are you feeling?"
- One positive interaction per student per day minimum
- Students who feel connected are more ready to learn

**3. Regulation Before Rigour**
- Teach emotional regulation skills explicitly ("When you're frustrated, try...")
- Provide calm-down spaces (NOT punishment corners)
- Sensory breaks — movement, tactile tools, quiet spaces
- Co-regulation: model calm breathing, regulate together before demanding work
- Recognise when a student is in "fight-flight-freeze" — they cannot access learning in that state

**4. Language and Communication**
- Calm, neutral tone — avoid commands where possible
- Offer choices rather than demands ("Would you like to...?")
- Avoid public corrections — private redirect only
- Acknowledge effort and progress, not just outcomes
- Use "I notice..." language ("I notice you're finding this hard — here's what we can do...")

**5. Behaviour as Communication**
- "What's the behaviour telling me?" — not "How do I punish it?"
- Escalated behaviour often means a need isn't being met
- Trauma-informed behaviour support = understanding + meeting the need
- Record behaviour patterns — what triggers it? What helps?
- Work with wellbeing team, psychologist, support staff

### WA-Specific Trauma Resources
- **Student Support Services (SSS):** WA Department of Education psychologists
- **Handle With Care:** WA Police and Education partnership for children exposed to domestic violence
- **Kids' Helpline:** 1800 55 1800 (24/7)
- **School chaplain or wellbeing coordinator**
- **Aboriginal Education Officers** — for supporting Aboriginal students with trauma

### AITSL Alignment
- Standard 4.1: Establish supportive, orderly, safe learning environments
- Standard 4.4: Foster positive relationships based on mutual respect and trust
- Standard 4.3: Manage challenging behaviour using calm, respectful strategies

## UNIVERSAL DESIGN FOR LEARNING (UDL)

### What UDL Is

Universal Design for Learning (CAST, 2018) is a framework for designing flexible learning experiences that work for ALL students — not as an afterthought, but from the start.

### The Three UDL Principles

**1. Multiple Means of ENGAGEMENT (The WHY)**
- Tap into students' interests and motivations
- Offer choices to increase ownership
- Vary levels of challenge and support
- Foster collaboration and community
- Use rewards that are meaningful, not generic

**2. Multiple Means of REPRESENTATION (The WHAT)**
- Present information in multiple formats (text, audio, video, images)
- Define vocabulary and symbols explicitly
- Break down complex concepts into smaller parts
- Highlight patterns and critical features
- Guide information processing with graphic organisers

**3. Multiple Means of ACTION & EXPRESSION (The HOW)**
- Allow students to demonstrate learning in different ways
- Provide digital tools for writing and creating
- Allow verbal responses vs. written vs. visual
- Scaffold executive function (planning, organisation, time management)
- Use rubrics that clarify expectations for different forms of output

### UDL vs. Differentiation

| Aspect | Differentiation | UDL |
|--------|----------------|-----|
| **Approach** | Reactive — change something after it's not working | Proactive — design flexibly from the start |
| **Target** | Students with identified needs | ALL students |
| **Timing** | Teacher adjusts per student/group | Built into every lesson design |
| **Effort** | More effort per student | Less effort once designed |

**Best practice:** Use BOTH. UDL as the foundation (design), differentiation as targeted support for specific students.

### UDL Strategies for Australian Primary Classrooms

**Representation (Input):**
- Display content in text AND video AND audio
- Use visual timelines, graphic organisers, concept maps
- Pre-teach vocabulary with images and examples
- Highlight key words in texts (bold, colour)
- Provide anchor charts that remain visible

**Engagement (Motivation):**
- Offer student choice in topics, products, groupings
- Gamify where appropriate (points, challenges, competition)
- Connect content to students' lives and interests
- Build in collaboration — peer learning structures
- Vary the level of challenge (build in support AND extension)

**Action & Expression (Output):**
- Allow verbal explanations vs. written vs. visual diagrams
- Use digital tools (Padlet, Seesaw, Canva)
- Provide sentence starters and writing frames
- Allow extra time for some, not all
- Use rubric checklists so students know the path to success

### UDL and AC9 Alignment

UDL supports the AC9 general capabilities:
- Literacy (multiple modes of reading and composing)
- Numeracy (multiple representations of mathematical ideas)
- ICT Capability (digital tools for learning)
- Critical and Creative Thinking (multiple pathways to demonstrate thinking)

## DIFFERENTIATION IN PRACTICE — GENERATING DIFFERENTIATED CONTENT

### When PickleNickAI Generates Differentiated Content, It Must:

1. **Name the learner type** (EAL/D Beginning, Gifted Year 5, etc.)
2. **State the specific adjustment** (not just "differentiate")
3. **Show the adapted content** (not just describe it)
4. **Link to the AC9 descriptor** being addressed
5. **Include success criteria** for that student/group

### Example Output — Year 4 Narrative Writing Unit

**Standard task:** "Write a narrative story with a clear beginning, middle, and end."

**EAL/D Beginning adaptation:**
> Same narrative structure, but:
> - Sentence starters provided on a card: "First, I will tell you about..." / "Next, something unexpected happened..." / "Finally, I learned that..."
> - Vocabulary word bank: characters, setting, problem, solution + 5 Tier 2 words from the class word wall
> - Visual planning template: 4-box story map (Who? Where? What happened? How did it end?) with images and key words
> - Success criteria: "I can tell my story using at least 5 sentences with capital letters and full stops."

**Gifted extension:**
> Same narrative structure, but:
> - Choice: Write a narrative OR a graphic novel script (same narrative elements required)
> - Challenge: Incorporate a "twist ending" that the reader doesn't see coming. Justify your twist in a reflection paragraph.
> - Success criteria: "My narrative has a clear twist that is foreshadowed throughout the story. I can explain why the reader should be surprised."
