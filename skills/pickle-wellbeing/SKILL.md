# pickle-wellbeing

## Purpose
Equip PickleNickAI to support F-6 teachers with student wellbeing, social-emotional learning (SEL), and mental health literacy. This skill provides practical, classroom-ready knowledge — not clinical advice. When wellbeing needs arise, PickleNick draws on this skill to guide, suggest, and support.

## Trigger
Use this skill when:
- A teacher asks about student emotions, behaviour, or mental health
- Planning SEL lessons, check-ins, or wellbeing activities
- Discussing strategies for anxiety, friendship issues, or emotional regulation
- Referencing Zones of Regulation, RULER, CASEL, or Bounce Back
- Navigating WA school wellbeing systems (DAWES, BLS, SSS, NCCD)
- Addressing digital wellbeing or cyberbullying
- Linking wellbeing practices to AITSL standards

## 1. SOCIAL-EMOTIONAL LEARNING (SEL) FRAMEWORKS

### RULER (Yale Center for Emotional Intelligence)
A research-backed SEL approach built around five skills:

| Letter | Skill | What It Means in the Classroom |
|--------|-------|-------------------------------|
| **R** | Recognise | Identify emotions in self and others (facial cues, body language, context) |
| **U** | Understand | Know what caused the emotion and its impact |
| **L** | Label | Use precise emotional vocabulary (not just "happy/sad/mad") |
| **E** | Express | Communicate emotions appropriately for the situation |
| **R** | Regulate | Use strategies to manage emotions effectively |

**Key RULER tools:**
- **Mood Meter** — 4-quadrant grid (pleasantness × energy): Red (high energy, unpleasant), Yellow (high energy, pleasant), Blue (low energy, unpleasant), Green (low energy, pleasant). Students plot their emotion daily.
- **Meta-Moment** — Pause before reacting; connect to your "best self"
- **Blueprint** — Planning how to handle emotionally charged situations
- **Charter** — Class agreement on how we want to feel together and what we'll do to get there

**How to use RULER in F-6:**
- F-2: Focus on Recognise + Label — mood meter, feeling word walls
- 3-4: Add Understand + Express — cause/effect conversations, role play
- 5-6: Emphasise Regulate — personal regulation plans, Meta-Moment practice

---

### CASEL 5 Competencies
The Collaborative for Academic, Social, and Emotional Learning framework. Five core competencies:

| Competency | Definition | Classroom Examples |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Self-Awareness** | Understanding own emotions, strengths, values, growth areas | Journaling, strengths spotting, mood check-ins |
| **Self-Management** | Regulating emotions, setting goals, persisting through challenges | Goal-setting, breathing strategies, homework routines |
| **Social Awareness** | Empathy, perspective-taking, understanding diverse backgrounds | "How would you feel if...?", community circles |
| **Relationship Skills** | Communication, cooperation, conflict resolution, help-seeking | Partner work, class agreements, restorative conversations |
| **Responsible Decision-Making** | Thoughtful choices about behaviour considering consequences | Ethical dilemmas, problem-solving frameworks, reflection tasks |

**CASEL in practice:** These competencies don't require a separate "SEL lesson." Embed them across the day — maths pair talk (relationship skills), reading discussions (social awareness), assessment reflection (self-awareness).

### Zones of Regulation
A curriculum framework (Leah Kuypers) helping students understand and manage their emotional state using four colour-coded zones.

| Zone | Colour | Feeling State | Examples |
|------|--------|--------------|---------|
| **Blue Zone** | 🔵 Blue | Low energy, "slow" | Sad, tired, bored, sick, moving slowly |
| **Green Zone** | 🟢 Green | Calm, focused, ready to learn | Happy, calm, focused, content |
| **Yellow Zone** | 🟡 Yellow | Heightened alertness, still in control | Excited, nervous, silly, frustrated, surprised |
| **Red Zone** | 🔴 Red | Intense emotions, out of control | Angry, terrified, aggressive, overwhelmed, panicked |

**Key teaching points:**
- All zones are okay and normal — we're not aiming for always-green
- Blue and yellow are fine in the right context (sport, excitement, rest)
- Red zone = safety priority; learning cannot happen here
- Goal: Help students recognise their zone and move toward green when needed

**Strategies by zone:**

*Blue Zone → move toward Green:*
- Movement break, jump/shake/stretch
- Talk to a friend
- Drink of water
- Upbeat music

*Yellow Zone → move toward Green:*
- Deep breathing (box breathing, 5-4-3-2-1)
- Squeeze a stress ball
- Take a walk
- Count to 10 slowly
- Mindfulness minute

*Red Zone → safety first, then Green:*
- Remove from stimulus (quiet corner, calm space)
- Do NOT engage in discussion — wait until green
- Heavy work: push-ups, carry books, wall push
- Teacher presence (calm, low voice, no demands)
- After calm: brief restorative conversation

**When to use Zones:** Introduce whole-class in Term 1. Post zone charts in classrooms. Morning check-ins: "What zone are you in?" Use as shared language for behaviour conversations ("I can see you're in the yellow zone — what might help?").

### Bounce Back (Australian Wellbeing Program)
An evidence-based Australian wellbeing and resilience program for F-8 (Helen McGrath & Toni Noble).

**What it covers:**
- **Core values:** Respect, responsibility, fairness, care, inclusion
- **Resilience skills:** Coping with setbacks, managing anxiety, bouncing back from mistakes
- **Social skills:** Making and keeping friends, dealing with bullying, conflict resolution
- **Positive emotions:** Optimism, gratitude, humour, hope
- **Units:** Courageous Coping, Relationships, Emotions, Optimistic Thinking, Humour, Achieving Goals, Being Safe, Positive Thinking

**Bounce Back acronym:**
- **B** — Bad times don't last
- **O** — Other people can help
- **U** — Unhelpful thinking makes things worse
- **N** — Nobody is perfect
- **C** — Concentrate on the positives
- **E** — Everybody experiences sadness and failure
- **B** — Blame fairly
- **A** — Accept what you can't change
- **C** — Catastrophising makes things worse
- **K** — Keep things in perspective

**How to use it:** Bounce Back is a structured program with teacher manuals by year level. Schools purchase the program. PickleNick can reference its principles without requiring the manuals.

## 2. MENTAL HEALTH FOUNDATION (PRIMARY)

### Trauma-Informed Practice — Practical Reminders
Trauma includes: abuse, neglect, domestic violence, loss, medical trauma, community trauma. Many students carry invisible loads.

**Core principles (simple version):**
- **Safety first** — Physical and emotional safety before anything else. A dysregulated child cannot learn.
- **Predictability helps** — Routines, clear expectations, consistent responses reduce anxiety
- **Relationships are the intervention** — One trusted adult can be the turning point
- **Behaviour is communication** — Ask "what happened to this child?" not "what's wrong with this child?"
- **Don't take it personally** — Outbursts are rarely about you
- **Avoid power struggles** — Offer choices, stay calm, come back later
- **Repair matters** — After conflict, reconnect. "I'm glad you're here."

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't isolate as punishment
- Don't shame or lecture when dysregulated
- Don't demand eye contact from all students (culturally sensitive + trauma)
- Don't take away break time from students who need movement most

---

### Anxiety in Children — Signs & Strategies
**Signs of anxiety in F-6 students (not clinical — teacher-observable):**
- Frequent complaints of stomach aches, headaches before school tasks
- Avoidance of activities, school refusal
- Excessive worry ("what if...?"), seeking repeated reassurance
- Difficulty concentrating, seems distracted or on edge
- Tearfulness, irritability, clinginess
- Perfectionism, fear of making mistakes
- Social withdrawal or reluctance to participate

**Classroom strategies (non-clinical):**
- **Validate first:** "I can see that feels really scary. That makes sense."
- **Gradual exposure:** Don't force avoidance; gently scaffold participation
- **Predictability:** Give advance notice of changes, prep students for transitions
- **Breathing tools:** Box breathing (4-4-4-4), 4-7-8 breathing — teach whole-class
- **Safe spaces:** Identify a low-stimulation corner or alternative workspace
- **Check-ins:** Brief, private "how are you going?" moments build trust
- **Don't catastrophise back:** Stay calm, model "we can handle this"
- **Avoid over-reassurance loops:** Instead of "it'll be fine" → "let's think through it"

**When it's beyond the classroom:** Refer to school psychologist or welfare coordinator if:
- Anxiety is persistent (weeks, not days)
- Significantly impacting learning or attendance
- Child reports it themselves as overwhelming
- You suspect a clinical anxiety disorder

---

### Self-Harm in Children — Warning Signs & What To Do
**Note: Always follow your school's mandatory reporting protocols. This is teacher-awareness guidance, not clinical assessment.**

**Warning signs (observable):**
- Unexplained cuts, scratches, bruises (especially arms, legs — covered even in hot weather)
- Wearing long sleeves/pants consistently in warm weather
- Withdrawn behaviour, change in mood or social patterns
- Giving away possessions
- Talking or writing about hopelessness, wanting to disappear
- Flinching when touched, protective of certain body areas

**If a student discloses or you suspect:**
1. **Stay calm** — your reaction matters. Don't show shock or horror.
2. **Listen and validate:** "Thank you for telling me. I'm glad you trusted me."
3. **Do not promise secrecy** — "I want to help keep you safe, and that means I need to let some other people know."
4. **Report immediately** to your Deputy Principal, welfare coordinator, or school psychologist.
5. **Do not leave the student alone** if you believe there is immediate risk.
6. **Mandatory reporting:** If you believe a child is at risk of significant harm, you are required to report under WA's *Children and Community Services Act 2004*. When in doubt, report.
7. **Document** what you observed or heard — exact words, behaviours, date/time.

**Do not:**
- Dismiss or minimise ("it's probably nothing")
- Tell other students
- Handle it alone — always escalate

| Situation | Who to Refer To |
|-----------|----------------|

## 3. STUDENT WELLBEING METRICS IN WA

### DAWES — Department of Education Wellbeing Framework
**What it is:** WA Department of Education's framework for understanding and supporting student wellbeing across public schools.

**Key domains (DAWES focuses on):**
- Social and emotional wellbeing
- Physical wellbeing
- Cognitive wellbeing (learning engagement)
- Positive relationships

**How it's used:**
- Informs school wellbeing planning and improvement cycles
- Guides data collection and reporting on student wellbeing
- Connects to the School Improvement and Accountability framework
- Schools use it to identify whole-school wellbeing priorities

**Teacher-level:** You likely won't interact with DAWES directly — it's a school/system-level framework. But your check-in data, referrals, and classroom observations feed into it.

---

### BLS — Behaviour and Learning Support
**What it is:** WA Department of Education's tiered support system for students with behaviour and learning needs.

**Three tiers:**
| Tier | Who | What |
|------|-----|------|
| **Universal (Tier 1)** | All students | Whole-school positive behaviour support, classroom strategies |
| **Targeted (Tier 2)** | Students needing more support | Small group interventions, behaviour plans, check-in check-out |
| **Intensive (Tier 3)** | Students with complex needs | Individual plans, specialist support, external agency involvement |

**PBIS (Positive Behaviour in Schools):** Many WA schools run PBIS — a school-wide framework with agreed expectations, explicit teaching of behaviours, and positive reinforcement systems. If your school runs PBIS, align your classroom language with the whole-school expectations.

**BLS referral process (typical):**
1. Teacher documents concerns
2. Raises at Student Support Team (SST) meeting
3. Strategies trialled and documented
4. If insufficient → referral to school psychologist or SSEN:BE
5. Formal behaviour support plan developed

---

### NCCD — Nationally Consistent Collection of Data
**What it is:** Federal data collection on students with disability, including those with mental health conditions (anxiety, depression, autism, ADHD, etc.) that substantially impact their education.

**Why it matters:** NCCD drives funding. If a student's mental health/wellbeing significantly impacts their learning, they may be counted in NCCD.

**Four levels of adjustment:**
| Level | Description |
|-------|------------|
| **Quality Differentiated Teaching Practice** | Good teaching for all |
| **Supplementary** | Some additional support |
| **Substantial** | Significant, consistent adjustments |
| **Extensive** | Highly individualised, intensive |

**Teacher's role:** Document adjustments you're making for identified students. This evidence supports NCCD counting and ensures students get appropriate funding and support.

**Mental health + NCCD:** Diagnosed mental health conditions (anxiety disorder, depression, PTSD) can qualify under "mental health" disability category if they have an educational impact. Work with your school psychologist and SENCO to determine eligibility.

---

### Student Support Services (SSS)
**What SSS provides in WA public schools:**
- School Psychologists — assessment, consultation, counselling (brief)
- Visiting Teachers (Hearing, Vision, Physical) — specialist support
- School Chaplains — pastoral care (opt-in)
- Student Welfare Coordinators — case management, family liaison
- SSEN:BE consultants — complex behaviour and engagement support

**How to access SSS:**
- Speak to your school's SENCO, Deputy Principal, or Welfare Coordinator
- Raise at Student Support Team meeting
- Complete school referral form (varies by school)

## 4. CLASSROOM WELLBEING STRATEGIES

### Morning Check-Ins
**Why:** Sets the emotional tone for the day. Gives you real-time data on who needs support.

**Options by year level:**

*F-2:*
- **Thumbs Up/Down/Sideways** — Quick physical check-in at the door
- **Feeling faces chart** — Point to how you're feeling
- **Weather report** — "I'm feeling sunny / cloudy / stormy today"
- **Heart chart** — Peg or sticker on how you're feeling

*3-4:*
- **Mood Meter** — Four-quadrant grid; place your name/peg
- **Number check-in** — Rate yourself 1-5 (1=struggling, 5=great)
- **Word of the day** — One word that describes how you're feeling

*5-6:*
- **Written reflection** — 2-3 sentences in a wellbeing journal
- **Traffic light check-in** — Red/yellow/green (emoji or card)
- **Share with a partner** — Brief pair check-in, then whole-class share

**What to do with the data:** Scan for outliers. A student consistently in "red/stormy/1" = follow up privately. Don't call attention publicly.

---

### Emotional Vocabulary Building
**Why it matters:** Students who can name emotions can regulate them. "Name it to tame it" (Dan Siegel).

**Strategies:**
- **Feeling word walls** — 50+ feeling words displayed in the classroom
- **Mood Meter vocabulary** — Teach precise words (anxious vs worried vs terrified)
- **Emotion of the week** — Explore one emotion deeply each week (what causes it, what it feels like, how to respond)
- **Books as a mirror** — Use picture books and novels to explore character emotions
- **Sentence starters:** "I feel ___ because ___. I need ___."

**Australian picture books for emotions (F-2):**
- *The Invisible String* — connection and grief
- *The Red Beast* — anger
- *Wombat Stew* — silliness and consequences
- *Ruby's Worry* — anxiety
- *The Huge Bag of Worries* — worry

---

### Calm-Down Strategies
**Teach these explicitly — don't assume students know them.**

**Breathing techniques:**
| Technique | How | Best For |
|-----------|-----|---------|
| **Box Breathing** | Breathe in 4, hold 4, out 4, hold 4 | Yellow/red zone |
| **5-Finger Breathing** | Trace fingers, breathe in/out on each | F-2 |
| **4-7-8 Breathing** | In 4, hold 7, out 8 | Older students, anxiety |
| **Belly Breathing** | Hand on belly, slow deep breaths | Whole class relaxation |

**Sensory tools:**
- Stress balls, putty, fidgets
- Calm-down kit (small box: stress ball, headphones, visual timer, breathing card)
- Weighted lap pad (sensory seeking students)
- Nature table (shells, smooth stones, natural textures)

**Movement breaks:**
- Shake it out (literal shaking)
- 5 jumping jacks
- Wall push-ups
- Yoga stretches (tree pose, child's pose)
- Walk a lap of the oval

**Calm corner / cosy corner:**
- Designated quiet space in the classroom
- Not for punishment — for self-regulation
- Contents: breathing cards, fidgets, visual schedule, feelings chart
- Teach students when and how to use it

---

### Positive Psychology in the Classroom
**Core concepts (Martin Seligman's PERMA model):**
- **P** — Positive Emotions: gratitude, joy, pride, love
- **E** — Engagement: flow states, deep work
- **R** — Relationships: belonging, connection
- **M** — Meaning: purpose, contributing to something bigger
- **A** — Accomplishment: mastery, effort, growth

**Classroom practices:**

*Gratitude:*
- Daily gratitude share (3 things, spoken or written)
- Gratitude journals (Years 3-6)
- "Shout-outs" board — peer appreciation

*Strengths Spotting:*
- VIA Character Strengths (values in action) — identify top strengths
- Strength of the week — celebrate a different strength each week
- "I notice your strength of..." — teacher language

*Growth Mindset (Carol Dweck):*
- "Yet" language — "I can't do it yet"
- Praising effort over outcome
- Mistake celebrations ("What did we learn?")
- Famous failures display

---

### Restorative Practices
**Philosophy:** When harm occurs, focus on repairing relationships rather than punishing rule-breaking.

**Restorative vs Punitive:**
| Punitive | Restorative |
|---------|------------|
| What rule was broken? | What happened? |
| Who's to blame? | Who has been affected? |
| What's the punishment? | How do we repair this? |

**Restorative questions (for conversations after conflict):**
1. What happened?
2. What were you thinking at the time?
3. Who has been affected, and how?
4. What do you think you need to do to make things right?
5. How can we make sure this doesn't happen again?

**Restorative circles:** Whole-class or small group — sitting in a circle, talking stick, everyone gets a turn. Used proactively (building community) and reactively (addressing conflict).

**Circle time (F-2):** Regular class meetings sitting in a circle. Topics: kindness, problem solving, class agreements. Builds community before conflict arises.

---

### Building Connections
**Teacher-Student Relationship:**
- Greet students at the door by name — every day
- 2x10 strategy: 2 minutes a day for 10 days with a struggling student — personal conversation only, no academics
- Notice and name positives: "I noticed you helped Jake today — that's kindness."
- Repair after conflict: "I'm glad you're back. Let's start fresh."
- Learn about students' interests and reference them

**Peer Relationships:**
- Structured partner and group work (teach collaboration explicitly)
- Buddy systems (Year 6 buddies with Foundation students)
- Lunch clubs for students who struggle socially (LEGO club, book club)
- Class agreements developed together — student ownership
- Catch-up time after absence (peer buddy system)

| Year | Focus | Key Concepts | Activities |
|------|-------|-------------|-----------|
| **Foundation** | Feelings identification, sharing, turn-taking | Basic emotions (happy, sad, angry, scared, surprised), "my turn / your turn" | Mood match (match face to feeling), feelings corner, circle time with talking stick, read-alouds about emotions |

## 6. DIGITAL WELLBEING

### Screen Time Awareness
- No clinical limits to enforce — but model and discuss healthy habits
- "Green time vs screen time" — balance of outdoor and digital activity
- Digital Sabbath concept: times/places with no screens (meals, before bed)
- Discuss: How does screen time make you feel? Energised or drained?

### Cyberbullying
**Signs a student may be experiencing cyberbullying:**
- Upset or withdrawn after using devices
- Avoids talking about online activity
- Reluctance to attend school (social media drama carries into school)
- Suddenly stops using devices they loved
- Unexplained changes in mood, sleep, appetite

**What to do:**
1. Take it seriously — online harm is real harm
2. Document (screenshot) evidence before reporting
3. Report to platform (most have reporting tools)
4. Report to Deputy Principal — it may require eSafety Commissioner involvement
5. Support the student: "This is not your fault. We will sort this out."
6. Do not tell the student to "just ignore it"

**Prevention:**
- Class discussions on what cyberbullying looks like
- Digital citizenship lessons (eSafety Commissioner resources: esafety.gov.au)
- "Think before you post" — THINK acronym: True, Helpful, Inspiring, Necessary, Kind

**eSafety Commissioner (Australia):** esafety.gov.au — excellent free teacher resources, lessons, reporting tools.

### Positive Online Relationships
- Online relationships are real relationships — same values apply (respect, kindness, honesty)
- What does a good online friend look like?
- Model and practise digital communication (tone, emojis, misunderstandings)
- Discuss: Would you say that to their face?

### Digital Footprint Awareness
- "The internet remembers forever" — age-appropriate discussions from Year 3+
- What is a digital footprint? (Everything posted online leaves a trace)
- Activity: What would someone know about you from your online activity?
- Privacy basics: What not to share online (full name, address, school, photos)
- For Years 5-6: Reputation and future opportunities (jobs, sports teams, schools)

## 7. AITSL ALIGNMENT

This skill supports multiple Australian Professional Standards for Teachers:

### Standard 4.1 — Support student participation
*"Establish supportive, orderly, and safe learning environments."*
- Zones of Regulation creates shared language for emotional safety
- Calm-down strategies embedded in classroom routines
- Morning check-ins surface needs before they become behaviours
- Predictable, trauma-informed environments reduce anxiety

### Standard 4.3 — Manage challenging behaviour
*"Manage challenging behaviour using evidence-based strategies."*
- Restorative practices as alternative to punitive discipline
- Behaviour as communication — understanding triggers and unmet needs
- De-escalation strategies and calm-down toolkits
- BLS tiered support framework guides teacher response

### Standard 4.4 — Maintain student safety
*"Describe strategies for student wellbeing and safety working within school and/or system, curriculum and legislative requirements."*
- Mandatory reporting awareness (WA: *Children and Community Services Act 2004*)
- Referral pathways (school psychologist, welfare coordinator, SSS)
- Crisis protocols for self-harm/disclosure
- Student Support Services navigation

### Standard 1.5 — Differentiate teaching to meet diverse needs
*"Demonstrate knowledge and understanding of strategies for differentiating teaching to meet the specific learning needs of students across the full range of abilities."*
- SEL by year level ensures developmentally appropriate practice
- NCCD documentation for students with mental health-related disability
- Adjustment strategies for anxious, dysregulated, or trauma-affected students
- Digital wellbeing as part of holistic student support

### Standard 3.4 — Select and use resources
*"Demonstrate knowledge of a range of resources, including ICT, that engage students in their learning."*
- eSafety Commissioner digital resources
- Zones of Regulation curriculum tools
- Bounce Back program (school purchase)
- RULER tools (Mood Meter, Charter, Meta-Moment)

## QUICK REFERENCE CARD

**Student seems dysregulated → Zones language:** "I can see you might be in the yellow zone. What might help you get back to green?"

**Student discloses self-harm → STOP:** Listen, validate, do NOT promise secrecy, report immediately to DP/psychologist.

**Anxiety showing up as avoidance → Scaffold:** Don't force. Validate, offer choice, gradual step-in.

**Morning check-in shows a student is struggling → 2x10:** Invest 2 minutes a day for 10 days in genuine personal connection.

**Conflict between students → Restorative questions:** What happened? Who was affected? How do we repair it?

**Cyberbullying disclosed → Document, report, support:** Evidence first, eSafety if needed, "not your fault."

**NCCD student with anxiety impacting learning → Document adjustments:** Record every adjustment you're making — it supports funding and support access.
