## 1. AC9 THE ARTS STRUCTURE (F–6)

### Five Art Forms

| Code | Art Form |
|------|----------|
| V | Visual Arts |
| MU | Music |
| D | Drama |
| DA | Dance |
| M | Media Arts |

### AC9 Code Format

`AC9A[band][V|MU|D|DA|M][number]`

**Bands:**
- `F` — Foundation
- `12` — Years 1–2
- `34` — Years 3–4
- `56` — Years 5–6

**Examples:**
- `AC9AFV01` — Foundation Visual Arts, Achievement Standard 1
- `AC9A34MU02` — Years 3–4 Music, Achievement Standard 2
- `AC9A56D03` — Years 5–6 Drama, Achievement Standard 3
- `AC9A12DA01` — Years 1–2 Dance, Achievement Standard 1
- `AC9A56M04` — Years 5–6 Media Arts, Achievement Standard 4

### Two Strands (All Art Forms)

1. **Explore** — Responding: Students explore, view, listen to, and interpret artworks and performances
2. **Express/Create** — Making: Students create and perform their own artworks

Each art form has sub-strands under these two broad headings. Consult AC9 curriculum documents for the full detail of each code.

## 2. VISUAL ARTS (V)

### What Students Learn

**Foundation (F)**
- Explore colours, shapes, textures, and materials
- Create images and objects using senses and imagination
- Respond to own and others' artworks (likes/dislikes)

**Years 1–2**
- Use various materials: paint, crayon, pencil, clay, collage
- Explore visual elements: line, shape, colour, texture, space
- Create 2D and 3D artworks
- Share interpretations of artworks

**Years 3–4**
- Apply design principles: balance, contrast, repetition, pattern
- Create sustained artworks over multiple sessions
- Explore symbolic representations in artworks
- Describe how ideas are communicated in artworks

**Years 5–6**
- Select materials and techniques purposefully
- Develop personal style and artistic voice
- Critique artworks using visual analysis language
- Explore how artists interpret social/cultural contexts

### Practical Classroom Ideas

**Drawing Skills Progression**
- F–1: Mark-making (scribbles, lines, circles) → recognisable forms
- 2–3: Basic shapes → combining shapes into representations
- 4–6: Observational drawing, shading, perspective, detail

**Colour Theory for Primary**
- Primary colours: red, yellow, blue
- Secondary colours: orange, green, purple (mix primaries)
- Complementary pairs: red/green, blue/orange, yellow/purple
- Warm vs cool colours
- Tints and shades

**Indigenous Australian Art**
- Dot painting (Papunya Tula tradition) —的意义, patterns, symbols
- Cross-hatching (Arnhem Land) — lines creating texture
- Bark painting — natural ochres on eucalyptus bark
- Wandjina figures (Kimberley) — cloud/rain spirits
- Rarrk (cross-hatching) as both decoration and story
- Student-safe versions: cotton buds for dot work, brown paper for bark painting

**Famous Australian Artists**
- **Pro Hart** — outback life, mining, humorous scenes (Queensland)
- **Emily Kame Kngwarreye** — Alhawa (Yam) dreaming, bold abstract (Utopia, NT)
- **Margaret Olley** — still life, generosity of spirit (NSW)
- **Clifford Possum Tjapaltjarri** — Papunya, men's ceremonial art
- **Emily Kame Kngwarreye** — "All Over" painting style

**Assessment: Visual Art Rubrics**
Assess BOTH process and product:

*Process (40%):*
- Planning and preparation
- Use of class time
- Experimentation with materials
- Process documentation (photos, diary notes)

*Product (60%):*
- Application of visual elements
- Technical skill with chosen medium
- Creativity and originality
- Communication of idea/intention
- Craftsmanship (presentation)

## 3. MUSIC (MU)

### What Students Learn

**Foundation (F)**
- Keep a steady beat (clapping, percussion)
- Sing simple songs with confidence
- Explore sounds: high/low, loud/soft, long/short
- Move to music

**Years 1–2**
- Read simple pitch notation (so/mi, or simple staff)
- Play un-tuned percussion with control
- Sing in tune, matching pitch
- Recognise and respond to fast/slow, loud/soft

**Years 3–4**
- Read and write basic melodic notation (ta, ti-ti, so, la, mi)
- Play recorder, boomwhackers, or simple melodic instruments
- Compose short sequences using known rhythms
- Identify and perform dynamics: piano, forte, crescendo, decrescendo

**Years 5–6**
- Perform with expression using dynamics, tempo, articulation
- Understand timbre: instrument families, synthesised sounds
- Compose and notate with graphic or traditional notation
- Analyse how music communicates mood, culture, story

### Practical Classroom Ideas

**Un-Pitched Percussion**
- Body percussion: clapping, patting, stamping, clicking
- Found percussion: containers, wooden blocks, shakers
- Djembe or frame drum: bass/tone/slap
- Rhythm cards: show ta (quarter), ti-ti (eighth pairs), rest

**Singing Games**
- Traditional: "A Sailor Went to Sea", "Head and Shoulders Baby"
- Contemporary: call-and-response songs
- Warm-ups: lip trills, "mum" on descending scale, "nya" for resonance

**Music Elements (acronym: BRS D TIM)**
- **B**eat
- **R**hythm (longer/shorter sounds on beat)
- **S**tructure (verse, chorus, repeat)
- **D**ynamics (loud/soft, changes)
- **T**empo (fast/slow, speeding up/slowing down)
- **I**nstruments (families, sounds)
- **M**elody (high/low, scale)

**Indigenous Australian Music**
- Didgeridoo: drone instrument, only male players traditionally
- Clapsticks (ci W): rhythmic accompaniment
- Torres Strait Islander music: clapped songs, dancing
- Welcome to Country protocols
- Contemporary Indigenous artists: Geoff Gurrumul Yunupingu

**Music Technology (Beginner)**
- **GarageBand** (iPad/Mac): loops, record voice, mix
- **Soundtrap**: browser-based, collaborative compositions
- **Chrome Music Lab**: accessible, free, simple

## 4. DRAMA (D)

### What Students Learn

**Foundation (F)**
- Pretend play and dramatic exploration
- Mime familiar actions and objects
- Express emotions through movement and voice
- Share dramatic play with others

**Years 1–2**
- Improvise simple scenes and situations
- Use facial expressions and body language
- Sustain role in short role-plays
- Respond to drama experiences (what happened?)

**Years 3–4**
- Work with scripted drama text
- Develop characterisation (voice, movement, gesture)
- Create scenes with setting, character, tension
- Use elements of drama: tension, contrast, narrative drive

**Years 5–6**
- Devise original drama using dramatic forms
- Use theatrical conventions: slow motion, tableau, direct address
- Evaluate own and others' drama work
- Explore how drama communicates social issues and perspectives

### Practical Classroom Ideas

**Tableau / Freeze Frames**
- Groups of 3–4 create a frozen picture showing a moment from a story
- Add levels (high/middle/low) and proximity (close/far)
- "Curtain call" — reveal each group's tableau to the class

**Conscience Alley**
- Class forms two lines facing each other
- One student walks between while others voice "for" and "against" arguments
- Character stands at end to decide
- Great for moral dilemmas in texts/stories

**Hot Seating**
- One student sits in "the hot seat" as a character
- Others ask questions; character responds in role
- Develops empathy, deepens characterisation

**Teacher in Role**
- Teacher adopts a character role to drive drama forward
- Students respond to teacher-as-character
- Useful for introducing dilemmas, historical scenarios

**Story Drama**
- Retell a known story (Little Red Riding Hood, The Rainbow Serpent) through drama
- Add tension points, frozen moments, thought tracking
- Students make drama choices; teacher facilitates

## 5. DANCE (DA)

### What Students Learn

**Foundation (F)**
- Express ideas through movement
- Follow simple movement patterns and sequences
- Respond to music with whole-body movement
- Share movement with others

**Years 1–2**
- Explore basic dance elements: action, space, time, energy
- Create short movement phrases (3–5 moves)
- Mirror and match伙伴's movements
- Describe how their body moves

**Years 3–4**
- Create and perform dance sequences (8–16 counts)
- Respond to music: identify beat, mood, tempo
- Use spatial awareness: pathways, levels, formations
- Describe and evaluate own and others' dance

**Years 5–6**
- Choreograph using all dance elements: action, space, time, energy, relationships
- Perform with control, accuracy, and expression
- Explore choreographic devices: canon, unison, repetition, contrast
- Analyse how dance communicates meaning

### Practical Classroom Ideas

**Movement Warm-Ups**
- Travel: walk, jog, skip around space
- Body parts: isolate head, shoulders, hips, feet
- Shapes: curved, angular, twisted, symmetrical/asymmetrical
- Mirroring: partner work (leader/follower)
- Shapes in space: high, middle, low; wide, narrow, small

**Response to Music**
- Play a piece; students move freely — "what does the music make your body want to do?"
- Then narrow: "now move only using your arms"
- Debrief: "what did you notice about your movement?"

**Aboriginal Dance Traditions**
- Different language groups have different traditional dances
- Movement often reflects animals, plants, weather, hunting
- Consult local knowledge-holders or Deadly Dance resources
- Movement stories: students create their own "country" dances

**Choreography Challenges (Given Constraints)**
- "Create an 8-count phrase using only floor levels"
- "Add one jump, one turn, one stillness"
- "Your phrase must include a mirror image with your partner"
- "Use the space from here to there — fill it interestingly"

**Dance Notation (Simple Sequencing)**
- Write movement as symbols or arrows
- Use letters or icons for body parts
- Count beats beneath
- Video documentation (Seesaw, ClassDojo) for portfolio

## 6. MEDIA ARTS (M)

### What Students Learn

**Foundation (F)**
- Take photographs using tablets
- Create simple digital drawings
- Explore how media represents familiar things
- Understand media is made by people

**Years 1–2**
- Create simple media texts: posters, digital images, slideshows
- Plan simple media products (draw first, then make)
- Identify purpose: to inform, entertain, or persuade
- Basic stop-motion using tablets

**Years 3–4**
- Plan and create media texts with clear purpose and audience
- Use composition: framing, angle, foreground/background
- Edit and combine images and sound
- Reflect on why media texts look the way they do

**Years 5–6**
- Edit and combine media using apps/software
- Understand media representations: whose perspective is shown?
- Analyse media texts: codes, conventions, target audience
- Create media products with intentional design choices

### Practical Classroom Ideas

**Stop-Motion Animation**
- Apps: Stop Motion Studio (iPad), Lego Movie Maker
- Materials: LEGO, playdough, drawings on paper (cut-outs), toys
- Process: 10–24 frames per second, minimum 3 seconds long
- Story: must have beginning, middle, end

**Digital Storytelling**
- **Storymaker** (iPad): combine images, text, voice recording
- **Book Creator**: eBooks with embedded audio
- **Adobe Express**: poster, slideshow, video

**Photography Composition**
- Rule of thirds: imagine grid overlay, place subject on lines/intersections
- Framing: use natural frames (doorways, windows, branches)
- Leading lines: paths, fences, roads draw the eye
- Angle: bird's eye, worm's eye, straight-on — each tells a different story

**Movie Trailers**
- Classic trailer structure: hook, brief clips, mystery, resolution
- Plan: storyboard first (6–8 shots)
- Film: use tripod or steady hand
- Edit: iMovie, Adobe Express, CapCut
- Sound: add music, brief voice-over, sound effects

**Media Literacy**
- "Who made this? Who is it for? What do they want you to think/do?"
- Analyse TV commercials: what techniques are used? (music, camera angle, colours, words)
- News media: fact vs opinion, whose voice is heard/not heard
- Compare: same story from different news sources

## 7. CROSS-CURRICULUM LINKS

| Learning Area | Arts Link | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **HASS** | Visual storytelling, Indigenous perspectives, cultural celebrations, timelines as art | Students create a painting about a historical event; design a visual representation of a cultural celebration |
| **English** | Narrative structure, visual texts, oral language, visual literacy, responding to stories through drama/dance | Act out a story's climax; create a picture book; perform poetry with movement |
| **Maths** | Patterns, symmetry, spatial reasoning, geometric shapes in art, rhythm as fractions | Tessellation art; symmetry in Indigenous dot patterns; count beats in rhythm patterns (fractions) |
| **Science** | Scientific observation and recording, nature as subject, documenting change over time | Observational drawings of plants/animals; stop-motion of plant growth; soundscapes of the environment |
| **Geography** | Mapping as art, place representation, environmental art | Students create artworks representing their local area; compare how different places are depicted in art |
| **Health** | Expressing emotions through art, body awareness in dance, respectful relationships in drama | Role-play scenarios about friendships; dance expressing feelings; poster campaigns |

## 8. ASSESSMENT IN THE ARTS

### Process Journals
- Students document artistic choices throughout the process
- Written or drawn entries: "Today I tried... because... next I will..."
- Photographs of work in progress
- Useful for showing growth over time, not just final product

### Performance Rubrics
Separate executable skills from expressive skills:

**Executable Skills (how well):**
- Technical accuracy
- Control of materials/voice/body
- Following sequences correctly

**Expressive Skills (how meaningfully):**
- Interpretation
- Communication of idea/feeling
- Engagement and presence
- Risk-taking and creativity

### Peer Feedback
- Structured feedback protocols:
  - "I noticed you... (specific observation)"
  - "One thing that worked well was..."
  - "One thing that could be even better is..."
- Gallery walks: work displayed, students rotate with sticky notes

### Student Self-Reflection
- "What did I learn about myself as an artist?"
- "What was challenging? How did I handle it?"
- "What would I do differently next time?"
- Traffic light exit tickets: "I feel confident / okay / need more help"

### Digital Portfolios
- **Seesaw**: student-driven, parent access, voice recording
- **Seaworld** (Seaworld/Seesaw confusion — likely means Seesaw): same as above
- **Google Sites**: more structured, good for Years 5–6
- Collects photos, videos, written reflections, final works

| **Standard 3.2** — Set challenging learning goals | Differentiating for individual students in arts contexts, creating individual learning goals for process and product, using assessment data to plan next steps |
| **Standard 4.4** — Foster positive relationships through collaborative arts practice | Group devising in drama, ensemble work in music, collaborative choreography, shared creation in media arts — creating a safe space for creative risk-taking |
