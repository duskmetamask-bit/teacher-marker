# Ecosystems, Adaptation and Survival — Biological Sciences Unit Plan

**Unit Duration:** 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons
**Year Level:** Year 4
**Curriculum Link:** Australian Curriculum Science v9 — Biological Sciences
**Unit Big Idea:** Ecosystems are shaped by environmental changes — living things must adapt or move to survive. Understanding how organisms respond to environmental changes is key to protecting biodiversity.
**Cold Task:** Week 1, Lesson 1 — Design an investigation to find out how environmental changes affect living things
**Hot Task:** Week 8, Lesson 1 — Analyse how a specific Australian ecosystem responds to environmental change and propose conservation strategies

---

## Unit Overview

**Learning Intention:**
We are learning to analyse the effects of environmental changes on ecosystems, describe how living things adapt to environmental changes, investigate structural and behavioural adaptations, and propose evidence-based conservation strategies.

**Success Criteria:**
- I can describe how ecosystems respond to natural and human-induced environmental changes.
- I can explain structural and behavioural adaptations of living things.
- I can analyse how adaptations help organisms survive in specific environments.
- I can evaluate the effectiveness of conservation strategies.
- I can use science words: adaptation, environment, ecosystem, structural, behavioural, survival, biodiversity, conservation.

## WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: Ecosystems and Change

### Lesson 1: Cold Task — Design an Environmental Change Investigation
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Assessment

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Hook (10 min) | Show images of: drought-affected farm, healthy farm, deforested area, regenerating forest. "What changed? What happened to the living things?" | Observe |
| Task (30 min) | Cold Task: "Design an investigation to find out how environmental changes affect living things. Choose ONE change (drought, flood, fire, deforestation). What will you investigate? How will you measure the impact on living things? What evidence will you look for? Draw your investigation." | Design investigation |
| Share (10 min) | 3–4 students share designs | Share |

**Success Criteria Check:** Student designs an investigation with a variable, measurement approach, and prediction.

### Lesson 2: What Is an Ecosystem?
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Engage

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Ecosystem Definition (15 min) | "ECOSYSTEM = community of living organisms + non-living environment interacting together. ABIOTIC factors: temperature, water, light, soil, air. BIOTIC factors: plants, animals, fungi, bacteria. All interconnected." | Learn |
| Identify Ecosystems (15 min) | Sort: pond, desert, coral reef, city park, eucalypt forest, Antarctic ocean. Explain why each is an ecosystem. | Sort and explain |
| Food Webs in Ecosystems (15 min) | Draw a simple pond food web: algae (producer) → water fleas → fish → heron. Show interconnected chains. | Draw |
| Exit Ticket (5 min) | "What is the difference between biotic and abiotic factors in an ecosystem?" | Written response |

**Success Criteria Check:** Defines ecosystem and correctly categorises at least 5 factors as biotic or abiotic.

### Lesson 3: Changes in Ecosystems
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Engage

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Types of Change (15 min) | Natural changes: seasons, drought, flood, fire, volcanic eruption, predator-prey cycles. Human changes: deforestation, pollution, urbanisation, agriculture, introduced species. | Learn |
| Impact Scale (15 min) | Some changes are temporary (seasonal rain), some are long-term (deforestation), some are irreversible (species extinction). Sort examples. | Sort |
| Ecosystem Response (15 min) | When an ecosystem changes: organisms must ADAPT, MOVE, or DIE. Some species are resilient (bounce back), some are sensitive (cannot survive changes). | Learn |
| Write (5 min) | "Ecosystems change due to natural events and human activities. Living things must respond to these changes to survive." | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Classifies at least 4 changes as natural or human-caused and describes possible ecosystem responses.

## WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Structural Adaptations

### Lesson 1: Physical Features That Help Survival
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explore

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What Is a Structural Adaptation? (15 min) | "A STRUCTURAL ADAPTATION is a physical feature of an organism's body that helps it survive in its environment. Examples: polar bear's white fur (camouflage + warmth), cactus spines (reduce water loss + protection), eagle's sharp talons (catch prey), shark's streamlined body (swim fast)." | Learn |
| Structural Adaptation Sort (15 min) | Animals: camel, chameleon, duck, platypus, cheetah. Features: humps (fat storage), colour change (camouflage), webbed feet (swimming), electroreception (detect prey), speed (chase prey). | Sort |
| Design an Organism (15 min) | "Design an animal for a desert environment. Give it 3 structural adaptations. Explain why each helps it survive." | Design |
| Share (5 min) | 3–4 students share | Share |

**Success Criteria Check:** Identifies at least 5 structural adaptations and explains their survival function.

### Lesson 2: Australian Structural Adaptations
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explore

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Australian Adaptations (20 min) | KOALA: grey fur (camouflage in eucalypt trees), large nose (sense smell of leaves), no tail (sit comfortably in forks). PLATYPUS: waterproof fur (stays warm underwater), flat tail (steering and storing fat), bill (electroreception to find prey in murky water). EMU: long legs (run fast up to 50km/h), double-jointed knees (striding motion), grey-brown feathers (desert camouflage). | Learn |
| Adaptations Chart (15 min) | Create table: ANIMAL — ENVIRONMENT — STRUCTURAL ADAPTATION — HOW IT HELPS SURVIVE. | Create table |
| Research (15 min) | Students research one other uniquely Australian adaptation. | Research |

**Success Criteria Check:** Researches and describes at least 3 Australian animals' structural adaptations with survival explanations.

### Lesson 3: Structural Adaptation Investigation
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explore

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Beak Investigation (20 min) | "Beak shape is a structural adaptation." Provide bird beak models or images: hooked beak (hawk — tears meat), cone beak (finch — cracks seeds), long thin beak (hummingbird — drinks nectar), wide flat beak (duck — filters water). Match to food source. | Investigate |
| Wing Shape Investigation (15 min) | Compare wing shapes: broad rounded (forest birds — maneuverable), long narrow (soaring birds — glide long distances), pointed (fast fliers — aerodynamic). Connect to habitat. | Investigate |
| Write Conclusions (15 min) | "Structural adaptations are physical features that help organisms survive. The shape of a beak or wing tells us about the organism's habitat and diet." | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Investigates structural adaptations of beaks and wings, correctly connecting shape to function and habitat.

## WEEK 3 — EXPLAIN: Behavioural Adaptations

### Lesson 1: What Animals Do — Behavioural Adaptations
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explain

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What Is a Behavioural Adaptation? (15 min) | "A BEHAVIOURAL ADAPTATION is something an animal DOES (behaviour) to help it survive. Unlike structural adaptations, behavioural adaptations are actions, not body parts." Examples: birds migrating south for winter, bears hibernating in winter, possums playing dead to escape predators, meerkats standing guard while others feed. | Learn |
| Sort Behaviours (15 min) | Sort into: MIGRATION, HIBERNATION, DEFENSE, FORAGING, SOCIAL. Label each. | Sort |
| Why Behaviours Evolved (15 min) | "Behavioural adaptations evolved because animals that behaved these ways were more likely to survive and pass on their genes. Playing dead (thanatosis) — predator loses interest, animal escapes. Guard duty in meerkats — one watches while others eat safely." | Learn |
| Exit Ticket (5 min) | "Name one behavioural adaptation and explain how it helps an animal survive." | Written response |

**Success Criteria Check:** Distinguishes behavioural from structural adaptations and correctly categorises at least 5 behaviours.

---

### Lesson 2: Australian Behavioural Adaptations
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explain

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Australian Behaviours (20 min) | KANGAROO: thermoregulation — licking forearms (evaporative cooling), hopping (energy-efficient movement). GOANNA: climb trees to escape predators, bask in sun to warm up (cold-blooded). BUDGERIGAR: flock behaviour — thousands of birds moving together (confuses predators, finds water). BUSH STONE-CURLEW: lies still with eyes open when threatened (freezes to avoid detection). | Learn |
| Investigation (15 min) | Research one Australian animal's behavioural adaptation: How does it behave? Why did this behaviour evolve? What happens if the behaviour doesn't work? | Research |
| Present (15 min) | 3–4 students present research | Present |

**Success Criteria Check:** Researches and presents one Australian behavioural adaptation with survival explanation.

### Lesson 3: Plant Adaptations
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explain

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Plant Structural Adaptations (15 min) | "Plants also have structural adaptations. WATTLE: deep root system (access water in drought), hard seed coat (survives fire, germinates after rain). EUCALYPT: waxy leaves (reduce water loss), shed bark (removes parasites, prevents fire spreading up trunk). SUNDEW: sticky tentacles (trap insects — supplement nutrient-poor soil)." | Learn |
| Plant Behavioural Adaptations (15 min) | "Plants have behavioural adaptations too. SUNFLOWER TRACKING: flowers follow the sun across the sky (maximise photosynthesis). LEAVES DROPPING: deciduous trees drop leaves in drought (reduce water loss). SEED GERMINATION TIMING: some seeds only germinate after fire (clears competition, adds nutrients)." | Learn |
| Compare Plants to Animals (15 min) | Venn: PLANT ADAPTATIONS ←→ ANIMAL ADAPTATIONS. Same: both structural and behavioural types. Different: plants can't move to migrate, animals can. | Compare |
| Write (5 min) | "Plants have structural and behavioural adaptations just like animals." | Write |

**Differentiation:** Gifted students: research carnivorous plants in Australia (Venus flytrap is not Australian, but Australian sundews and pitcher plants are).

**Success Criteria Check:** Identifies at least 4 plant adaptations (structural and behavioural) and explains survival benefits.

## WEEK 4 — ELABORATE: Responding to Change

### Lesson 1: Environmental Change
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Types of Environmental Change (15 min) | Gradual changes: climate change (slow warming), drought (months to years), urbanisation (decades). Rapid changes: bushfire, flood, volcanic eruption. Human-caused: pollution, deforestation, introduced species. | Learn |
| How Do Organisms Respond? (15 min) | MOVE (migration — temporary or permanent), ADAPT (structural or behavioural change over generations), DIE (if cannot move or adapt). Short-term vs long-term changes. | Learn |
| Case Study — Drought Response (15 min) | "During drought: kangaroos move to areas with more water. Emus may wander great distances. Wattle trees drop leaves. Frogs burrow into mud and aestivate (sleep through drought). Insects may die but eggs survive." | Learn |
| Sort Responses (5 min) | Sort: kangaroo, frog, wattle, bird migrating, lizard basking. Into: MOVE / ADAPT / DIE. | Sort |

**Success Criteria Check:** Classifies at least 5 organisms' responses to environmental change.

### Lesson 2: Migration and Hibernation
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Migration (20 min) | "MIGRATION = regular movement from one place to another with the seasons. LONG-DISTANCE: Arctic tern (poles to poles — 70,000km round trip annually). AUSTRALIAN: grey nomad birds (northern Australia → southern Australia for summer), humpback whales (Antarctica → warm northern waters to breed). SHORT-DISTANCE: possums may move locally." | Learn |
| Hibernation and Aestivation (15 min) | "HIBERNATION: deep winter sleep (bats, echidnas). Body temperature drops, heartbeat slows, stored fat sustains life. AESTIVATION: summer sleep for animals in hot/dry climates. CORROBOREE FROG: aestivates in dried mud for years during drought." | Learn |
| Why Not Just Stay? (10 min) | "Why migrate? Food availability changes with seasons. If you stay, you might starve. Hibernation? Some environments are too cold/wet to find food in winter. Migration and hibernation are behavioural adaptations to avoid harsh conditions." | Discuss |
| Draw Migration Map (5 min) | Draw a simple migration route for one Australian migratory species. | Draw |

**Success Criteria Check:** Explains the difference between migration and hibernation/aestivation and provides at least 2 examples of each.

### Lesson 3: Seasonal Changes
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Seasonal Adaptations (15 min) | "Organisms adapt to seasonal changes. BREEDING SEASONS: many birds breed in spring when food is plentiful. PLANT CYCLES: eucalypts drop old leaves in spring when new growth appears (not autumn like northern hemisphere). FUR/SHEDDING: some mammals grow thicker winter coats, shed in spring." | Learn |
| Australian Seasonal Changes (15 min) | Indigenous Australian calendars: some Aboriginal groups recognised 6 seasons based on environmental signs, not just 4. Kakadu area: 6 seasons: Garrthalara (cool dry), Mid-dry (hot dry), Bangardell (early wet), Gurunga (mid wet), Yekke (late wet), K O'MELAK (early dry transition). Plants and animals respond to these seasons. | Learn |
| Investigation (15 min) | Track seasonal changes in school ground over 4 weeks: bird activity, plant growth/flowering, insect presence. Record. | Investigate |
| Write (5 min) | "Seasonal changes cause organisms to change their behaviour and appearance throughout the year." | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Tracks seasonal changes in the school environment and documents at least 3 organism responses.

## WEEK 5 — ELABORATE: Human Impact on Ecosystems

### Lesson 1: Case Study — Great Barrier Reef
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Introduce the Reef (15 min) | "Great Barrier Reef: 2,300km long, 1,500+ fish species, world's largest coral reef ecosystem. CORAL BLEACHING: triggered by rising ocean temperatures (climate change). Coral expels symbiotic algae (zooxanthellae), loses colour, turns white, becomes vulnerable to disease and death." | Learn |
| Investigate in Groups (20 min) | Groups research: What is coral bleaching? What causes it? What species are affected? What is being done? Create fact sheet. | Research |
| Impact Chain (10 min) | Build impact chain: Rising ocean temperatures → coral bleaching → fish lose habitat → predator food chains break → reef ecosystem collapses → tourism and fishing industries suffer. | Build chain |
| Write (5 min) | "Coral bleaching shows how a small environmental change can cascade through an entire ecosystem." | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Describes coral bleaching, identifies at least 3 affected species, and explains the cascading impact.

### Lesson 2: Case Study — Australian Bushland
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Bushland Changes (15 min) | "Australian bushland has been dramatically altered: 54% of Australia's land environment is affected by land use change. Major causes: agriculture (clearing for crops, livestock grazing), urbanisation (Perth, Sydney urban sprawl), mining. Remnant vegetation = isolated patches of original vegetation surrounded by changed landscape." | Learn |
| Impact on Wildlife (15 min) | "Fragmented bushland: animals become isolated, genetic diversity decreases, species like Leadbeater's possum (endangered) lose habitat connectivity. Bushfire risk increases in fragmented landscapes. Weeds invade edges. Introduced predators (foxes, cats) affect small marsupials." | Learn |
| Investigation (15 min) | Research one threatened Australian species: What is the threat? What is being done? Present. | Research |
| Conservation Strategies (5 min) | "Conservation strategies: wildlife corridors (connecting vegetation patches), revegetation, fox and cat control, fire management." | Learn |

**Success Criteria Check:** Researches one threatened Australian species and evaluates conservation strategies.

### Lesson 3: Conservation Strategies
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Conservation Strategies (20 min) | "PROTECTED AREAS: National parks and marine parks protect ecosystems. WILDLIFE CORRIDORS: vegetated strips connecting habitat patches, allowing animal movement. BUSHFIRE MANAGEMENT: strategic burning to reduce fuel load while protecting biodiversity." | Learn |

## WEEK 6 — EXPLAIN: Biodiversity

### Lesson 1: What Is Biodiversity?
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explain

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Three Levels of Biodiversity (20 min) | "BIODIVERSITY = the variety of all living things in an area. THREE LEVELS: (1) GENETIC DIVERSITY: genetic variation within a species (different coat colours, sizes). (2) SPECIES DIVERSITY: variety of different species in an area. (3) ECOSYSTEM DIVERSITY: variety of different ecosystems in a region." | Learn |
| Biodiversity Walk (15 min) | Walk school ground. Count different species of: plants, insects, birds. Create a simple biodiversity inventory. | Walk and count |
| Why Is Biodiversity Important? (15 min) | "High biodiversity = ecosystem resilience. Many species means if one is lost, others can fill its role. Low biodiversity = fragile ecosystem — one change can collapse the whole system." | Learn |
| Write (5 min) | "Biodiversity is the variety of life at three levels: genetic, species, and ecosystem." | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Defines biodiversity, identifies the three levels, and conducts a simple biodiversity inventory.

---

### Lesson 2: Why Biodiversity Matters
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explain

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Ecosystem Services (15 min) | "ECOSYSTEM SERVICES: benefits humans receive from ecosystems. POLLINATION: bees and insects pollinate crops (>$6 billion/year in Australia). WATER PURIFICATION: wetlands filter pollutants. SOIL FORMATION: decomposers recycle nutrients. CLIMATE REGULATION: forests absorb CO2. FISHERIES: healthy oceans provide fish. Without biodiversity, these services collapse." | Learn |
| Food Web Complexity (15 min) | "Complex food webs are more resilient. Simple example: if you remove bees, apple trees can't be pollinated → no apples. Complex example: in a diverse forest, if bees disappear, other pollinators partially compensate." | Learn |
| Case Study — Loss of a Keystone Species (15 min) | "KEYSTONE SPECIES: a species that has a disproportionate effect on its environment relative to its abundance. SEA OTTERS in kelp forests: eat sea urchins. Without otters, urchins overgraze kelp → kelp forests collapse → fish lose habitat. Without sea otters, the entire ecosystem changes dramatically." | Learn |
| Write (5 min) | "Keystone species play crucial roles in maintaining ecosystem structure. Their removal causes dramatic changes." | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Explains ecosystem services and identifies at least 1 keystone species with its ecosystem role.

### Lesson 3: Threats to Biodiversity
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Five Major Threats (20 min) | "1. HABITAT LOSS: destruction and fragmentation of ecosystems. 2. CLIMATE CHANGE: shifts temperature zones, coral bleaching, changed fire regimes. 3. POLLUTION: plastic in oceans, chemical run-off into waterways. 4. INTRODUCED SPECIES: cats, foxes, rabbits, cane toads outcompete or prey on native species. 5. OVEREXPLOITATION: overfishing, overhunting depletes species." | Learn |
| Australian Examples (15 min) | Habitat loss: 80% of temperate grassland, 50% of malleefowl habitat destroyed. Climate change: 50% of Great Barrier Reef bleached since 2016. Introduced species: Cane toad introduced in 1935 to control cane beetles, now widespread and toxic to native predators. | Learn |
| Conservation Response (10 min) | "What can be done? The same 5 strategies from last lesson. PLUS: community education, sustainable practices, Indigenous land management partnerships." | Learn |
| Exit Ticket (5 min) | "Name 2 threats to biodiversity and one way each can be reduced." | Written response |

**Success Criteria Check:** Identifies at least 4 threats to biodiversity and evaluates at least 1 conservation response.
