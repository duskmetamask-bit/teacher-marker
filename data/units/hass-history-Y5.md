# World War I: An Australian Story — History Unit Plan

**Unit Duration:** 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons
**Year Level:** Year 5
**Curriculum Link:** Australian Curriculum HASS v9 — History (Years 5–6)
**Unit Big Idea:** World War I was one of the most significant events in modern history and profoundly shaped Australia as a nation. Understanding the causes, experiences, and aftermath of WWI helps us understand the modern world, Australia's place in it, and the ongoing impact of war.
**Cold Task:** Week 1, Lesson 1 — Investigate the causes and consequences of World War I and explain its lasting impact on Australia
**Hot Task:** Week 8, Lesson 1 — Investigate a specific aspect of WWI and present a considered analysis of its impact, legacy, and relevance today

---

## Unit Overview

**Learning Intention:**
We are learning to investigate World War I — its causes, experiences, consequences, and ongoing legacy — and to develop critical perspectives on war and its impact on nations and people.

**Success Criteria:**
- I can describe the causes of World War I and explain how they interacted.
- I can describe Australia's involvement in WWI including key battles and the human cost.
- I can describe at least 2 perspectives on WWI (soldiers, nurses, families, Aboriginal soldiers, Turkish soldiers).
- I can explain at least 3 effects of WWI on Australia.
- I can use at least 2 types of sources to investigate WWI.
- I can evaluate the legacy of WWI and explain its relevance today.

## WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: A World at War

### Lesson 1: Cold Task — A World at War
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Hook (5 min) | Show an image of a WWI battlefield (trench, no man's land). "This is World War I. It was one of the deadliest conflicts in human history. 17 million people died. What do you know about it? What questions do you have?" | Observe and question |
| Introduction (5 min) | "In this unit, we will investigate World War I — one of the most significant events in modern history. WWI changed everything: empires fell, new nations were created, and Australia's identity was forged in battle. We will examine this with critical eyes — both the heroism AND the horror." | Listen |
| Cold Task (30 min) | "Write an investigation of World War I. Part 1: What were the causes of WWI? Part 2: How did Australia become involved? Part 3: What was the human cost? Part 4: What is one lasting impact of WWI on Australia today? Write 6–10 sentences with evidence." Year 5 expectation: structured, evidence-based, multiple causes, lasting impact identified. | Independent |

**Differentiation:** Image word bank. Sentence starters: "The causes of WWI were..." / "Australia became involved because..." / "The human cost was..." / "One lasting impact of WWI is..."

**Success Criteria Check:** Student identifies at least 2 causes of WWI and at least 1 lasting impact.

### Lesson 2: The World in 1914
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Engage (Hook)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Europe in 1914 (10 min) | "In 1914, Europe was divided into two main power blocks: the TRIPLE ENTENTE (Britain, France, Russia — the Allied powers) and the TRIPLE ALLIANCE (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy — the Central powers). This division was the result of decades of competition, pride, and fear." | Learn |
| Imperialism and Competition (10 min) | "European powers were competing for COLONIES — territories around the world that provided raw materials and markets. This competition created tension. Germany and Britain were in an ARMS RACE — both building massive navies. Britain saw Germany as the greatest threat to its empire." | Learn |
| The Balkan Tinderbox (10 min) | "The BALKANS (in south-eastern Europe) was a 'tinderbox.' Multiple ethnic groups (Serbian, Croatian, Bosnian, Albanian, Macedonian) were fighting for independence from the Ottoman and Austro-Hungarian empires. Nationalism was intense. One spark could ignite the whole region." | Learn |
| Map (10 min) | Label a map: Triple Entente / Triple Alliance / major powers / colonial territories / Balkans. "Which alliance system covers more of the world? What does this tell us?" | Label |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can label the two alliance systems and describe the competitive environment of 1914.

### Lesson 3: Causes of World War I
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Engage (Concept Introduction)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| The Five Causes (15 min) | "The causes of WWI are often remembered as ALLIANCES + ASSASSINATION — but it is more complex. FIVE causes: (1) ALLIANCES — countries promised to defend each other (2) ARMS RACE — massive military buildup (3) IMPERIALISM — competition for colonies (4) NATIONALISM — intense pride and conflict (5) MILITARISM — belief that war was a valid political tool. These five together created a volatile mixture." | Learn |
| The Assassination (10 min) | "On 28 June 1914, Gavrilo Princip (a Bosnian Serb nationalist, backed by Serbia) assassinated Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary in Sarajevo. Austria-Hungary blamed Serbia. Germany backed Austria-Hungary. Russia backed Serbia. Within 5 weeks, all major European powers were at war." | Learn |
| Why Did It Escalate? (10 min) | "Why did the assassination cause a world war? Because of ALLIANCES — one country's war became every country's war. The arms race meant countries were ready for war. Leaders believed their alliances meant they would not face war alone. No one wanted to be the first to back down." | Learn |
| Write (5 min) | "Write 4 sentences: What were the 5 causes of WWI? Which do you think was the most important? Why?" | Write |

**Differentiation:** EAL students create a visual cause diagram. Gifted students: argue which cause was most important with evidence.

**Success Criteria Check:** Can name all 5 causes of WWI and explain how they interacted.

## WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Australia Goes to War

### Lesson 1: Australia Responds
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Australia in 1914 (10 min) | "In 1914, Australia was a young nation of just 5 million people — still closely tied to Britain. When Britain declared war on Germany, Australia automatically followed. The Australian government supported Britain without debate. This was called 'loyalty to the Empire.'" | Learn |
| The Call to Arms (10 min) | "Australia responded with ENTHUSIASM. Men volunteered in massive numbers. Posters, newspapers, and community pressure encouraged enlistment. Many felt it was their DUTY to fight for the Empire. Recruiting posters used language of adventure, duty, and manliness." | Learn |
| Enlistment Statistics (10 min) | "Australia's enlistment: 416,000 served overseas (about 38.7% of the male population aged 18–44). 60,000 died. 152,000 were wounded (including many with permanent injuries). The scale was MASSIVE for a small population." | Observe |
| Why Did Men Enlist? (10 min) | "Men enlisted for many reasons: PATRIOTISM (love of country and Empire) / PEER PRESSURE (everyone was doing it) / ADVENTURE (young men wanted excitement) / ECONOMICS (soldiers were paid) / BELIEF IN THE CAUSE (they believed they were fighting for civilisation). Not all enlistments were heroic — some were pressured, some were naive about what war would be like." | Discuss |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe Australia's response to WWI and at least 3 reasons why men enlisted.

### Lesson 2: The ANZACs
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What Was the AIF? (10 min) | "The Australian Imperial Force (AIF) was the official name for Australian troops in WWI. They were volunteers — not conscripted. Soldiers had to be aged 18–35, physically fit, and approved by a medical board. By 1916, 2 in 3 eligible Australian men had enlisted." | Learn |
| Who Were the ANZACs? (10 min) | "ANZAC stands for Australian and New Zealand Army Corps. The first ANZACs arrived in Egypt in late 1914 for training before being sent to the Gallipoli campaign. ANZAC became a label for the identity of Australian soldiers — brave, tough, egalitarian, resourceful, irreverent." | Learn |
| Identity and Myth (10 min) | "The ANZAC identity became central to Australian national identity. This was partly REAL (Australian soldiers were very brave) and partly CREATED (the legend grew after the war to serve national identity needs). This is a key question in history: How do myths and legends shape national identity?" | Understand |
| Write (5 min) | "Write 3 sentences: Who were the ANZACs? What did the ANZAC legend become? How is it both true and created?" | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe the AIF and ANZAC identity and explain the complex relationship between real events and myth.

### Lesson 3: Gallipoli
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| The Campaign (10 min) | "On 25 April 1915, ANZAC forces landed at Gallipoli, Turkey (then part of the Ottoman Empire). The plan: seize Constantinople (Istanbul), knock Turkey out of the war, and help Russia. It was a bold plan that FAILED. But the landing created the ANZAC legend." | Learn |
| Why Did It Fail? (15 min) | "Gallipoli failed because: the Ottomans were well-prepared / the terrain was incredibly difficult (steep cliffs, narrow gullies) / the intelligence was poor / the Allied powers were disorganised / the landings were poorly coordinated / once committed, reinforcements were too slow. The campaign lasted 8 months. The Allies evacuated in December 1915." | Learn |
| Gallipoli's Casualties (10 min) | "At Gallipoli: 8,700 Australians died / 19,700 Australians were wounded / total Allied casualties: 140,000 / total Ottoman casualties: 200,000+ / one of the most brutal campaigns in WWI despite not being the largest. The human cost was enormous." | Observe |
| Why Does Gallipoli Matter? (5 min) | "Write 3 sentences: Why is Gallipoli significant in Australian history? What did it create?" | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe the Gallipoli campaign, its failure, and at least 3 casualty statistics.

## WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: The Western Front

### Lesson 1: The Western Front
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Where Was the Western Front? (10 min) | "The WESTERN FRONT was a 700km line of trenches running from the Swiss border to the English Channel, through Belgium and France. It was the main theatre of WWI in Europe. From 1914 to 1918, both sides dug in and fought a brutal war of attrition." | Learn |
| Life in the Trenches (15 min) | "Trench life was HELLISH: soldiers lived in mud and water / rat infestations / lice and trench foot / constant noise (shelling, machine guns) / no sleep / food was canned rations / psychological trauma was immense. Soldiers did 'front line' duty for about 10 days, then moved to 'support' trenches, then 'reserve' — but the horror never stopped." | Learn |
| What Was the Front Like? (10 min) | "The landscape was devastation: no-man's-land (between the trenches) was cratered, filled with bodies and barbed wire / artillery turned the earth into a moonscape / chemical weapons (gas) caused terrible burns and death / soldiers called it 'the crater' and 'the morgue of Europe.'" | Observe |
| Exit Ticket (5 min) | "Write 2 sentences: What was the Western Front? What was life like in the trenches?" | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe the Western Front and at least 3 aspects of trench life.

### Lesson 2: Key Battles on the Western Front
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Major Australian Battles (15 min) | "Australian soldiers fought in major Western Front battles: THE SOMME (1916, France — first major engagement for Australian troops on the Western Front) / BAPAUME (1917, France — after Messines victory) / PASSCHENDAELE (Belgium, 1917 — one of the worst battles, 5,250 Australian casualties in one battle) / VILLERS-BRETONNEUX (1918, France — first tank vs tank battle, Australians stopped German breakthrough)." | Learn |
| The Somme (1916) (10 min) | "The Somme (1 July – 18 November 1916) was one of the bloodiest battles in history. On the FIRST DAY, the British lost 57,470 men (19,240 killed). Australian forces fought near Pozières — 23,000 Australian casualties in 6 weeks. The battle achieved almost nothing strategically but showed the devastating cost of WWI." | Learn |
| Why So Many Casualties? (10 min) | "Why were casualties so high? Because of WEAPONS TECHNOLOGY vs TACTICS: Machine guns (defensive) vs infantry charges (offensive) / Artillery (indiscriminate death from distance) / Chemical weapons (gas) / Trenches (trench warfare was designed to prevent breakthrough but at enormous cost) / Commanders (generals) often used outdated tactics (wave attacks) that resulted in mass slaughter." | Learn |
| Write (5 min) | "Write 3 sentences: What was the Western Front? What did soldiers experience there? Why were casualties so high?" | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 2 Western Front battles involving Australian forces and explain why casualties were so high.

### Lesson 3: The Human Cost
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|

| The Impact on Families (10 min) | "The impact was not just military — it was SOCIAL: thousands of widows / thousands of orphans / broken families and communities / mental health impacts (then called 'shell shock,' now understood as PTSD) / memorial projects began (theReturned and Services League, war memorials) / economic impact on families who lost breadwinners." | Learn |

(10 min) | "WWI's impact on Australia: population setback (deaths meant fewer births) / economic disruption (post-war reconstruction needed) / social change (women's role changed, returned soldiers challenged old authority) / changed relationship with Britain (loyalty questioned after so many died for an empire that did not defend Australia directly)." | Learn |

### Lesson 3: Aboriginal Soldiers
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Aboriginal Australians in WWI (10 min) | "Approximately 1,000 Aboriginal Australians enlisted in WWI. They served despite DISCRIMINATION — they were not counted as citizens, could not vote, and faced widespread oppression. The military was one of the few areas where they were treated more equally." | Learn |
| Why Did They Enlist? (10 min) | "Some enlisted for PATRIOTISM (love of country, proving loyalty) / some for ADVENTURE / some for PAY / some because they wanted equality. Many were motivated by a desire to prove that Aboriginal Australians were worthy of the same rights as other Australians." | Learn |
| After the War (10 min) | "After the war, Aboriginal veterans returned to DISCRIMINATION. They were not granted the same medals, recognition, or support as non-Indigenous soldiers. Some returned to mission restrictions, to the oppression they left behind. This was a profound injustice." | Learn |
| The Forgotten Heroes (10 min) | "Aboriginal WWI service was largely ERASED from official history until recently. Activists, historians, and communities have worked to recover their stories. This is an example of whose perspectives were included in history and whose were excluded." | Learn |
| Write (5 min) | "Write 3 sentences: What was the experience of Aboriginal soldiers in WWI? Why were their stories 'forgotten'? Why does remembering them matter?" | Write |

**Note:** Teach with respect. Acknowledge the courage and injustice.

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe the experience of Aboriginal soldiers in WWI, explain why their stories were forgotten, and evaluate why remembering them matters.

## WEEK 5 — EXPLAIN: The Aftermath

### Lesson 1: Armistice and Peace
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| The Armistice (10 min) | "On 11 November 1918, at 11am, the guns fell silent — the ARMISTICE (ceasefire). World War I was over. The timing (the 11th hour of the 11th day of the 11th month) is commemorated every year on REMEMBRANCE DAY. It was a moment of immense relief." | Learn |

| The Collapse of Empires (10 min) | "WWI caused the collapse of four empires: the Ottoman Empire (Turkey) / the Austro-Hungarian Empire / the German Empire / the Russian Empire (which became the Soviet Union). These collapses created new nations, new conflicts, and long-lasting instability." | Learn |
| Write (5 min) | "Write 3 sentences: What was the Armistice? What did the Treaty of Versailles do? What empires collapsed?" | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe the Armistice, the Treaty of Versailles, and at least 2 impacts of the post-war settlement.

## WEEK 6 — ELABORATE: Legacy of War

### Lesson 1: The Memorial
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate (Application)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Why Do We Build Memorials? (10 min) | "MEMORIALS are structures built to remind us of people who have died. They serve: to HONOUR the dead (recognise sacrifice) / to HELP the living grieve (a place to mourn) / to TEACH future generations (what happened, why it matters) / to SHAPE national identity (who we are through memory)." | Learn |
| Memorials Are Selective (10 min) | "Memorials do NOT include everyone equally. Historically: Aboriginal veterans were largely EXCLUDED from memorials / ethnic communities who fought for Australia were often not included / women were represented as nurses or mourners, not soldiers / the perspectives of Turkish soldiers (the enemy) were excluded." | Learn |
| The Australian War Memorial (10 min) | "The Australian War Memorial (Canberra) is the national memorial to Australian war service. It has: the Memorial (monument) / the Museum (exhibits) / the Research Centre (records). It is one of the most significant memorial-museums in the world." | Learn |
| Design a Memorial (10 min) | "Design a memorial for WWI. It must: honour those who served and died / include at least 3 groups (soldiers, nurses, families) / tell the truth about the war / be accessible to the community." | Design |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 2 purposes of war memorials and evaluate whose perspectives are included and excluded.

### Lesson 3: Lessons of War
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate (Application)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What Have We Learned? (10 min) | "What have we learned from WWI? The lesson most commonly cited: 'Never again.' But has 'never again' been achieved? | Write (5 min) | "Write 3 sentences: What lessons should we have learned from WWI? Is it relevant today? Why?" | Write |
