# Informative Writing Unit: Investigating Our World
**Unit Duration:** 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons
**Year Level:** Years 3–4
**Curriculum Link:** Australian Curriculum English v9 — Informative Writing
**Topics Covered:** Reports, explanations, factual texts about science and HASS topics
**Cold Task:** Pre-assessment — write a report on an animal of choice
**Hot Task:** Post-assessment — same format, measured for growth

## Unit Overview

**Learning Intention:**
We are learning to write informative texts that inform, explain and share knowledge about real-world topics with accuracy and clarity.

**Success Criteria:**
- I can plan an informative text with an introduction, body and conclusion
- I can use factual, accurate information from research
- I can organise ideas using headings and subheadings
- I can use diagrams, labels and captions to support my text
- I can use a glossary to explain unfamiliar words
- I can use language that is objective and informative (not persuasive)

## WEEK 1 — WHAT IS INFORMATIVE WRITING?

### Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Assessment

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Hook (5 min) | Show two texts: one narrative story about dolphins, one informative report about dolphins. Ask: What's different? Which tells you more facts? | Observe and share observations |
| Task (35 min) | Cold Task: Choose any animal. Write a report about it — what it looks like, where it lives, what it eats. Try to include at least 3 facts. Year 3: 100-200 words. Year 4: 150-300 words. | Independent writing |

**Differentiation:** Word bank for EAL students. Sentence starters available.

---

### Lesson 2: Features of Reports (I Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (15 min) | Bring in 3-4 published儿童 information books. Pass around. Identify features together: title, contents page, headings, facts, diagrams, captions, glossary. | Handle books, identify features |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Feature chart on board: WHAT IT DOES / WHERE IT'S FOUND / WHAT IT EATS / SPECIAL FEATURES. Compare with narrative features. | Note differences in writing notebooks |
| Share (10 min) | Students share one thing they noticed about how informative texts are different from stories | |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can name at least 5 features that make a text informative.

### Lesson 3: Choosing a Topic (We Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Guided — Topic Selection

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (5 min) | Brainstorm topics as a class: animals, habitats, natural disasters, ancient civilizations, how things work. Display word splash. | |
| We Do (15 min) | Students narrow down to 3 potential topics. Discuss: What do you already know? What do you want to find out? Use KWL chart (Know / Want to know / Learned). | Complete KWL chart for 3 topics |
| You Do (15 min) | Choose final topic. Begin filling in the K column. | Select topic, start KWL |

## WEEK 2 — RESEARCH AND NOTE-TAKING

### Lesson 1: Finding Facts (I Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|

| You Do (10 min) | Take notes on 3 more facts about your topic — in your own words. | Independent note-taking |

### Lesson 3: Organising Notes (You Do)

## WEEK 3 — STRUCTURE: INTRODUCTION + BODY

### Lesson 1: Writing the Introduction (I Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (15 min) | Analyse 3 introductions from published reports. Pattern: General topic sentence → Hook fact → Outline of report. Model writing one live on the board. | Identify pattern |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Introduction formula: "The [NAME] is a [TYPE OF ANIMAL/THING] that [ONE KEY FACT]. It is found [LOCATION]. This report will explore [3-4 KEY POINTS TO BE COVERED]." | Note formula |
| You Do (15 min) | Draft introduction for your report using the formula. | Independent drafting |

---

### Lesson 2: Body Paragraphs (I Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Each body paragraph = Topic Sentence + Facts + Example. Use one paragraph to model: "The Great Barrier Reef is home to over 1,500 species of fish." → explain, add facts, give example. | Watch paragraph construction |
| We Do (15 min) | Together write one body paragraph about a shared topic. Focus on: Does each sentence support the topic sentence? | Collaborate on paragraph |
| You Do (15 min) | Write one body paragraph about your topic. Check: does every sentence relate to the topic sentence? | Independent paragraph |

---

### Lesson 3: Topic Sentences (We Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (5 min) | Show: Body paragraph without topic sentence → Body paragraph with topic sentence. What's the difference? | |
| We Do (15 min) | Given 5 mixed sentences, identify which ones work as topic sentences. Justify why. | Practice identification |
| You Do (15 min) | Add or improve topic sentences in your existing body paragraph. | Revise paragraphs |

## WEEK 5 — OBJECTIVE LANGUAGE

### Lesson 1: Informative vs Persuasive (I Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (15 min) | Read two texts about the same topic (e.g. "Dogs are great pets"). One informative (facts), one persuasive (opinion). Highlight: facts vs opinions. Discuss: Why is it important for informative writing to be objective? | Compare texts |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Clue words for opinions: I think, best, amazing, everyone should, most people agree. Clue words for facts: according to, research shows, data indicates, studies confirm. | Note clue words |
| You Do (10 min) | Sort 10 sentences into "Fact" or "Opinion" piles. | Sort sentences |

---

### Lesson 2: Fact vs Opinion (We Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Guided Practice

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| We Do (20 min) | Given a paragraph about a topic, underline facts in blue, circle opinions in red. Discuss disagreements. | Edit paragraphs |
| You Do (15 min) | Read through your own notes. Mark each one: F (fact I can verify) or O (opinion or unverified). Remove or rephrase opinions. | Check notes |

---

### Lesson 3: Using Precise Vocabulary (I Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Show: Vague → Precise. "The animal is big" → "The elephant seal weighs up to 3,000kg." "The plant lives in water" → "The water lily grows in freshwater lakes and ponds across southern Australia." | Compare precision |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Technical vocabulary: Every subject has specific terms. List 5 technical terms from your topic and their everyday equivalents. | Research vocabulary |
| You Do (15 min) | Revise 3 sentences in your report to use more precise, technical language. | Revise sentences |

## RUBRIC — Informative Writing (Years 3–4)
**AC English v9 Alignment:** AC9E3LY02 (Yr3) / AC9E4LY02 (Yr4) — Informative Writing

| Criterion | A (Excellent) | B (Strong) | C (Satisfactory) | D (Developing) | E (Beginning) |
|-----------|---------------|------------|-----------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Text Structure** | Well-crafted introduction clearly establishes topic. Logical body paragraphs with clear topic sentences. Conclusion summarises without introducing new information. | Clear introduction. Logical paragraphs. Conclusion present. | Introduction and conclusion present. Paragraphs mostly logical. | Structure attempted but uneven. Some paragraphs off-topic. | No clear structure. Difficult to follow. |
| **Accuracy + Research** | All facts are accurate and verifiable. Multiple sources consulted. Technical vocabulary used correctly throughout. | All facts accurate. Some sources evident. Technical vocabulary mostly correct. | Most facts accurate. Limited sources. Some technical vocabulary. | Some inaccuracies present. Sources unclear. Technical vocabulary limited. | Facts inaccurate or missing. No sources. |
| **Organisation + Features** | All key features present: headings, subheadings, diagram with caption, glossary, index. Layout is professional and logical. | Most features present. Layout clear and readable. | Some features present. Basic layout. | Few features present. Layout disorganised. | No features. No layout. |
| **Language (Objective)** | Consistently objective. Precise technical vocabulary used purposefully. No opinions presented as facts. | Mostly objective. Good technical vocabulary. Occasional subjective language. | Some objective language. Some technical vocabulary. Some opinion-as-fact. | Mostly subjective or vague. Limited technical vocabulary. | Subjective throughout. No technical vocabulary. |
| **Mechanics** | Accurate spelling, punctuation and grammar. No errors. | Minor errors. Good control. | Some errors. Meaning clear. | Errors sometimes affect clarity. | Significant errors affect clarity. |

## ADAPTATIONS

### EAL Students
- Provide bilingual glossary for key technical terms
- Allow home language notes during research phase
- Sentence starters for introduction and conclusion
- Visual timeline for report structure
- Teacher aide support during research sessions

### Gifted Students
- Add a comparison section (compare your animal to a similar species)
- Write a "day in the life" first-person imaginative section (framed as factual — e.g. "A day in the life of a saltwater crocodile involves...")
- Include a map showing global distribution
- Research and include a debate on a conservation issue related to the topic

### Students with NEPs
- Reduce section requirements (complete intro + 2 body paragraphs + conclusion)
- Provide pre-researched fact sheet with 5 facts
- Allow typed final copy instead of handwritten
- Extra time for research and writing phases
- Provide paragraph writing frames

## DIAGRAM SUCCESS CRITERIA

A strong informative diagram includes:
- [ ] Clear, accurate illustration
- [ ] 5-7 labelled parts/features
- [ ] Labels printed clearly (not handwritten)
- [ ] Caption that adds new information
- [ ] Source cited if copied/referenced
