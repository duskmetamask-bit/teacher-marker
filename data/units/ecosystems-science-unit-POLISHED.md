# Ecosystems and Living Things — Science Unit Plan

**Unit Duration:** 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons
**Year Level:** Years 3–4
**Curriculum Link:** Australian Curriculum Science v9 — Biological Sciences
**Mentor Text:** *The Lorax* by Dr. Seuss (1971)
**Unit Big Idea:** All living things need each other. Changes to an ecosystem affect all living things.
**Cold Task:** Week 1, Lesson 1 — Draw and label what you think an ecosystem looks like
**Hot Task:** Week 8, Lesson 1 — Describe an ecosystem and explain how living things depend on each other

## Unit Overview

**Learning Intention:**
We are learning to investigate how living things depend on each other and their environment to survive, and to describe how systems support healthy ecosystems.

**Success Criteria:**
- I can identify and describe the components of an ecosystem (living and non-living).
- I can explain the relationship between producers, consumers, and decomposers in a food chain.
- I can describe how living things adapt to their environment.
- I can explain the effects of natural and human changes on ecosystems.
- I can communicate scientific ideas using appropriate vocabulary and diagrams.

## WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: The Lorax and What is an Ecosystem?

### Lesson 1: Cold Task — Draw an Ecosystem
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Hook (5 min) | Show images of three ecosystems: a coral reef, an Australian bushland, and a pond. "What do you see? What do you think all of these have in common?" | Observe images, share observations |
| Task (35 min) | Cold Task: "Draw and label what you think an ecosystem looks like. Include at least 5 living things and 2 non-living things. Explain in writing why they belong together." Year 3: 5–8 labels + 2–3 sentences. Year 4: 8–10 labels + 4–5 sentences. | Independent drawing, labelling and writing |

**Differentiation:** Word bank of living/non-living things available. Sentence starters provided for writing: "I think this is an ecosystem because..." / "These things belong together because..." EAL students may label in home language first then add English.

**Success Criteria Check:** Student produces a labelled drawing showing some understanding of the living/non-living distinction.

### Lesson 2: Meet The Lorax
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Engage (Hook)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Read Aloud (20 min) | Read *The Lorax* aloud in full, showing illustrations. Stop at key moments: the Once-ler describing the Truffula trees, the Lorax emerging from the stump, the regulatory actions of the Lorax. | Listen, observe illustrations |
| Notice and Wonder (10 min) | "What do you notice?" (Record on chart paper — living things, environment, characters) / "What questions do you have?" (Record — why did the trees disappear? Who is responsible?) | Share observations and questions |
| Connect (10 min) | "The Lorax is a story about an ecosystem. Who are the living things? What has happened to their environment? Why?" | Contribute to class discussion |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can identify at least 3 living things and 1 change to the environment from The Lorax.

### Lesson 3: What is an Ecosystem?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Engage (Concept Introduction)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Build the Definition (10 min) | Write on board: "ECOSYSTEM = all living things + non-living things working together in an area." Use The Lorax to model: truffula trees (living) + air/water (non-living) + animals (living) = ecosystem. | Contribute examples |
| Vocabulary Building (10 min) | Introduce word wall: ecosystem, environment, habitat, community, population, species. Use images. | Add words to science notebooks |
| Apply the Concept (15 min) | Students revisit their Cold Task drawing. "Now that you know what an ecosystem is, add or change one thing in your drawing." Share with partner. | Revise drawing, discuss with partner |
| Exit Ticket (5 min) | "In one sentence, explain what an ecosystem is." | Written response |

**Differentiation:** Visual word wall displayed permanently in classroom. EAL students may draw their definition. Gifted students: research a specific ecosystem type in Australia (coral reef, desert, etc.) for Week 4 extension.

**Success Criteria Check:** Can define an ecosystem in own words using at least 2 vocabulary words.

## WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Living vs Non-Living

### Lesson 1: Living or Non-Living?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Quick Sort (10 min) | Place items on tables: feather, rock, leaf, plastic plant, real plant, toy car, shell, pinecone, dried pasta, photograph of a dog. "Living or non-living? How do you know?" | Physically sort items, justify decisions |
| Establish the Rules (10 min) | What do ALL living things have in common? Movement, breathing/respiration, growth, reproduction, response to environment. MRS GREN acronym introduced. | Copy MRS GREN into science notebooks |
| Sort and Justify (15 min) | In pairs, students sort the remaining items. They must explain WHY each item is living or non-living using MRS GREN. | Pair work sorting and justifying |
| Share (5 min) | Pairs share one surprising sort decision | Report back |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can identify at least 4 characteristics of living things and correctly classify 8+ items.

---

### Lesson 2: Mini Ecosystem Investigation
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Hands-On Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Set Up (10 min) | Distribute clear containers with pond water or leaf litter samples. "We are going to observe a REAL mini ecosystem. We are scientists." Explain observation rules: observe only, don't disturb. | Receive samples, observe |
| Observe (20 min) | Circulate and prompt: "What can you see? How many different living things can you find? What are they doing? What non-living things can you see?" Magnifying glasses and white paper provided. | Observe, draw, count, describe |
| Predict (5 min) | "What do you think will happen to your mini ecosystem if we leave it on the desk for a week? What might change?" | Make predictions in science notebooks |
| Record (5 min) | Students complete observation sheet: name, date, what living things found, what non-living things found, one interesting observation | Complete observation sheet |

**Safety Note:** Remind students not to touch water or leaf litter directly. Wash hands after lesson.

**Success Criteria Check:** Completes observation sheet with at least 3 living things identified and 2 non-living components noted.

---

### Lesson 3: Recording Observations — What Did We Find?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Data Recording and Reflection)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Compare Samples (10 min) | Students share what they found in their samples. Create a class tally: most common creatures found (mosquito larvae, water fleas, worms, snails, algae). | Contribute to class tally |
| Surprising Discoveries (10 min) | "What surprised you? Was there anything unexpected?" Discuss. Connect to ecosystem concept: tiny creatures have specific roles. | Share surprises |
| What We Know Now (10 min) | Revise Cold Task drawings: add labels for MRS GREN characteristics. What would you change? Why? | Revise drawings with new knowledge |
| Connect Forward (10 min) | "Next week we are going to learn about food chains. How do you think the tiny creatures in your sample fit into a food chain?" | Think and share predictions |

**Differentiation:** EAL students may use labelled diagrams instead of written descriptions. NEP students: partner supported observation recording.

**Success Criteria Check:** Can explain at least one way living things in the mini ecosystem depend on each other.

## WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: Food Chains

### Lesson 1: What Do Living Things Need to Survive?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Prior Knowledge Probe (10 min) | Brainstorm: "What do ALL living things need to survive?" Record on chart. Sun, water, food, air, shelter, space. Connect to pond water sample: "What were your creatures using for food? What do they breathe?" | Contribute to brainstorm |
| Mini-Lesson (15 min) | Every living thing needs ENERGY. Where does all energy start? THE SUN. Plants use sunlight to make food (photosynthesis). Animals eat plants or other animals. Energy flows from the sun → plants → animals. Draw on board. | Copy diagram into science notebooks |
| Application (10 min) | "Use The Lorax. Where does the Bar-ba-loot get its energy? Where does the Once-ler get his energy? Where does the Lorax fit?" | Identify energy sources in the story |
| Exit Ticket (5 min) | "Where does all energy in an ecosystem come from? How does it reach animals?" | Written response |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can identify the sun as the source of all energy and trace it through at least one living thing.

---

### Lesson 2: Producers, Consumers and Decomposers
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Introduce the Roles (10 min) | Write three categories on board: PRODUCERS (make their own food using sun — plants), CONSUMERS (eat other living things — animals), DECOMPOSERS (break down dead things — fungi, bacteria). Use The Lorax examples: truffula trees = producers, Bar-ba-loots = consumers, the Lorax speaks for the trees = protection. | Listen and categorise |
| Food Chain from The Lorax (15 min) | Build on board: Sun → Truffula trees (producer) → Bar-ba-loot (consumer) → [decomposer when trees die]. Connect: when the trees are cut down, the Bar-ba-loots have no food. This is what happens when one link breaks. | Contribute to food chain construction |
| Sort Activity (10 min) | Students receive cards with organisms. Sort into producers/consumers/decomposers. Cards: grass, cow, mushroom, sun, eagle, plankton, worm, apple tree, shark, mold. | Sort organism cards |
| Share (5 min) | Pairs share their sorted groups. Were there any disagreements? | Share and discuss |

**Success Criteria Check:** Correctly categorises at least 8/10 organisms and explains why each belongs in its category.

---

### Lesson 3: Food Chains vs Food Webs
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain + Elaborate

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Food Chain Model (10 min) | Draw a simple food chain: grass → kangaroo → dingo. Label producer/consumer. Add arrows showing energy flow. "Energy goes FROM the plant TO the animal. The arrow points the way energy travels." | Draw in notebooks |
| Food Web Introduction (10 min) | "But in real ecosystems, animals eat MANY things." Add to the diagram: grass also eaten by emu, dingo also eats lizard, emu eaten by dingo. This is a FOOD WEB — interconnected food chains. | Observe and add to diagrams |
| What Happens If One Link Breaks? (10 min) | "The Lorax showed us what happens when the truffula trees disappear. The Bar-ba-loots have no food. The Swomey Swomeys can't sing. The Humming-Fish can't hum." Model: what if the dingo disappeared from our Australian ecosystem? | Discuss chain reactions |
| You Do — Draw a Food Web (10 min) | Year 3: Draw a food chain with 4 organisms. Year 4: Draw a food web connecting 5+ organisms in an Australian ecosystem. | Independent food web drawing |

**Differentiation:** EAL students use pre-drawn template with word labels. Gifted students: create a food web for the Great Barrier Reef with at least 8 organisms. NEP students: draw a 3-organism food chain with labels.

**Success Criteria Check:** Draws a food chain with correct directional arrows and can explain what would happen if one organism was removed.

## WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Australian Ecosystems

### Lesson 1: Australian Ecosystems — Desert, Rainforest, Ocean, Bush
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Introduce the Four Ecosystems (10 min) | Show images of four Australian ecosystems: Great Victoria Desert, Daintree Rainforest, Great Barrier Reef, eucalypt bushland. "What is the same about all of these? What is different?" Record observations. | Observe and compare |
| Ecosystem Comparison (15 min) | Create a comparison chart on the board: DESERT — sparse water, extreme heat, animals: kangaroo, goanna, dune grass / RAINFOREST — high rainfall, dense canopy, animals: cassowary, tree frog, python / OCEAN — saltwater, coral, animals: coral trout, turtle, shark / BUSH — moderate rainfall, eucalypts, animals: koala, kookaburra, wattle bird. Students copy chart. | Copy comparison chart |
| Why These Animals Live Here (10 min) | "The koala lives in the bush because it eats eucalypt leaves. The cassowary lives in the rainforest because it eats rainforest fruits. Each animal is SUITED to its environment." Introduce the word ADAPTATION. | Listen and note |
| Apply: Find the Ecosystem (5 min) | Show 4 animal cards: dingo, clownfish, emu, green tree python. Students match to the correct ecosystem and explain WHY. | Match and justify |

**Success Criteria Check:** Correctly matches at least 3 Australian animals to their ecosystem and gives a reason.

### Lesson 2: How Do Living Things Adapt?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What is an Adaptation? (10 min) | "An adaptation is a特徴 (feature or behaviour) that helps a living thing survive in its environment." Physical adaptation: the camel has a hump to store water in a desert. Behavioural adaptation: the frog burrows underground in summer to escape heat. | Listen and give examples |
| Australian Adaptations (15 min) | Show examples: Desert: kangaroo rat — large ears to release heat / Fennec fox (not Aussie but great example) / Dingo — hunts at dawn/dusk (cooler). Eucalypt: koala — pouch (rearing young), strong sense of smell to find food / Rainforest: cassowary — bright colours for mating, strong legs to run. | Take notes with pictures |
| Compare Across Ecosystems (10 min) | "How is the way a desert kangaroo keeps cool different from the way a rainforest animal keeps cool?" Compare behavioural adaptations across two ecosystems. | Compare adaptations in pairs |
| Exit Ticket (5 min) | "Name one adaptation an Australian animal has. Explain why this adaptation helps it survive." | Written response |

**Success Criteria Check:** Names one adaptation and explains its survival purpose.

### Lesson 3: The Lorax Connection — What Happens When the Trees Are Cut Down?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate (Application)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Return to The Lorax (10 min) | "Last time we read The Lorax, the trees were cut down. What living things lost their homes? What happened to the ecosystem?" Re-read the final pages of The Lorax — the Once-ler's reflection. | Recall and discuss |
| Ecosystem Impact Map (15 min) | Build on board: Once-ler cuts down truffula trees → Bar-ba-loots lose food → Swomey Swomeys lose their singing trees → Humming-Fish lose clean water → Lorax speaks for the trees → Ecosystem collapses. "Every living thing depended on those trees." | Contribute to impact map |
| What Could Have Been Different? (10 min) | "What if the Once-ler had kept some trees? What if there was a rule to protect the forest? What could the Lorax have done differently?" Apply understanding: solutions for ecosystem protection. | Brainstorm solutions |
| Apply to Australia (5 min) | "In Australia, we have cut down huge areas of forest for farms and cities. What might have happened to animals like the koala when their forests were cleared?" | Connect to Australian context |

**Differentiation:** Gifted students: research a specific Australian ecosystem under threat (Great Barrier Reef, leadbeater's possum habitat). NEP students: complete a partial impact map with word bank support.

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 3 consequences of removing trees from the ecosystem in The Lorax.

## WEEK 5 — ELABORATE: Changes to Ecosystems

### Lesson 1: Natural Changes vs Human Changes
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate (Application)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Two Types of Change (10 min) | Natural changes: drought, flood, bushfire, volcanic eruption, season changes. Human changes: deforestation, pollution, building cities, farming, fishing. Sort examples on board. | Observe sorting |
| Impact Continuum (15 min) | "Some changes recover quickly. Some changes permanently damage an ecosystem. Let's sort these by how much impact they have and how long they last." Students receive scenario cards and sort onto a spectrum: Low Impact/Recoverable → High Impact/Long-term. | Sort and justify scenario cards |
| Case Study Preview (5 min) | "Next lesson we will look at a real Australian ecosystem that has changed. Think about what you've learned about ecosystems — what questions would you want to answer about a changing ecosystem?" | Generate questions for next lesson |
| Connect to The Lorax (10 min) | "What type of change was happening in The Lorax? Natural or human? What could have happened if it was a natural drought instead?" | Classify and discuss |

**Success Criteria Check:** Correctly classifies at least 4 changes as natural or human-caused and explains the difference.

### Lesson 2: Case Study — A Real Australian Ecosystem
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate (Case Study)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Present the Case — Great Barrier Reef (10 min) | Show images and brief facts: 2,300km long, 1,500+ species of fish, coral bleached due to rising ocean temperatures (human-caused climate change). Coral bleaching = coral expels algae, turns white, dies. The ecosystem is disrupted — fish lose habitat, predator food chains break. | Observe and take notes |
| Investigate in Pairs (15 min) | Students receive an information sheet about ONE changed ecosystem: (A) Great Barrier Reef coral bleaching, (B) Tasmania's old-growth forest logging, (C) Murray-Darling Basin water issues, (D) Perth's urban sprawl affecting banksia woodland. Answer: What changed? What living things were affected? What is being done? | Pair investigation |
| Jigsaw Share (10 min) | Each pair joins with another pair who studied a different ecosystem. Share findings. Create a class list of: what changed, who was affected, what is being done. | Share and compile |
| Reflection (5 min) | "What surprised you most about how ecosystems change?" | Think-pair-share |

**Success Criteria Check:** Describes one real ecosystem change with at least 2 affected species and one solution being attempted.

### Lesson 3: How Do Living Things Adapt When Their Environment Changes?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate (Application)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Three Adaptive Strategies (10 min) | Migration: animals move to a better environment (birds fly north in winter). Hibernation/Aestivation: animals sleep through harsh conditions (bush stone-curlew goes quiet in dry season). Camouflage and protection: animals blend in or hide (gecko changes colour). | Take notes on three strategies |
| Australian Examples (15 min) | Migration: grey nomad birds fly to Australia for summer, then north again / Hibernation: echidna goes into torpor in cold / Camouflage: thorny devil blends into desert sand / Seasonal colour change: Alpine pygmy possum changes fur colour with seasons | Record Australian examples |
| Design Challenge (10 min) | "An ecosystem is experiencing a 3-month drought. Design an animal that could survive this drought. Give it one behavioural adaptation and one physical adaptation. Draw it and label the adaptations." | Design activity |
| Share (5 min) | 3–4 students share their designed animals | Share designs |

**Differentiation:** EAL students draw and label adaptations in visual format. Gifted students: design an animal for an extreme environment (underwater cave, salt lake) with two physical and two behavioural adaptations. NEP students: use template with adaptation word bank.

**Success Criteria Check:** Designs an organism with one physical and one behavioural adaptation and explains how each helps it survive.

## WEEK 6 — ELABORATE + EVALUATE: Human Impact

### Lesson 1: The Lorax Revisited — Predict and Respond
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Revisit The Lorax Ending (10 min) | Read the final pages again. The Once-ler says: "I don't know what it was. But I can UNSPEAK." He hands the last truffula seed to the reader. "Unless someone like you cares a whole awful lot, nothing is going to get better. It's not." | Listen and reflect |
| Predict (10 min) | "What do you think happens next? Does the reader plant the seed? Does the ecosystem recover? What does the reader owe the environment?" | Write a prediction paragraph |
| What Would You Do? (15 min) | "Imagine you are the Lorax. You can speak for the trees, the animals, the fish. What would YOU do differently from the Once-ler? How would you protect the ecosystem?" Students write a response from the perspective of the Lorax. | Write from the Lorax's perspective |
| Share (5 min) | Select 2–3 students to share their Lorax responses | Listen and respond |

**Success Criteria Check:** Writes a prediction or perspective response that demonstrates understanding of ecosystem interdependence.

### Lesson 2: Design a Solution — How Could We Protect This Ecosystem?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate (Design Thinking)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Introduce the Design Process (10 min) | Problem → Research → Design → Present. Model with The Lorax ecosystem: PROBLEM = trees cut down, animals lose homes / RESEARCH = how many trees? Which animals? / DESIGN = replanting program, protected area, rules / PRESENT = proposal to the community. | Understand design process |
| Design a Proposal (25 min) | Students choose ONE ecosystem (from Week 5 case studies OR their own local ecosystem). They create a "Save Our Ecosystem" proposal: What is the problem? What will you do? Why will it work? How much will it cost? Who will help? | Design their proposal |
| Peer Feedback (5 min) | Pairs exchange proposals. One thing they did well + one question they have. | Give peer feedback |

**Differentiation:** EAL students may use drawings and labelled diagrams to accompany their proposal. NEP students: focus on the "What will you do?" section with scaffolded template.

**Success Criteria Check:** Creates a proposal that identifies one ecosystem problem and proposes one solution with justification.

### Lesson 3: Save Our Ecosystem — Student Presentations
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Evaluate (Presentation and Assessment)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Prepare to Present (5 min) | Students organise their materials. Each student presents for 2–3 minutes. Remind audience: listening politely, asking one question per presentation. | Prepare materials |
| Presentations (30 min) | Rotating presentations: half the class presents while half observes and writes one star (great thing) and one wish (question). Then switch. Teacher uses rubric to assess. | Present and observe |
| Debrief (5 min) | "What was the most interesting solution you heard from a classmate? Why?" | Share reflections |

**Differentiation:** EAL students may present to the teacher individually or in a small group if uncomfortable presenting to the whole class. NEP students may use visual poster instead of verbal presentation.

**Success Criteria Check:** Presents a "Save Our Ecosystem" proposal clearly explaining the problem and solution. Teacher uses Communication and Science as Human Endeavour criteria from rubric.

## WEEK 8 — HOT TASK

Include a food chain with at least 3 organisms and explain what would happen if one part of the food chain was removed." Year 3: drawing + 5–8 sentences. Year 4: detailed diagram + 8–12 sentences. | Independent completion |

### Lesson 3: Celebration — Acknowledging Our Learning Journey
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Celebration

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |

## DIFFERENTIATION NOTES

### EAL Learners
- **Visual Word Wall:** Permanently displayed in classroom. Key vocabulary: ecosystem, producer, consumer, decomposer, adaptation, food chain, habitat, environment, interdependence, survive. Updated each week.
- **Sentence Starters:** Provided for all written tasks. Example: "All living things need..." / "The sun is important because..." / "If [organism] was removed, then..." / "An adaptation is..."
- **Bilingual Glossary:** Key terms with home language translations where available (compiled with EAL teacher support).
- **Home Language Allowance:** Students may use home language for initial brainstorming, concept mapping and planning. English used for final published work.
- **Visual Scaffolding:** All diagrams pre-labelled with key terms. Students complete partially completed diagrams.
- **Partner Support:** EAL students paired with supportive partners during group work and investigations.

### Gifted Learners
- **Research Depth:** Research a specific Australian ecosystem in depth — include biotic and abiotic components, food web (not just chain), and human impact history.
- **Food Web Challenge:** Create a food web for a chosen Australian ecosystem connecting 8+ organisms with energy flow arrows.
- **Creative Extension:** Write a sequel to The Lorax — what happens when the seed is planted? Create a new ecosystem story with scientific accuracy.
- **Extreme Environment Design:** Design an ecosystem for an extreme environment (Mars colony, deep ocean, underground cave). Justify every organism's adaptations.
- **Case Study Investigation:** Research and present on a real Australian conservation program — what worked, what didn't, and why.

### Students with NEPs (National Education Program / Learning Support)
- **Length Adjustments:** Written work expectations reduced to 3–5 sentences for Year 3 and 5–7 sentences for Year 4 on written tasks.
- **Word Banks:** Provided for all written tasks. Key words highlighted in task instructions.
- **Oral Presentation Option:** Students may present verbally instead of in writing for any task. Teacher records response on observation sheet.
- **Extra Time:** Additional time allocated for all written and investigation tasks.
- **Scaffolded Templates:** Partially completed graphic organisers, food chain templates, and writing frames provided.
- **Seating and Resources:** Front-of-room seating, simplified task instructions, one-to-one check-ins during independent work.

## ASSESSMENT NOTES

### Formative Assessment (Ongoing Throughout Unit)
- Exit tickets (Lessons 1.3, 3.1, 4.2, 7.1)
- Observation during investigations (Weeks 2–3)
- Cold Task review (Lesson 2.3)
- Exit ticket comparison (Lesson 7.1)
- Self-reflection journals (Lessons 7.2, 7.3)

### Summative Assessment
- **Cold Task (Week 1, Lesson 1):** Pre-assessment drawing with labels. Establishes prior knowledge baseline. Used for growth comparison at end of unit.
- **Hot Task (Week 8, Lesson 1):** Post-assessment comparing ecosystem understanding before and after unit. Assessed against rubric.
- **Growth Analysis (Week 8, Lesson 2):** Students complete self-assessment comparing Cold Task to Hot Task. Teacher uses rubric to quantify growth using A–E scale.

### Data for Reporting
- Rubric marks for Hot Task across all four criteria (Science Understanding, Investigation Skills, Science as Human Endeavour, Communication)
- AC9S3U01 and AC9S4U01 codes assigned based on student performance
- Anecdotal records from investigation observations
- Self-reflection journal entries (Weeks 7–8)
