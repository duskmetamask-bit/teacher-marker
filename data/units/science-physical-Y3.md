# Heat and Light — Physical Sciences Unit Plan

**Unit Duration:** 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons
**Year Level:** Year 3
**Curriculum Link:** Australian Curriculum Science v9 — Physical Sciences
**Unit Big Idea:** Heat and light are forms of energy that can be produced, transferred, and detected. Understanding heat and light helps us design technologies that improve our lives.
**Cold Task:** Week 1, Lesson 1 — Design and draw an experiment to measure how light behaves when it hits different surfaces
**Hot Task:** Week 8, Lesson 1 — Design a solution using light or heat energy to solve a real-world problem

---

## Unit Overview

**Learning Intention:**
We are learning to investigate heat and light as forms of energy, describe how light travels and interacts with different materials, explain how heat transfers between objects, and apply this knowledge to solve real-world problems.

**Success Criteria:**
- I can explain that heat and light are forms of energy that can be detected and measured.
- I can describe how light travels in straight lines and reflects off surfaces.
- I can investigate how light is absorbed, transmitted, or reflected by different materials.
- I can explain how heat transfers by conduction, convection, and radiation.
- I can design investigations to test properties of light and heat.
- I can use evidence to explain how light and heat technologies work.

## WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: What Are Heat and Light?

### Lesson 1: Cold Task — Design a Light Experiment
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Hook (5 min) | Turn off classroom lights, shine a torch. "What is happening? What is this stuff coming from the torch?" (Light.) "What IS light? Can you draw it? Can you catch it?" | Observe and wonder |
| Task (35 min) | Cold Task: "Design an experiment to find out how light behaves when it hits different surfaces. Draw your experiment. Label: what you will do, what you will use, what you think will happen, what surfaces you will test. Year 3: 1 surface. Year 4: 3 surfaces with prediction for each." | Design experiment |
| Share (10 min) | 3–4 students share their experimental designs. What surfaces will they test? What do they predict will happen? | Share designs |

**Differentiation:** EAL students draw their experiment and point to labels. NEP students: draw one surface test with teacher support.

**Success Criteria Check:** Student designs an experiment to test light behaviour with surfaces, showing test variable and prediction.

### Lesson 2: Light and Heat Everywhere
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Engage (Concept Introduction)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Energy Introduction (10 min) | "Light and heat are both TYPES OF ENERGY. Energy is the ability to do work or cause change. Light energy lets us SEE. Heat energy lets us feel WARM and changes the temperature of things." | Learn about energy |

| Detect Light and Heat (15 min) | Students investigate in groups: (1) How do we DETECT light? (Eyes — we see it.) (2) How do we DETECT heat? (Skin — we feel it, thermometers — we measure it.) | Write (10 min) | "Light and heat are both forms of energy. Light energy travels from sources to our eyes. Heat energy travels from hot things to cold things." Write in science books with diagram. | Write definition |

**Success Criteria Check:** Correctly distinguishes light and heat as separate forms of energy and identifies how each is detected.

## WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Light and How It Behaves

### Lesson 1: Light Travels in Straight Lines
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Evidence for Straight-Line Travel (15 min) | Teacher demonstration: Three cardboards with holes, aligned → light passes through. Misalign one → light blocked. "Light travels in STRAIGHT LINES! This is why we can only see objects directly in front of us through a straight line." | Observe and reason |
| Ray Box Investigation (20 min) | Students use ray box or torch to test: Can light bend around corners? (No — shine torch into a corner, the area around the corner stays dark.) Can light go through curved tubes? (No — test with straight vs curved paper tubes.) Record observations. | Test light travel |
| Practical Application (10 min) | "Why do we use periscopes in submarines? (Two mirrors at 45° angles — light reflects twice, letting you see over surfaces.) Periscopes work because light travels in straight lines and reflects predictably." | Learn periscope |
| Draw (5 min) | Draw: LIGHT SOURCE → three aligned cards with holes → light passes through. Label: LIGHT TRAVELS IN STRAIGHT LINES. | Draw diagram |

**Success Criteria Check:** Provides evidence that light travels in straight lines and correctly explains why aligned holes are necessary for light to pass through.

### Lesson 2: Reflection Investigation
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What is Reflection? (10 min) | "When light HITS a surface, it BOUNCES BACK. This is REFLECTION. Light reflects off mirrors, water, glass, metal." Demonstrate with torch and mirror: angle in = angle out. | Learn reflection |
| Law of Reflection Investigation (25 min) | Students use mirrors and ray boxes (or torches): Measure angle of incidence (light going IN) vs angle of reflection (light coming OUT). Test 5 different angles. Record in table. Draw diagram showing: INCIDENT RAY, REFLECTED RAY, NORMAL LINE, ANGLE OF INCIDENCE, ANGLE OF REFLECTION. | Measure and record angles |
| Reflection Patterns (10 min) | "What pattern did you notice? The angle IN equals the angle OUT! This is the LAW OF REFLECTION. Scientists discovered this over 2,000 years ago!" | Identify law of reflection |
| Exit Ticket (5 min) | "If light hits a mirror at 30°, at what angle does it reflect?" | Written response |

**Success Criteria Check:** Measures angles of incidence and reflection, identifies that they are equal, and states the law of reflection.

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|

CONCAVE mirrors (curved inward) make things look bigger — used in makeup mirrors and flashlights. CONVEX mirrors (curved outward) make things look smaller but show a wider area — used in car side mirrors and security." | Learn mirror types |

**Success Criteria Check:** Builds a working periscope using the law of reflection and successfully views objects over/around obstacles.

## WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: Light and Materials

### Lesson 1: Transparent, Translucent, Opaque
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Three Categories (10 min) | "When light hits a material, three things can happen: ABSORBED (light is taken in and turned to heat — dark materials), TRANSMITTED (light passes through — transparent materials), REFLECTED (light bounces off — mirrors)." | Learn three categories |
| Transparent, Translucent, Opaque (15 min) | Define: TRANSPARENT = light passes through clearly (clear glass, water, air). TRANSLUCENT = some light passes through, but you can't see clearly (frosted glass, tissue paper). OPAQUE = no light passes through (wood, metal, your body). | Learn definitions |
| Material Testing Investigation (15 min) | Test materials: plain glass, coloured glass, wax paper, cardboard, aluminium foil, plastic bag, water, oil, cotton cloth, wooden block. For each: hold up to light — is the light coming through clearly, partly, or not at all? Classify and record. | Test materials |
| Sort and Explain (5 min) | Sort all tested materials into three groups. Write the rule for each group. | Sort and write rules |

**Success Criteria Check:** Tests at least 8 materials, correctly classifies each as transparent, translucent, or opaque, and writes a rule for each category.

### Lesson 2: Shadows and Light
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| How Shadows Form (10 min) | "SHADOWS form when OPAQUE objects block light. Since light travels in straight lines, an opaque object casts a dark shadow on the opposite side from the light source. The shadow is the ABSENCE of light." Demonstrate with torch and objects. | Learn shadow formation |
| Shadow Investigation (20 min) | Students investigate: (1) Distance: move object closer to light — shadow gets BIGGER. Move object farther — shadow gets SMALLER. (2) Angle: move light source higher — shadow gets SHORTER. Move light source lower — shadow gets TALLER. (3) Object shape: different shapes cast different shadow shapes. Record all findings. | Conduct shadow investigation |
| Pattern Discovery (10 min) | "What patterns did you notice? As distance increases, shadow size increases. As angle of light increases, shadow height decreases." | Identify patterns |
| Real-World Application (10 min) | "Why are shadows longer in the morning and evening? (Sun is low — long shadows.) Why are they short at noon? (Sun is directly overhead.) Why do sundials work? (Shadow position tells us time because Earth rotates and the sun's apparent position changes.)" | Apply to real world |

**Success Criteria Check:** Investigates shadow formation, identifies the relationship between distance/angle and shadow size, and applies this to explain real shadow phenomena.

### Lesson 3: Colour and Light
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| White Light Contains All Colours (10 min) | "WHITE light (from the sun or a light bulb) is actually made of ALL COLOURS mixed together. When white light hits a RED apple, the apple REFLECTS red light and ABSORBS all other colours. A BLACK object absorbs ALL colours. A WHITE object REFLECTS ALL colours." | Learn colour absorption |
| Colour Wheel Demonstration (15 min) | Use a colour wheel spinner or show a colour wheel. "The primary colours of light are RED, GREEN, and BLUE. When all three are mixed together, you get WHITE. This is ADDITIVE colour mixing — used in TV screens and phones." | Learn additive colour |
| Filter Investigation (15 min) | Students test coloured filters (cellophane) over white light: Red filter — only red light passes through. Blue filter — only blue light passes through. Green filter — only green light passes through. Place two filters together: red + blue = magenta (mixed). Record findings. | Test colour filters |
| Real-World Colour (10 min) | "Why is the sky blue during the day? (Blue light from the sun scatters more than other colours, spreading across the sky.) Why are sunsets red/orange? (Blue light scattered away, red/orange travels directly to our eyes.)" | Learn sky colour |

**Differentiation:** EAL students draw colour filter experiments. Gifted students: research why the ocean is blue (similar to sky — water absorbs red light, reflects blue).

**Success Criteria Check:** Explains why objects appear coloured and tests coloured filters showing which colours pass through.

## WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Heat Energy

### Lesson 1: Heat as Energy
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Heat vs Temperature (15 min) | "HEAT is a FORM OF ENERGY — the total energy of all the tiny particles moving in a substance. TEMPERATURE is a MEASUREMENT — how hot or cold something is. A cup of hot tea and a swimming pool might be the same temperature, but the pool has MUCH more heat energy because there are more particles." | Learn heat vs temperature |
| Particle Model (15 min) | Draw: COLD WATER — particles move slowly, close together / HOT WATER — particles move quickly, spread apart. "When things heat up, particles move faster. When things cool down, particles slow down. Heat is the transfer of energy from fast-moving particles to slower ones." | Learn particle model |
| Evidence of Heat Transfer (15 min) | Show: ice cube in warm water melts (heat transfers from water to ice). Metal spoon in hot soup gets hot (heat transfers from soup to spoon through conduction). Sun warms the Earth (radiation). Three types of heat transfer. | Learn three types |
| Write (5 min) | "Heat is a form of energy that transfers from hot things to cold things. Temperature measures how hot something is, but heat is the total energy of all moving particles." | Write definition |

**Success Criteria Check:** Distinguishes between heat (energy) and temperature (measurement) and explains that heat transfers from hot to cold.

---

### Lesson 2: Conduction
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| How Conduction Works (15 min) | "CONDUCTION: heat transfers through a SOLID when particles bump into each other, passing on their energy. Fast-moving hot particles bump slow-moving cold particles, speeding them up. Eventually the whole object becomes the same temperature." Demonstrate with metal rod and pegs with wax. | Learn conduction |
| Conduction Investigation (20 min) | Teacher demonstration: metal rod with thumbtacks attached with wax. Heat one end. Thumbtacks fall in order as heat conducts along the rod. "Which end fell first? (Closest to heat.) The heat CONDUCTED along the rod." Students draw and label the process. | Observe conduction |
| Conductors vs Insulators (10 min) | "Metals are GOOD CONDUCTORS — heat passes through easily. Non-metals (wood, plastic, air, wool) are POOR CONDUCTORS / INSULATORS — they trap heat." | Learn conductors vs insulators |
| Application (5 min) | "Why are saucepans made of metal? (Conduct heat to food.) Why are saucepan handles made of plastic/wood? (Insulate hands from heat.) Why do we wear wool in winter? (Traps air close to body to keep us warm.)" | Apply understanding |

**Success Criteria Check:** Explains conduction as particle-to-particle energy transfer and correctly classifies at least 5 materials as conductors or insulators.

---

### Lesson 3: Convection and Radiation
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Convection (15 min) | "CONVECTION: heat transfers through LIQUIDS and GASES when warm fluid rises and cool fluid sinks, creating a CIRCULATION current." Demonstrate: food colouring in warm water — coloured water rises, cool water sinks. "This is how heating works in your home — hot air rises, cool air comes in to replace it. This is a CONVECTION CURRENT." | Learn convection |
| Convection Investigation (15 min) | Students observe convection: Place ice at top of a beaker of water, heat source at bottom. Add food colouring. Watch: cool coloured water sinks, warm water rises. Draw the convection current. | Observe convection |
| Radiation (15 min) | "RADIATION: heat transfers through EMPTY SPACE as INFRARED LIGHT waves. This is how the SUN heats the Earth — there is no air in space to conduct or convect, so heat travels as radiation. All hot objects emit infrared radiation. Darker objects absorb MORE radiation than lighter objects." Demonstrate: paper — white side vs black side. Heat lamp shines on both. Black side gets hotter (absorbs more heat). | Learn radiation |
| Exit Ticket (5 min) | "How does the sun's heat reach Earth if there is no air in space?" | Written response |

**Differentiation:** EAL students draw all three heat transfer methods. Gifted students: research how a Thermos flask minimises all three types of heat transfer.

**Success Criteria Check:** Explains all three heat transfer methods (conduction, convection, radiation) and provides an example of each.

## WEEK 5 — ELABORATE: Heat in Our World

### Lesson 1: Insulators and Conductors in Real Life
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate (Application)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Testing Real Materials (20 min) | Students test common materials for conductivity: metal spoon, plastic spoon, wooden spoon, glass rod, rubber band. Method: dip one end in hot water (teacher setup), touch other end after 30 seconds. Record whether it feels hot (conducted heat) or stays cool (insulator). | Test real materials |
| Real-World Application (15 min) | "Home design for hot climates: Light-coloured roofs reflect heat (less radiation absorbed). Thick walls with air pockets insulate (reduce conduction). Shade cloth over windows blocks direct sunlight (reduce radiation). These choices reduce heat transfer into the house." | Learn passive cooling |
| Design a Cool Box (10 min) | "Design a box to keep an ice block frozen for 2 hours. You have: cardboard, aluminium foil, bubble wrap, newspaper, cotton wool, plastic bag. Which materials will you use INSIDE the box? Which on the OUTSIDE? Why?" Draw and label design. | Design insulation |
| Share (5 min) | 3–4 students share designs | Share |

**Success Criteria Check:** Tests at least 5 real materials, correctly classifies as conductors or insulators, and designs an insulated cool box with justification.

### Lesson 2: Heat and Temperature
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate (Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Why Temperature Feels Different (10 min) | "Why does a metal floor feel colder than a wooden floor at the same temperature?" (Both are same temperature, but metal conducts heat from your feet FASTER, so it feels colder. Wood is an insulator — heat transfers slowly, so your feet stay warm.) "This is why tile feels cold but carpet feels warm — same room temperature!" | Learn sensation vs temperature |
| Investigation — Rate of Heating and Cooling (25 min) | Students set up: beaker of water with thermometer, heat source. Measure temperature every 2 minutes for 10 minutes as water heats. Plot a graph: TIME vs TEMPERATURE. Compare: water vs oil heated for same time — which gets hotter faster? Record. | Measure heating rates |
| Graph Analysis (10 min) | Students draw graphs. "What does the graph show? Does temperature change at the same rate throughout? (No — it heats faster at the start, then slows as it approaches the heat source temperature.)" | Analyse graphs |
| Write Conclusion (5 min) | "Different materials heat and cool at different rates. Conductors heat up faster than insulators. This is called THERMAL MASS." | Write conclusion |

**Success Criteria Check:** Measures temperature changes over time, plots data on a graph, and draws conclusions about heating rates.

### Lesson 3: Managing Heat — Technologies
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate (Application)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Heat Management Technologies (15 min) | "Humans have invented technologies to MANAGE heat: THERMOSTATS — sense temperature and turn heating/cooling on/off (air conditioner, oven). INSULATED BEVERAGE BOTTLES — double walls with vacuum between to prevent conduction and convection. SOLAR PANELS — absorb light/heat energy and convert to electrical energy. REFRIGERATORS — use evaporation and condensation to remove heat from food." | Learn technologies |
| Investigate a Thermos/Insulated Bottle (15 min) | Disassemble a plastic thermos if possible (or show diagram). Identify: double wall, air gap (prevents conduction), reflective inner surface (prevents radiation). Pour hot water in, measure temperature over 30 minutes. Compare to open cup. Record. | Investigate thermos |
| Heat Technology Presentation (15 min) | Groups research one heat technology: refrigerator, air conditioner, electric blanket, solar water heater, thermos. Present: How does it work? What heat transfer methods does it use? | Research and present |
| Exit Ticket (5 min) | "Name one way to reduce heat transfer into your home on a hot day." | Written response |

**Differentiation:** EAL students draw the thermos diagram with labels. Gifted students: research phase-change materials (PCMs) — materials that absorb heat as they change from solid to liquid.

**Success Criteria Check:** Explains how at least one heat management technology works, identifying the heat transfer methods involved.

## WEEK 6 — ELABORATE: Light Technologies

### Lesson 1: How Do We See?
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| The Vision Process (15 min) | "You SEE when light enters your EYE: LIGHT SOURCE → light travels in straight lines → enters eye through CORNEA (protective window) → passes through PUPIL (black opening) → LENS focuses light → falls on RETINA at back of eye → RETINA sends signals via OPTIC NERVE to BRAIN → brain interprets the image." | Learn vision process |
| Eye Model Investigation (10 min) | Students create a simple eye model: magnifying glass (lens), white paper screen (retina). Use torch as light source. Move lens closer/farther to focus. Observe image on screen — what happens? (Image is upside down on retina — brain flips it.) | Create eye model |
| Eye Care (10 min) | "How do we protect our eyes? Sunglasses block UV light (radiation damage). Reading glasses help focus (aging lens). Rest eyes every 20 minutes (reduce eye strain). Regular eye tests check retina and lens health." | Learn eye care |
| Draw Labeled Eye (10 min) | Draw and label the eye: cornea, pupil, lens, retina, optic nerve. | Draw and label |
| Exit Ticket (5 min) | "Why do we say the retina receives an upside-down image?" | Written response |

**Success Criteria Check:** Explains the vision process and correctly sequences light entering the eye, being focused, and being sent to the brain.

### Lesson 2: Optical Technologies
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Magnifying Glass and Lenses (15 min) | "A MAGNIFYING GLASS is a CONVEX lens — it curves outward. It bends light INWARD, making objects look BIGGER. This is how microscopes and telescopes work — they use multiple lenses to magnify tiny or distant objects." | Learn lenses |

### Lesson 3: Light and Colour Technologies
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate (Application)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| LED vs Incandescent Bulbs (15 min) | "Old incandescent bulbs: electrical energy passes through a thin wire (filament), it gets SO HOT it glows — 90% of energy becomes HEAT, only 10% becomes light. LED bulbs: electrical energy passes through a special material that emits light DIRECTLY — very little heat, most energy becomes light. LEDs are 80% more efficient!" | Learn LED efficiency |
| RGB Colour Screens (15 min) | "Phone and TV screens use RGB (Red, Green, Blue) pixels. Each pixel has three tiny lights. By turning them on/off at different levels, the screen creates MILLIONS of colours. This is ADDITIVE colour mixing — colours are created by ADDING light together. When all three are full, you see WHITE." | Learn RGB |
| Investigate Screen Colours (10 min) | Use a magnifying glass to examine a phone/TV screen up close. What do you see? (Tiny red, green, blue squares.) What happens when you look at white text from very close? (You see the individual RGB pixels.) | Examine screens |
| Reflection on Technology (10 min) | "Why do we prefer LED lights? (More efficient, less heat, longer lasting, cheaper to run.) How has understanding light helped us design better technologies?" | Discuss |

**Differentiation:** EAL students draw LED vs incandescent comparison. Gifted students: research OLED screens and how they differ from LED/LCD screens.

**Success Criteria Check:** Explains the difference between incandescent and LED light production and explains how RGB screens create colours using additive mixing.

## WEEK 8 — HOT TASK

### Lesson 1: Hot Task — Design a Light or Heat Solution
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Summative Assessment

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Set Up (5 min) | "Today you design your own solution to a real-world problem using light or heat energy. Follow your plan and present your solution clearly." | Receive instructions |
| Design and Present (40 min) | Students complete their designs and prepare a brief explanation. Teacher circulates and assesses using rubric. | Independent design |
| Share (5 min) | 2–3 students share their solutions | Share |

**Assessment Note:** Use rubric to assess.

**Differentiation:** EAL students may verbally explain their design with teacher scribing. NEP students may use template and visual presentation.

### Lesson 2: Compare and Reflect
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Evaluate (Self-Assessment)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Gallery Walk (15 min) | All Hot Tasks displayed. Students walk and leave sticky notes: "I like how you applied..." | Walk gallery |
| Growth Reflection (20 min) | Write: "The most important thing I learned about heat/light is..." / "I designed my solution by applying..." / "One thing I want to investigate more is..." | Write reflection |
| Share (15 min) | Volunteers present their solutions | Share |

**Success Criteria Check:** Completes self-reflection showing understanding of heat and light energy.

## DIFFERENTIATION NOTES

### EAL Learners
- **Visual Word Wall:** Permanently displayed with picture-word pairs for all key vocabulary.
- **Home Language First:** Students explain investigations in home language first, then add English.
- **Partner Support:** EAL students paired with supportive partner during investigations.
- **Sentence Starters:** Provided for all written explanations: "Light reflects when..." / "Heat conducts through..." / "I predict that..."

### Gifted Learners
- **Research Extension:** Research fibre optic cables — how do they transmit light around corners using total internal reflection?
- **Advanced Design:** Design a solar oven that cooks food using only sunlight. Calculate the energy efficiency.
- **Wavelength Investigation:** Research the electromagnetic spectrum — beyond visible light, what other forms of light energy exist?

### Students with NEPs
- **Reduced Variables:** Focus on 2 variables instead of 3+.
- **Oral Presentation Option:** Student verbally presents their investigation — teacher records on observation sheet.
- **Extra Time:** Additional time for all tasks.
- **Scaffolded Templates:** Provide recording sheets with word banks and sentence starters.
- **Partner Support:** Work alongside a supportive partner during investigations.
