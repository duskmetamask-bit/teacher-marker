## BIG UNDERSTANDINGS

Students will understand that:
1. A fraction names equal parts — the parts must be the **same size**
2. The denominator names **how many equal parts** the whole is divided into; the numerator names **how many parts are selected**
3. The same fraction can look different (equivalent fractions) — they name the **same amount**
4. Fractions and decimals are two different ways of writing parts of a whole
5. Fractions exist on a number line — they have size and order
6. You can only add or subtract fractions that refer to the **same sized parts** (same denominator)

## ESSENTIAL QUESTIONS

- How do we share something so everyone gets a fair amount?
- How does the denominator change what each piece is worth?
- Can the same amount have different names?
- Where do fractions live on a number line?
- How are fractions and decimals connected?

## DIFFERENTIATION FRAMEWORK

### EAL/D Students
- Pre-teach key vocabulary with visual dictionary cards before each lesson
- Sentence frames for every open response: *"This fraction shows ___ because ___"*
- Bilingual anchor charts where possible
- Pair with a supportive English-speaking partner during We Do activities
- Use concrete materials (fraction tiles, pattern blocks) before moving to symbols
- Accept oral explanation in place of written where appropriate
- Allow additional processing time (minimum 20 seconds wait time after questions)
- Reduce written output requirement — focus on mathematical demonstration

### Students Identified as Gifted/Advanced
- Extend to comparing fractions with unlike denominators
- Investigate improper fractions and mixed numerals beyond the unit scope
- Challenge: If 1/3 = 2/6 = 3/9, what is the pattern? Can you write a rule?
- Introduce percentage as a third way of naming parts of a whole
- Problem-posing: students create their own *Doorbell Rang* style problems with different numbers
- Extend decimals to thousandths; connect to measurement (km, kg)
- Open investigations: Which fractions can be written as terminating decimals? Why?

### Students Not Yet at Expected Level (NEP)
- Consolidate halves and quarters before introducing thirds, fifths, sixths
- Use physical cookie-cutting (paper circles) to build the equal-parts concept
- Fraction wall always available as a reference tool
- Focus on Part A and Part B of each lesson — not expected to reach extension tasks
- Modified success criteria with teacher: 3 "I can" statements rather than all 10
- Pre-lesson micro-session (5 minutes, pull-aside): preview the concrete model
- Reduce denominator range: halves, quarters, eighths only if needed
- Peer support pairing with a student who has mastered the concept

## LESSON 1.2 — Equal Sharing: What Makes a Fraction?

**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Type:** Concept Introduction (I Do → We Do)
**Resources:** Paper circles (pre-cut — "cookies"), scissors, fraction vocabulary cards, whiteboard

---

### Learning Intentions
- I am learning that a fraction names equal parts of a whole
- I am learning that the parts MUST be equal for it to be a fraction

### Success Criteria
- I can explain what "equal parts" means using my cookie
- I can fold a circle into halves and explain why both pieces are halves

---

### I DO (Teacher-Led Direct Instruction — 15 min)

Teacher Action:
> "Let's go back to *The Doorbell Rang*. At the start, there are 12 cookies and 2 people. How many does each person get?"

- Draw 12 dots on board. Circle groups of 6.
> "Each person gets 6 out of 12 cookies — HALF. But here's my question: is every 'half' actually a half?"

- Hold up two paper circles. Tear one unevenly.
> "Is this a half? Why not?"

- Key concept: *For something to be a fraction, the parts must be EQUAL in size.*

Teacher explicitly states (and writes on board):
**"A fraction names equal parts of a whole. The parts must be the same size."**

- Demonstrate folding a paper circle into exact halves. Open and hold up.
> "These are halves. Each piece is ONE out of TWO equal parts. We write that as 1/2."
> "The 2 — the bottom number — tells us how many equal parts the whole is cut into."
> "The 1 — the top number — tells us how many parts we're talking about."

Write on board:
```
    1   ← numerator (how many parts we have)
   ───
    2   ← denominator (how many equal parts the whole is cut into)
```

---

### WE DO (Guided Practice — 25 min)

Teacher Action:
> "Now you're going to be the cookie cutters. Take your paper circle — your cookie. I'll tell you how many people are sharing."

Round 1 — 2 people sharing:
- Students fold circles in half. Check: are both pieces the same?
> "Hold your half up. What fraction of the cookie do you have? Write it."
- Circulate. Prompt: "How do you know they're equal?"

Round 2 — 4 people sharing:
- Students fold in half, then half again (quarters).
> "How many equal pieces? What is each piece called? Write the fraction."

Round 3 — Challenge: 3 people sharing
> "This is trickier. Try to fold your cookie into THREE equal pieces."
- Students attempt. Discuss: it's harder — estimate is okay.
> "This is thirds. We write one third as 1/3."

Whole class discussion:
> "What changes each time we share between more people? What happens to the size of each piece?"

**Key takeaway (students complete in books):**
> "When we share between MORE people, each piece gets _________ (smaller). When we share between FEWER people, each piece gets _________ (bigger)."

---

### YOU DO (Independent Practice — 15 min)

Students work independently:
1. Draw a rectangle. Shade 1/3 of it. Label the numerator and denominator.
2. Draw a circle. Shade 3/4 of it. Write what the numerator means and what the denominator means.
3. "My friend says this shape shows halves:" *(Show two unequal pieces)* "Do you agree? Explain."
4. EXTENSION: If 12 cookies are shared equally between 6 people, what fraction of the cookies does each person get? Show two ways.

---

### Differentiation
- **EAL/D:** Vocabulary cards on desk (numerator, denominator, equal, whole). Sentence frame: "This is ___ because the parts are ___."
- **NEP:** Focus on halves only. Use physical folding only. Question 3 only.
- **Gifted:** Extension question + additional: Can you draw a shape that shows 5/8? Explain what the 8 tells you.

## LESSON 1.3 — Naming Fractions: Reading and Writing

**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Type:** Concept Introduction + Guided Practice
**Resources:** Fraction strips (pre-printed), whiteboard, individual whiteboards

---

### Learning Intentions
- I am learning to read and write fractions correctly
- I am learning the names for halves, thirds, quarters, fifths, sixths

### Success Criteria
- I can write any fraction using the correct numerator and denominator
- I can name fractions I see in diagrams

---

### I DO (15 min)

Teacher Action:
> "Yesterday we learned that the bottom number tells us how many equal parts. Today we're going to name ALL the unit fractions we'll use this term."

Build fraction vocabulary wall together:

| Denominator | Name | Fraction Symbol |
|-------------|------|-----------------|
| 2 | halves | 1/2 |
| 3 | thirds | 1/3 |
| 4 | quarters (fourths) | 1/4 |
| 5 | fifths | 1/5 |
| 6 | sixths | 1/6 |

> "A unit fraction is a fraction with a 1 on top. It's one part out of however many equal parts."

Model reading non-unit fractions:
> "3/4 — I read this as 'three quarters.' Three parts out of four equal parts."
> "5/6 — 'five sixths.' Five parts out of six equal parts."

Key rule: **"When the numerator equals the denominator, we have ONE whole. 4/4 = 1."**

---

### WE DO (25 min)

**Activity: Fraction Strip Exploration**
Distribute fraction strips (whole, halves, thirds, quarters, fifths, sixths).

Guided questions (teacher models first, then students explore):
1. Which strip shows 1/2? Hold it up. How many of those fit in the whole?
2. Which is bigger — 1/3 or 1/4? How can you tell using your strips?
3. How many sixths make a whole? Write the fraction: ___/6 = 1

Partner Task:
- Partner A points to a fraction strip piece; Partner B reads the fraction aloud
- Swap after 5 goes

Whiteboard Check:
Teacher calls out fractions; students write on individual whiteboards and hold up:
- "Show me three quarters." "Show me two thirds." "Show me five sixths."
- Teacher scans responses, notes misconceptions.

---

### YOU DO (15 min)

1. Write the fraction shown by the shaded part: *(4 diagrams — circles and rectangles divided into equal parts with sections shaded)*
2. Draw your own diagram to show: a) 2/3   b) 5/6   c) 3/5
3. "I ate 3/8 of a pizza. My friend ate 5/8. Together, did we eat the whole pizza? How do you know?"
4. EXTENSION: Write three different fractions that are greater than 1/2 but less than 1. Prove it using a diagram.

---

### Differentiation
- **EAL/D:** Fraction strip always available; write fraction name in English and home language if helpful
- **NEP:** Focus on 1/2, 1/4, 3/4 only. Questions 1 and 2a only.
- **Gifted:** Question 4 + investigate: what fraction of *The Doorbell Rang*'s cookie total does each character get at the end of the story?

### Lesson Close
> "Thumbs up/middle/down — how confident are you reading and writing fractions right now?"
> "Brilliant. Next week we're going in deeper — fractions of collections, and where fractions live on the number line."

## LESSON 2.1 — Area Model: Fractions of Shapes

**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Type:** Explore
**Resources:** Grid paper, coloured pencils, fraction tiles, *The Doorbell Rang* illustrations (projected)

---

### Learning Intentions
- I am learning to represent fractions as equal parts of an area (shapes)
- I am learning to identify whether a shape is correctly divided into equal parts

### Success Criteria
- I can shade the correct fraction of a shape
- I can explain whether a shape is correctly divided using the word "equal"

---

### ENGAGE (5 min)

Project an illustration from *The Doorbell Rang* showing cookies on a tray.
> "These cookies are arranged in rows and columns. If I want to give one quarter to each of four people, how might I cut this tray?"
- Take 2–3 responses. Do not confirm yet.

---

### I DO (15 min)

**Area model — rectangles:**

Draw a rectangle on board. Divide into 4 equal columns.
> "I've divided this rectangle into 4 equal parts. Each part is one quarter — 1/4."
- Shade 3 columns. "I've shaded 3/4. Three quarters."

Draw another rectangle. Divide unevenly into 4 parts.
> "Is this showing quarters? Why not?"
→ Key point: The parts must be EQUAL AREA, not just equal number.

**Area model — circles:**

Draw a circle. Divide into 3 equal parts (like a Mercedes logo).
> "Three equal parts — each is one third. 1/3."
> "If I shade two of them: 2/3 — two thirds."

Model: Divide circle into 6 parts. Ask students: "How many sixths make one half?"
Place hands on chin, count with students: "1/6, 2/6, 3/6 — STOP. Three sixths = one half."

---

### WE DO (25 min)

**Grid paper activity:**
Students draw rectangles (3×2 grids, 4×2 grids, 3×3 grids, etc.) and:
1. Divide into halves — shade one half (two different ways)
2. Divide a rectangle into thirds — shade 2/3
3. Divide a rectangle into sixths — shade 5/6
4. Partner check: "Is my partner's shape correctly divided into equal parts?"

**Discussion prompt:** "Can the same fraction look different and still be correct? How?"

**Class share:** 3–4 students share their rectangles divided into thirds — notice they all look different but are all correct.

---

### YOU DO (15 min)

1. Shade 3/4 of each shape: a) a rectangle   b) a circle   c) a cross shape *(drawn for students)*
2. "Tick the shapes that correctly show 1/3. Cross the ones that do not. Explain one cross."
   *(Show 4 diagrams — some with equal parts, some unequal)*
3. Draw TWO different ways to show 2/4 on a rectangle. Are they the same amount?
4. EXTENSION: Draw a shape of your choice. Divide it into eighths. Shade 5/8. Write how you know your parts are equal.

---

### Differentiation
- **EAL/D:** Pre-drawn grids provided. Sentence frame: "This shape shows ___ because ___."
- **NEP:** Pre-divided shapes given — students shade the correct number of parts only
- **Gifted:** Extension + investigate: can you divide a triangle into equal thirds? Show your thinking.

## LESSON 2.2 — Fractions of Shapes: Multiple Representations

**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Type:** Explore
**Resources:** Pattern blocks, geoboards, digital fraction tools (optional), books

---

### Learning Intentions
- I am learning that the same fraction can be shown in many different ways
- I am learning to use different materials to represent fractions

### Success Criteria
- I can show the same fraction using three different representations
- I can explain what stays the same and what changes across representations

---

### I DO (12 min)

> "Here is the fraction 3/4. Watch — I'm going to show it four completely different ways."

1. **Diagram:** Rectangle divided into 4, shade 3
2. **Number line:** Mark 0 and 1, divide into 4 equal jumps, place dot at 3rd mark
3. **Collection:** 4 counters, circle 3
4. **Words:** "Three quarters — three out of four equal parts"

> "Same fraction. Four completely different looks. This is what mathematicians call multiple representations."

Key question: "What stays the SAME across all four? What CHANGES?"
→ Same: the amount (three out of four equal parts)
→ Different: what the whole looks like; how we show the equal parts

---

### WE DO (25 min)

**Rotation exploration (5 min per station, 5 stations):**

*Station 1 — Pattern Blocks:*
If the yellow hexagon = 1 whole, what fraction is the red trapezoid? The blue rhombus? The green triangle?

*Station 2 — Number Lines:*
Mark halves, thirds, and quarters on separate number lines (0 to 1). Students place given fractions.

*Station 3 — Drawing:*
Represent 3/5 three different ways (diagram, collection, number line).

*Station 4 — Word Problems:*
"Sam ate 2/6 of a pizza. Draw this. Write this as a fraction. Show it on a number line."

*Station 5 — Sorting:*
Sort fraction cards into groups — "same amount, different look" (e.g. 1/2, 2/4, 3/6 all together).

---

### YOU DO (15 min)

1. Show the fraction 2/3 in three different ways. Label each representation.
2. "Which representation do you find easiest? Why?"
3. "My friend drew a number line and put 1/4 closer to 1 than to 0. Are they correct? Explain."
4. EXTENSION: Use pattern blocks to find and write 5 different fractions using the hexagon as the whole.

---

### Differentiation

**Type:** Guided Practice

### ENGAGE — Mentor Text Hook (5 min)

## LESSON 4.2 — Finding Equivalent Fractions: The Pattern

**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Type:** Explain / Concept Development (Symbolic)
**Resources:** Whiteboards, fraction strips, multiplication charts

---

### Learning Intentions
- I am learning the pattern for generating equivalent fractions (multiply/divide numerator and denominator by the same number)
- I am learning to find equivalent fractions without always using diagrams

### Success Criteria
- I can explain the rule: "multiply the top and bottom by the same number"
- I can find equivalent fractions quickly using the rule

---

### I DO (15 min)

> "Last lesson we found equivalent fractions using our strips. Today I want to show you the PATTERN so you can find them without the strips."

Write: 1/2

> "I'm going to multiply the top AND the bottom by 2."
→ 1×2 / 2×2 = 2/4

> "Multiply by 3."
→ 1×3 / 2×3 = 3/6

> "The KEY is: whatever I do to the bottom, I MUST do to the top. If I multiply, divide, add the same number to both — I get equivalent fractions."

Show on board:
```
× 2         × 3         × 4
1/2 → 2/4   1/2 → 3/6   1/2 → 4/8
```

> "12/16 — divide top and bottom by 4: 3/4. The simplest form of 12/16 is 3/4."

- "Find two fractions equivalent to 1/3"
- "Find three fractions equivalent to 2/5"
- "Simplify 4/8"

- 2/5 = 6/___   (answer: 15)
- ___/12 = 1/4   (answer: 3)

"1/2 = ___/___" → swap and solve

## LESSON 5.1 — Adding Fractions: Same Denominator

**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Type:** Explain / Concept Development
**Resources:** Fraction tiles, fraction strips, books

### Learning Intentions
- I am learning to add fractions with the same denominator

### I DO (15 min)

*Problem D (Extension):* "Describe the entire cookie-sharing sequence from *The Doorbell Rang* in fractions. For each time the doorbell rings, write: total people, fraction per person, and how the fraction changed."

## LESSON 6.1 — What Is a Decimal? Introducing Tenths

**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Type:** Elaborate / Concept Introduction
**Resources:** Base-10 blocks, decimal grids (10-column), place value charts, books

---

### Learning Intentions
- I am learning what a decimal is and how it connects to fractions
- I am learning that tenths are fractions of 10 equal parts, written as decimals

### Success Criteria
- I can explain that 1/10 = 0.1
- I can read and write decimal tenths

---

### I DO (15 min)

> "We've spent five weeks writing fractions as parts of a whole — like 1/2 or 3/4. Today I'm going to show you another way to write parts of a whole — called DECIMALS."

Show a grid divided into 10 equal columns.

> "This is 1 whole — split into 10 equal parts. Each part is called ONE TENTH. We can write it as the fraction 1/10."

> "But there's another way to write it: 0.1. Zero point one."

Write on board:
```
1/10  =  0.1
2/10  =  0.2
5/10  =  0.5
10/10 =  1.0  (= 1 whole!)
```

> "The decimal point separates the WHOLE part (left) from the PARTS (right). The first place after the decimal point is TENTHS."

Place value chart:
| Hundreds | Tens | Ones | . | Tenths |
|----------|------|------|---|--------|
| | | 0 | . | 1 |
| | | 0 | . | 5 |
| | | 1 | . | 3 |

> "1.3 means 1 whole and 3 tenths — or 1 and 3/10."

---

### WE DO (25 min)

**Grid colouring activity:**
Students have grids divided into 10 equal columns.
- Colour 3 columns → "What fraction? What decimal?"
- Colour 7 columns → "What fraction? What decimal?"
- Colour 10 columns → "What fraction? What decimal? What whole number?"

**Place value chart practice (whiteboards):**
Teacher calls: "Show me 0.4 in your place value chart." Students write. Teacher scans.
Series: 0.6, 0.9, 1.2, 2.5, 0.1

**Matching activity (pairs):**
Cards with fractions (3/10, 7/10, 1/10) matched to decimal cards (0.3, 0.7, 0.1) matched to grid diagrams.

---

### YOU DO (15 min)

1. Write as a decimal: a) 4/10   b) 9/10   c) 1/10   d) 10/10
2. Write as a fraction: a) 0.6   b) 0.2   c) 0.8   d) 1.0
3. Shade the grid to show 0.7. Write the matching fraction.
4. "Is 0.5 the same as 1/2? Show how you know."
5. EXTENSION: "My friend says 0.3 is the same as 3. Do you agree? Explain."

---

### Differentiation
- **EAL/D:** Fraction-decimal anchor chart always visible; sentence frames for comparison
- **NEP:** Focus on 1/10, 5/10, 10/10 only; physical grid colouring only
- **Gifted:** Extension + investigate: which common fractions can be written as simple decimal tenths? (1/2, 1/4, 1/5...)

## LESSON 6.2 — Tenths on a Number Line and Place Value

**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Type:** Elaborate / Deepening
**Resources:** Pre-printed number lines (0–1 and 0–2), place value charts, MAB/base-10 blocks

---

### Learning Intentions
- I am learning to place decimal tenths on a number line
- I am learning to read decimal tenths using place value language

### Success Criteria
- I can locate 0.1–0.9 on a number line
- I can read 1.4 as "one and four tenths"

---

### I DO (15 min)

Draw number line 0 to 1. Divide into 10 equal parts.

> "I've divided 0 to 1 into 10 equal parts. Each mark is one tenth — 0.1."

Label: 0, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3... 1.0

> "Where is 0.5? Right in the middle — exactly the same as 1/2. We already knew that!"

Extend to 0–2: place 1.3
> "1.3 — past the 1, then 3 more tenths. One whole and three tenths."

Use MAB blocks: 
- Long rod = 1 whole
- 1 small cube = 1/10 of the rod = 0.1

Build 1.4: 1 rod + 4 cubes = 1.4

---

### WE DO (25 min)

**Number line placement (whole class → partners):**
Series of numbers called out — students place on personal number lines:
0.3, 0.7, 1.2, 0.5, 1.8, 0.9

**MAB building:**
Partners build numbers, then read to each other:
- Build 2.3 — read aloud — write in words
- Build 1.7 — read aloud — write fraction + decimal

**Ordering decimals:**
Sort set of decimal cards from smallest to largest: 0.4, 1.2, 0.9, 0.1, 1.0, 0.6
Justify: "How did you know 0.9 > 0.6?"

---

### YOU DO (15 min)

1. Place on a number line: 0.2, 0.8, 1.5, 0.5
2. Order from smallest to largest: 1.3, 0.7, 1.0, 0.4, 1.9
3. "My friend placed 0.9 closer to 0 than to 1. Are they correct? Explain."
4. Write in words: a) 0.3   b) 1.6   c) 2.9
5. EXTENSION: "What decimals come between 0.5 and 1.0? List them all (tenths). How many are there?"

## LESSON 6.3 — Converting: Fractions ↔ Decimals (Tenths)

**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Type:** Guided Practice
**Resources:** Fraction-decimal reference charts, decimal grids, whiteboards

---

### Learning Intentions
- I am learning to convert between fractions and decimals (tenths)
- I am learning to recognise common fraction-decimal equivalences

### Success Criteria
- I can convert any fraction with denominator 10 to a decimal
- I can convert decimals (tenths) to fractions

---

### I DO (12 min)

> "By now you've seen that 1/10 = 0.1. Converting between them is straightforward when the denominator is 10."

Build the conversion table together:

| Fraction | Decimal | Words |
|---------|---------|-------|
| 1/10 | 0.1 | one tenth |
| 3/10 | 0.3 | three tenths |
| 7/10 | 0.7 | seven tenths |
| 10/10 | 1.0 | one whole |

> "But what about 1/2? The denominator is 2, not 10. Can I still convert it to a decimal?"

> "I need to make an equivalent fraction with denominator 10: 1/2 = 5/10 = 0.5"

| Common Fraction | Equivalent Tenths | Decimal |
|----------------|------------------|---------|
| 1/2 | 5/10 | 0.5 |
| 1/5 | 2/10 | 0.2 |
| 2/5 | 4/10 | 0.4 |
| 1/10 | 1/10 | 0.1 |

> "Some fractions can't easily be written as tenths — like 1/3. That's okay — we'll learn more in secondary school!"

---

### WE DO (25 min)

**Conversion practice — whiteboard rounds:**
Teacher calls a fraction → students convert and hold up decimal, and vice versa.

**Match-up game (pairs):**
Set of 20 cards — fractions and decimals to match. Includes diagrams.

**Mini investigation:**
"Which fractions with denominator 2, 4, 5, 10 can be written as decimal tenths? Which can't? Why?"

---

### YOU DO (15 min)

1. Convert to decimal: a) 6/10   b) 1/2   c) 3/5   d) 9/10
2. Convert to fraction: a) 0.4   b) 0.9   c) 0.5   d) 0.2
3. "Order from smallest to largest: 1/2, 0.3, 3/5, 0.8"
4. "I say 3/5 > 0.5. Is this true? Show your reasoning."
5. EXTENSION: "Can 1/4 be written as a decimal tenth? A decimal hundredth? Investigate."

## LESSON 7.1 — Hundredths: The Next Column

**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Type:** Elaborate / Concept Development
**Resources:** Hundredths grids (10×10 squares), place value charts, base-10 blocks

---

### Learning Intentions
- I am learning that hundredths are ten times smaller than tenths
- I am learning to read and write hundredths as fractions and decimals

### Success Criteria
- I can explain that 1/100 = 0.01
- I can read decimal numbers with tenths and hundredths (e.g. 0.34 = 34/100)

---

### I DO (15 min)

Show a 10×10 grid (100 squares).

> "You know a tenths grid has 10 columns. This grid has 100 squares. Each tiny square is one HUNDREDTH — 1/100 — or 0.01."

> "One column of 10 squares = 10/100 = 1/10 = 0.1. A column is still a tenth!"

Build place value chart with hundredths column:
| Ones | . | Tenths | Hundredths |
|------|---|--------|-----------|
| 0 | . | 3 | 4 |

> "0.34 — zero ones, three tenths, four hundredths. Or: 34 hundredths."

Write: 0.34 = 34/100

Model more:
- 0.07 = 7/100 (7 hundredths — zero tenths, 7 hundredths)
- 0.50 = 50/100 = 5/10 = 1/2
- 1.25 = 1 whole, 2 tenths, 5 hundredths

Key connection: 0.50 = 0.5 — the trailing zero doesn't change the value.

---

### WE DO (25 min)

**Hundredths grid colouring:**
1. Colour 34 squares → write as fraction and decimal
2. Colour 75 squares → write as fraction and decimal
3. Colour 50 squares → write as fraction and decimal — "Is there another name for this?"
4. What fraction is NOT coloured in question 1?

**Place value chart practice:**
Build numbers using place value chart: 0.45, 1.23, 0.09, 2.50

**Decimal dictation (whiteboard):**
Teacher reads: "Three tenths and seven hundredths." Students write: 0.37

---

### YOU DO (15 min)

1. Write as a decimal: a) 45/100   b) 7/100   c) 3/10 and 9/100   d) 60/100
2. Write as a fraction: a) 0.23   b) 0.08   c) 0.90   d) 1.45
3. "My friend says 0.50 is bigger than 0.5. Are they correct?"
4. "Write 1/4 as a decimal. Show your working."
5. EXTENSION: "Order: 0.4, 0.04, 0.40, 0.44, 0.14. Justify your order."

---

### Differentiation
- **EAL/D:** Always use the grid; shade then label; sentence frame: "This shows ___ hundredths which is ___."
- **NEP:** Focus on tenths only; hundredths grid used for counting only
- **Gifted:** Extension + investigate money as decimals: $1.35 = how many hundredths of a dollar?

## LESSON 7.2 — Ordering and Comparing Decimals

**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Type:** Elaborate / Guided Practice
**Resources:** Decimal number lines, card sets, whiteboards

---

### Learning Intentions
- I am learning to compare and order decimals using place value
- I am learning a strategy: compare ones first, then tenths, then hundredths

### Success Criteria
- I can order a set of decimals from smallest to largest
- I can explain my ordering strategy using place value language

---

### I DO (15 min)

> "Common mistake: students think 0.9 > 1.2 because 9 > 1. But 0.9 is less than ONE whole, and 1.2 is more than one whole."

> "Strategy: COMPARE PLACE BY PLACE."

1. Compare the ONES digit first → biggest whole number part wins
2. If tied → compare TENTHS
3. If tied → compare HUNDREDTHS

Model:
Compare 1.3 and 1.7: same ones (1) → compare tenths (3 vs 7) → 1.3 < 1.7
Compare 0.45 and 0.5: same ones, compare tenths (4 vs 5) → 0.45 < 0.5
 → "Even though 45 looks bigger than 5, the TENTHS matter: 5 tenths > 4 tenths"

> "A handy trick: write 0.5 as 0.50. Now compare 0.45 and 0.50 — easy!"

---

### WE DO (25 min)

**Number line placement race:**
Teams of 4. Each person receives a decimal card. First team to correctly order themselves on a number line wins.

Rounds:
- Round 1: 0.2, 0.8, 0.5, 0.1
- Round 2: 1.3, 0.9, 1.1, 1.7
- Round 3: 0.45, 0.4, 0.54, 0.5
- Round 4: 0.09, 0.9, 0.19, 0.90

**Whiteboard comparison:**
Teacher shows two decimals — students hold up >, <, or =.

---

### YOU DO (15 min)

1. Use >, <, or = to compare: a) 0.7 ○ 0.3   b) 1.2 ○ 0.9   c) 0.45 ○ 0.5   d) 0.60 ○ 0.6
2. Order from smallest to largest: 0.3, 0.03, 1.3, 0.31, 0.13
3. "Between 0.4 and 0.5, name three decimals (hundredths)."
4. "My friend ordered 0.9, 0.19, 0.91, 0.09 as: 0.9, 0.91, 0.19, 0.09. Find and correct the error."
5. EXTENSION: "Write 5 decimals between 0.1 and 0.2. How many possible decimals are there? Can you list them all?"
