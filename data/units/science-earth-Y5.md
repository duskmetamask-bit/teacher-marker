# Science Unit: Earth's Cycles — Water Cycle, Rock Cycle and Carbon Cycle

**Unit Duration:** 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons
**Year Level:** Year 5
**Curriculum Link:** Australian Curriculum Science v9 - Earth and Space Sciences
**AC9S5U01** — Explain conservation of matter by describing physical changes of substances in changing conditions

**Mentor Text/Context:** Environmental science, physical sciences, ecological balance
**Writing Focus:** Scientific explanations, cause and effect, cycle diagrams, evidence-based reasoning

## Unit Overview

**Learning Intention:**
We are learning about Earth's major cycles — the water cycle, rock cycle, and carbon cycle — and how matter is conserved and transformed through these natural processes.

**Success Criteria:**
- I can explain the water cycle and describe each stage: evaporation, condensation, precipitation, collection.
- I can describe the rock cycle and explain how rocks change between igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic forms.
- I can explain the carbon cycle and understand why carbon cycling is important for life on Earth.
- I can use evidence to explain how matter is conserved in these cycles — it changes form but doesn't disappear.
- I can create accurate diagrams and written explanations of these cycles.

## WEEK 1 — INTRODUCTION TO EARTH'S CYCLES

### Lesson 1: What Is a Cycle? (I Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Hook (10 min) | "A CYCLE is something that goes round and round — it keeps going and never really ends. In nature, matter cycles through different forms and places. Water evaporates, forms clouds, falls as rain, flows to the ocean, evaporates again. Rock is melted, cools, erodes, forms new rock, melts again. Carbon moves from the atmosphere to plants to animals to the ground, back to the atmosphere." | Understand cycles |
| I Do (15 min) | "Cycles are important because they show CONSERVATION OF MATTER — matter doesn't appear from nowhere or disappear to nothing. It changes form. Water doesn't disappear when it evaporates — it becomes invisible water vapour. Carbon in a tree doesn't vanish when the tree dies — it goes into the soil." | Learn conservation |
| You Do (25 min) | "Draw a simple cycle diagram of something you know: The day cycle (morning → midday → afternoon → evening → night → morning) OR the water cycle you already know OR the cycle of how you get ready for school. Circle the parts that repeat. Label the type of cycle." | Draw |
| Share (5 min) | Show your cycle diagram. What makes it a cycle rather than a line? | Share |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can explain what a cycle is and why cycles demonstrate conservation of matter.

### Lesson 2: Conservation of Matter (We Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (15 min) | "Conservation of matter: In a closed system, matter cannot be created or destroyed — it can only change form. In nature, the Earth is essentially a closed system for matter (some small gains from meteors, small losses to space). So the total amount of matter on Earth stays the same — it just moves and changes." | Learn conservation |
| We Do (25 min) | "Demonstration: Put water in a sealed plastic bag. Weigh it. Observe as you place it in sunlight (evaporation). Weigh again (same mass — water is now water vapour inside the bag). Put in cool place (condensation). Weigh again (same). Pour out — mass decreases (now we open the system). But before we poured, mass was constant." Weigh, observe, record. | Experiment |
| You Do (10 min) | Write: "The water cycle conserves matter because..." / "If matter was not conserved, one possible consequence would be..." | Write |

## WEEK 2 — THE WATER CYCLE

### Lesson 1: Evaporation and Condensation (I Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (20 min) | "The WATER CYCLE: 1. EVAPORATION — Liquid water (from oceans, lakes, rivers, plants) is heated by the sun and changes to water VAPOUR (a gas — invisible). This rises into the atmosphere. 2. CONDENSATION — As water vapour rises, it cools. Cool air can't hold as much water vapour. The vapour condenses into tiny water droplets, forming CLOUDS. This is why clouds form — water changes from gas to liquid droplets." | Learn evaporation/condensation |
| You Do (30 min) | "Demonstration: Hot water + cold plate above it. Watch condensation form. Record observations. Also: Watch ice being placed over warm water — condensation forms on the cold surface. Draw and label what you observed." | Observe |
| Share (5 min) | Where have you seen evaporation happen in real life? Where have you seen condensation? | Share |

**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |

## WEEK 4 — THE ROCK CYCLE

### Lesson 1: The Three Rock Types (I Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (20 min) | "The three rock types: 1. IGNEOUS — Formed from molten rock (magma/lava) that cools and solidifies. If cooled slowly (underground) = coarse texture (granite). If cooled quickly (surface) = fine texture (basalt). 2. SEDIMENTARY — Formed from layers of sediment (sand, mud, shells) compressed over millions of years. Often has visible layers (sandstone, limestone). May contain fossils. 3. METAMORPHIC — Formed when existing rocks are changed by HEAT and PRESSURE (but not melted). Limestone → marble. Shale → slate. | Learn |
| You Do (30 min) | Examine rock samples: Granite (igneous), Basalt (igneous), Sandstone (sedimentary), Limestone (sedimentary), Marble (metamorphic), Slate (metamorphic). For each: Describe texture, appearance, possible origin. Write which type each is with reasoning. | Examine |
| Share (5 min) | How can you tell a sedimentary rock from an igneous rock? | Explain |

---

### Lesson 2: Changing Between Types (We Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | "The rock cycle shows how rocks change between types: Igneous rock (from cooling magma) → Weathering/Erosion (broken into sediment) → Sedimentary rock → Heat and Pressure → Metamorphic rock → Melting → Magma → Cool → Igneous rock again. The cycle can start at any point and go in any direction." | Learn cycle |
| We Do (30 min) | "Trace a rock through the cycle: You find a granite rock (igneous). It gets weathered (wind, rain, temperature changes break it into small pieces). These pieces are carried by rivers and deposited in layers. Over millions of years, they compress into sandstone (sedimentary). Heat and pressure from Earth's movements change it into quartzite (metamorphic). If it melts again, it becomes magma — and the cycle continues. Write this story." | Trace |
| You Do (10 min) | Write: "The rock cycle conserves matter because..." / "If Earth's interior cooled completely, the rock cycle would stop because..." | Write |

## WEEK 6 — CONNECTING THE CYCLES

### Lesson 1: How Cycles Interconnect (I Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (20 min) | "The cycles are CONNECTED. Water cycle + Carbon cycle: Plants absorb water (water cycle) AND carbon (carbon cycle) through photosynthesis. When plants transpire (release water), they also release oxygen. Water + Rock cycle: Water causes weathering of rocks (erosion part of rock cycle). Water carries sediment to form sedimentary rocks. Carbon + Rock cycle: Fossil fuels (carbon store) are formed from ancient organic matter (dead plants/animals) → compressed into coal/oil/gas. Ocean (carbon store) has calcium carbonate shells → limestone (sedimentary rock)." | Learn connections |
| You Do (30 min) | "Draw a concept map: In the centre, write 'Earth's Cycles'. Draw 3 circles: Water cycle, Rock cycle, Carbon cycle. Draw lines between them where they connect. At each connection, write HOW they connect. Colour code the connections." | Create map |
| Share (5 min) | Which cycle is most connected to the others? Why? | Discuss |

## ADAPTATIONS

### EAL Students
- Visual cycle diagrams with labels and arrows
- Word wall: evaporation, condensation, precipitation, erosion, deposition, metamorphic, photosynthesis, respiration
- Sentence starters for cycle descriptions: "First, ___. Then, ___. Finally, ___."
- Colour-coded diagrams (use same colours for same processes across all three cycles)
- Home language resources for key vocabulary if available

### Gifted Students
- Research the carbon cycle in relation to ocean acidification and coral bleaching
- Investigate deep time carbon cycles (how carbon moved between atmosphere and rocks over billions of years)
- Explore how the rock cycle relates to plate tectonics
- Research biochar or carbon capture technology as solutions

### Students with Additional Needs
- Focus on one cycle in depth (water cycle) before introducing others
- Provide pre-labelled diagram templates to fill in
- Reduce to key vocabulary: evaporation, condensation, rain, rock types
- One-on-one support for written explanations
- Extra time
- Allow oral presentation instead of written for some tasks
