---

## Unit Overview

**Learning Intention:**
We are learning to distinguish between formal and informal language registers, construct sophisticated paragraph structures with logical cohesion, use discourse markers appropriately, and apply precision in modality and hedging.

**Success Criteria:**
- I can write in formal and informal registers appropriate to audience and purpose.
- I can construct paragraphs with clear rhetorical structure (introduction, development, conclusion).
- I can use a wide range of discourse markers and conjunctions to signal logical relationships.
- I can use hedging and boosting language to convey certainty and uncertainty appropriately.
- I can use the passive voice to foreground information and maintain formality.
- I can edit for grammatical accuracy and register consistency.

### Lesson 2: Register Distinction (I Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Mini-Lesson (20 min) | Register = the level of formality in language. FORMAL: objective, impersonal, precise, full sentences, no contractions, no slang. IN FORMAL: subjective, personal, conversational, can use slang, contractions okay. Examples: "I believe that..." (formal) vs "I think..." (informal). "It was determined that..." (formal) vs "We decided..." (informal). Boyne writes simply but the implications are deeply serious. | Learn |
| Activity (25 min) | Distinguish registers in sentences: "The fence was constructed in 1940." (formal — passive, precise) vs "They built the fence in 1940." (informal — active, less precise). Do 10 examples. Identify features: passive, nominalisations, hedging, long sentences vs short, contracted vs full forms. | Practice |
| Share (10 min) | "When would you use each register?" Discuss audience and purpose. | Discussion |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can distinguish formal from informal language and explain why.

### Lesson 3: Choosing Register (We Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Guided Practice

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| We Do (35 min) | Transform informal sentences to formal: "They put the Jews in a camp." → "Jewish people were relocated to a detention facility." "Bruno didn't know what was happening." → "Bruno was unaware of the events taking place." "It was a bad thing." → "The situation was morally reprehensible." Each transformation shows register choice. | Guided transformation |
| You Do (20 min) | Write 4 sentences about the story in formal register. Write 4 sentences about the story in informal register. Label each. Explain the difference in effect. | Written practice |

**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Coherence = the LOGIC of ideas. Cohesion = the LINGUISTIC links between them. A paragraph must have both to work. Coherence checklist: Do ideas follow logically? | We Do (35 min) | Evaluate a weak paragraph: Ideas are present but not logically connected. Add cohesive devices and discourse markers. Reorganise for coherence. Discuss what changed. Write an improved version. Do 2 paragraph improvements together. | Guided improvement |
| You Do (10 min) | Write a coherent, cohesive paragraph about a key scene. Check both coherence and cohesion. | Written practice |

## WEEK 3 — DISCOURSE MARKERS

### Lesson 1: Adding and Linking (I Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Mini-Lesson (20 min) | Discourse markers = words and phrases that SIGNAL the logical relationship between ideas. ADDITION: moreover, furthermore, in addition, also, additionally. CONTRAST: however, nevertheless, on the other hand, conversely. CAUSE/EFFECT: therefore, consequently, as a result, thus. EXEMPLIFICATION: for instance, for example, specifically, such as. Boyne uses simple discourse markers (but, and, then) in dialogue; more sophisticated markers appear in narrative descriptions. | Learn |
| Activity (25 min) | Add discourse markers to a text: "Bruno was confused." (add: Furthermore, Bruno was confused by the fence.) "He wanted to explore." (add: However, he wanted to explore the area beyond the fence.) Insert appropriate markers in 10 sentences. Discuss why each marker fits. | Practice |
| You Do (10 min) | Write 5 sentences about the story using different discourse markers. Label each one. | Written practice |

---

### Lesson 2: Logical Signals (I Do → We Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Logical signals guide the reader through an argument. SEQUENCING: firstly, secondly, thirdly, finally. EMPHASIS: above all, most importantly, significantly. CONSEQUENCE: hence, accordingly, in that case. SUMMARY: in conclusion, to summarise, overall. Each signals a different rhetorical function. | Learn |
| We Do (35 min) | Build a paragraph using logical signals: "Bruno's innocence is significant because... First... Second... Third... Most importantly... Therefore..." Insert signals at appropriate points. Discuss: Does the signal match the logical relationship? Do 2 paragraph constructions. | Guided construction |
| You Do (10 min) | Write an argument paragraph using at least 4 different logical signals. | Written practice |

## WEEK 4 — HEDGING AND BOOSTING

### Lesson 1: Certainty and Uncertainty (I Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Mini-Lesson (20 min) | Hedging = language that expresses uncertainty or caution. Boosting = language that expresses certainty or emphasis. HEDGING: seems, appears, may, might, could, possibly, perhaps, it is possible that, arguably, in some cases. BOOSTING: clearly, obviously, certainly, definitely, absolutely, undoubtedly, it is evident that, undoubtedly. In formal writing about sensitive historical topics, hedging is crucial — we cannot be 100% certain about historical motivations. | Learn |
| Activity (25 min) | Transform boosting to hedging: "The Nazis were evil." → "The Nazis' actions were, arguably, morally reprehensible." "Bruno understood the situation." → "Bruno appeared to understand the situation to some extent." "The fence was built for security." → "The fence was constructed, presumably, for security purposes." Do 10 transformations. Discuss why hedging is more appropriate for historical analysis. | Practice |
| You Do (10 min) | Write 4 hedged claims about the story. Write 4 boosted claims about the story. Label each. | Written practice |

---

### Lesson 2: Hedging Appropriately (I Do → We Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Hedging appropriately: Too much hedging = sounds weak and uncertain. Too little = sounds absolute and potentially unfair. In historical analysis, we hedge when discussing motivations ("It is possible that..." "Arguably...") but we boost when stating facts ("The camp was established in 1940." "The evidence clearly shows..."). Balance is key. | Learn |
| We Do (35 min) | Evaluate sentences for appropriate hedging: "The father definitely knew what was happening." (too confident — maybe hedging is needed) vs "The father may have known what was happening." (appropriate hedging). Find the balance. Edit 10 sentences for appropriate modality. | Guided evaluation |
| You Do (10 min) | Write a paragraph about a character's knowledge. Use hedging for assumptions and boosting for facts. Check: Is the balance appropriate? | Written practice |

---

### Lesson 3: Boosting Claims (You Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Independent

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| You Do (55 min) | Write a formal paragraph analysing the fence in the story. Use boosting for facts: "The fence was constructed of metal." "It stood three metres high." Use hedging for interpretations: "It may have symbolised..." "The fence arguably represented..." "The characters appeared to..." Check the balance of boosting and hedging. | Formal paragraph writing |

## WEEK 5 — PASSIVE VOICE

### Lesson 1: When to Use Passive (I Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Mini-Lesson (20 min) | Passive voice = when the subject receives the action. ACTIVE: "The soldiers built the fence." PASSIVE: "The fence was built by the soldiers." Use passive when: 1. The agent is unknown (the fence was built — who built it?). 2. The agent is less important than the action (the fence was built for security). 3. You want to maintain formal, objective tone. In formal historical writing, passive is common: "People were detained," "Camps were constructed." | Learn |
| Activity (25 min) | Transform active to passive: "The Nazis built the camps." → "The camps were built." "Bruno observed the fence." → "The fence was observed by Bruno." "The family lived in Berlin." → "Berlin was where the family lived." Do 10 transformations. Discuss when passive is more appropriate than active. | Practice |
| You Do (10 min) | Write 5 passive sentences about the story. Identify the agent when known, leave agent unknown when appropriate. | Written practice |

---

### Lesson 2: Passive Construction (I Do → We Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Passive construction patterns: Simple present: "The fence is watched." Simple past: "The fence was watched." Present perfect: "The fence has been watched." Past perfect: "The fence had been watched." Modal: "The fence must be watched." All follow: BE + PAST PARTICIPLE. Add BY AGENT for specific agents. | Learn |
| We Do (35 min) | Use different passive tenses: "The camp is guarded." (present) "The camp was guarded." (past) "The camp has been guarded." (present perfect) "The camp had been guarded." (past perfect) "The camp must be guarded." (modal). Write sentences using each pattern about the story. | Guided construction |
| You Do (10 min) | Write 4 passive sentences using different tenses and modal passives. | Written practice |

---

### Lesson 3: Passive in Formal Writing (You Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Independent

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| You Do (55 min) | Write a formal paragraph about conditions in the camp. Use passive voice throughout to maintain objective, formal tone. Include: is/are + past participle, was/were + past participle, has/have been + past participle, modal passives (must be, could have been). Check: Is the passive creating the right formal effect? | Formal paragraph writing |

## WEEK 6 — COMPLEX SENTENCE STRUCTURES

### Lesson 1: Nominalisation (I Do)
**Time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Mini-Lesson (20 min) | Nominalisation = turning a verb or adjective into a noun. This makes writing more formal and concise. "The Nazis decided to relocate the prisoners." → "The Nazis' decision to relocate the prisoners was..." "Bruno was innocent." → "Bruno's innocence was..." "The camp was destroyed." → "The destruction of the camp was..." Formal writing favours nominalisations because they compress information and allow for more precise expression. | Learn |
| Activity (25 min) | Transform sentences using nominalisation: "The soldiers guarded the camp." → "The soldiers' guarding of the camp was..." "The father commanded the camp." → "The father's command of the camp was..." "Bruno didn't understand." → "Bruno's lack of understanding was..." Do 10 transformations. Discuss: Does the nominalisation add formality? | Practice |
| You Do (10 min) | Write 5 sentences using nominalisation about the story. Circle each nominalisation. | Written practice |

---

### Lesson 2: Cleft Sentences (I Do → We Do)
**time:** 55 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Cleft sentences = splitting a sentence to emphasis one part. "The fence was built in 1940." → "It was the fence that was built in 1940." (emphasis on fence) "Bruno saw the camp." → "What Bruno saw was the camp." (emphasis on what Bruno saw). Cleft sentences foreground information for emphasis and are common in formal writing. | Learn |
| We Do (35 min) | Transform sentences into cleft sentences: "The fence stood three metres high." → "It was the fence that stood three metres high." "Bruno wanted to explore." → "What Bruno wanted was to explore." "The situation was tragic." → "What made the situation tragic was..." Do 10 transformations. Discuss: What is being foregrounded in each? | Guided transformation |
| You Do (10 min) | Write 4 cleft sentences about key moments in the story. Circle what is being foregrounded. | Written practice |

## ADAPTATIONS

### EAL Students
- Provide register word lists (formal vs informal)
- Sentence starters for formal paragraphs
- Allow L1 paragraph construction first, then translate
- Visual grammar posters
- Pair with supportive partner

### Gifted Students
- Explore subjunctive mood for formality
- Analyse nominalisation patterns in academic texts
- Write with extreme register contrast
- Explore concessive clauses with formality
- Research hedging in academic discourse

### Students with NEPs
- Reduce to 1 paragraph per task
- Focus on register distinction and discourse markers
- Provide sentence starters
- Extended time
- Simplify: Focus on register + discourse markers only
