# Reading Comprehension Unit — Year 3

**Unit Duration:** 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons
**Year Level:** Year 3
**Curriculum Link:** AC9E3LY01, AC9E3LY02, AC9E3RE01, AC9E3RE02, AC9E3RE03
**Focus:** Summary, main idea, point of view, cause and effect, text evidence
**Mentor Text:** *The Boy Who Harnessed the Wind* by William Kamkwamba and Bryan Mealer (2012)
**Cold Task:** Week 1, Lesson 1 — Summarise and analyse the first half of the story
**Hot Task:** Week 8, Lesson 1 — Same task, measure growth

---

## Unit Overview

**Learning Intention:**
We are learning to read and comprehend a biographical narrative, identify key themes, analyse point of view, and support interpretations with text evidence.

**Success Criteria:**
- I can summarise key events and main ideas using my own words.
- I can identify themes and explain how they develop across a text.
- I can analyse a character's point of view and explain how it differs from others.
- I can explain cause and effect relationships in the story.
- I can use text evidence to support my interpretations and opinions.

## WEEK 1 — INTRODUCTION TO THE STORY

### Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Assessment

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Hook (5 min) | Show cover of *The Boy Who Harnessed the Wind*. "This is a true story from Africa about a boy who solved a big problem. What do you think a boy in a poor village can do to change his world?" | Predict and discuss |
| Read (20 min) | Read Chapters 1–4 (through the famine). Display photos of Malawi. Show the setting. | Listen, follow, visualise |
| Task (20 min) | Cold Task (same as Hot Task): Write a summary of what you have read. Explain: What is this story about? Who is the main character and what do they want? What is the problem? What do you think will happen next? Support your ideas with evidence from the text. Year 3: 150–250 words. | Independent written response |

**Differentiation:** Chapter summary sheet with key events. Word bank for setting vocabulary.

---

### Lesson 2: Malawi and the Context (I Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Read (10 min) | Show map of Malawi. Explain: subsistence farming, drought, poverty, village life. Connect to what William describes. | Learn context |
| Mini-Lesson (15 min) | Background knowledge is essential for comprehension. Model: "I know Malawi has droughts → I understand why the crops failed." Build schema. Record new vocabulary: famine, drought, subsistence, village, maize, windmill. | Note-taking |
| You Do (15 min) | Create a schema organiser: What I know about Africa | What I know about drought | What I want to learn about Malawi. Connect to William's story. | Prior knowledge activation |
| Share (5 min) | Share one thing you want to learn about Malawi | Discussion |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can explain the setting of Malawi and why it matters for understanding the story.

---

### Lesson 3: Meeting William (We Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Guided Practice

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Model building a character profile for William: Name, Age, Where he lives, What he loves, What he wants, What is in his way. Chapter 1 details. | Observe |
| We Do (20 min) | Together, fill in the character profile using evidence from the text. Add quotes. Add page numbers. | Guided analysis |
| You Do (15 min) | Create your own character profile for William with text evidence | Independent work |

## WEEK 2 — SUMMARY AND MAIN IDEAS

### Lesson 1: What Happened? (I Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Read (15 min) | Read Chapters 5–8 (William's education, the library, reading about windmills). | Listen |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Summary strategy: 1. WHO and WHERE. 2. WHAT happened. 3. HOW the character felt/responded. Keep it brief — 3–5 sentences. Do NOT copy the text. Use your own words. | Learn strategy |
| You Do (20 min) | Write a summary of Chapters 5–8 using the 3-step strategy. Check: Did I use my own words? Did I include the main events? | Written summary |

---

### Lesson 2: Main Ideas (I Do → We Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Main idea vs topic: Topic = "windmills." Main idea = "William learned about windmills from books and this gave him hope." Model with example from text. | Learn distinction |
| We Do (20 min) | Find the main idea of each chapter read so far. Create a main idea chart: Chapter | Main Idea | Supporting Details. | Guided analysis |
| You Do (15 min) | Complete the chart for Chapters 9–10 (William's plan begins). | Independent analysis |

---

### Lesson 3: Summarising Skills (We Do → You Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Guided → Independent

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (5 min) | Paraphrasing = putting someone else's ideas into your own words. Show how to change one sentence from the book into your own words. | Model |
| We Do (20 min) | Practice paraphrasing 3 key sentences from Chapters 9–10 together. Check: Is it shorter? Is it different words? Did I keep the meaning? | Group practice |
| You Do (20 min) | Paraphrase 3 key passages from Chapters 11–12 in your reading journal | Independent practice |

## WEEK 3 — CAUSE AND EFFECT

### Lesson 1: Why Did It Happen? (I Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Read (15 min) | Read Chapters 13–15 (the drought worsens, families suffer). | Listen |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Cause = why something happened. Effect = what happened. CAUSE → EFFECT. Example: Cause: no rain for months → Effect: crops died, families went hungry. Model with examples from text. | Learn concept |
| You Do (20 min) | Identify 3 cause-effect relationships from Chapters 13–15. Draw a cause-effect chain for each. | Written analysis |

---

### Lesson 2: Chain Reactions (I Do → We Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Chain reaction: One cause leads to an effect, which causes another effect. Drought → no crops → no food → families leave → school closes. Model with text events. | Model chain |
| We Do (25 min) | Map the chain reaction of events in Chapters 13–17. Show: What happened first? What happened because of that? What happened because of that? | Group chain mapping |
| You Do (10 min) | Write a chain reaction paragraph about the famine using cause-effect words (because, so, therefore, as a result). | Written practice |

---

### Lesson 3: Cause-Effect Maps (You Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Independent

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| You Do (45 min) | Create a cause-effect map for the entire first half of the book. Show: The drought → Its effects on William and his family → William's response → How this leads to his windmill idea. Use arrows and text boxes. | Comprehensive mapping |

## WEEK 4 — POINT OF VIEW

### Lesson 1: Whose Eyes? (I Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Read (10 min) | Read Chapter 18 (William faces his first failure). How does this chapter make us feel? Whose perspective is shown? | Identify perspective |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Point of view = Whose story is this being told from? *The Boy Who Harnessed the Wind* is told from William's first-person perspective. He tells us what he sees, thinks, and feels. Other characters have different perspectives. | Learn concept |
| You Do (25 min) | Find 5 quotes that show William's perspective: What he sees, what he thinks, what he feels. Label each: SEE / THINK / FEEL. | Evidence collection |

---

### Lesson 2: William's Perspective (I Do → We Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | How does William's perspective affect what we know? What does he tell us? What might he NOT tell us (because he's a kid, because he wants to protect family)? | Analyze |
| We Do (25 min) | Identify what William tells us (first-hand experience) vs what we can infer. Create an "What William Shows Us" chart: Explicit information | What We Can Infer | Gaps in William's Knowledge. | Guided analysis |
| You Do (10 min) | Write: How would this story be different if it was told by William's mother? By the village chief? | Perspective writing |

---

### Lesson 3: Other Perspectives (You Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Independent

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| You Do (45 min) | Choose one other character from the story (mother, sister, friend, teacher). Write a short passage from their perspective about the same events William describes. Show how their perspective differs from William's. | Creative perspective writing |

## WEEK 5 — THEMES AND BIG IDEAS

### Lesson 1: What Is the Story Really About? (I Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Read (15 min) | Read Chapters 19–22 (building the windmill, facing ridicule, persisting). | Listen |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Theme = the big message or lesson the author wants us to understand. Examples: Never give up / Knowledge is power / One person can change the world / Hope can overcome hardship. Themes are not stated directly — we infer them. | Learn concept |
| You Do (20 min) | Identify possible themes in the story so far. Write: "I think one theme is... because (text evidence)..." | Written analysis |

---

### Lesson 2: Theme Evidence (I Do → We Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | To prove a theme, you need MULTIPLE pieces of evidence from different parts of the text. Model: "Theme: Never give up → Evidence 1: William keeps reading at library. Evidence 2: He builds smaller models first. Evidence 3: He persists even when people laugh." | Model |
| We Do (25 min) | Build a theme evidence chart for the theme "Knowledge is power": Evidence from beginning | Evidence from middle | Evidence from recent chapters. Use specific page references. | Group analysis |
| You Do (10 min) | Build your own theme evidence chart for "Never give up" | Independent practice |

---

### Lesson 3: Themes Across Text (You Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Independent

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| You Do (45 min) | Choose one theme and write an analytical paragraph: "In *The Boy Who Harnessed the Wind*, one theme is [THEME]. This is shown when [EVENT 1 with page], also when [EVENT 2 with page], and again when [EVENT 3 with page]. This theme teaches us [WHAT IT MEANS]." | Written thematic analysis |

## WEEK 6 — TEXT EVIDENCE AND INTERPRETATION

### Lesson 1: Finding Proof (I Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Read (10 min) | Read Chapters 23–25 (the windmill works, the village responds). | Listen |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Evidence-based writing rule: Every interpretation needs proof. "William is brave" → PROOF: "He kept working even though everyone laughed at him (p. X)." Model building an evidence chain. | Learn rule |
| You Do (25 min) | Make an interpretation about the story (e.g., "The village learned to believe in new ideas"). Find 2 pieces of text evidence to support it. Write page numbers and quotes. | Written evidence practice |

---

### Lesson 2: Building Arguments (I Do → We Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Structure of an evidence-based paragraph: CLAIM → EVIDENCE → EXPLANATION → LINK. Model: "William shows that one person can change a community (CLAIM). In chapter 24, his windmill brings electricity to his house (EVIDENCE). This shows an individual can create real change (EXPLANATION). This inspires others to try new things (LINK)." | Learn structure |
| We Do (25 min) | Write an evidence-based paragraph together about one theme. Use the CLAIM-EVIDENCE-EXPLANATION-LINK structure. | Group writing |
| You Do (10 min) | Write a second evidence-based paragraph independently | Independent writing |

---

### Lesson 3: Presenting Interpretations (You Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Independent

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| You Do (45 min) | Prepare for discussion: Write 3 interpretations about the story with evidence. Write 2 questions you have about the text. Be ready to defend your interpretations with text evidence. | Preparation |

## ADAPTATIONS

### EAL Students
- Provide chapter summaries in home language when available
- Pre-teach vocabulary before each section
- Allow bilingual dictionary use
- Sentence starters for evidence-based writing
- Provide a note-taking template with prompts
- Allow oral analysis with teacher transcription
- Visual theme maps to complete

### Gifted Students
- Research Malawi and create a multimedia presentation
- Compare to other biographies of young change-makers
- Analyse the photographic essay vs the text
- Write an alternative ending
- Create a documentary script about William's story
- Research windmill engineering and write a technical explanation

### Students with NEPs
- Provide pre-digested chapter summaries
- Reduce to 3 key events to summarise
- Allow picture-based responses
- Extended time for all tasks
- One-on-one reading support
- Provide a peer helper
- Simplify to one theme per session
