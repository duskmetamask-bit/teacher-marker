# Persuasive Writing Unit: The Rabbits — colonisation, perspective and power

**Unit Duration:** 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons
**Year Level:** Years 5–6
**Curriculum Link:** Australian Curriculum English v9 — Persuasive Writing
**Mentor Text:** *The Rabbits* by John Marsden and Shaun Tan (1998)
**Writing Focus:** Persuasive writing — constructing arguments, author's viewpoint, bias, and rhetorical devices
**Cold Task:** Pre-assessment (Week 1, Lesson 1) — Write a persuasive piece arguing whether a change should be made at school
**Hot Task:** Post-assessment (Week 8, Lesson 1) — Same task as Cold Task; measure growth

---

## Unit Overview

**Learning Intention:**
We are learning to write a persuasive text that presents a clear viewpoint, supports it with structured arguments and evidence, and uses rhetorical devices to persuade the reader.

**Success Criteria:**
- I can identify the author's viewpoint and bias in a persuasive text.
- I can structure a persuasive text with a hook, thesis, three arguments, counterargument, and conclusion.
- I can use rhetorical devices: rule of three, repetition, emotive language, expert opinion, analogy, and rhetorical questions.
- I can analyse how persuasive texts work — in advertisements, speeches, articles, and visual texts.
- I can write my own persuasive piece (essay, letter, speech, or editorial) with a clear position and supporting evidence.

## WEEK 1 — INTRODUCTION TO PERSUASIVE WRITING

### Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Assessment

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Hook (5 min) | Show three images: a clean school playground vs. a littered one, a student reading vs. playing a game, a school garden vs. bare concrete. Ask: Which of these would you prefer? How could you convince someone to agree? | Share observations |
| Task (35 min) | **Cold Task — same prompt as Hot Task (Week 8):** Think about something at school that should be changed. Year 6: 300–450 words. | Independent writing |

### Lesson 2: What is Persuasion? (I Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|

| Analysis (10 min) | Together, identify persuasion in everyday life: TV ads, signs, petitions, speeches, reviews. Why do people try to persuade us? | Discuss examples |
| Share (5 min) | Students share one example of persuasion they saw or heard recently | Share with class |

### Lesson 3: Finding the Viewpoint (We Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Guided Practice

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Show a short advertisement (e.g., a sports drink ad). Ask: What does this ad want me to believe? What viewpoint is presented? Whose viewpoint might be missing? Model finding the VIEWPOINT = what side the writer takes. | Observe and discuss |
| We Do (15 min) | Show two contrasting news headlines about the same event. Identify each author's viewpoint. Create a T-chart: Viewpoint A vs. Viewpoint B. Discuss: How can two writers see the same event so differently? | Work through T-chart together |
| You Do (10 min) | Think: What is YOUR viewpoint on something important to you? Write 2–3 sentences stating your position on ONE thing you care about (e.g., homework, screen time, school uniform). | Independent writing |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can identify the writer's viewpoint in at least two different texts.

## WEEK 2 — THE RABBITS — PERSPECTIVE AND BIAS

### Lesson 1: Whose Voice is Heard? (I Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Mentor Text

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Read + Think (20 min) | Read *The Rabbits* aloud (all pages). Allow time to absorb the illustrations. Ask: What do you see? What do you notice? What do you feel? | Listen, look, respond |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Discuss: *The Rabbits* is about colonisation. Marsden and Tan tell the story from the perspective of the native animals (the bilbies, bandicoots, emus). They are the RABBITS' rabbits — the ones being colonised. Ask: Whose viewpoint is presented? What side is the author on? How can you tell? | Identify perspective |
| Analysis (10 min) | Find evidence in the text and images: How does the language make you feel about the rabbits? How are the rabbits shown visually? Create a LIST: things the rabbits do in the book vs. things the native animals do. | Compile evidence |
| Share (5 min) | Students share one observation about whose side the authors are on | |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can identify whose perspective is presented in *The Rabbits* and give textual evidence.

---

### Lesson 2: Whose Voice is Absent? (We Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Guided Analysis

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Re-read selected pages of *The Rabbits*. Ask: We hear from the native animals. But do we hear from the rabbits? What do the rabbits say? What is absent from this story? | Think and respond |
| We Do (15 min) | The concept of BIAS = presenting one side while leaving out the other. When an author chooses a perspective, they also choose what NOT to show. Discuss: What would the rabbits' side of the story sound like? If the rabbits told this story, what would change? | Participate in discussion |
| You Do (15 min) | Write a SHORT paragraph (5–8 sentences) from the rabbits' perspective. Pretend you are one of the rabbit soldiers in the book. What would you say to justify what you are doing? (This is an exercise in understanding another viewpoint — not an endorsement.) | Write from opposing perspective |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can explain that bias comes from whose voice is present AND whose voice is absent.

---

### Lesson 3: What Argument Does the Book Make? (I Do → We Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | THESIS = the main argument a writer is trying to prove. Pose: What is Marsden and Tan's thesis in *The Rabbits*? Brainstorm possibilities. Model: "The book argues that colonisation was destructive and unfair." / "The book argues that the native animals lost everything." | Engage with thesis concept |
| We Do (15 min) | Students work in pairs to identify: What is ONE clear argument the book makes? Write it as a thesis statement. Share with class. Discuss: Is there more than one possible thesis? | Collaborate and share |
| You Do (15 min) | Think about a topic you feel strongly about. Write your own thesis statement — one sentence that clearly states your position on this issue. (We'll return to this thesis throughout the unit.) | Write personal thesis |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can articulate the thesis of *The Rabbits* and write their own thesis statement.

## WEEK 3 — TEXT STRUCTURE — THE PERSUASIVE ESSAY

### Lesson 1: Hook and Thesis (I Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Hook Example (10 min) | Show four opening lines — which one grabs you? "Many people think homework is good." vs. "Imagine coming home after a seven-hour school day and being told you still have two more hours of work to do." vs. "Homework is a topic that divides opinion." vs. "Homework should be abolished." Discuss what makes a strong HOOK. | Analyse opening lines |
| Mini-Lesson (15 min) | Structure of a persuasive essay: **HOOK** → **THESIS** → **ARGUMENT 1** → **ARGUMENT 2** → **ARGUMENT 3** → **COUNTERARGUMENT** → **CONCLUSION**. The THESIS states your position — it must be ARGUEABLE (someone could disagree). Model thesis: "Too much homework harms students' wellbeing" — someone COULD argue the opposite. "Homework should be banned" — this is argueable. "Homework exists" — this is NOT argueable. | Take notes on structure |
| You Do (10 min) | Take the thesis you wrote in Week 2 Lesson 3. Refine it so it is argueable. Write a HOOK for your topic — a question, a surprising fact, a vivid image, or a bold statement. | Refine thesis; draft hook |

**Success Criteria Check:** Has an argueable thesis and a hook for their persuasive piece.

---

### Lesson 2: The Three Arguments (I Do → We Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Why THREE arguments? Three is the RULE OF THREE — the most persuasive number. Your three arguments are your three biggest reasons WHY your reader should agree with you. Each argument needs: REASON + EVIDENCE/EXAMPLE. Model: Thesis: "School uniforms should be required." Argument 1: It creates equality (evidence: students can't be judged by expensive brands). Argument 2: It saves time (evidence: no deciding what to wear each morning). Argument 3: It builds school pride (evidence: students feel part of something). | Observe structure |
| We Do (15 min) | Together, plan three arguments for a sample thesis: "Primary school students should not have homework." List possible arguments. Choose the three strongest. Write one as a full argument with REASON + EVIDENCE. | Participate in collaborative planning |
| You Do (15 min) | Return to your thesis. List as many potential arguments as you can (aim for 5–6). Choose the three strongest. Write each one as a heading in your planning document. | Independent planning |

**Success Criteria Check:** Has three distinct, strong arguments to support their thesis.

---

### Lesson 3: Counterargument and Conclusion (We Do → You Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Guided → Independent

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | COUNTERARGUMENT = acknowledging the other side before rebutting it. Show two versions: WITHOUT counterargument: "My view is right and anyone who disagrees is wrong." WITH counterargument: "Some people believe X. However, on closer examination, Y is actually true because..." Why is counterargument powerful? It shows you've considered both sides — it makes YOU more credible. | Understand counterargument function |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | CONCLUSION: Don't just repeat your thesis. Elevate it. Signal the end of your argument. Model techniques: Return to your hook / Make a broader statement / Call to action. BAD conclusion: "So that's why I think this." GOOD conclusion: "If we want students to thrive, not just survive, we must act now." | Note conclusion techniques |
| You Do (20 min) | Plan your counterargument (what would someone who disagrees say?) and your conclusion strategy. Write a full outline of your persuasive piece: Hook / Thesis / Argument 1 / Argument 2 / Argument 3 / Counterargument + Rebuttal / Conclusion. | Complete full outline |

**Success Criteria Check:** Full outline complete with hook, thesis, three arguments, counterargument, and conclusion.

## WEEK 4 — RHETORICAL DEVICES

### Lesson 1: Rule of Three + Repetition (I Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Hook (5 min) | Say: "Read to write, write to read, revise to rise." Ask: What do you notice? Why does it stick? | Notice the pattern |
| Mini-Lesson (15 min) | RULE OF THREE: Things come in threes — they are memorable, satisfying, and persuasive. Examples: "Life, liberty and the pursuit of happiness." "Government of the people, by the people, for the people." "Stop, look, and listen." Find the rule of three in *The Rabbits* ("They came. They built. They took."). | Identify rule of three in texts |
| REPETITION: Repeating a word or phrase for EMPHASIS. Martin Luther King Jr.: "I have a dream... I have a dream... I have a dream." Show how repetition creates rhythm and power. | Notice repetition |
| You Do (15 min) | Find two examples of the rule of three in ads, speeches, or songs. Then write three sentences for your persuasive piece using the rule of three. Write one paragraph using repetition for emphasis. | Identify examples; write with devices |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can identify and use rule of three and repetition in their writing.

---

### Lesson 2: Emotive Language + Expert Opinion (I Do → We Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | EMOTIVE LANGUAGE = words that make the reader FEEL something. Show two sentences: "The factory closed and people lost their jobs." vs. "The factory closed, leaving families devastated, communities shattered, and hundreds of workers abandoned without hope." Same facts — very different emotional impact. In *The Rabbits*, Marsden uses words like "screamed," "burned," "lost," "terrified." | Compare and contrast |
| Expert Opinion (10 min) | EXPERT OPINION = citing authorities to strengthen your argument. "According to Dr. Jane Smith, a child psychologist..." "Research from the University of Melbourne shows..." Why does it work? It moves the argument from opinion to evidence. | Understand function |
| We Do (10 min) | Students find one example of expert opinion in a news article or advertisement. Share with class. Discuss: Is this expert credible? How can you tell? | Find and evaluate examples |
| You Do (10 min) | Write one paragraph of your persuasive piece using at least three emotive words. Write one sentence using expert opinion (can be fictional — "According to researchers..."). | Apply to own writing |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can identify and use emotive language and expert opinion in their writing.

---

### Lesson 3: Analogy + Rhetorical Questions (I Do → We Do → You Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided + Independent

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | ANALOGY = comparing something complex or unfamiliar to something simpler. "Writing a persuasive essay is like building a house — you need a solid foundation (thesis), strong walls (arguments), and a roof to seal it in (conclusion)." MLK: "The arc of the moral universe is long, but it bends toward justice." Why do analogies work? They make the abstract concrete. | Understand analogy |
| Rhetorical Questions (10 min) | RHETORICAL QUESTION = a question asked for effect, not expecting an answer. "Isn't it time we stopped pretending homework helps learning?" "How many more children must suffer before we act?" Students find examples in *The Rabbits* or ads. Discuss: Does the writer expect an answer? What does the rhetorical question achieve? | Identify rhetorical questions |
| We Do (5 min) | Class brainstorms three analogies for their own thesis topic | Participate |
| You Do (15 min) | Write two analogies for your persuasive piece. Write one rhetorical question to include in your introduction and one in your conclusion. | Apply to own piece |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can identify and use analogy and rhetorical questions in their writing.

## WEEK 5 — ANALYSING REAL PERSUASIVE TEXTS

### Lesson 1: Advertisements (We Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Guided Analysis

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Show 3–4 advertisements (print or video). For each ad: What is the HOOK? What is the THESIS (what claim does the ad make)? What RHETORICAL DEVICES are used? Model a full analysis of one ad. | Observe analysis method |
| We Do (15 min) | In pairs, analyse a different ad each. Use the analysis framework: HOOK / THESIS / DEVICES (rule of three, emotive language, expert opinion, analogy, rhetorical question) / COUNTERARGUMENT (is there one? would it weaken the ad?) / TARGET AUDIENCE. | Pair analysis |
| You Do (15 min) | Choose your favourite ad from the analysis session. Write a paragraph explaining what makes it effective — or ineffective. Which devices does it use? Who is it trying to persuade? | Write analysis paragraph |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can analyse an advertisement using the full framework (hook, thesis, devices, counterargument, audience).

### Lesson 2: Speeches (I Do → We Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (15 min) | Play or read a short excerpt from a famous speech (e.g., Greta Thunberg, Malala Yousafzai, or Martin Luther King Jr. — age-appropriate excerpts). Analyse: What is the speaker's thesis? Find: a rule of three, an emotional moment, a rhetorical question, an analogy, expert or evidence-based language. | Listen and identify |
| We Do (15 min) | Watch a 2-minute advertisement or short speech as a class. Students complete the full analysis framework on paper: Hook / Thesis / Argument 1 / Argument 2 / Argument 3 / Counterargument / Conclusion / Devices used / Target audience. | Complete framework together |
| You Do (10 min) | Think: What speech would YOU give? What is one issue in your school or community that you feel strongly about? Write a one-sentence thesis for a speech you could give. | Draft thesis for original speech |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can identify the structure and rhetorical devices in a speech.

### Lesson 3: News Articles and Opinion Pieces (You Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Independent Analysis

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Mini-Lesson (5 min) | DISTINGUISH: A NEWS article = presents facts. An OPINION PIECE = presents a viewpoint and tries to persuade. Both appear in newspapers. Discuss examples: news article about a sports match vs. an editorial about school funding. | Distinguish between types |
| You Do (30 min) | Students read a provided opinion article or editorial (teacher-selected, age-appropriate). Complete a full analysis: VIEWPOINT / THESIS / THREE ARGUMENTS / COUNTERARGUMENT / DEVICES USED / BIAS — whose viewpoint is missing? | Independent analysis task |
| Share (5 min) | 2–3 students share one insight from their article analysis | Share findings |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can distinguish a news article from an opinion piece and identify bias in texts.

## WEEK 6 — PLANNING AND DRAFTING

### Lesson 1: Plan Your Persuasive Piece (We Do → You Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Planning

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Return to your outline from Week 3 Lesson 3. Today we refine it and add detail. For EACH of your three arguments: Add a FACT or EXAMPLE. Add EMOTIVE LANGUAGE. Add a RHETORICAL DEVICE (rule of three, analogy, or rhetorical question). Add EXPERT OPINION if relevant. | Refine arguments |
| We Do (10 min) | Model refining Argument 1 for a sample thesis on the board. Show how to expand a bare argument into a full, persuasive paragraph. | Observe modelling |
| You Do (20 min) | Complete a full detailed plan for your persuasive piece: Hook (with device) / Thesis (clearly stated) / Argument 1 (reason + evidence + emotive language + device) / Argument 2 (same) / Argument 3 (same) / Counterargument + rebuttal / Conclusion (strategy chosen). | Complete detailed plan |

**Success Criteria Check:** Detailed plan complete with all structural components and rhetorical devices planned.

---

### Lesson 2: Draft — Introduction and Arguments (You Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Independent Writing

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| You Do (35 min) | Using your detailed plan, write the DRAFT of your persuasive piece. Include: INTRODUCTION (hook + thesis) / ARGUMENT 1 / ARGUMENT 2 / ARGUMENT 3. Aim for: Year 5 = 250–350 words. Year 6 = 300–450 words. This is a ROUGH DRAFT — focus on getting your ideas down. | Focused independent writing |
| Share (5 min) | 2–3 students share their introduction for feedback | Listen and offer feedback |

**Differentiation:** EAL students may write introduction and one argument only. Gifted students: add a fourth argument or write a second persuasive piece from the opposing viewpoint.

### Lesson 3: Draft — Counterargument and Conclusion (You Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Independent Writing

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| You Do (35 min) | Complete your draft: COUNTERARGUMENT + REBUTTAL / CONCLUSION. Read your full draft aloud to yourself. Does it sound persuasive? Does the argument flow? | Finish full draft |

## WEEK 7 — REVISING AND EDITING

## RUBRIC — Persuasive Writing (Years 5–6)

**AC English v9 Alignment:** AC9E5LY01 (Year 5) / AC9E6LY01 (Year 6) — Persuasive Writing

| Criterion | A (Excellent) | B (Strong) | C (Satisfactory) | D (Developing) | E (Beginning) |
|-----------|---------------|------------|-----------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Thesis & Viewpoint** | Clear, arguable thesis stated early. Viewpoint is consistent and strongly expressed throughout. Reader knows exactly where the writer stands. | Clear thesis stated. Viewpoint consistent throughout. Minor absence of nuance. | Thesis present and arguable. Viewpoint mostly clear. Some parts ambiguous. | Thesis unclear or not arguable. Viewpoint inconsistent. Reader uncertain of position. | No clear thesis. Viewpoint absent or contradictory. |
| **Argument Structure** | Hook → Thesis → 3 Arguments → Counterargument → Conclusion. All components present and skillfully integrated. Ideas flow logically. | Clear structure with all components. Minor logic gaps between arguments. | Structure present. Hook and thesis clear. Counterargument brief. Some logical inconsistency between arguments. | Partial structure. Missing counterargument or conclusion. Arguments lack logical development. | No clear structure. Components absent or random. |
| **Rhetorical Devices** | Skilled use of rule of three, repetition, emotive language, expert opinion, analogy, and rhetorical questions throughout. Devices enhance persuasiveness. | Good use of 4–5 rhetorical devices. Devices mostly effective. | Adequate use of 2–3 rhetorical devices. Some devices used mechanically. | Limited use of 1–2 rhetorical devices. Some devices inaccurate or inappropriate. | No rhetorical devices used, or devices used incorrectly. |
| **Evidence & Examples** | Specific, relevant evidence and examples for each argument. Expert opinion used credibly. Facts strengthen credibility. | Good evidence for most arguments. Expert opinion present. Minor gaps in specificity. | Some evidence present. Not all arguments supported. Evidence may be generic. | Minimal evidence. Generalisations without support. | No evidence or examples provided. |
| **Counterargument & Rebuttal** | Counterargument acknowledged thoughtfully and rebutted with logic and evidence. Demonstrates sophisticated understanding of opposing view. | Counterargument present and rebutted. Rebuttal logical but may lack depth. | Counterargument mentioned but rebuttal weak or brief. | Counterargument absent or dismissed without reasoning. | No counterargument present. |
| **Language & Tone** | Tone consistently persuasive and appropriate for audience. Precise, varied vocabulary. Emotive language used purposefully. No informal language inappropriate to register. | Mostly consistent persuasive tone. Good vocabulary range. Minor tone slips. | Some persuasive language. Vocabulary adequate but repetitive. Tone varies. | Tone inconsistent. Limited vocabulary. Informally phrased. | No persuasive register. Conversational tone throughout. |
| **Punctuation, Grammar & Presentation** | Accurate punctuation and grammar throughout. Varied sentence structures. No spelling errors. Published version carefully presented. | Minor errors. Good sentence variety. Clear presentation. | Some errors in punctuation or grammar. Reasonable sentence variety. Adequate presentation. | Noticeable errors that occasionally affect clarity. Limited sentence variety. | Significant errors affect clarity. Poor sentence variety. Presentation neglected. |

## ADAPTATIONS

### EAL Students
- Provide a sentence starter bank for each structural component (hook, thesis, argument, counterargument, conclusion)
- Allow oral rehearsal of arguments before writing — record and transcribe
- Word bank displayed in classroom: persuasive verbs (argue, claim, demand, urge), transition words (however, although, consequently), emotive vocabulary
- Provide a partially completed outline to reduce cognitive load during drafting
- Allow use of home language for initial brainstorming and planning
- One-on-one conference to clarify task before each writing session
- Provide a model persuasive essay as reference (annotated with structural labels)
- Extra time for all writing tasks
- Allow typing rather than handwriting if available

### Gifted Students
- Write a counterargument FIRST (from the opposing viewpoint), then rebut it — deepens understanding of both sides
- Write the same persuasive piece in TWO different genres (e.g., one as a speech, one as a newspaper editorial) — analyse what changes
- Analyse a real political speech or debate — identify all devices used and rate effectiveness
- Write a rebuttal to a published opinion piece
- Explore the difference between persuasion and manipulation — discuss ethical boundaries of persuasion
- Add a visual element: design a poster or advertisement that embodies the same argument

### Students with NEPs (Non-Engagement Plans)
- Adjust word count expectations accordingly
- Provide a fully structured graphic organiser with sentence starters for each paragraph
- Allow oral recording or transcription of ideas
- Break each lesson into shorter bursts (2 × 15-minute writing blocks with a break)
- Provide a peer writing buddy for drafting sessions
- Allow the use of text-to-speech for editing
- Simplify success criteria to three key goals:
  1. I have a clear thesis
  2. I have three arguments
  3. I have used at least two rhetorical devices
- Extra time for all writing and assessment tasks
- Provide a partially completed draft that the student finishes (rather than writing from scratch)

## ASSESSMENT NOTES

### Formative Assessment (Ongoing)
- Exit tickets: "One rhetorical device I used today. One thing I need to work on."
- Drafts and planning documents assessed mid-unit (Week 4)
- Teacher conferences during drafting sessions (Week 6, Lessons 2–3)
- Peer feedback using the analysis framework (Week 6 Lesson 3)

### Summative Assessment
- Cold Task (Week 1) → Hot Task (Week 8) compared using the same rubric
- Published persuasive piece assessed with full rubric
- Self-reflection with goal-setting

### Tracking Progress
Document the following for each student:
1. Cold Task score → Hot Task score on each rubric criterion
2. Specific growth in rhetorical devices used (count: Cold Task vs. Hot Task)
3. Evidence of structural improvement (counterargument present? hook effective?)
4. Student self-assessment comparison
5. Next unit writing goal

### AC v9 Cross-Curriculum Priorities Addressed
- **Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Histories and Cultures:** *The Rabbits* is a key text for exploring colonisation from First Nations perspectives. The unit explicitly discusses whose voice is heard and whose is absent — building critical literacy around perspective and bias in historical narratives.
- **Ethical Understanding:** Students engage with the ethics of persuasion, distinguishing between legitimate persuasion and manipulation. The counterargument component builds intellectual empathy.
- **Intercultural Understanding:** By writing from the rabbits' perspective (Week 2 Lesson 2), students develop perspective-taking skills and engage with the experiences of colonised peoples.
