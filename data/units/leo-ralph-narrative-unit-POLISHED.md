# Narrative Writing Unit: Leo and Ralph

**Unit Duration:** 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons
**Year Level:** Years 3–4
**Curriculum Link:** Australian Curriculum English v9 - Narrative Writing
**Mentor Text:** *Leo and Ralph* by Peter Carnavas (2024)
**Writing Focus:** Imaginative narrative with a theme of belonging and imaginary friends
**Cold Task:** Pre-assessment (Weeks 1, Lesson 1) - Write a narrative about an imaginary friend
**Hot Task:** Post-assessment (Week 8, Lesson 1) - Same as Cold Task

## Unit Overview

**Learning Intention:**
We are learning to write an imaginative narrative that explores themes of friendship, belonging, and identity through the use of structured writing techniques.

**Success Criteria:**
- I can plan a narrative with a beginning, middle, and end.
- I can create a character with specific traits and emotions.
- I can use descriptive language effectively.
- I can show characters' emotions through actions and dialogue.
- I can use paragraphs for clear organization.

## WEEK 1 — INTRODUCTION TO NARRATIVE

### Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Assessment

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Hook (5 min) | Read *Leo and Ralph* pp. 1–10 aloud. Discuss: What do you notice about Leo and Ralph? | Listen and share observations |
| Task (35 min) | Cold Task — same prompt as Hot Task (Week 8): Write a narrative about a child with an unusual or special imaginary friend. Year 3: 150–250 words. Year 4: 200–350 words. | Independent writing |

**Differentiation:** Word bank and sentence starters available for EAL/writing support students.

---

### Lesson 2: What Makes a Narrative? (I Do)
**Time:** 30 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Read + Think (15 min) | Read *Leo and Ralph* pp. 11–35. Stop and identify: Who is the main character? What do they want? What happens in the beginning? | Track characters and events on a story map |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Introduce: Beginning → Problem → Resolution. Use *Leo and Ralph* as the example. | Copy the structure into writing notebooks |
| Share (5 min) | Students share one observation about how the story is structured | |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can identify beginning, problem and resolution in *Leo and Ralph*.

---

### Lesson 3: Creating Your Character (We Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Guided Practice

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do — Character Deep Dive (10 min) | Model: "Leo loves space; he is quiet, struggles to fit in, and wants a friend." Build a character profile on the board: NAME / APPEARANCE / PERSONALITY / WHAT THEY WANT / WHAT THEY LOVE | Observe and contribute |
| We Do (15 min) | Together, build a character for a shared class story using a character profile template | Contribute ideas, fill in template |
| You Do (10 min) | Brainstorm your own character for your unit story | Complete character profile template |

**Success Criteria Check:** Has a character with at least 3 personality traits and a clear want/need.

## WEEK 2 — SETTING AND BEGINNING

### Lesson 1: Where and When? (I Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Read (10 min) | Read *Leo and Ralph* pp. 36–60. Highlight how Carnavas describes Leo's bedroom, school, and town. | Mark descriptive passages in their copies |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Setting: WHOLE → SPECIFIC → SENSES. Model: "The town was quiet" → "Leederville was a small town where everyone knew each other" → "In Leederville, you could hear the hum of the main street and smell the dust on the summer wind." | |
| We Do (10 min) | Together, describe a setting using the 3-level approach | Participate collaboratively |
| You Do (10 min) | Draft the WHERE and WHEN of your narrative | Independent drafting |

---

### Lesson 2: Strong Openings (I Do → We Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Show 3 opening lines — one boring, two engaging. Discuss what makes them work. *Leo and Ralph* opens with: "Leo loved space. He knew everything about it." Why does this work? | Analyze examples |
| Mini-Lesson (5 min) | Types of strong openings: Start with a character's voice / Start with action / Start with a problem | Take notes |
| We Do (10 min) | Class writes 3 different openings for the shared class story | Engage and suggest options |
| You Do (10 min) | Write 2 different openings for your narrative | Independent work |

---

### Lesson 3: Planning Your Narrative (We Do → You Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Guided → Independent

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (5 min) | Show how to plan *Leo and Ralph* on a story map: BEGINNING → PROBLEM → ENDING | Observe model |
| We Do (10 min) | Class collaboratively plans a new story about an imaginary friend using a story map template | Participate in group planning |
| You Do (20 min) | Students complete their own story maps for their unit narrative | Independent completion |

**Success Criteria Check:** Story map has beginning, problem and resolution.

## WEEK 3 — BUILDING THE BEGINNING

### Lesson 1: Writing Your Beginning (You Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Independent Writing

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|

**Differentiation:** Scaffolding graphic organiser available. EAL students may use bilingual dictionaries.

---

### Lesson 2: Showing vs Telling Emotions (I Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | TELLING: "Leo was sad." SHOWING: "Leo's shoulders dropped. He stared at his shoes. 'I don't want to go,' he whispered." Model the difference using *Leo and Ralph* | Discuss differences |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Emotion thesaurus: sad → shoulders drop, voice goes quiet, pulls away / happy → bounces, laughs, talks fast / angry → voice rises, fists clench, walks away | Add to writing toolkits |
| We Do (10 min) | Transform 3 telling sentences into showing sentences together | Participate |
| You Do (10 min) | Revise 1 paragraph of your beginning to SHOW emotions | Independent revision |

---

### Lesson 3: Dialogue (I Do → We Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Read *Leo and Ralph* pp. 61–80. Find examples of Ralph and Leo's dialogue. What do we learn about their friendship from what they say? | Notice dialogue usage |
| Mini-Lesson (5 min) | Punctuation for dialogue: "Help me," Leo said. "I can't do it alone," said Ralph. | Note rules |
| We Do (10 min) | Write 3 lines of dialogue between a child and their imaginary friend | Group writing activity |
| You Do (10 min) | Add at least one line of dialogue to your narrative beginning | Enhance personal drafts |

## WEEK 5 — THE RESOLUTION AND ENDING

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|

## RUBRIC — Narrative Writing (Years 3–4)
**AC English v9 Alignment:** AC9E3LY01 (Yr3) / AC9E4LY01 (Yr4) — Narrative Writing

| Criterion | A (Excellent) | B (Strong) | C (Satisfactory) | D (Developing) | E (Beginning) |
|-----------|---------------|------------|-----------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Narrative Structure** | Sophisticated, well-crafted beginning, middle and end. Problem clearly established and resolved with emotional depth. | Clear beginning, middle and end. Problem resolved satisfyingly. | Beginning, middle and end present. Problem resolved. | Structure present but uneven. Beginning or ending unclear. | Minimal structure. Difficult to follow. |
| **Character Development** | Character vividly drawn with distinct personality, motivations and emotions. Shows growth/change. | Character has clear traits and motivations. Some emotional development shown. | Character has at least 3 identifiable traits. Some showing of emotions. | Character underdeveloped or inconsistent. Mostly telling rather than showing. | Character flat or unclear. |
| **Setting & Atmosphere** | Setting vividly established with sensory detail. Distinct atmosphere created throughout. | Setting established with some sensory detail. Atmosphere mostly consistent. | Setting mentioned. Limited sensory detail. Some atmosphere. | Setting unclear or generic. Little atmosphere created. | No clear setting. |
| **Language Features** | Rich, precise vocabulary. Uses showing throughout. Dialogue purposeful and realistic. | Good word choices. Shows emotions through actions/dialogue. Dialogue advances story. | Some strong word choices. Some showing of emotions. Dialogue present. | Repetitive language. Mostly telling emotions. Dialogue limited or unnatural. | Limited vocabulary. No showing. |
| **Punctuation & Grammar** | Accurate throughout. Varied sentence structures. No spelling errors. | Minor errors. Good sentence variety. | Some errors in punctuation or grammar. Reasonable sentence variety. | Noticeable errors sometimes affect clarity. Limited sentence variety. | Significant errors affect clarity. |

## ADAPTATIONS

### EAL Students
- Character profile template with visual prompts
- Sentence starters for each paragraph component
- Allow use of home language for initial brainstorming
- Word bank displayed on classroom wall
- One-on-one conference to clarify task
- Visual word walls for key vocabulary

### Gifted Students
- Add a second character (the imaginary friend's perspective)
- Write the narrative from the imaginary friend's POV
- Add a subplot
- Extend to a chapter book format with multiple linked narratives
- Explore alternative narrative tenses (first person, third person limited)

### Students with NEPs
- Adjust word count expectations accordingly
- Provide graphic organiser for story map
- Allow oral retelling as alternative (transcribed by teacher or aide)
- Extra time for all writing tasks
- Provide a peer buddy for editing support
- Simplify success criteria to 3 key goals
