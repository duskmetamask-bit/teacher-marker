# Changes in Community and Country — HASS Unit Plan

**Unit Duration:** 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons
**Year Level:** Years 3–4
**Curriculum Link:** Australian Curriculum HASS v9 — Geography and History
**Mentor Text:** *The Rabbits* by John Marsden and Shaun Tan (1988)
**Unit Big Idea:** Australia has changed over time. People and events have shaped our communities and country. Understanding the past helps us understand who we are and where we're going.
**Cold Task:** Week 1, Lesson 1 — Draw and label what you know about "old Australia" and "Australia today"
**Hot Task:** Week 8, Lesson 1 — Compare an aspect of Australia in the past to today, and explain how it has changed

---

## Unit Overview

**Learning Intention:**
We are learning to describe how Australia has changed over time and to identify the people and events that have shaped our communities and country.

**Success Criteria:**
- I can describe at least three ways Australia has changed from the past to today.
- I can identify who has lived in Australia over time and describe how their experiences have differed.
- I can use sources (photos, objects, stories) to find out about the past.
- I can describe how an aspect of daily life has changed in Australia.
- I can explain why it is important to remember and learn from the past.

## WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: Old and New Australia

### Lesson 1: Cold Task — Old Australia and Australia Today
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Hook (5 min) | Show two images: a 1950s Australian street scene and a 2020s Australian street scene. "What is the same? What is different? What do you notice?" | Observe images, share observations |
| Task (35 min) | Cold Task: "Draw and label what you know about 'old Australia' and 'Australia today.' Show at least 3 things that are different between the past and today. Write 2–3 sentences explaining one change." Year 3: 3 differences + 2 sentences. Year 4: 5 differences + 4 sentences. | Independent drawing, labelling and writing |

**Differentiation:** Image word bank provided. Sentence starters: "One difference is..." / "Australia has changed because..." EAL students may label in home language first then add English.

**Success Criteria Check:** Student produces a drawing showing at least 2 differences between past and present Australia.

---

### Lesson 2: Meet The Rabbits
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Engage (Hook)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Read Aloud (15 min) | Read *The Rabbits* by John Marsden and Shaun Tan aloud, showing illustrations. Read slowly — the text is sparse and the illustrations carry the story. Pause at key spreads: first arrival, colonial buildings, displacement of animals, environmental destruction. | Listen, observe illustrations |
| Notice and Wonder (10 min) | "What do you notice?" (Record — the animals, the landscape, what is being built, what is being lost) / "What questions do you have?" (Who are the rabbits? What happened to the land? Is this Australia?) | Share observations and questions |
| Connect (10 min) | "The Rabbits is a story about Australia — about colonisation. The rabbits represent one group of people who came to Australia. What do you think life was like before they arrived? What changed after?" | Contribute to class discussion |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can identify at least 2 changes shown in The Rabbits illustrations.

---

### Lesson 3: What is History?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Engage (Concept Introduction)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What is History? (10 min) | "History is the story of what happened in the past. History helps us understand who we are and why things are the way they are. Without history, we wouldn't know how our country, our community, or our families got to where they are today." | Listen and discuss |
| History Sources (10 min) | Introduce sources: photos, letters, diaries, objects, buildings, paintings, stories passed down. Show 3 examples: a sepia photo of a 1920s school, a digeridoo (object), a story from a grandparent. | Examine and discuss |
| Apply the Concept (15 min) | Students revisit their Cold Task drawing. "Now that you know what history is, what question would you like to find the answer to about Australia's past?" Share with partner. | Revise drawing, discuss questions |
| Exit Ticket (5 min) | "Why is it important to learn about history?" | Written response |

**Differentiation:** Visual sources displayed permanently. EAL students may draw their response. Gifted students: bring in one object from home that tells a story about the past.

**Success Criteria Check:** Can explain what history is and give one example of a historical source.

## WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Who Has Lived in Australia?

### Lesson 1: First Australians — Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Opening (5 min) | "Australia is the oldest continent on Earth. Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples have lived here for over 65,000 years — making them one of the oldest continuous cultures in the world." | Listen |
| Introducing Country and Culture (15 min) | Explain: Country/Cultural Areas, connection to land and water, Dreaming stories, over 250 language groups. Show a map of Australia with cultural areas marked. | Observe and note |
| What Was Life Like? (15 min) | Before 1788: sustainable living, complex societies, trade networks, seasonal calendars, oral storytelling. Use *The Rabbits* to contrast: the opening spread shows a rich, biodiverse land before arrival. | Compare and contrast |
| Reflect (5 min) | "What surprised you about what life was like for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples before colonisation?" | Think-pair-share |

**Note:** This lesson must be taught with respect and in consultation with local Aboriginal education support. Use local language groups and cultural knowledge where possible. Recommend a partnership with a local Aboriginal Education Officer.

**Success Criteria Check:** Can name Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples as the First Australians and describe one aspect of their life before 1788.

---

### Lesson 2: Colonisation and Its Effects
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Introduce Colonisation (10 min) | In 1788, people from Britain arrived in Australia and established a colony. This is called colonisation. The British did not understand that Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples already owned and lived on this land. | Listen and note |
| What Changed? (15 min) | Use *The Rabbits* spreads to identify changes: land taken, animals displaced, trees cut down, new buildings built, new laws introduced. The rabbits represent the colonists — bringing changes that disrupted the existing world. | Identify changes from The Rabbits |
| Effects on First Australians (10 min) | Describe: displacement from Country, loss of language and culture, introduction of new diseases, frontier conflict. Age-appropriate and factual — not graphic. | Listen and discuss |
| Connect to Today (5 min) | "Many of these effects are still felt today. Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples have fought hard to keep their culture and language alive. We will learn more about this as we go." | Understand connection to present |

**Note:** Handle with sensitivity. Consult with Aboriginal Education Officer regarding appropriate resources and language for your specific community context.

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 2 effects of colonisation on Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples.

### Lesson 3: Migration to Australia
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What is Migration? (5 min) | "Migration means moving from one place to live in another. Australia has been shaped by waves of migration — people coming from other places to live here." | Listen |
| Waves of Migration (15 min) | Timeline of migration to Australia: 1788 — British colonists / 1850s — Gold rush (Chinese, European) / 1900s — Post-WWII immigration from Europe / 1970s onwards — multicultural Australia (Vietnam, Lebanon, India, China, Africa, Middle East and many more). | Note the waves |
| Why Do People Migrate? (10 min) | People migrate for: work, safety (refugees), education, family, freedom. Show images of migrant families arriving in Australia. | Discuss reasons |
| Our Class Community (10 min) | "Who in our class has family that migrated to Australia? You don't have to share where — just raise your hand." Acknowledge diversity. "Our class is a microcosm of Australia's migration story." | Reflect |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can name at least 2 groups of people who migrated to Australia and describe one reason why they came.

## WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: Changes in Daily Life

### Lesson 1: How Did People Communicate?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Then vs Now (10 min) | Show images: letter writing, telegram, telephone (rotary), mobile phone, internet. "How did people send a message in 1950? How do you send a message today?" | Observe and compare |
| Communication Through Time (15 min) | 1788–1850: spoken word, letters written by hand, word of mouth / 1850–1950: telegraph, telephone, newspapers, radio / 1950–2000: television, fax machines, mobile phones / 2000–present: internet, social media, video calls, instant messaging | Take notes |
| The Rabbits Connection (5 min) | "In The Rabbits, the animals brought their own ways of communicating — building towns, creating laws, writing documents. What did they replace?" | Connect to the text |
| Apply (10 min) | "Choose one type of communication from the past. Draw what it looked like and write 2 sentences about how it has changed today." | Independent activity |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe how one form of communication has changed over time.

---

### Lesson 2: How Did People Travel?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Then vs Now (10 min) | Show images: horse and cart, steam train, early car, Boeing 707, spaceship. "How did people travel in 1900? How do you travel to school? How did your grandparents travel to school?" | Observe and discuss |
| Transport Through Time (15 min) | Before colonisation: walking, canoe, horse / Colonial era: horse and cart, sailing ships / 1800s: steam train, Cobb & Co coach / 1900s: car, tram, early aircraft / 1950s–present: planes, freeways, high-speed rail, electric vehicles | Take notes |
| What Changed? (10 min) | Speed, safety, accessibility, cost, environmental impact. "What are the advantages and disadvantages of modern transport compared to old transport?" | Compare advantages/disadvantages |
| Apply (5 min) | "Draw a mode of transport from the past and a mode of transport from today. How are they different?" | Independent drawing |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can compare one mode of transport from the past to today and describe at least one difference.

### Lesson 3: How Did People Live?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Housing Through Time (10 min) | Before colonisation: gunyah, mia mia, bark shelters, connection to Country / Colonial: simple timber and stone cottages, tents / 1900s: Federation homes, weatherboard / 1950s–60s: brick and tile, suburbs / Today: sustainable design, apartments, smart homes | Observe changes |
| Daily Life Routines (15 min) | Compare a day in the life of a 1900 Australian child vs a 2020s Australian child: school, work, leisure, food, family life. | Compare and contrast |
| Food and Shopping (10 min) | 1900: local shops, fresh food, no refrigerators / 1950s: supermarkets begin / Today: global supply chains, online shopping, farmers markets | Discuss changes |
| Apply (5 min) | "Write 2 sentences: How is the way you live today different from the way people lived 100 years ago?" | Written response |

**Differentiation:** EAL students use image sorting activity. Gifted students: interview a family member about what life was like when they were growing up and report back.

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 2 differences between daily life 100 years ago and today.

## WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Changes in Our Community

### Lesson 1: Our Local Community Then and Now
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Local History Introduction (10 min) | Show historical photos of the local area (from local library, council archives, or Trove). Identify the location — is it the same place? What has changed? | Observe and identify |
| Changes in the Local Area (15 min) | Using photos: What buildings have appeared/disappeared? Who lived here? What did the area look like? What is now there that wasn't before? Create a class comparison chart. | Contribute to chart |
| Using Sources (10 min) | Introduce Trove (trove.nla.gov.au) as a free source of historical Australian photos and newspapers. Demonstrate searching for the local town/suburb. | Observe demonstration |
| Apply (5 min) | "What is one question you would like to answer about our local area's history?" | Write question |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 2 changes that have happened in the local community.

### Lesson 2: Significant Events in Our Community
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What Makes an Event Significant? (10 min) | "A significant event is something that changed the community — good or bad. A new hospital opening, a flood, a centennial celebration, a building being built or torn down." | Listen and note |
| Significant Events in Australian History (15 min) | 1788 — First Fleet arrives / 1851 — Gold rush begins / 1901 — Federation of Australia / 1967 — Indigenous Australians counted in census / 1988 — bicentennial / 2022 — Indigenous voice to parliament referendum. Explain each briefly. | Take notes |
| Connect to Community (10 min) | "What significant events have happened in OUR community? Has there been a major flood? A new building? A sports team winning a championship? A famous person who grew up here?" | Discuss local significance |
| Apply (5 min) | "Which of the events we discussed do you think is the most significant for Australia? Why?" | Write response |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can identify at least 2 significant events in Australian history and explain why they were important.

### Lesson 3: Community Leaders Then and Now
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What is a Leader? (5 min) | "A leader is someone who makes a difference — who changes things for the better, or who inspires others." | Discuss |
| Australian Change Makers (20 min) | Introduce key figures: Sir Edmund Hillary (first to summit Everest) / Eddie Mabo (land rights hero) / Dame Mary Gilmore (poet, activist) / Faith Bandler (civil rights) / Weary Dunlop (WWII) / Ruby Daisy (civil rights) / Kelvin "K.C." (community activist). Show images, brief biography, what they changed. | Take notes on 2–3 figures |
| Local Leaders (10 min) | "Who are leaders in OUR community? Teachers, doctors, coaches, Elders, volunteers?" Acknowledge the leaders present in students' lives. | Identify local leaders |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can name at least 1 change maker and describe what they did.

## WEEK 5 — ELABORATE: Changes in the Environment

### Lesson 1: The Land Before and After
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate (Application)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Opening (5 min) | "Australia is a unique continent. It has plants and animals found nowhere else on Earth. But since 1788, our environment has changed dramatically." | Listen |
| Environmental Changes (20 min) | Before colonisation: rich biodiversity, Aboriginal fire management, sustainable land use / After colonisation: deforestation, introduction of foreign species (rabbits, foxes, cats — shown in The Rabbits), mining, urban sprawl, waterway changes. Use The Rabbits spreads to identify environmental damage — cleared land, displaced animals, pollution. | Note changes |
| Species Loss (10 min) | Australia has the highest rate of mammal extinction in the world. Show images of extinct or endangered animals: thylacine (Tasmanian tiger), Paradise parrot, Leadbeater's possum. Connect: The Rabbits shows this — a rich world becoming empty. | Observe and discuss |
| Apply (5 min) | "What is one way Australia's environment has changed since 1788?" | Written response |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 1 environmental change in Australia since colonisation.

---

### Lesson 2: Protecting Country
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate (Application)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What is Environmental Protection? (10 min) | "Environmental protection means looking after our land, water, and air so that plants, animals, and people can survive and thrive." | Listen |
| Who Protects Country? (15 min) | Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples have been protecting Country for over 65,000 years through practices like controlled burning, marine management, and sacred site protection. Today: national parks, environmental laws, Indigenous land management programs, wildlife reserves. | Note who protects and how |
| What Can We Do? (10 min) | Students generate ideas: reduce waste, protect local wildlife, plant trees, clean up waterways, learn from Indigenous knowledge. Connect to The Rabbits message: "Unless someone like you cares..." | Brainstorm actions |
| Apply (5 min) | "What is one thing YOU could do to help protect Australia's environment?" | Written response |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can name at least 1 way people protect Australia's environment.

---

### Lesson 3: The Rabbits Connection — What Has Changed and What Can We Do?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Elaborate (Synthesis)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Return to The Rabbits (10 min) | Re-read The Rabbits, focusing on the final spreads — the empty landscape, the few remaining animals. "What has been lost? What could have been done differently? What can WE do?" | Re-engage with the text |
| Impact Mapping (15 min) | Create a class impact map: Colonisation → land cleared → native animals displaced → rabbits and other introduced species multiply → environmental damage → what we do now (protect, restore, learn). | Contribute to impact map |
| Personal Commitment (10 min) | "The Rabbits ends on a note of possibility — 'but not all of it.' What could YOU do to help restore or protect an Australian environment? Write 3 things you could do." | Write personal commitment |
| Share (5 min) | Volunteers share one thing they could do. Collect commitments on a class "We Care" poster. | Share |

**Differentiation:** Gifted students: research a specific environmental protection program in Australia (e.g., Great Barrier Reef protection, dingoes on K'gari). NEP students: draw 3 things they could do to help the environment.

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 1 environmental change shown in The Rabbits and 1 action to help protect the environment.

## WEEK 6 — ELABORATE + EVALUATE: Changes in Our Rights

### Lesson 1: Who Had Rights?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What are Rights? (10 min) | "Rights are things every person should have — the right to be treated fairly, to go to school, to have a home, to speak their mind. But not everyone in Australia's history has had the same rights." | Discuss |
| Rights Through Time (15 min) | 1788–1900: Only white British men who owned property could vote. Women could not vote in federal elections until 1902. Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples were not counted as citizens and could not vote in federal elections until 1967. Children worked in factories and mines. | Note the limits |
| Who Didn't Have Rights? (10 min) | Discuss: women (voted later), Indigenous Australians (1967), children (child labour laws changed in 1900s), people with disabilities (deinstitutionalisation movement), migrants (White Australia Policy existed until 1973). Age-appropriate. | Discuss |
| Reflect (5 min) | "Do you think it is fair that not everyone had the same rights? Why or why not?" | Think-pair-share |

**Note:** Handle with sensitivity. Consult with Aboriginal Education Officer. This lesson is about change over time — progress has been made and continues to be made.

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 1 group of people who did not have equal rights in the past.

---

### Lesson 2: How Did Rights Change?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| How Do Rights Change? (10 min) | Rights don't change by accident. People fight for them — activists, community members, leaders. It takes time and courage. | Listen |
| Key Moments in Rights Change (15 min) | 1902 — Women gain the right to vote in federal elections / 1934 — Children no longer allowed to work in factories (child labour laws) / 1948 — All Indigenous Australians become citizens / 1965 — End of the White Australia Policy / 1967 — Indigenous Australians counted in the census and allowed to vote federally / 1975 — Racial Discrimination Act / 1992 — Native Title recognised (Mabo decision) / 2023 — Indigenous Voice to Parliament referendum. | Take notes on key dates |
| Who Fought for Change? (10 min) | Key figures: Sir Henry Parkes (Federation, women's suffrage) / Eddie Mabo (Native Title) / Faith Bandler (1967 referendum) / Charles Perkins (civil rights) / Glow (the dogsled) / Indigenous elders and communities across Australia | Note key figures |
| Apply (5 min) | "What is one right that you have today that someone 100 years ago might not have had?" | Write response |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 1 way rights have improved in Australia over time.

---

### Lesson 3: Rights Today — What Do We Have? What Still Needs to Change?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Evaluate (Critical Thinking)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Rights Today (10 min) | Today, all adult Australian citizens have the right to vote (compulsory). All children have the right to go to school. All people in Australia have the right to free speech (with some limits). The right to healthcare and education. | Note rights today |
| What Still Needs to Change? (15 min) | Honest, age-appropriate discussion: Indigenous Australians still face inequality in health, education, employment, and incarceration rates. The Voice referendum (2022) did not pass — but the conversation continues. Reconciliation and truth-telling remain important. | Discuss honestly |
| The Rabbits Connection (10 min) | "The Rabbits is ultimately a story about power — who has it, who doesn't, and what happens when one group takes over. The lorax-like figure in our Australian story is every person who stands up for what is right." | Connect to the text |
| Apply (5 min) | "If you could change one thing about Australia to make it fairer, what would it be? Write 2–3 sentences." | Written response |

**Differentiation:** EAL students draw their response. Gifted students: research one contemporary Indigenous activist or community leader working for change.

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 1 right they have today and 1 area where Australia still needs to improve.

### Lesson 2: Cold Task vs Now — What Do We Know Now?

## WEEK 8 — HOT TASK

### Lesson 1: Hot Task — Show What You Know
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Summative Assessment

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Set Up (5 min) | "Today is your Hot Task. This is your chance to show everything you've learned about changes in Australia. Read the question carefully. Plan before you write. Take your time." | Receive task, read carefully |
| Planning Time (10 min) | Allow 10 minutes for planning: students may create a rough draft plan, diagram, or organiser before writing. | Plan response |
| Hot Task (25 min) | Students write/draw their response independently. "Compare an aspect of Australia in the past to today. Describe what it was like in the past, describe what it is like today, and explain how it has changed." Year 3: drawing + 5–8 sentences. Year 4: detailed comparison + 8–12 sentences. | Independent completion |

**Assessment Note:** Use rubric (pages 10–12) to assess. Compare to Cold Task for growth analysis in Lesson 2.

**Differentiation:** EAL students may use bilingual dictionaries and word banks. NEP students may dictate responses or use oral presentation format. Extra time provided where applicable.

## DIFFERENTIATION NOTES

### EAL Learners
- **Visual Word Wall:** Permanently displayed in classroom. Key vocabulary: history, past, present, change, colonisation, migration, environment, rights, significant, perspective, source. Updated each week.
- **Sentence Starters:** Provided for all written tasks. Example: "One difference between the past and today is..." / "In the past, people..." / "Now, people..." / "I think this changed because..."
- **Bilingual Glossary:** Key terms with home language translations where available (compiled with EAL teacher support).
- **Home Language Allowance:** Students may use home language for initial brainstorming and planning. English used for final published work.
- **Visual Scaffolding:** All timelines and comparison charts pre-labelled with key terms. Image-based activities used wherever possible.
- **Partner Support:** EAL students paired with supportive partners during group work and discussions.

### Gifted Learners
- **Research Depth:** Research a specific historical event or figure in depth — include multiple perspectives, primary sources, and connections to today.
- **Comparative Study:** Compare Australia's history to another country's history (e.g., New Zealand, Canada) — how were the experiences of Indigenous peoples similar or different?
- **Creative Extension:** Write a sequel to The Rabbits — what happens after? Create a new story about Australia that addresses what has been learned and what could be different.
- **Museum Curation:** Research a real Australian museum exhibit and create a virtual exhibit about a period or event studied in the unit.
- **Contemporary Connections:** Research a current social justice issue in Australia connected to the unit themes (e.g., Indigenous youth education, climate change, refugee rights) and present findings.

### Students with NEPs (National Education Program / Learning Support)
- **Length Adjustments:** Written work expectations reduced to 3–5 sentences for Year 3 and 5–7 sentences for Year 4 on written tasks.
- **Word Banks:** Provided for all written tasks. Key words highlighted in task instructions.
- **Oral Presentation Option:** Students may present verbally instead of in writing for any task. Teacher records response on observation sheet.
- **Extra Time:** Additional time allocated for all written and investigation tasks.
- **Scaffolded Templates:** Partially completed timelines, Venn diagrams, and writing frames provided.
- **Seating and Resources:** Front-of-room seating, simplified task instructions, one-to-one check-ins during independent work.
- **Visual Timelines:** Large, visual timelines displayed in classroom showing key dates and events covered in the unit.

## ASSESSMENT NOTES

### Formative Assessment (Ongoing Throughout Unit)
- Exit tickets (Lessons 1.3, 3.3, 4.2, 7.1)
- Cold Task review (Lesson 2.3)
- Observation during discussions (Weeks 2–4)
- Exit ticket comparison (Lesson 7.1)
- Self-reflection journals (Lessons 7.2, 7.3)
- Impact maps and comparison charts (Weeks 5, 6)

### Summative Assessment
- **Cold Task (Week 1, Lesson 1):** Pre-assessment drawing with labels. Establishes prior knowledge baseline. Used for growth comparison at end of unit.
- **Hot Task (Week 8, Lesson 1):** Post-assessment comparing understanding of changes in Australia before and after unit. Assessed against rubric.
- **Growth Analysis (Week 8, Lesson 2):** Students complete self-assessment comparing Cold Task to Hot Task. Teacher uses rubric to quantify growth using A–E scale.

### Data for Reporting
- Rubric marks for Hot Task across all five criteria (Historical Knowledge, Chronological Understanding, Historical Sources, Perspectives, Communication)
- AC9HS3K01, AC9HS4K01, AC9GEO3K01, AC9GEO4K01 codes assigned based on student performance
- Anecdotal records from discussions and source analysis activities
- Self-reflection journal entries (Weeks 7–8)
