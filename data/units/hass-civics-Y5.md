# Being an Informed Citizen — Civics and Citizenship Unit Plan

**Unit Duration:** 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons
**Year Level:** Year 6
**Curriculum Link:** Australian Curriculum HASS v9 — Civics and Citizenship (Years 5–6)
**Unit Big Idea:** Democracy requires informed citizens who understand how government works, who can evaluate information critically, and who actively participate in civic life. In an era of information overload and misinformation, being an informed citizen is both more important and more challenging than ever.
**Cold Task:** Week 1, Lesson 1 — Investigate how the Australian Constitution works and evaluate its strengths and weaknesses
**Hot Task:** Week 8, Lesson 1 — Investigate a current civic issue, evaluate information sources critically, present an informed position, and explain how citizens can participate in the democratic process

---

## Unit Overview

**Learning Intention:**
We are learning to understand how the Australian Constitution works, evaluate information sources critically, understand citizens' rights and responsibilities, and actively participate in democratic life as informed citizens.

**Success Criteria:**
- I can describe how the Australian Constitution works and its significance.
- I can evaluate information sources and identify misinformation and bias.
- I can explain the rights and responsibilities of Australian citizens.
- I can describe at least 3 ways citizens can actively participate in democracy.
- I can develop and defend an informed position on a civic issue.
- I can use sources to investigate civic issues and evaluate their reliability.

### Lesson 2: What Is a Constitution?
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Engage (Hook)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What Is a Constitution? (10 min) | "A CONSTITUTION is the supreme law of a country. It: sets out the STRUCTURE of government / defines the POWERS of each branch of government / defines the RIGHTS of citizens (in some constitutions) / can only be changed through a SPECIAL PROCESS. Everything else — laws, policies, decisions — must comply with the Constitution." | Learn |
| Australia's Constitution (10 min) | "The Australian Constitution was written in the 1890s and came into effect on 1 January 1901. It was designed to unite the 6 colonies into a federation while preserving states' powers. It is one of the oldest written constitutions still in use in the world." | Learn |
| Key Features (15 min) | "Key features of the Australian Constitution: ESTABLISHES the federal system (division of powers between federal and state) / CREATES the High Court (guardian of the Constitution) / SETS OUT the composition of parliament / CAN BE AMENDED (changed) only by a referendum (popular vote, requiring double majority — national majority AND state majority)." | Learn |
| What It Does NOT Include (10 min) | "Critically, the Australian Constitution does NOT include: a comprehensive Bill of Rights (rights are protected in a patchwork of laws) / explicit protection of freedom of speech / explicit right to privacy. This is a key difference from many other democracies." | Learn |
| Write (5 min) | "Write 3 sentences: What is a constitution? What does the Australian Constitution do? What is one thing it does NOT include?" | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe what a constitution is, what the Australian Constitution does, and at least 1 thing it does not include.

### Lesson 3: The Australian Constitution — Analysis
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Engage (Concept Introduction)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Reading the Constitution (10 min) | "The Constitution is a legal document — written in formal, old-fashioned language. It is divided into CHAPTERS and SECTIONS. Key sections: Section 1 — the Parliament / Section 51 — powers of the federal parliament / Section 106 — state constitutions / Section 128 — how to amend the Constitution." | Learn |
| Division of Powers (15 min) | "The Constitution divides powers: FEDERAL POWERS (defence, immigration, trade, tax) / STATE POWERS (education, police, local government) / CONCURRENT POWERS (both can make laws on these). If state and federal laws conflict, federal law wins." | Learn |
| How to Change the Constitution (10 min) | "The Constitution can ONLY be changed by REFERENDUM: a proposal is voted on nationally / it must achieve a DOUBLE MAJORITY: national majority (>50%) AND majority of states (at least 4 of 6 states) / since 1901, only 8 of 44 referendums have passed. This makes constitutional change very hard." | Learn |
| Exit Ticket (5 min) | "Write 3 sentences: How can the Australian Constitution be changed? Why is it so hard to change?" | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe the division of powers between federal and state government and explain how the Constitution can be amended.

### Lesson 2: Checks and Balances
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What Are Checks and Balances? (10 min) | "CHECKS AND BALANCES: mechanisms that prevent any one person or institution from having too much power. Key checks: PARLIAMENT checks the government (MPs debate and vote) / COURTS check the parliament (can strike down laws as unconstitutional) / MEDIA checks the government (exposes problems) / OPPOSITION checks the government (questions, challenges) / CITIZENS check the government (vote, protest, media)." | Learn |
| The High Court as Check (10 min) | "The HIGH COURT is the ultimate guardian of the Constitution. It can rule that a law passed by parliament is UNCONSTITUTIONAL and therefore invalid. This is a powerful check — even democratically elected governments cannot override the Constitution." | Learn |
| Famous High Court Cases (15 min) | "The High Court has made significant rulings: MABO (1992) — recognised native title, overturning terra nullius / PARDON (1999) — released Harry Blackburn from jail — a landmark case about rights / 2019 — the Canberra federal case about whether a minister could spend money without parliamentary approval." | Learn |
| Write (5 min) | "Write 3 sentences: What are checks and balances? How does the High Court act as a check on parliament?" | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 3 checks and balances and explain how the High Court checks the power of parliament.

### Lesson 3: The Australian Federal System
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Federalism in Australia (10 min) | "FEDERALISM: a system where power is divided between a national government and state/territory governments. Australia is a federation — established in 1901. Federalism means there are TWO levels of government, each with their own powers." | Learn |
| Federal vs State Powers (15 min) | "FEDERAL POWERS (Constitution Section 51): defence / immigration / foreign affairs / trade / taxation / marriage / corporations / quarantine / BANKING. STATE POWERS (Constitution Section 107): schools / hospitals / police / roads / local government / property / most criminal law." | Learn |
| State Governments (10 min) | "Each state has its own parliament (Western Australia, NSW, Victoria, Queensland, South Australia, Tasmania) with its own constitution. States can make laws on everything except federal matters. WA's Premier leads the state government." | Learn |
| Write (5 min) | "Write 3 sentences: What is federalism? What is the difference between federal and state powers? Which level of government is responsible for schools?" | Write |

**Differentiation:** EAL students create a visual of the federal/state divide. Gifted students: research one specific example of federal-state conflict.

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe the federal system and identify at least 2 federal powers and 2 state powers.

## WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: Rights and Responsibilities

### Lesson 1: Constitutional Rights
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What Rights Does the Constitution Protect? (10 min) | "The Australian Constitution provides LIMITED rights protections: the RIGHT TO VOTE in federal elections / FREEDOM OF RELIGION (implied) / RIGHT TO BE COMPENSATED when property is acquired by government / the HIGH COURT can protect certain rights in federal criminal cases. This is much LESS than most comparable democracies." | Learn |
| What Rights Are NOT Protected? (15 min) | "Rights NOT protected: explicit FREEDOM OF SPEECH (not constitutionally protected, only protected by common law and some legislation) / RIGHT TO PRIVACY / RIGHT TO EDUCATION / RIGHT TO HEALTHCARE / RIGHT TO HOUSING. These are provided by ordinary laws, not the Constitution — meaning they can be removed more easily." | Learn |
| The Rights Debate (10 min) | "There is an ongoing debate: SHOULD Australia have a comprehensive Bill of Rights? Arguments FOR: better protection of individual rights / alignment with international standards / Australia is out of step with other democracies. Arguments AGAINST: our current system works / rights could be exploited / courts could overreach / parliamentary sovereignty should be preserved." | Debate |
| Exit Ticket (5 min) | "Write 3 sentences: What rights does the Australian Constitution protect? What rights are missing? What is the debate?" | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe what rights the Constitution protects and does not protect, and articulate the debate about a Bill of Rights.

### Lesson 3: The Rights Debate
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|

## WEEK 4 — EXPLAIN: Information and Democracy

### Lesson 1: Misinformation and Democracy
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What Is Misinformation? (10 min) | "MISINFORMATION: false or misleading information shared without intent to harm. DISINFORMATION: false information shared WITH intent to harm. Both are threats to democracy because: voters make decisions based on wrong information / trust in institutions is eroded / public health decisions are undermined / social cohesion is damaged." | Learn |
| Misinformation in Politics (15 min) | "Misinformation in politics: POLITICAL ADVERTISING (can contain misleading claims) / SOCIAL MEDIA (viral misinformation spreads faster than corrections) / DEEPFAKES (AI-generated fake videos) / SELECTIVE FACTS (true facts taken out of context). All parties use misleading tactics — it is not just one side." | Learn |
| Case Study: Misinformation (10 min) | "A real example: COVID misinformation (vaccines cause infertility — false / COVID is just a flu — misleading / treatments that don't work). This misinformation cost lives. Democracy requires accurate information for citizens to make informed decisions." | Learn |
| Write (5 min) | "Write 3 sentences: What is misinformation? How does it threaten democracy?" | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can define misinformation and describe at least 2 ways it threatens democracy.

### Lesson 2: Evaluating Sources
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| How to Evaluate Sources (10 min) | "To evaluate a source, ask: WHO wrote this? (author/organisation) / WHEN was it written? (currency matters) / WHY was it written? (inform? persuade? sell?) / WHERE was it published? (reputable outlet? social media?) / WHAT evidence is provided? (sources cited? peer-reviewed?)" | Learn |
| Evaluating Online Information (15 min) | "For online information: CHECK the URL (is it the real site?) / CHECK the author (do they exist?) / CHECK the date (is it current?) / CHECK other sources (do they agree?) / CHECK for bias (is one side missing?) / CHECK the evidence (what proof is offered?)." | Learn |
| Lateral Reading (10 min) | "LATERAL READING: instead of evaluating a page on its own, open NEW TABS and check what OTHER sources say. This is what professional fact-checkers do. If multiple reputable sources say the same thing, it is more likely accurate." | Learn |
| Practice (5 min) | "Evaluate a sample webpage. Is it reliable? Why or why not? Use the checklist." | Practice |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can apply at least 5 evaluation criteria to assess the reliability of a source.

### Lesson 3: Bias and Perspective
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| All Sources Have Perspective (10 min) | "All sources have perspective — no source is completely neutral. Perspective is shaped by: the author's beliefs and values / the organisation's interests / the audience they are writing for / what they omit / the language they use. Recognising perspective is not the same as dismissing information." | Learn |
| Types of Bias (10 min) | "Types of bias: CONFIRMATION BIAS (seeking information that confirms existing beliefs) / SELECTION BIAS (choosing facts that support one view) / LANGUAGE BIAS (using loaded words) / SOURCE BIAS (citing only one side) / COMMERCIAL BIAS (driven by advertising revenue) / POLITICAL BIAS (supporting one political position)." | Learn |
| Reading Against the Grain (10 min) | "READING AGAINST THE GRAIN: actively seek out perspectives you disagree with. This is how you develop nuanced understanding. It is not about accepting all views as equally valid — it is about understanding what different people believe and why." | Learn |
| Write (5 min) | "Write 3 sentences: What is bias? How can you identify bias in a source? Why is it important to read against the grain?" | Write |

**Differentiation:** EAL students evaluate a source visually. Gifted students: analyse the bias profile of a specific news outlet.

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 3 types of bias and apply lateral reading to evaluate a source.

### Lesson 2: Arguments and Evidence

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Strong vs Weak Arguments (10 min) | "STRONG arguments: based on evidence, logic, and facts / acknowledge counter-arguments / consider different perspectives / address the strongest version of the opposing view.
