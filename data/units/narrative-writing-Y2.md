# Narrative Writing Unit: The Twits

**Unit Duration:** 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons
**Year Level:** Year 2
**Curriculum Link:** Australian Curriculum English v9 - Narrative Writing
**Mentor Text:** *The Twits* by Roald Dahl (1981)
**Writing Focus:** Imaginative narrative with comic villain characters, disgusting humour, trickster plots
**Cold Task:** Pre-assessment (Weeks 1, Lesson 1) - Write about a tricky or silly character
**Hot Task:** Post-assessment (Week 8, Lesson 1) - Same as Cold Task

## Unit Overview

**Learning Intention:**
We are learning to write an imaginative narrative about silly, tricky or surprising characters with funny problems and endings.

**Success Criteria:**
- I can write a story with a beginning, middle and end.
- I can create a silly or tricky character with funny traits.
- I can make my characters solve a problem in a funny way.
- I can use story language: once upon a time, then, next, finally.
- I can write sentences that make sense.

## WEEK 1 — INTRODUCTION TO THE TWITS

### Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Assessment

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Hook (5 min) | "Today we are going to write about a silly, tricky or surprising character. Think about someone who is very funny or does surprising things." | Listen and think |
| Task (30 min) | Cold Task: Write about a silly or tricky character and something funny they do. Draw a picture too. Year 2: 50–100 words. Focus: Can you create a character and tell a short story? | Independent writing |

**Differentiation:** Word bank with silly words. Sentence starters available.

---

### Lesson 2: Meet the Twits (I Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Read (15 min) | Read *The Twits* — Chapters 1–3 (Mr and Mrs Twit introduction). Stop to discuss: What do they look like? What do they do? Are they like other characters? | Listen and note |
| Discuss (10 min) | Mr and Mrs Twit are villains — they are horrible to each other and to animals. But they are also VERY funny. How does Dahl make them funny? List ideas on board | Analyse author techniques |
| Share (10 min) | "What is the silliest thing about the Twits? What is the most surprising?" | Share opinions |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 2 funny/surprising things about the Twits.

---

### Lesson 3: What Makes a Good Character? (We Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | What makes Mr and Mrs Twit memorable? 1. They look unusual 2. They do horrible things 3. They are silly even when mean 4. They get what they deserve. Make a list on board | Observe |
| We Do (15 min) | Create a character traits chart together: WHAT THEY LOOK LIKE / WHAT THEY DO / WHAT MAKES THEM FUNNY | Build chart collaboratively |
| You Do (10 min) | Think of a funny character you know (from a book, TV or your imagination). Write 3 things about them. | Character brainstorming |

## WEEK 2 — SILLY CHARACTERS

### Lesson 1: Creating a Silly Character (I Do → We Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Model: "Mrs Chipps was a woman who never, ever smiled. She had teeth like piano keys and wore a hat made of dead fish." What makes THIS character funny? Discuss | Analyse character creation |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Silly character ingredients: Funny name + Funny look + Funny habit = Character worth writing about | Take notes |
| We Do (10 min) | Class invents a silly character together: Name? Look? Habit? Write it on board | Collaborate |
| You Do (5 min) | Start planning your own silly character — just the name for now | Idea generation |

---

### Lesson 2: Character Traits (We Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Use The Twits examples: Mr Twit — greedy, mean, stupid, spiteful. Mrs Twit — cunning, cruel, clever at tricks. "What words describe YOUR character?" | Discuss traits |
| We Do (15 min) | Build a character profile on large paper: NAME / LOOKS / HABITS / TRAITS / WHAT THEY WANT | Build class profile |
| You Do (10 min) | Fill in YOUR character profile: 5 traits, 3 habits, 1 thing they want | Independent profile work |

---

### Lesson 3: Our Character's Looks (You Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Art + Writing

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| You Do (35 min) | Draw your character. Add labels for: funny hair, unusual clothes, special feature, silly accessory. Write 3 sentences about what they look like. | Draw and write |

## WEEK 3 — A TRICKY PROBLEM

### Lesson 1: What's the Problem? (I Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Read (10 min) | Recap The Twits. "What problems do the Twits cause each other? What problems happen TO them?" The circus birds trick, the tree trick, being shrunk. | Identify problems |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | In The Twits, problems are caused by tricks. "What kind of problems could YOUR character have?" Problem types: Someone is mean to them / They want something they can't get / They make a mistake | Understand problems |
| You Do (15 min) | Decide: What problem does your character face? Write 2 sentences about it. | Problem definition |

---

### Lesson 2: A Clever Trick (We Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | "The Twits play tricks on each other. What tricks do they play?" List tricks from the book: upside-down cake trick, frog trick, glass eye trick, getting shrunk. What makes these tricks funny? | Analyse tricks |
| We Do (15 min) | Brainstorm: "What clever trick could YOUR character use to solve their problem?" Make a class list of trick ideas | Collaborate |
| You Do (10 min) | Decide on YOUR trick — the clever way your character solves their problem | Choose solution |

---

### Lesson 3: Planning Our Story (You Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Planning

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (5 min) | Show a story map: BEGINNING (setup) → MIDDLE (trick) → END (result) | Observe |
| You Do (30 min) | Complete a story map: Draw BEGINNING, MIDDLE, END. Label each part in 1–2 words. | Independent planning |

**Success Criteria Check:** Story map shows character, problem, trick and ending.

## WEEK 4 — WRITING THE BEGINNING

### Lesson 1: Starting Our Story (I Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | "Once upon a time there was a..." This is how stories start. But The Twits starts differently. "Mr and Mrs Twit were not the sort of people you would want to invite to tea." This tells us something important. | Analyze opening |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Story openings can: Tell us who the character is / Tell us something funny / Set the scene. Model writing 3 different openings for a story | Note techniques |
| You Do (15 min) | Write 2 different openings for your story. Choose the one you like best. | Draft openings |

---

### Lesson 2: Setting the Scene (We Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | Where does your story happen? Write 2 sentences describing the setting. Make it match your character. | Model setting |
| We Do (15 min) | Share settings with class. If character is messy, setting might be messy. If character is magical, setting might be magical. Discuss. | Discuss relationship |
| You Do (10 min) | Write your setting sentences into your story | Write setting |

---

### Lesson 3: Introducing Characters (You Do)
**Time:** 35 minutes | **Type:** Independent

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| You Do (30 min) | Write the BEGINNING of your story: 2–3 sentences introducing your character and setting. Check: Does it tell us who? Where? What is funny? | Write beginning |
| Share (5 min) | Read 2 beginnings to class | Share work |

## WEEK 5 — THE MIDDLE

### Lesson 1: The Trick Unfolds (I Do → We Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching + Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | "In the middle of the story, the trick happens. The problem gets bigger. Things get funnier." Read example from The Twits — the cake scene. What happens? Why is it funny? | Analyze middle |
| We Do (15 min) | Class writes the MIDDLE of the class story together. Use the trick brainstorm from Week 3. | Collaborative writing |
| You Do (15 min) | Write the MIDDLE of your story: What does your character do? How does the trick work? | Write middle |

---

### Lesson 2: Building to the Climax (I Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | The CLIMAX is the funniest or most surprising moment. In The Twits, the climax is when the Twits are shrunk. Write the moment when your trick reaches its funniest point. | Understand climax |
| You Do (30 min) | Write the CLIMAX of your story. Make it the most surprising or funny part. | Write climax |

---

### Lesson 3: Writers Workshop (You Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Peer Feedback

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Independent Writing (30 min) | Write or revise any part of your story. Conference with 4–5 students. | Draft and seek feedback |
| Share (10 min) | One student shares their funniest part for class feedback | Give feedback |

## WEEK 6 — THE FUNNY ENDING

### Lesson 1: How Does It End? (I Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Read (10 min) | How does The Twits end? The Twits are stuck shrinkified, the animals are free. The good characters win. "The end was funny because..." | Analyze ending |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | Endings in silly stories: The trick works and everyone laughs / The mean character gets what they deserve / Something surprising happens | Note ending types |
| You Do (20 min) | Write the ENDING of your story. Make it funny and surprising. | Write ending |

---

### Lesson 2: Polishing Our Writing (We Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Revision

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|

| We Do (15 min) | Swap with partner. Read their story. Give 2 stars (things you liked) and 1 wish (something to improve). | Peer feedback |
| You Do (20 min) | Revise your story based on feedback. Fix anything confusing. Add more funny details. | Revise |

## RUBRIC — Narrative Writing (Year 2)
**AC9E2LY01** — Create short imaginative texts using familiar words and images

| Criterion | A (Excellent) | B (Strong) | C (Satisfactory) | D (Developing) | E (Beginning) |
|-----------|---------------|------------|-----------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Story Structure** | Clear beginning, middle and end. Events in logical order. Satisfying ending. | Has beginning, middle and end. Mostly logical order. Ending clear. | Beginning, middle and end mostly clear. Some order confusion. | Attempt at structure. Story unclear or jumps around. | No clear structure. |
| **Character** | Highly memorable character with specific funny traits. Character's behaviour consistent and funny. | Memorable character with clear funny traits. Behaviour mostly consistent. | Character described with some traits. Some consistency. | Character vague or inconsistent. Not very funny. | No clear character. |
| **Trick/Problem** | Clever, original trick that solves the problem in a very funny way. | Reasonable trick that works to solve problem. Funny. | Trick present but may not fully solve problem. Some humour. | Problem present but solution unclear or not funny. | No clear problem. |
| **Language** | Uses story language effectively. Sentences clear and funny. Some humour in word choices. | Uses some story language. Sentences mostly clear. Some funny words. | Some story language. Simple sentences. Limited humour. | Limited story language. Very simple. | No story language. |
| **Punctuation & Presentation** | Accurate punctuation. Neat presentation. Good effort in illustration. | Minor errors. Neat presentation. | Some errors. Adequate presentation. | Noticeable errors. Untidy. | Significant errors. Very untidy. |

## ADAPTATIONS

### EAL Students
- Allow home language planning and simple English story
- Sentence starters: "Once there was...", "One day...", "Then they...", "Finally..."
- Word bank with silly words
- Allow verbal storytelling with teacher scribe
- Pre-teach vocabulary from The Twits

### Gifted Students
- Add a second character who causes problems for the main character
- Write a sequel to your story
- Create a comic strip version
- Add dialogue between characters
- Write from a different character's point of view

### Students with Additional Needs
- Provide story map with boxes and pictures
- Allow scribe for final copy
- Reduce word count requirement
- Allow drawings to carry story if writing is difficult
- One-on-one check-ins during writing time
- Extra time for all tasks

## ASSESSMENT NOTES

### Formative Assessment (Ongoing)
- Character profile sheets
- Story maps
- Draft writing
- Teacher conferences (Weeks 3, 5, 6)
- Peer feedback sessions

### Summative Assessment
- Cold Task → Hot Task comparison (same rubric, measure growth)
- Published story assessed with rubric
- Student self-reflection: "My funniest part was..." "Next time I will..."

### Tracking Progress
Use the comparison template to document:
1. Cold Task score → Hot Task score
2. Character creativity and consistency
3. Story structure and logic
4. Next writing goal
