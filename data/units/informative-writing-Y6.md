# Informative Writing Unit: A Long Walk to Water

**Unit Duration:** 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons
**Year Level:** Year 6
**Curriculum Link:** Australian Curriculum English v9 - Informative Writing
**Mentor Text:** *A Long Walk to Water* by Linda Sue Park (2011)
**Writing Focus:** Informative writing — true stories, ethics of representation, research-based narrative non-fiction, dual perspective
**Cold Task:** Pre-assessment (Week 1, Lesson 1) - Write an information report about the Lost Boys of Sudan and the water crisis
**Hot Task:** Post-assessment (Week 8, Lesson 1) - Same as Cold Task

## Unit Overview

**Learning Intention:**
We are learning to write an information report about a true humanitarian story — researching with integrity, representing lived experience respectfully, structuring complex information, and writing with precision and empathy.

**Success Criteria:**
- I can research the Lost Boys of Sudan and the water crisis with accuracy and empathy.
- I can structure a complex information report with: Introduction, Historical Context, The Crisis, The Response, Reflection and Relevance.
- I can write with factual, precise language — showing the human cost of numbers.
- I can represent lived experience with integrity — not exploiting, not simplifying.
- I can include primary sources (quotes, data) and secondary analysis.
- I can write a conclusion that connects the historical to the present.

## WEEK 1 — INTRODUCTION TO A LONG WALK TO WATER

### Lesson 1: Cold Task (Assessment)
**Time:** 50 minutes | **Type:** Assessment

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Hook (5 min) | "Linda Sue Park's *A Long Walk to Water* is based on the true story of Salva Dut, one of the Lost Boys of Sudan — thousands of children who walked hundreds of kilometres to escape war in Sudan in the 1980s. It is also about the water crisis in Sudan, which kills thousands of children every year. Today YOU will write about this crisis — researching it with accuracy and empathy." | Think |
| Task (45 min) | Cold Task: Write an information report about the Lost Boys of Sudan and/or the water crisis in South Sudan. Year 6: 400–600 words. Focus: Can you gather facts and write a structured, respectful report? | Independent writing |

**Differentiation:** Background information sheet available. Sentence starters.

---

### Lesson 2: Salva's Story (I Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Read (20 min) | Read *A Long Walk to Water* — Chapters 1–5 (Salva's escape, joining the group, the journey). Park weaves two narratives: Salva's true story in 1985 and a fictional girl named Nya who collects water. The structure itself is an argument — the past and present are connected. | Analyze |
| Discuss (15 min) | "What do we learn about the Lost Boys? What do we learn about the water crisis? How does Park make us CARE about the facts?" Park does not just give statistics — she shows one person's experience. This is ethical representation: the individual is not lost in the data. | Analyze technique |
| Share (5 min) | "What is the connection between Salva's story and the water crisis?" | Note connection |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can explain the dual narrative structure and its purpose.

---

### Lesson 3: The Dual Narrative (We Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | "Park tells two stories: Salva (1985, lost boy) and Nya (present day, water collector). Why two stories? Because they show the SAME CRISIS from two time periods. The connection: Nya's village is near where Salva grew up. Salva, as an adult, returns and builds a well. The stories CONVERGE." | Understand dual narrative |
| We Do (20 min) | Map the two narratives: Salva's arc / Nya's arc / Where they connect. Discuss: Why is this structure powerful? It shows change (or lack of change) over time. It makes the reader follow individuals, not just data. | Map structure |
| You Do (10 min) | Write in journals: What is the "water crisis"? What does it mean to have no clean water? Why does it matter? | Reflect |

## WEEK 3 — RESEARCH — THE WATER CRISIS

### Lesson 1: The Global Water Crisis (I Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (15 min) | "The water crisis: 2 billion people lack safely managed drinking water. 422 million people in sub-Saharan Africa lack basic water access. In South Sudan, 61% of people have no clean water. Women and girls spend 200 million hours EVERY DAY collecting water. Water-related diseases kill 485,000 people per year." | Present data |
| Mini-Lesson (10 min) | "These numbers are staggering. But numbers without stories are abstract. Stories without numbers lack weight. The best information writing combines both." Park's technique: The numbers contextualise, the individual story makes it real. | Note technique |
| You Do (15 min) | Research: Find the most current statistics you can on the water crisis in South Sudan and sub-Saharan Africa. Record sources. Write: What does this number mean in human terms? | Research |

### Lesson 2: Numbers and Lives (We Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| We Do (20 min) | As a class: Take a statistic (e.g., "200 million hours collecting water per day") and translate it into human impact. "200 million hours = 8.3 million days = 22,831 years of human time spent collecting water every single day." How does this land differently from just the number? | Translate numbers |
| You Do (20 min) | Choose 2 statistics from your research. Translate each one into human impact — a calculation and a description. Add to your report notes. | Translate statistics |

## WEEK 4 — REPORT STRUCTURE

### Lesson 1: The Architecture of Complex Reports (I Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explicit Teaching

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (15 min) | A complex information report on a humanitarian crisis has 5 sections: 1. INTRODUCTION — Who is this report about and why does it matter? (Start with an individual, then widen to the crisis.) 2. HISTORICAL CONTEXT — What happened? The Lost Boys, the civil war, the displacement. 3. THE CRISIS — The water crisis in numbers and human stories. What is the current situation? 4. THE RESPONSE — What has been done? What works? What challenges remain? 5. REFLECTION AND RELEVANCE — Why does this matter NOW? What can we learn? What can we do? | Note structure |
| You Do (25 min) | Plan your report: Write a heading for each section. Under each, write 3–5 bullet points of what you will include. Decide: Will you focus on the Lost Boys, the water crisis, or both? | Plan |

---

### Lesson 2: Writing the Introduction (I Do → You Do)
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Writing

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | "The introduction should start with an INDIVIDUAL — a person — then widen to the crisis. Park starts with Salva, one boy, in the middle of a class. Then she zooms out. This is powerful because it makes us care before we know the scale." | Note |
| You Do (30 min) | Write the INTRODUCTION: Start with an individual fact (one person's experience), then state the crisis (millions affected). Hook the reader — make them want to know more. Write 2 versions, choose the best. | Write introduction |

### Lesson 2: The Ethics of Representation (We Do)
**Time:** 45 minutes | **Type:** Guided

| Phase | Teacher | Students |
|-------|---------|---------|
| I Do (10 min) | "When we write about people who have suffered, we have an ETHICAL responsibility. Rules: Don't exploit suffering for dramatic effect. Don't reduce people to their victimhood — they are also strong, creative, hopeful. Don't simplify — the situation is complex. Don't speak FOR people — let them speak for themselves through quotes and testimony." | Note ethics |
| We Do (25 min) | Read your draft. Check: Have I used any exploitative language? Have I reduced people to victims? Have I included their own words? Have I shown both suffering AND strength? Revise any problematic passages. | Check ethics |
| You Do (10 min) | Add a quote from a Lost Boy or someone affected by the water crisis if you have not already. Ensure you have credited them. | Add quote |

## RUBRIC — Informative Writing (Year 6)
**AC9E6LY02** — Plan, draft and publish informative texts

| Criterion | A (Excellent) | B (Strong) | C (Satisfactory) | D (Developing) | E (Beginning) |
|-----------|---------------|------------|-----------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Structure** | All 5 sections present and compelling. Architecture sophisticated. Sections flow seamlessly. Conclusion connects powerfully to present. | All 5 sections present. Logical flow. Strong conclusion. | Structure present but some sections thin or conclusion weak. | Missing sections or unclear structure. | No clear structure. |
| **Research & Facts** | Comprehensive, accurate research. Multiple high-quality sources. Primary sources included (testimony, quotes). Facts are specific, powerful, and credible. | Good research from reliable sources. Primary source present. Facts accurate. | Research present but primary sources limited. Some facts vague. | Limited research. No primary sources. | No real research. |
| **Data Integration** | Data distributed naturally throughout. Numbers translated into human impact. Data serves narrative, not dominates it. | Data mostly integrated. Some translations to human impact. | Data present but may be bunched or not translated. | Data missing or not integrated. | No data. |
| **Ethics of Representation** | Exceptional ethical sensitivity. Lived experience represented with dignity. No exploitation. Voice of affected people present. | Good ethical awareness. Representation mostly respectful. | Some ethical awareness but may be surface-level. | Representation problematic or exploitative. | No ethical consideration. |
| **Sources** | 4+ sources including primary. Fully cited. Format correct. | 4 sources. Mostly reliable. Cited. | 3 sources. Some reliability issues. | Limited sources. | No sources. |
| **Language & Presentation** | Precise, empathetic factual language. No opinions. Professional structure. Visual timeline/map present. | Mostly factual. Good presentation. | Some opinions or vague language. | Many opinions. | No factual language. |

## ADAPTATIONS

### EAL Students
- Pre-teach: genocide, civil war, refugee, humanitarian crisis, epidemic,脱水 (dehydration)
- Provide background information sheet with key terms in home language if available
- Sentence starters for each section
- Allow home language initial research
- Visual timeline to support comprehension

### Gifted Students
- Compare Park's representation of the crisis to another author (e.g., Ishmael Beah's *A Long Way Gone`)
- Research the current status of South Sudan water access — how has it changed since the book was written?
- Write about a different water crisis (Yemen, Bangladesh) and compare approaches
- Study the ethics of Western writers telling non-Western stories — examine the concept of "saviour narratives"

### Students with Additional Needs
- Provide pre-researched fact sheets with key data
- Reduce to 3 sections (Introduction, Crisis, Response)
- Reduce word count to 300–400
- Provide timeline template to complete
- Extra time
- One-on-one support
