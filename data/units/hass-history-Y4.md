# Australia: Past, Present, and Future — History Unit Plan

**Unit Duration:** 8 weeks × 3 lessons per week = 24 lessons
**Year Level:** Year 4
**Curriculum Link:** Australian Curriculum HASS v9 — History (Years 3–4)
**Unit Big Idea:** Australia's history is complex — it is both the story of ancient Indigenous civilisations and a relatively new colonial nation. Understanding the full depth of Australian history, including its challenges and achievements, helps us understand who we are and who we might become.
**Cold Task:** Week 1, Lesson 1 — Investigate a significant period or event in Australian history and explain its impact on the present
**Hot Task:** Week 8, Lesson 1 — Investigate a significant period or event in Australian history, evaluate its impact, and explain how it shapes the Australia of today

---

## Unit Overview

**Learning Intention:**
We are learning to investigate significant periods, events, and people in Australian history, and to evaluate their impact on the Australia of today.

**Success Criteria:**
- I can describe at least 3 significant periods or events in Australian history.
- I can explain at least 3 ways Australian history has shaped the Australia of today.
- I can describe the perspectives of different groups of people in Australian history.
- I can use sources (photos, documents, objects) to investigate history.
- I can sequence key events in Australian history on a timeline.
- I can evaluate the significance of a historical event or period.

## WEEK 1 — ENGAGE: Australia's Deep History

### Lesson 1: Cold Task — Significant Australian History
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Assessment (Formative Pre-Assessment)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Hook (5 min) | Show an image of the Aboriginal flag alongside the Australian flag. "Both flags fly together. Why? What story does this tell about Australia? What do you know about Australia's history?" | Observe and wonder |
| Introduction (5 min) | "In this unit, we will explore Australian history — from 65,000 years ago to today. We will discover that Australia's history is complex, sometimes difficult, and always significant. Understanding our history helps us understand who we are." | Listen |
| Cold Task (30 min) | "Choose ONE significant period or event in Australian history (from images provided or your knowledge). Describe: What happened? When and where? Who was involved? ## WEEK 2 — EXPLORE: Colonial Australia

### Lesson 1: First Contact
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What Happened at First Contact? (10 min) | "FIRST CONTACT between Aboriginal peoples and British settlers was complex and traumatic. The British arrived with no understanding of the sophisticated societies that existed. They declared the land 'Terra Nullius' — empty land. This was legally and factually wrong." | Learn |
| Effects of Contact (15 min) | "First contact had devastating effects: NEW DISEASES (smallpox, measles, influenza — killed up to 90% of some Aboriginal communities) / VIOLENCE (frontier conflicts, massacres — documented but not yet fully counted) / DISPLACEMENT (people pushed off Country) / LOSS OF LANGUAGE AND CULTURE (children taken, cultural practices suppressed). These effects continue today." | Learn |
| Different Experiences (10 min) | "Different Aboriginal communities had different contact experiences: some communities had sustained peaceful contact / some had immediate violent conflict / some had trade relationships first / some maintained their distance. No single story fits all of Australia." | Learn |
| Write (5 min) | "Write 3 sentences: What happened at first contact between Aboriginal peoples and British settlers? What were the effects?" | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 2 effects of first contact on Aboriginal peoples.

### Lesson 2: Early Colonies
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Sydney, Hobart, Brisbane, Perth (10 min) | "Britain established colonies at different times: Sydney (1788) / Hobart (1804) / Brisbane (1824) / Perth (1829). Western Australia was founded separately from Swan River Colony (1829) by James Stirling. Each colony had different challenges — Perth was the most isolated." | Learn |
| Convict vs Free (10 min) | "Early Australia was mostly a penal colony. Convicts were sent for crimes (often minor — theft of food). Over time, free settlers arrived. By the 1850s, free settlers outnumbered convicts. But Australia's convict origins shaped our culture: egalitarian (anti-authority), pragmatic, self-reliant." | Learn |
| Convict Life (10 min) | "Convict life was harsh but sometimes surprising: convicts could earn money, buy property, and marry / emancipated convicts (ticket-of-leave) became respected community members / some convicts were better educated than their overseers / the 'convict stain' faded quickly — many of Australia's founders were former convicts." | Learn |
| Convict Architecture (10 min) | "Convict architecture still stands in Australia: Hyde Park Barracks (Sydney) / Port Arthur (Tasmania) / Fremantle Prison (WA). These buildings tell us about convict life — the design, the materials, the purpose. What do these buildings say about convict society?" | Examine |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 3 aspects of early colony life in Australia.

### Lesson 3: Gold and Growth
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| The Gold Rush Transforms Australia (10 min) | "Gold discovered in 1851 (Bathurst, NSW; Ballarat and Bendigo, VIC) was one of the most transformative events in Australian history. In 10 years, Australia's population tripled. New cities and towns were built. Australia became one of the world's richest countries per capita." | Learn |
| Who Came? (15 min) | "The gold rush brought people from everywhere: Britain, Europe, China, USA, New Zealand. By 1861, 25% of Victoria's population was Chinese. This diversity caused tension — the White Australia Policy (1901) was partly a response. Chinese people contributed enormously to Australian development despite facing discrimination." | Learn |
| Chinese Australians (10 min) | "Chinese Australians have been part of Australia since the gold rush. They established businesses, built railways, worked in market gardens. Their contribution to Australia is enormous and often under-acknowledged. The White Australia Policy discriminated against them for 70 years." | Learn |
| Draw a Gold Rush Scene (5 min) | "Draw a gold field scene. Label at least 3 things you see in the scene." | Draw |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe the gold rush and at least 2 ways it changed Australia.

## WEEK 3 — EXPLORE + EXPLAIN: Federation and Nationhood

### Lesson 1: The Road to Federation
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explore (Investigation)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Why Did Colonies Unite? (10 min) | "By the 1890s, Australia's 6 British colonies were considering uniting into one nation. Why? Defence (a united country is easier to defend) / Economic reasons (free trade between colonies) / National pride / Pressure from labour movements / A desire to be seen as a mature, self-governing nation. But there were also debates about Indigenous inclusion." | Learn |
| The Federation Process (15 min) | "The road to Federation: 1891 — First draft constitution rejected / 1897–98 — Second constitutional convention, national debate / 1899 — Votes in each colony / 1 January 1901 — Australian nation born at Centennial Park, Sydney. Sir Henry Parkes (NSW) called 'Father of Federation' — he pushed for it for 30 years." | Learn |
| The Constitution (10 min) | "Australia's Constitution (1901): established federal government / powers divided between states and federal government / original constitution EXCLUDED Indigenous Australians from counting in the census. This was changed in 1967." | Learn |
| Write (5 min) | "Write 3 sentences: What is Federation? How did it happen? Who were the key figures?" | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe Federation and at least 1 reason why it occurred.

### Lesson 2: Life in the New Nation
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Early 20th Century Australia (10 min) | "In the early 1900s, Australia was: a young, optimistic nation / predominantly British in population and culture / agrarian (wool and wheat were major exports) / politically progressive in some areas (secret ballot, compulsory voting, women's suffrage) / but deeply discriminatory in others (White Australia Policy, Indigenous exclusion)." | Learn |
| The White Australia Policy (15 min) | "The WHITE AUSTRALIA POLICY (1901–1973): restricted non-European immigration. Implemented through the Immigration Restriction Act 1901 (known as the 'White Australia' law). Tests for potential migrants (written in any language except European ones). It was designed to keep Australia 'British.' It was discriminatory and has been formally apologised for." | Learn |
| Women's Suffrage (10 min) | "Australia led the world in women's suffrage. South Australia (1894) and Western Australia (1899) were among the first places in the world where women could vote. By 1902, women could vote in federal elections. Australia was the second nation in the world (after New Zealand) to grant women's suffrage." | Learn |
| What Does This Tell Us? (5 min) | "Write 2 sentences: What does early 20th century Australia tell us about our values? What was good? What was problematic?" | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe at least 2 aspects of early 20th century Australia including the White Australia Policy.

### Lesson 3: The ANZAC Legend
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| What Is the ANZAC Legend? (10 min) | "ANZAC Day (25 April) commemorates Australian and New Zealand Army Corps soldiers who fought at Gallipoli in 1915. The 'ANZAC legend' is the idea that Australian soldiers were uniquely brave, tough, egalitarian, and resilient. This legend became central to Australian national identity." | Learn |
| The Gallipoli Campaign (15 min) | "Gallipoli (1915): Allied forces (British, French, Australian, New Zealand, Indian) tried to force the Ottoman Empire out of WWI by capturing Constantinople (Istanbul). It was a military failure — but Australian soldiers distinguished themselves at Lone Pine and the Nek. 8,700 Australians died at Gallipoli." | Learn |
| Why Does It Matter? (10 min) | "The ANZAC legend matters because: it was Australia's first major engagement as a nation / it created a sense of national identity / the 'digger' soldier became an Australian archetype / it is a story of sacrifice / it created institutions (RSL,Returned and Services League) that still serve veterans." | Learn |
| The Controversy (5 min) | "Some historians argue the ANZAC legend has been OVERUSED — that it promotes militarism, ignores the horror of war, and is used for political purposes. Others argue it is a legitimate story of courage and sacrifice. Both views deserve consideration." | Discuss |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe the ANZAC legend, at least 2 facts about Gallipoli, and explain why it is significant.

### Lesson 2: Indigenous Rights
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| 1967 — The Referendum (10 min) | "In 1967, Australians voted in a REPUBLICAN REFERENDUM (a vote to change the Constitution). The question: Should Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples be counted in the census and should the federal government make laws about them? 90.77% voted YES — one of the highest 'yes' votes in Australian history." | Learn |
| What Changed (10 min) | "1967 changed: Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples were counted in the census / the federal government could now make laws specifically for Indigenous peoples / it was a symbolic moment of recognition / it enabled the Aboriginal Loans Commission (1960s) and land rights frameworks (1970s). It was not perfect — it did not grant rights directly, but it was a crucial step." | Learn |
| Ongoing Struggles (10 min) | "1967 did NOT fix everything: the Stolen Generations (children removed from families 1910–1970) were not addressed / land rights were still needed / Indigenous health, education, and incarceration gaps were not fixed / the referendum was one step in a longer journey." | Learn |
| Key Figures (10 min) | "Key figures in Indigenous rights: CHARLES PERKINS (Aboriginal activist, civil rights leader, first Indigenous university graduate) / EDDIE MABO (land rights hero — Mabo decision 1992 recognised Native Title) / CHARLOTTE RING (activist for Indigenous women's rights). Many others — elders, community members, activists." | Learn |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe the 1967 referendum and at least 2 ways Indigenous rights have been fought for.

### Lesson 3: Migration and Multiculturalism

## WEEK 5 — EXPLAIN: Australia and the World

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| WWI and Australia (10 min) | "Australia joined WWI as part of the British Empire (1914–1918). 416,000 Australians served (out of 5 million population). 60,000 died. 152,000 were wounded. Australia had one of the highest casualty rates per capita of any participating nation." | Learn |

| Social Impact (10 min) | "WWI impacted Australia: grief in every community (every family affected) / changed relationship with Britain (loyalty questioned) / women's suffrage strengthened (women took on men's roles) / changed the economy (post-war reconstruction) / created the Returned and Services League (RSL) / the ANZAC legend." | Learn |
| Write (5 min) | "Write 3 sentences: How did WWI affect Australia? What was the human cost?" | Write |

### Lesson 2: World War II and Post-War
**Time:** 40 minutes | **Type:** Explain (Explicit Teaching)

| Phase | Teacher (I Do) | Students |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| WWII and Australia (10 min) | "WWII (1939–1945) changed Australia fundamentally. Japan attacked Darwin (1942) — first time Australian mainland was bombed. | Why the Immigration Program? (10 min) | "Australia launched mass immigration for: POPULATION (Australia was sparsely populated, vulnerable to being absorbed by larger neighbours) / ECONOMIC GROWTH (more workers, more consumers) / DEFENCE (more people, harder to invade) / It was also partly about 'populate or perish' — a phrase used at the time." | Learn |
| Exit Ticket (5 min) | "Write 2 sentences: Why did Australia launch a big post-war immigration program? How did it change Australia?" | Write |

**Success Criteria Check:** Can describe the post-WWII immigration program and at least 2 ways it changed Australia.

## DIFFERENTIATION NOTES

### EAL Learners
- **Home Culture Research:** Students may investigate their family's connection to Australian history (migration, settlement).
- **Visual Presentations:** Focus on visual timelines, maps, and images.
- **Partner Support:** Paired for research activities.
- **Cultural Sensitivity:** Respect all perspectives on Australian history.

### Gifted Learners
- **Research Extension:** Investigate a specific period in depth (e.g., the Stolen Generations, one gold field, the 1967 referendum).
- **Comparative Essay:** Write a comparative essay on two periods of Australian history.
- **Local History Project:** Create a full local history guide.

### Students with NEPs
- **Reduced Written Expectation:** 5–7 sentences for written tasks.
- **Oral Option:** Students may present verbally.
- **Extra Time:** Additional time for all tasks.
- **Word Banks:** Provided for all written tasks.
- **Scaffolded Templates:** Timeline template, investigation template.
